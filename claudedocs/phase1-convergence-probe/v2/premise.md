# Premise — The Reckoning at Harlow Mill

A fresh, original premise. Invent everything else.

## Setting

Harlow Mill, a failing river town, on the night a once-a-generation storm crests
the old dam upstream. The mill's flood-sluice is the only thing that can bleed the
swelling millpond before the dam overtops. **Sela Harrow**, the gatekeeper's
daughter, has been the keeper in fact since her father's stroke left him voiceless
in a back room — though the town still calls him keeper and her a girl playing at
his work. By law and by the owner's word, only the keeper may open the sluice.

## The cast (invent the rest)

- **Sela Harrow** — the de-facto keeper; competent, unproven in the town's eyes.
- **Garrick Vane** — the mill's owner; he must, at dawn, publicly name whether what
  Sela did in the night was a keeper's duty or a crime. His word is the town's verdict.
- **The lower row** — the poorest cottages, downstream of the sluice. If the sluice
  is opened to bleed the pond, the lower row floods first.

## The inciting turn (the shared spine, before any choice)

In the first hours of the storm Sela discovers the upstream relief gate is jammed —
someone wedged it earlier in the season to keep the pond high for the mill's failing
wheel, and never freed it. So the pond cannot drain the safe way; the dam will
overtop within the hour unless the mill's own sluice is opened. **This wedged-gate
fact is planted early and pays off late** — at dawn it is what decides whether Sela
acted alone or carried out a danger someone else made. (Author it as a setup that
pays off across whatever choice she makes.)

## The fork — ONE real, exclusive choice (mid-story)

Sela reaches the sluice with the crest minutes away and must choose, and she cannot
do both:

- **(A) Open the sluice.** The pond bleeds, the dam holds — but the lower row floods
  in the dark before anyone can be woken, and lives are lost there. Sela is the hand
  that opened the water onto the poorest.
- **(B) Hold the sluice and ride to wake the lower row.** No one is deliberately
  drowned — but the dam overtops, the valley takes the flood unshaped, and the mill
  itself (the town's living) is wrecked.

These are genuinely different worlds: different dead, different ruin, different town
mood, different things Garrick and the lower row know by morning.

## The convergence — REQUIRED (this is the spine of the assignment)

Whichever path the night takes, the story MUST arrive at the SAME dawn reckoning:

1. **The mill-yard reckoning.** At first light the town gathers in the mill yard and
   Sela stands before them. Garrick must say aloud whether she acted as keeper or as
   criminal — the SAME confrontation, the SAME question, the SAME moment, on BOTH
   paths. What is said and who is standing there differs by what the night did, and
   the wedged-gate truth surfacing here is what turns Garrick's verdict — but it is
   ONE reckoning that both world-lines reach, not two unrelated endings.
2. **The river's edge.** After the verdict, on BOTH paths, Sela walks alone to the
   river's edge in the grey light and looks at the water that did what it did. The
   SAME final image closes both world-lines.

The two paths converge: they run apart through the night and come back together at
the one dawn that judges them. Author the story so this convergence is real — the
reckoning and the river's edge are the shared destination both choices lead to, and
they carry forward what each path made true.

## What the story needs (scope)

- ~16–20 scenes total. A shared opening spine (the storm, the jammed gate, up to the
  sluice), then the ONE exclusive fork, then each path's distinct middle, then the
  shared dawn reckoning + the river's edge that BOTH paths reach.
- The wedged-gate setup planted early and paid off at the reckoning on every path
  (a real long-range setup→payoff that crosses the fork).
- At least one character who believes something false and acts on it (e.g. the town
  believing Sela's father is still the keeper, or someone believing Sela opened the
  sluice out of spite) — distinct from the ground truth.
- Tell the best, most coherent version of this story you can. Make every consequence
  at the reckoning trace to what the night actually did.
