## sc-05 — The lantern-house — the boy's missing master

The lantern-house leaned at the edge of town where the road gave up and became the descent, and a boy of maybe twelve kept it lit. Tam, he said his name was, scrubbing at his nose with the back of a sooty hand. The lamps were his master's trade — were, he caught himself, and then said *are* very firmly, as if the word could hold a man up.

"He went down," Tam told them. "Last of all of them. Said he'd be back by the third night and I should keep the big lamp burning so he'd have a light to climb to." The boy's eyes went to the big lamp, burning still, and came back. "That was twelve nights ago."

And he had not gone down idle, the boy was quick to tell them, as if it might matter to the finding. His master had got a notion in his head these last weeks — about the dead going into the water. Fresh dead, new bodies, where there'd no business be any. "He said somebody was *putting* them down there. Feeding the flood. And he meant to learn who." Tam said it proudly and a little fearfully both, the way a child repeats a dangerous thing a grown man said. "He went down to follow where the new ones came from. Said he'd have a name for it when he climbed back up." The boy did not seem to hear what he'd told them — only that his master had gone down on a purpose and a purpose was a thing a man came back from.

He did not ask the way a beggar asks. He asked the way a child asks who has decided the asking will work because it has to. "You're going down. Find him. He's only lost — it's dark down there, anybody could get turned around in the dark. Just find him and bring me word." Pip, who had been half a step apart from the others the whole morning, found the boy looking straight at her, and took the errand. "We'll bring you word," she said. "On my hand, we will." The boy nodded as though that settled it.
