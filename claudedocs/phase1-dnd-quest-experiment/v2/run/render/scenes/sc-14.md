## sc-14 — The journal names the corpse-tithe

They dried their hands and crowded the lamp and Lysa read it out, because she read fastest and her voice didn't shake. The delver had been a careful man to the end, and the journal was the careful man's whole hunt set down day on day. He'd done what they were doing — followed the rising back toward its cause — only he'd got further, because he'd had longer and gone alone and been afraid the right amount. He'd marked where the fresh dead went into the water. He'd watched the floodwater take new bodies, night on night, and he'd not been content to know *that* it happened; he'd set himself to learn whose hand did it. Page on page he tracked it — a watch kept, a path followed, the source of the corpse-tithes run down at last to the one who fed them. And the last pages told where the tracking had led him, and the writing of it had got him killed.

It had led him to the shrine. To the shrine-keeper. To Brother Vane.

It sat among them in the lamplight like a fifth body. The delver had followed the fresh dead to the very hand that put them in the water — the warm brother with both hands out, the friend who'd blessed their bowed heads and asked only that they bring him the crown to keep it safe. He was the living hand behind the whole season of the drowned dead. The buried cause of it, that the reeve had called a curse and Maeve had called a hand and this dead man had hunted down to a name — surfaced, here, with the name on it in a dead man's careful script.

Lysa lowered the journal and set the rest of it together, fast and cold and certain, the way she set anything together. She laid the journal's hunt beside the two things they'd carried down into the dark without quite knowing what they were worth. Maeve's lore, first — *a tithe wakes nothing twice; if the dead are walking now, someone living is feeding the water now.* Someone living. And then the thing Pip had just laid over the body: that the new tithe smelled of incense, of shrine-work. Three pieces, and they were the one piece. The wise-woman's living hand, the rogue's shrine-smell, and a dead man's hunt run to ground at Brother Vane's own door. "It was never a curse," Lysa said. "There's no curse in any of it. It was him. It was Vane that woke the warden, and we've three witnesses to it now and one of them died getting it." She had no more doubt of it than she had of the runes on the wall.
