## sc-18 — The crown's true nature, read in runes

With the warden's record read whole, Lysa could tell them at last what the crown actually *was*, and it was stranger than any of them had braced for. The Hollow Crown did not merely command the dead. It held *Orrek* — his shade, bound into the relic as surely as the dead were bound to him. The warden and the crown were one keeping, and to deal with the one was to deal with the other. There were three roads the relic allowed and no more, she told them, and she named them out plainly so the choice would stand clear when it came.

The first was to *unmake* it — to shatter the crown by an unmaking she alone had read in the runes and alone could speak, and so end the binding by ending the thing that bound. The second was to *claim* it — to take the crown up in living hands and pass its power to a new holder, the leash of the dead changing grip but not breaking. And the third — this was the one the runes had given up last, and the one that changed the colour of the other two — the third was the *oath*. For the record said Orrek kept the dead under *duty*, not malice; a keeper worn past the end of his charge, holding on only because no one had ever come to take the holding from him kindly. A shade like that would hear a fair oath fairly sworn. It could be bound by a swearing — the rising laid down, the dead set at rest, the crown neither broken nor seized. Three roads, she said again, so none of them would mistake it: unmake it, claim it, or bind it by an oath. To leave it on his brow and walk away was no road at all — that was only refusing to choose, and the water would go on rising while they refused.

Mother Henna heard the third road named and something in her settled and stood up at the same time. She understood, the moment Lysa said it, that the oath was a thing she herself could do — no shattering and no seizing in it at all. She could speak to him. She could swear to Orrek's shade and bind the dead by the swearing, and lay the whole rising down without ever breaking the crown or taking it. The thought of it steadied her hand the rest of the way down to the door.
