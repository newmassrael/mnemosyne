## sc-13 — The last delver's body and journal

They found him in a side-cell off the passage, half out of the floodwater, one arm still hooked over a broken bench as if he'd dragged himself up to die a little drier. He'd been dead a while. The water had been kind enough to keep him whole and unkind enough to make sure no one would ever set the face right in a mother's memory.

It was Pip who waded in and went through him, gently, the way you go through the dead when you mean them no disrespect, only need what they're past needing. His journal was still on him, oilskin-wrapped, the wrapping done careful — a man's last care, keeping his words dry when he could no longer keep himself so. She lifted it free and held it a moment before she said anything, because she already knew, looking at the careful wrapping and the careless body, what it meant. This was Tam's master. This was the answer she'd sworn to bring the boy. He was not lost. He was dead, and the proof of it was in her hands, and so was whatever he'd died learning.

And there was a thing she'd been carrying since Maeve's door that she'd told no one — kept it the way she kept everything, close, until it had somewhere to sit. It had somewhere to sit now. She crouched there over the drowned man with the journal in her hand and she said it out at last, low, to the three of them. "The old woman. Maeve. When you'd all gone out I heard her talking to her fire. She said the new tithe had a smell on it." Pip looked from the careful oilskin to the careful body. "Incense, she said. Said it smelled like shrine-work." She let the word fall and lie there beside the dead man's last care, and did not say what it meant, because she didn't have to. "I should've said it sooner. I'm saying it now."
