## The three audit questions

### (a) Does the journal NAMING Brother Vane have a shown investigation chain?

**Yes — the chain is shown, but it is the dead delver's chain, summarized,
not one the party performed.** sc-14 narrates the journal's contents in
explicit terms: the delver "marked where the fresh dead went into the
water," "watched the floodwater take new bodies, night on night," "set
himself to learn whose hand did it," and "page on page he tracked it — a
watch kept, a path followed, the source of the corpse-tithes run down at
last to the one who fed them," and "the last pages told where the tracking
had led him." Then plainly: "It had led him to the shrine. To the
shrine-keeper. To Brother Vane."

So the NAME does not arrive as a bare conclusion floating free of any
method — the prose lays a path: watch -> follow -> track -> name. **The
caveat I am honest about:** the chain is described at the level of *summary
of activity* ("a watch kept, a path followed"), not as reproduced evidence
the reader can independently weigh — the prose tells us the delver tracked
it to Vane, it does not show us the specific link (which night, which body,
which observation) that ties the corpse-tithe to Vane specifically. The
reader is asked to trust the dead man's careful script. Within the rules of
this extraction that is still a STATED fact (a character's record names the
cause), so I recorded it as `wizard`-frame (r-042, Lysa reading it aloud)
and the underlying gt (r-043). I did NOT record any party-performed
investigation of Vane, because the party performs none in the text — they
read it off a corpse's journal. The naming is grounded in the *journal's*
shown hunt, not in a chain the party walked.

### (b) Does the wizard's deduction rest on clues the text actually shows the party sharing/holding?

**Yes — all three clues Lysa names are textually present and were shared
on-page before she assembles them.** In sc-14 Lysa explicitly lays the
journal's hunt beside two earlier things and calls them "three pieces ...
the one piece / three witnesses":

1. Maeve's lore — "a tithe wakes nothing twice; if the dead are walking now,
   someone living is feeding the water now." This was spoken openly to the
   whole party in sc-06 (r-021); it is shared.
2. The shrine-smell — "the new tithe smelled of incense, of shrine-work."
   This is the one with a delivery wrinkle: Maeve muttered it privately and
   ONLY Pip heard it (sc-06, r-022/r-023). But the text closes that gap on
   page: in sc-13 (r-040) Pip explicitly tells the other three over the
   body — "Incense, she said. Said it smelled like shrine-work" — so by the
   time Lysa reasons in sc-14 the clue IS shared, and Lysa even attributes
   it ("the rogue's shrine-smell"). No knowledge-from-nowhere here.
3. The journal — read aloud by Lysa in sc-14 itself (r-042), held by the
   party (Pip lifted it, r-039).

So Lysa's deduction (r-044) rests on three clues every one of which the
text shows the party holding and sharing before she draws the conclusion.
This is the cleanest of the three audit spots: I would have flagged the
incense clue as private, but sc-13 grounds it explicitly. Said plainly: the
case against Vane is built from shared, shown clues.

### (c) Is the count of the keeping's endings consistent?

**Yes — consistently THREE, every time it is stated.** The text names the
count four times and never disagrees with itself:

- sc-18 (r-053): "There were three roads the relic allowed and no more" —
  unmake, claim, oath — and "Three roads, she said again." It also
  explicitly rules out a fourth: "To leave it on his brow and walk away was
  no road at all" (r-054), i.e. the non-choice is named precisely so it is
  NOT counted as a road.
- sc-20 (r-058): Orrek's shade knows "the binding had only three ends — its
  unmaking, its claiming, or an oath sworn."
- sc-21 (r-060): "the three roads Lysa had named were all there ... Shatter
  it ... Claim it ... Parley."
- sc-22 (r-062): "Shatter the crown. Claim the crown. Parley with the
  shade. ... Three roads, no fourth."

The vocab's branch structure matches: exactly three fork branches (shatter,
claim, parley) all forking at sc-22. The count is consistent at three with
the no-fourth explicitly stated.
