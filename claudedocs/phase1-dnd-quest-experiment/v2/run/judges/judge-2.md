# Judge 2 — verdict (arm X vs arm Y)

Both arms run the CLAIM road of "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren." They share
most scenes verbatim. The differences cluster in a handful of scenes that bear
directly on the rubric: sc-05, sc-13, sc-14, sc-18, sc-29c, sc-30c.

## Arm X — scores

**Knowledge realism — 3/5.**
The shared spine lays good groundwork: Maeve plants the "someone living is
feeding it now" lore and the incense/shrine-work mutter, which Pip alone hears
(sc-06); the journal does the naming (sc-14). The problem is the seam at the
hinge. In sc-13, Pip carries the incense clue but says nothing of it. So in
sc-14 the named accusation rests almost entirely on the journal's bare
assertion — "He'd tracked them to their source... The source was the
shrine-keeper" — and Lysa's synthesis cites the incense clue that the party was
never shown sharing on-page (sc-06 had Pip hear it alone and "said nothing").
The conclusion is plausible but arrives compressed: the investigative path is
named in retrospect rather than walked in front of the reader. In sc-05 the boy
only knows his master is missing, so the master's purpose (to hunt the
tithe-feeder) appears later with no town-side foreshadowing.

**Agency / branch integrity — 2/5.**
The fork commits cleanly enough at sc-22 ("They chose to claim it"), and the
CLAIM consequences are concrete (sc-24c warden re-chained, sc-25c dead to heel,
sc-28c–29c the reliquary trap springs). But sc-30c openly hedges the earned
outcome: "it came to the same end whichever way you turned it... whether it had
stayed on the party's brow or passed to Brother Vane's, the shape did not
change." This is the exact "same end whichever way" the brief warns against — it
tells the reader the choice between holding and surrendering the crown did not
matter, which deflates the consequence the road just paid for.

**Completion / polish — 3/5.**
Prose is strong and consistent throughout. Two visible seams: the dropped clue
in sc-13 (Pip sits on the incense lead through the body scene), and sc-18's
"a fourth thing she read" framing, which contradicts its own "three ways, no
more" by then naming a fourth (the oath) — a structural wrinkle in how the
roads are enumerated. Runnable, but with these draft seams showing.

**Overall — 3/5.**

## Arm Y — scores

**Knowledge realism — 4/5.**
Y closes the seams X leaves. sc-05 has the boy report his master "got a notion...
about the dead going into the water. Fresh dead... He said somebody was putting
them down there... and he meant to learn who" — so the delver's investigative
purpose is established town-side, before the descent, and the body found in
sc-13 reads as a hunt that ended, not just a corpse. sc-13 then has Pip actually
surface the incense/shrine-work clue aloud to the others ("I should've said it
sooner. I'm saying it now"), discharging the lead she'd carried. sc-14 makes the
journal a tracked hunt ("a watch kept, a path followed... run down at last")
and has Lysa assemble three independently-earned pieces — Maeve's lore, Pip's
incense, the dead man's hunt — into the accusation: "we've three witnesses to
it now and one of them died getting it." The naming of Vane is earned by a shown
chain of evidence rather than asserted.

**Agency / branch integrity — 4/5.**
Same clean fork commit at sc-22. The decisive improvement is sc-30c, which
commits instead of hedging: "This was the end their grasping had bought them,
and it had a name, and the name was Brother Vane... the only thing the party had
decided, down all those stairs, was whose brow it ruled from. They had reached
for it. They had got it. And in the getting they had delivered it." The outcome
is owned by the party's CLAIM choice and the specific error of carrying the crown
to Vane — the road feels chosen and the cost is the party's own. sc-29c also
shows the leash transfer mechanically ("The leash had run from the dead through
the crown to the four of them; now it ran to him") rather than asserting it.

**Completion / polish — 4/5.**
sc-18 is tightened to exactly three roads (unmake / claim / oath), folding the
oath in as the third rather than appending a contradictory "fourth thing," and
adds that leaving the crown on his brow "was no road at all." No dropped-clue
seam. Reads as a finished, run-as-is scenario.

**Overall — 4/5.**

## Forced A/B calls

- **Stronger knowledge realism:** **Y.** sc-05 plants the delver's purpose
  town-side and sc-13 surfaces the incense clue on-page, so sc-14's naming of
  Vane is a shown three-source synthesis rather than X's largely asserted
  journal conclusion.
- **Stronger agency:** **Y.** sc-30c commits to a party-earned outcome; X's
  sc-30c hedges with "the same end whichever way you turned it."
- **More finished overall:** **Y.** Y closes the sc-13 dropped-clue seam and the
  sc-18 "three roads / a fourth thing" enumeration wrinkle that X leaves visible.
- **Seam spot-check (a character knowing something with no path given):** The
  cleanest case is **arm X, sc-14**: Lysa's synthesis leans on the incense
  /shrine-work clue, but in X that clue was heard by Pip alone in sc-06 and never
  shown being shared with the party (X's sc-13 has Pip stay silent). The party
  reaches the named accusation citing evidence the text never put in their shared
  hands. Y repairs exactly this by having Pip voice the clue in sc-13. (No
  comparable unsupported-knowledge seam in arm Y.)

## What separates them

Same road, same prose voice — Y is the more finished arm because it walks the
investigation it asks the reader to believe (the clue is shared, the delver's
hunt is foreshadowed, the accusation is a shown three-witness synthesis) and
because its ending commits to a consequence the party's CLAIM choice earned,
where X both drops a clue and then hedges that the choice changed nothing.
