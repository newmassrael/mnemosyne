# Judge 3 — A/B verdict: "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren" (CLAIM road)

Both arms run the same shared opening (sc-01–sc-22) and the same CLAIM road
(sc-23c–sc-32c). They diverge in a handful of scenes — chiefly sc-13, sc-14,
sc-18, sc-29c, and sc-30c. The scoring turns almost entirely on those.

## Arm X

- **Knowledge realism — 5.** Every assertion of who is behind the rising has a
  shown path. At sc-14 the journal is not a verdict but an *investigation*:
  "Page on page he tracked it — a watch kept, a path followed, the source of the
  corpse-tithes run down at last to the one who fed them." Lysa then assembles
  the conclusion out of three explicitly-laid pieces and names them — Maeve's
  living-hand lore (sc-06), the shrine-smell clue, and the dead delver's hunt —
  "three witnesses to it now and one of them died getting it." Critically, the
  shrine-smell clue is properly *delivered* into the party at sc-13: Pip, who
  alone overheard Maeve mutter "incense... shrine-work" at sc-06, finally voices
  it over the body — "I should've said it sooner. I'm saying it now." Knowledge
  reaches the people who use it through a shown channel. No conclusion-from-nowhere.

- **Agency / branch integrity — 5.** The ending commits without hedging. sc-30c
  states the road's true stakes plainly: "the only thing the party had decided,
  down all those stairs, was *whose brow it ruled from*." The CLAIM outcome reads
  as a thing the party *chose* and earned (sc-22 "They chose to claim it"), with
  the betrayal (sc-29c) the specific, deserved price of that choice. The reliquary
  trap springs because they took the crown; the boy is left untold (sc-31c). The
  road feels chosen.

- **Completion / polish — 5.** Reads as a finished, runnable scenario. The
  numbering is internally consistent ("three roads" at sc-18, "three ends" at
  sc-20). The betrayal at sc-29c is fully dramatized — the leash visibly changes
  hands, Vane "did not pretend any longer," and the consequence cascades cleanly
  into Calder's hollow hall (sc-30c) and the boy's loose end (sc-31c). No seams.

- **Overall — 5.**

## Arm Y

- **Knowledge realism — 3.** The path to the Vane accusation is *told* more than
  *shown*. sc-14 compresses the journal to a result — "He'd tracked them to their
  source... The source was the shrine-keeper" — losing the depicted hunt. The
  decisive seam: sc-13 **drops** Pip's confession of the overheard incense clue
  entirely, yet sc-14 still lists "The incense Pip had heard the old woman mutter"
  among Lysa's assembled pieces. In sc-06 only Pip heard it ("Pip filed it...
  said nothing"); with the sc-13 hand-off removed, Lysa now wields a clue the
  text never routed to her. The conclusion is right, but one of its three legs
  arrives without a shown path.

- **Agency / branch integrity — 2.** The ending hedges in exactly the way the
  brief warns against. sc-30c: "So it came to the same end whichever way you
  turned it... Whether it had stayed on the party's brow or... passed to Brother
  Vane's, the shape of the thing did not change." This flattens the CLAIM choice
  into outcome-equivalence and undercuts the betrayal's weight — if the end is the
  same regardless, the party's choice (and Vane's coup) stopped mattering. The
  road no longer feels chosen; it feels foreordained.

- **Completion / polish — 3.** Visible seams. sc-18 introduces a numbering wobble:
  it names the third road as "left to him," then adds the oath as "a fourth thing
  she read" — but sc-20 still says "the binding had only three ends," and sc-21
  reverts to "three roads, no fourth." sc-29c is compressed to the point of losing
  the leash-changing-hands beat that makes the betrayal legible. Reads as a strong
  draft, not a finished scenario.

- **Overall — 3.**

## Forced A/B calls

- **Stronger knowledge realism:** **X.** X delivers the incense clue to the party
  on-page (sc-13) before Lysa uses it (sc-14); Y drops that hand-off yet still has
  Lysa cite the clue — a "how did she know that?" gap.
- **Stronger agency (ending commits and feels chosen):** **X.** X commits ("whose
  brow it ruled from," sc-30c); Y hedges with "the same end whichever way you
  turned it" (sc-30c).
- **More finished overall:** **X.** X is internally consistent and fully
  dramatized; Y carries a numbering wobble (sc-18 vs sc-20), a compressed betrayal
  (sc-29c), and the unrouted-clue seam (sc-13/sc-14).
- **Seam spot-check (a character knowing something with no path):** **Arm Y,
  sc-14.** Lysa assembles "the incense Pip had heard the old woman mutter" into
  her accusation, but Arm Y's sc-13 removed the only moment Pip shares that clue
  with the party; per sc-06 she alone heard it and "said nothing." In Arm X this
  exact gap is closed by Pip's sc-13 confession. (No comparable seam found in Arm X.)

## What separates them

The same delve, judged on a few scenes: Arm X *shows* how the party comes to
name Brother Vane (Pip's sc-13 confession + the three-witness assembly in sc-14)
and *commits* to the CLAIM ending as a chosen, paid-for outcome (sc-30c), while
Arm Y *tells* the accusation — leaving Lysa holding a clue the text never handed
her (sc-13/sc-14) — and *hedges* the ending into "the same end whichever way"
(sc-30c). X is the more finished, higher-completion adventure.
