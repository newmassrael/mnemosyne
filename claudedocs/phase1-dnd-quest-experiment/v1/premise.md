# Premise — "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren"

A fresh, original sword-and-sorcery dungeon-delve in the Dungeons-&-Dragons
register: a party of adventurers, a quest-giver, a flooded ruin, a relic, a
moral fork, and three distinct endings. No existing-IP names, places, or
creatures — invent freely within the genre.

This is the high-level direction only (the human's premise role). The author
invents the FACTS — every name, scene, person, and turn below is a seed to
build on, not a script to transcribe; deepen and reshape as the story demands.

## The setting

**Karrow's Reach** is a frontier town clinging to the lip of a drowned
dwarrow undercity called **Vael Mooren** — a great stone hold that sank a
lifetime ago when its deep cisterns burst. For a season now the drowned dead
have been rising out of the floodwater and walking the lower tunnels. Three
delvers hired to investigate have already vanished. The town's reeve hires a
party of four to go down, find what is waking the dead, and end it.

## The truth (what is really happening)

The rising is driven by the **Hollow Crown**, the relic of the hold's last
warden, whose drowned shade still keeps the deep vault. The crown is not a
curse blown in from outside: the town's own **shrine-keeper** has secretly
been feeding corpse-tithes into the floodwater to wake the warden, meaning to
raise the old hold's wealth and rule the Reach himself. The friendly
shrine-keeper is the hidden architect of the horror — and he gives the party a
"side errand" that is really a misdirection.

## The party (four adventurers, four points of view)

- **a front-line fighter** — sees what is in front of them; reads a fight,
  a guard, a collapsing floor; not a reader of runes.
- **a wizard / loremaster** — reads the old dwarrow runes; once she finds the
  warden's record she alone can learn the crown's true nature and how it might
  be unmade.
- **a rogue / scout** — finds the hidden ways, the locks, the dropped key;
  overhears what others would not say to the party's face.
- **a cleric / healer** — senses the dead, and alone can parley with the
  warden's shade rather than fight it.

Each adventurer knows their own craft and their own road through the hold —
not the others'. The wizard's lore, the rogue's overheard secret, the cleric's
sense of the dead, the fighter's read of the rival sellsword: each is a
fragment held by one mind.

## The other people (the Reach and the hold must stay peopled)

- **the reeve** — hires the party; the main quest-giver, in the town.
- **the drowned warden's shade** — keeps the deep vault; the crown holds him
  as much as he holds it; can be fought, freed, or parleyed with.
- **the shrine-keeper** — the hidden architect; appears as a helpful ally and
  gives a misdirecting errand.
- **a lantern-boy** — apprentice to one of the vanished delvers; gives the
  party word of the missing delver.
- **a rival sellsword** — a delver who got down first and wants the crown's
  worth for himself; a second faction in the dark.
- **a town wise-woman** — keeps the old lore of the cisterns and the first
  tithe.

Keep the Reach and the hold peopled to the last scene of every road: the
lantern-boy, the wise-woman, the rival, the warden, the reeve should still be
present, knowing, and acting in each ending's aftermath — not vanish after the
opening.

## The quests (a real game has objectives, prerequisites, and outcomes)

This is a quest-driven adventure. There is a main quest and a handful of side
quests, and they INTERLOCK — one gates another, one is a trap, one is optional:

- **MAIN — end the rising.** Given by the reeve in the town. The party must
  reach the deep vault and resolve the crown. This CANNOT be done until the
  vault is opened — it REQUIRES the key (below).
- **SIDE — the warden's key.** Discovered when the party reaches the locked
  vault door deep in the hold. The rogue must recover the key from the drowned
  warden's cell. Opening the vault depends on it: a real prerequisite.
- **SIDE — the last delver.** Given by the lantern-boy in the town. Find the
  delver who vanished. The party finds the body and a journal in the hold —
  and the journal is what reveals the corpse-tithe (who is really doing this).
  It can be finished, or left undone and the game still ended.
- **SIDE / TRAP — the shrine-keeper's reliquary.** The shrine-keeper asks the
  party to bring him the crown "to seal it safely." Handing it over is handing
  the villain his power. Whether this errand is fulfilled, refused, or turned
  back on him depends entirely on the road the party takes.

## The fork (a moral choice at the deep vault, at roughly the middle)

Once the party reaches the warden in the deep vault, they choose — and the
three roads are genuinely different games from there to the end:

- **SHATTER** — the wizard unmakes the crown. The rising ends; the warden is
  freed into death; the hold's wealth is lost; the rival sellsword, cheated of
  the prize, turns on the party.
- **CLAIM** — the party takes the crown's power. Now the shrine-keeper's errand
  can be fulfilled (hand it over — and be betrayed) or kept (and be corrupted).
  A dark road.
- **PARLEY** — the cleric binds the rising by oath with the warden's shade. The
  warden keeps the crown but stills the dead; the town must keep an eternal
  tithe-watch. A bittersweet third road.

## The secrets (held back until they are earned)

Some truths should land at a reveal, not be told at the start: that the
shrine-keeper is the cause (revealed by the delver's journal); the warden's
true identity and that he can be reasoned with (revealed when the wizard reads
his record); the rival's coming betrayal (revealed in the fork's aftermath).

## What to aim for

Tell the best, most coherent dungeon-delve you can in this world: a party of
four who each know their own craft and road, a town and a hold full of people
who each hold a fragment of the truth, quests that interlock and gate each
other, one hard moral fork into three real endings, and a buried cause that is
earned, not announced. Make it read like an adventure a table would actually
want to play.
