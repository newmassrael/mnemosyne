# Judge 1 — "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren"

A reviewer's verdict on whether this reads like a real adventure a table would
play. I read the quest briefing and one full played road (CLAIM, sc-23c–sc-32c).

## Axes

**Quest legibility — 5/5**
Every quest reads as a clean, wantable objective with a stated pickup, lead,
and outcome. "Still the Rising" is the spine ("find the thing that wakes the
dead, and put an end to it," sc-02); "The Warden's Key" is a legible hard lock
("only the warden's key turns this lock," sc-16); "The Last Delver" is a human
errand a player will *feel* obligated to ("On my hand, we will," sc-05); and
"The Reliquary" is a trap that reads as a kindness (sc-07). A player would know
exactly what each asks and what closing or leaving it open means.

**Prerequisite / structure — 5/5**
The gating is genuine, not decorative. The whole delve physically stops at the
locked vault (sc-16) and the main quest cannot resolve until the key is found
(sc-17) and the door grinds open (sc-19) — a real obstacle that reshapes the
party's task mid-dungeon. The Reliquary's hidden prerequisite ("only if the
party comes out holding the crown") is elegant: it can only fire on CLAIM, so
the trap is structurally welded to the choice rather than scripted to always
spring.

**Agency / branch integrity — 4/5**
The fork at sc-22 produces three materially different worlds — crown unmade,
mastered, or bound by oath — and the CLAIM road shows real teeth: the warden
stays chained under a new will (sc-24c), the delver's boy is *never told*
(sc-31c), and the reliquary trap actually springs (sc-29c). These are distinct
consequences, not a repaint. I dock one point because the briefing leans on the
SHATTER/PARLEY outcomes that I can only see described, not played, and because
sc-30c's "it came to the same end whichever way you turned it" slightly flattens
the agency *within* CLAIM (party-held vs. Vane-held collapse to one shape).

**Map coherence — 5/5**
The world is concretely placed: town on the cliff-lip, hold drowned below
(sc-01), shrine above the town (sc-04), lantern-house "where the road gave up
and became the descent" (sc-05), Maeve furthest out (sc-06), then a coherent
vertical descent — upper galleries (sc-08), hidden flooded stair (sc-10),
warden's cell off that stair (sc-17), the vault door (sc-16). The rising flood
that turns ankle-deep to calf-deep on the way out (sc-08) makes the geography
feel lived-in and consequential.

**Knowledge realism — 5/5**
This is the standout. Reveals are *earned*: the reeve honestly believes it's a
curse (sc-02), Maeve supplies the "someone living is feeding it now" frame
(sc-06), Pip alone overhears the "smells like shrine-work" mutter (sc-06), and
Vane's guilt is only nailed down by the dead delver's journal at sc-14 — not
blurted. Vane never tips his hand on the page ("not a word of that was in the
room," sc-07), and Henna's good-faith trust makes the later betrayal land as a
discovery, not a telegraph. The crown's true nature and the parley option are
gated behind Lysa reading the warden's record (sc-15, sc-18).

**Game-feel (overall) — 4/5**
Taken together this reads like a real adventure I'd want to run: a tight cast
with clear lanes (Doran holds, Pip finds, Henna feels, Lysa reads), a dungeon
that escalates, a villain hiding in plain sight, and a fork with genuine moral
weight. The CLAIM ending is bleak and satisfying. It stops just short of 5
because the played slice is competent-grim rather than surprising at the moment
of choice (sc-22 simply states "They chose to claim it"), and I am trusting the
briefing for the texture of the two roads I never saw.

## Forced calls

**Best road question.** The single thing that most makes this feel like a game
worth playing is the **Reliquary trap's structural honesty**: it is set up as a
warm favour (sc-07), its truth is buried (sc-14), and it can *only* spring if
the player chooses CLAIM and walks the crown back up (sc-28c → sc-29c) — the
betrayal is a consequence the party authors, not a cutscene. The one thing that
most undercuts it is sc-30c's "it came to the same end whichever way you turned
it," which briefly tells the player their in-road choices didn't matter — a
flattening note in an adventure whose whole pitch is that choices do.

**Overall — 4/5.** A real adventure, not an outline in costume: the quests
interlock, the world is placed, the reveals are earned, and the fork makes three
genuinely different worlds — held back from a 5 only because I judged two of the
three roads from description rather than play, and one line undersells the very
agency the design earns.
