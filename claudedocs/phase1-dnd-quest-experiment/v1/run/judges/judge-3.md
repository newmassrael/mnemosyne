# Judge 3 — "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren"

## Axes

**Quest legibility — 5/5**
Every quest reads as a clear, wantable objective with an obvious giver, place, and stake: "Still the Rising" is the reeve's plain charge ("Find it. End it.", sc-02), "The Last Delver" is the boy Tam's heartbreaking ask ("bring me word", sc-05), and "The Reliquary" is Brother Vane's too-kind favour (sc-07). What "finishing" means is legible per road, including the deliberately-OPEN ends — the boy never told on CLAIM (sc-31c), the trap never sprung when there is no crown to give. A player would understand and care about each one immediately.

**Prerequisite / structure — 5/5**
The gating is real, not cosmetic: the deep vault is a slab that cannot be forced (sc-16), and the whole main quest hard-stops behind "The Warden's Key" until Pip recovers it from Orrek's drowned cell (sc-17) and turns it (sc-19). The Reliquary's conditional prerequisite — fulfillable ONLY if the party surfaces holding the crown — turns a side-errand into a CLAIM-only payoff (sc-28c), which makes the world feel like a place where obstacles and consequences actually interlock rather than a checklist.

**Agency / branch integrity — 4/5**
The three roads are genuinely distinct in outcome and tone — SHATTER frees Orrek into death, CLAIM enthrones the crown and springs Vane's betrayal (sc-29c), PARLEY binds by oath under an eternal tithe-watch — and side quests resolve differently per road (Reliquary only on CLAIM, Last Delver left open on CLAIM). I dock one point only because I judge from a single played road (CLAIM); the briefing convinces me the others differ, but I cannot witness SHATTER/PARLEY consequences land in prose, and sc-30c's "same end whichever way you turned it" risks flattening CLAIM's internal choice (keep vs. hand over) into one fate.

**Map coherence — 5/5**
The geography is concrete and stacked: Karrow's Reach clings to the cliff-rim above (sc-01), with reeve's hall, shrine, lantern-house, and Maeve's wind-hut placed around town, then a descent that drowns the daylight "a flight at a time" (sc-08) through upper galleries, a hidden flooded stair (sc-10), Orrek's cell (sc-17), to the sealed deep vault (sc-16/20). The rising water is a consistent through-line — ankle-deep in becomes calf-deep out — so the place feels physically real and traversable.

**Knowledge realism — 5/5**
Reveals are earned, never blurted: the town calls it a "curse" (sc-02), Maeve narrows it to a living hand and mutters "smells like shrine-work" only to lingering Pip (sc-06), and Vane's guilt is paid off by the dead delver's journal deep in the hold (sc-14), not announced up top. Each head knows its own slice — Henna senses the leash, Doran reads footing and men, Lysa alone reads the runes (sc-15/18) — and Vane's villainy is hidden even from the narration's surface so the discovery genuinely lands.

**Game-feel (overall) — 5/5**
This reads like an adventure a table would eagerly run: a clean charge, a real lock-and-key spine, a fork with weight, and a dark CLAIM ending whose cost (the leash merely changes fists, sc-31c) is morally legible and stings. The unfilled Reliquary trap and untold boy on this road actively make me want to run SHATTER and PARLEY to see what the same pieces become — the mark of a branch structure worth replaying.

## Forced calls

**Best road question** — What most makes this feel like a real game: the buried-truth economy paid off through play. Vane greets you warm and asks the kind favour (sc-04/07), and only the delver's dried journal in the deep (sc-14) turns that favour into bait you carry unknowingly to sc-28c — a betrayal the party walks into because of a promise kept in good faith. What most undercuts it: sc-30c's "it came to the same end whichever way you turned it" partially collapses CLAIM's own sub-choice (hold the crown vs. give it to Vane), softening the agency the rest of the adventure works so hard to make matter.

**Overall — 5/5.** A real adventure: a tight lock-and-key spine, earned reveals, and three roads that genuinely diverge — not an outline wearing adventure clothes, but something a table would want to play and replay.
