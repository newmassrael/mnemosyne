# Judge 2 — "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren"

## Axis scores

**Quest legibility — 5/5**
Every quest reads as a clean, wantable objective with a clear pickup, ask, and
finish. "Still the Rising" (sc-02) is a blunt mercenary charge — "Find it. End
it." — while "The Last Delver" (sc-05) and "The Reliquary" (sc-07) are small,
human errands a player would take up without prompting. The briefing tells you
exactly where each lives, who leads it, and what each of the three roads does to
it, so there is no ambiguity about what completing — or abandoning — a quest
means.

**Prerequisite / structure — 5/5**
The gating is genuine, not decorative: the locked vault door (sc-16) reads "only
the warden's key turns this lock," which hard-blocks the main quest until "The
Warden's Key" is recovered (sc-17) and the slab grinds open (sc-19). That single
dependency reshapes the party's task in front of you ("so the errand changed
shape... before anything, the key") and makes the hold feel like a real obstacle
course rather than a checklist. The Reliquary's soft prerequisite — fulfillable
"only if the party comes out of the vault holding the crown" — ties a town errand
to a deep choice, which is elegant interlock.

**Agency / branch integrity — 4/5**
The fork at sc-22 genuinely forks: SHATTER, CLAIM, and PARLEY are described as
materially different endings (crown unmade vs. seized vs. oath-bound), and they
ripple outward — CLAIM uniquely springs the Reliquary trap (sc-28c/29c), uniquely
leaves the delver's boy untold (sc-31c), and uniquely turns Skell to kneeling
(sc-26c). The played CLAIM road has real teeth and its own consequences down to
the lamp still burning (sc-32c). I dock one point because sc-30c leans on a
"comes to the same end whichever way you turn it" framing for the crown's rule,
which slightly flattens CLAIM's distinctiveness from within — though the loose
ends (boy, betrayal) still make it clearly its own road.

**Map coherence — 5/5**
The world is laid out with real vertical geography: Karrow's Reach clings to the
cliff-rim (sc-01), the shrine sits "a little above the town" (sc-04), the
lantern-house is "where the road gave up and became the descent" (sc-05), and
Maeve lives "furthest out" (sc-06). The descent is consistent and physical — the
flood rises "always" so an ankle-deep step is calf-deep on return (sc-08), the
hidden flooded stair (sc-10) leads to the warden's cell (sc-17) and the deep
vault (sc-16/20). Town and hold connect into one navigable place.

**Knowledge realism — 5/5**
Reveals are earned, not blurted. The town calls it a curse (sc-02), Maeve
narrows it to "someone living is feeding the water now" (sc-06), and only the
dead delver's journal names Brother Vane at sc-14 — a discovery the party works
for. Crucially, Henna's "sense for falseness... found nothing" in Vane (sc-04)
because he genuinely conceals it; the crown's reasonable-with-shade nature is
read in runes at sc-18, not announced; even Skell's coming betrayal is something
Doran *infers* from type (sc-11) rather than being told.

**Game-feel (overall) — 5/5**
This reads like an adventure a table would eagerly run: a clean mercenary hook, a
detective thread (who feeds the dead?), a hard moral fork, and a traitor in the
party's own ranks. The CLAIM road is bleak and satisfying, and the briefing's
account of SHATTER and PARLEY genuinely makes me want to play them — I want to see
the keeper's trap never spring, and the oath sworn to a weary Orrek.

## Forced calls

**Best road question** — The single thing that most makes this feel like a game
worth playing is the **interlock between the Reliquary errand and the fork**: a
warm, trust-built town favor (sc-07) becomes a baited trap that *only* triggers if
you make one specific deep choice (CLAIM → sc-28c/29c), so a town-level decision
and a vault-level decision pay each other off — that is real systems design, not
a list of errands. The thing that most undercuts it is sc-30c's "came to the same
end whichever way you turned it" line, which momentarily argues against the
branch integrity the rest of the adventure works to build.

**Overall — 5/5.** A real adventure, not an outline wearing adventure clothes:
the quests interlock, the fork has distinct consequences, and the reveals are
earned — I want to play the other two roads.
