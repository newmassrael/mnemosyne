# Judge 3 — A/B verdict: does the longer opening earn its length?

Two versions of the same delve (the CLAIM road of "The Drowned Hold of Vael
Mooren"). Arm X opens with a four-beat prologue (sc-00a–sc-00d) following the
doomed delver Coombe down into the dark before the party is ever hired; Arm Y
begins with the party at the reeve's hall (sc-01). From sc-01 onward the two
arms are, scene for scene, the same prose. So the whole question is whether the
prologue in Arm X is worth carrying.

---

## Arm X — scores

**Knowledge realism — 5.**
This is where the prologue pays for itself. The reader is *shown* the corpse-
tithe being committed: the fresh dead in the lower galleries (sc-00a), the robed
figure tipping a body into the sluice by night (sc-00b), the trail climbing
toward the holy quarter of the upper town (sc-00c). Crucially, the prologue is
disciplined about what Coombe can know: he sees a robed hand and a path that
climbs toward the upper town, but he never sees a face and never writes a name —
he "left a clean blank … because he would not write down a guess as if it were a
knowing" (sc-00d). That restraint is the difference between foreshadowing and
spoiling. When Lysa names Vane at sc-14, it lands as the *closing* of a hunt the
reader watched begin, not a document-dump conclusion. The party's own chain of
knowing (Maeve's "someone living, now" at sc-06, Pip's overheard "smells like
shrine-work" at sc-06/sc-13, the journal at sc-14) is identical to Arm Y and
just as clean — every character knows only what they came by. The prologue adds
a seen path to the knowledge without ever handing the reader the name early.

**Opening / pacing — 4.**
The prologue is genuinely strong as prose — Coombe's hunter's-eye voice, the
held-breath watch at the sluice, the cut-off in the rising water — and it grounds
the whole mystery in a witnessed crime rather than rumor. It earns its length on
craft and on dramatic-irony grounds (below). The cost is real, though: it is
four scenes and a fair word-count before the actual party appears, and the hook
of the *adventure as a game* (the hiring, the charge) is deferred. A table
reading for "when do we start playing" waits longer. I dock one point because
the delay is a true cost, not because the writing fails. It's backstory that
mostly justifies itself, but it is still front-loaded.

**Dramatic tension — 5.**
The prologue converts the central mystery into dramatic irony done the *right*
way. The reader leaves Coombe knowing the shape of the crime (a living, robed,
shrine-ward hand) but not the name — so when Brother Vane comes down his steps
"with both hands out and a smile that warmed the whole grey morning" (sc-04) and
asks the party to bring him the crown (sc-07), the reader feels the dread the
party can't. We've seen the robe; we haven't been told it's his. That gap
(reader-suspicion vs. character-trust) builds tension without deflating the
mystery, because the *name* is still withheld until sc-14. It also retroactively
charges Tam's errand: we already know, from sc-00d, that the master is dead at
the bottom, so the boy's "he's only lost" (sc-05) aches from the first read.

**Completion / polish — 5.**
Reads as a finished, runnable scenario. The prologue is fully realized (not a
stub), the seams between it and the main body are clean (the journal Coombe
wraps against his chest in sc-00d is the same oilskin journal Pip recovers in
sc-13), and the CLAIM road resolves all its threads — the reliquary trap springs
(sc-29c), Skell turns (sc-26c), and the loose ends (the boy never told, sc-31c)
are deliberately, thematically left open rather than dropped.

**Overall — 5.**

---

## Arm Y — scores

**Knowledge realism — 5.**
Identical from sc-01 on, and on its own terms the chain to Vane is excellent.
The reveal is *built*, not asserted: Maeve plants "someone living is feeding the
water now" (sc-06); Pip alone overhears "smells like shrine-work" (sc-06) and
sits on it until sc-13; the dead delver's journal tracks the corpse-tithe to the
shrine-keeper's door (sc-14); and Lysa explicitly assembles the three pieces —
"the wise-woman's living hand, the rogue's shrine-smell, and a dead man's hunt"
(sc-14) — into the name. No character knows more than they could. The villain's
identity arrives at sc-14, well-paced and earned within the body. It does lean
more on the found document (the journal) as the load-bearing reveal, because the
reader has not independently seen the crime — but the document is corroborated by
two other earned threads, so it doesn't read as a bald conclusion. Strong, just
a hair more page-dependent than Arm X.

**Opening / pacing — 5.**
Opens with the party being hired (sc-02) almost immediately after the scene-
setting sc-01. The hook is the charge — "Find it. End it." — and it lands fast.
For a table this is the cleaner start: you are in the game by the second scene.
No length to earn because there's no detour to pay for.

**Dramatic tension — 4.**
The reader and the party learn in lockstep, which is a perfectly good mystery
structure — discovery alongside the protagonists. But it forgoes the dramatic
irony Arm X gets for free: when Vane asks for the crown at sc-07, nothing in the
text has yet made the reader suspect him, so the scene reads as the kind favor
it pretends to be. The dread is recovered retroactively at sc-14, but the
chilling re-read of sc-04/sc-07 (the warm friend we *already saw* dumping
bodies) isn't available on a first pass. Slightly less tension on the page,
purely from the absence of the prologue's set-up. Still solid.

**Completion / polish — 5.**
Same finished body as Arm X. Clean seams, the CLAIM road pays off every thread,
runnable as-is.

**Overall — 4.**

---

## Forced A/B calls

- **Stronger knowledge realism:** **Arm X.** Both lay an honest path to the
  villain, but X *shows* the reader the crime (robed hand, climbing trail, no
  name) before the journal confirms it — the identification feels seen, not
  merely concluded from a found document. Y leans more on the journal as the
  reveal's spine.

- **More finished overall:** **Arm X.** Same polished body, plus a fully
  realized prologue that seams cleanly into it (Coombe's wrapped journal = Pip's
  recovered journal). Both are runnable; X is the more complete artifact.

- **Keep-the-prologue call:** Arm X has the prologue (sc-00a–sc-00d). **KEEP it.**
  It is the rare prologue that does real work: it converts the mystery into
  earned dramatic irony, grounds the corpse-tithe as a witnessed crime, and gives
  the boy's errand its ache — all while being disciplined enough to withhold the
  name. The only cost is a slower first hook, which the payoff outweighs. A GM
  could read it aloud as a cold open or summarize it; either way it's worth
  having on the page.

- **Seam spot-check:** No character in either arm knows something the text never
  gave them, and the name is never spoiled before sc-14 in either. The one place
  worth flagging is the *prologue's own* discipline boundary: sc-00c lets Coombe
  conclude the hand belongs to "someone of the town … the quarter where the
  ground was holy," which steers the reader hard toward the shrine before any
  party-side evidence — but it stops short of a name and is consistent with what
  a tracker following a climbing trail could infer, so it reads as fair
  foreshadowing rather than a leak. No genuine seam break in either arm.

---

**Closing:** Arm X earns its extra length — the prologue is not backstory to cut
but the source of the story's dramatic irony and its strongest claim to a
*shown* (not merely documented) villain; Arm Y is the leaner, faster-to-the-table
read and loses nothing in correctness, only in the dread the prologue would have
bought it.
