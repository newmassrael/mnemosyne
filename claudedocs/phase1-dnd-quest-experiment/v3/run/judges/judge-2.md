# Judge 2 — verdict (A/B: does the longer opening earn its length?)

The two arms share an identical main body (sc-01 → sc-32c, the CLAIM road).
Arm Y prepends a four-scene investigator's prologue (sc-00a → sc-00d) that
follows Coombe — the boy Tam's missing master — down into the hold, watching a
robed figure feed the dead to a sluice and tracking the trail toward the holy
quarter before the flood cuts him off mid-hunt. Arm X opens cold on the party
at the reeve's hall. I judge the prologue's worth axis by axis below.

---

## Arm X

**Knowledge realism — 5/5.**
This is the arm's strongest axis. The party's identification of Brother Vane is
built from three SEEN clues, each planted before it pays off and each tied to a
character who could plausibly have it. Maeve's lore — "a tithe wakes nothing
twice; someone living is feeding the water now" (sc-06) — establishes a *living*
hand. Pip alone overhears Maeve mutter "incense… smells like shrine-work"
(sc-06) and carries it withheld until it has somewhere to sit (sc-13). The dead
delver's journal supplies the actual tracking that runs the corpse-tithe to
Vane's door (sc-14). Crucially, Lysa's accusation in sc-14 is staged as
*synthesis of three witnessed pieces*, not a bald document-read: "the
wise-woman's living hand, the rogue's shrine-smell, and a dead man's hunt run to
ground." The reveal lands at sc-14 — past the midpoint, after the reader has
seen every input — so it feels earned, not spoiled and not asserted. Vane's
guilt is also shown to the reader early via the close-3rd "he asked for nothing
because he already had what he wanted" (sc-04, sc-07), but the *party* never has
that access; the gap is honest.

**Opening / pacing — 4/5.**
Opens efficiently: the rising, the dead, the unnamed Hollow Crown (sc-01); the
charge taken at sc-02; the town's measure at sc-03. The hook (dead walking, water
rising, three delvers lost) lands inside sc-01. No throat-clearing. The cost is
that the reader and party arrive on equal footing — there is no dread the reader
holds going in. Slight drag is only that sc-03 retreads sc-01's "water's coming
up," but that is minor.

**Dramatic tension — 4/5.**
The reader-knows-more dread is well managed through the close narration in sc-04
and sc-07 (we know Vane is hollow; Henna likes him "at once"). That dramatic
irony pays off devastatingly at the betrayal (sc-28c → sc-29c): the party carries
the crown up and sets it "in his palm" exactly as warned. Skell's slow burn
(sc-11, "out in front, never at his back" → sc-26c kneeling "in arithmetic") is
clean foreshadow-and-payoff. What this arm lacks is any tension *before* the
party exists — the dread starts only once we meet Vane at sc-04.

**Completion / polish — 5/5.**
Reads as a finished, runnable scenario. Every scene has a clear function, the
three-road structure is named in-fiction (sc-18, sc-21, sc-22) so a table knows
the fork is real, and the CLAIM road is followed through to a complete, costed
ending (sc-30c reeve, sc-31c Maeve + boy, sc-32c coda). The journal/runes
exposition (sc-14, sc-15, sc-18) is dense but legible.

**Overall — 5/5.**
A tight, complete delve with model-grade knowledge hygiene. Nothing is asserted
the characters didn't come by; the villain reveal is the cleanest part.

## Arm Y

**Knowledge realism — 5/5.**
The shared body's knowledge chain is identical to X and just as clean (sc-06,
sc-13, sc-14). The prologue, importantly, does NOT damage this — it is written
with disciplined restraint. Coombe witnesses the *deed* (a robed figure tipping
the dead, sc-00b) and tracks it toward the "holy quarter" (sc-00c), but the text
goes out of its way to have him FAIL to see the face: "where the name should go
he wrote only that the figure was someone of the town" (sc-00d), and the journal
ends "with a clean space after it where a name was never set down" (sc-00d). So
the reader is given a *robe and a direction*, not the name "Vane." The party
still has to do the real synthesis at sc-14. The prologue raises suspicion toward
the shrine without spoiling the identification — a careful piece of work. I hold
the score at 5/5 (see seam note for the one caveat that keeps it from being
*better* than X here).

**Opening / pacing — 4/5.**
The prologue earns most of its length. It is not inert backstory: it is an active
hunt with rising dread (the cold getting "into the seams of him," sc-00b; the
"too hard" overreach near the top, sc-00c; the flood closing over him, sc-00d).
It also retro-fills a hole X leaves implicit — it SHOWS the corpse-tithe
mechanism the party only ever infers, and it gives the dead delver (just a body
and a journal in X) a face and a voice. The cost: four full scenes before the
party's charge at sc-02, and the prologue's outcome (he dies with the name
unwritten) is something the reader can largely predict from sc-05's framing in
either arm. It delays the *party's* hook without delaying the *story's* hook —
the rising is gripping from sc-00a. A defensible trade, not a free one.

**Dramatic tension — 5/5.**
This is where the prologue pays for itself. Because the reader has watched the
robed figure climb toward the holy quarter (sc-00c) and carry the dead "as easy
as climbing home from any night's labour" (sc-00b), the warm welcome at the
shrine (sc-04) and the reliquary errand (sc-07) play under a dread the party
cannot feel. When Henna "liked him at once" (sc-04), the reader already suspects
the robe was his. That converts the whole middle into sustained dramatic irony
rather than X's later-arriving version. The risk — that knowing too much
deflates the sc-14 mystery — is avoided precisely because the prologue withholds
the name. Reader has *suspicion*, party has *proof later*; the gap drives dread
without flattening the reveal.

**Completion / polish — 5/5.**
The prologue is fully realized (four scenes, a complete arc, a last entry that
explicitly motivates the party's later find), and it dovetails with the shared
body: the oilskin-wrapped journal "against his chest" (sc-00d) is exactly what
Pip lifts "oilskin-wrapped" in sc-13. That continuity is tight. Same complete
CLAIM ending as X.

**Overall — 5/5.**
The shared spine is already a 5; the prologue adds genuine dread and grounding
without breaking knowledge realism. It earns its place, though it is not free —
it front-loads four scenes before the party.

## Forced A/B calls

- **Stronger knowledge realism:** **X (narrowly).** Both are excellent and the
  party's synthesis is identical. X edges it because it carries ZERO risk of the
  reader pre-forming the conclusion: the shrine is never implicated until the
  party earns it. Y's prologue is disciplined (no name), but pointing the trail
  at the "holy quarter" (sc-00c) hands the reader a near-certain inference about
  *which* townsman before sc-14 does the work. X keeps the villain's
  identification purely a function of shown, in-party evidence. A close call.

- **More finished overall:** **Y.** Same complete spine, plus a fully realized
  prologue that closes the "who was the delver / how does the tithe actually
  work" gap X leaves to inference, and it interlocks cleanly with sc-13's journal.
  More material, all of it finished.

- **Keep-the-prologue call:** Arm **Y** has the prologue. **KEEP it.** It is the
  rare prologue that does real work: it manufactures dramatic irony for the entire
  middle (the warm Vane scenes now play under dread), it dramatizes the tithe the
  party only infers, and it gives the dead delver an arc and a voice that makes
  sc-13 land harder. It pays for its length on the tension axis. (Were it to NAME
  Vane I would cut it; its restraint is exactly what makes it keepable.)

- **Seam spot-check:** No hard violation — no character knows a thing the text
  didn't give them a path to. The one soft seam is in **Y, sc-00c**: Coombe
  reasons the trail climbs "toward the holy quarter… where the ground was holy"
  and concludes a townsman, which steers the reader's suspicion toward the shrine
  a touch more pointedly than Coombe's own evidence (a wet print, a robe-hem
  smear) strictly warrants — it edges from tracking into authorial signposting.
  It stops short of spoiling (the name is never written, sc-00d), so it is a tint,
  not a leak. In the shared body, the knowledge handoffs are clean: Pip's withheld
  incense clue (sc-06 → sc-13) and Lysa's three-piece synthesis (sc-14) are both
  properly grounded.

---

**Closing:** Both arms are complete, runnable, and knowledge-honest 5/5
scenarios; Y's investigator prologue is one of the few that earns its length —
it buys real dread for the whole middle without ever spoiling the villain — so
the longer opening is worth keeping, even though X's cold open holds the cleaner
claim to "most earned reveal."
