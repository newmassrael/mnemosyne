# Judge 1 — verdict: "The Drowned Hold of Vael Mooren" (arm X vs arm Y)

Read: judge brief, quest briefing, arm X, arm Y. I do not know how either was
made. Both arms are the same delve; arm X opens with a four-scene prologue
(sc-00a–sc-00d) following a doomed investigator, Coombe, before the party
arrives at sc-01. Arm Y begins at sc-01 with the party. From sc-01 onward the
two arms are identical.

---

## Arm X — scores

**Knowledge realism — 5/5.**
This is where the prologue pays its way. The party's identification of the
villain (sc-14) is built from three SEEN sources the reader has watched each
character earn: Maeve's lore that a living hand must be feeding the water now
(sc-06), Pip's overheard "incense — shrine-work" mutter (sc-06, deliberately
withheld and then surfaced at sc-13), and the dead delver's journal whose hunt
runs to Vane's door (sc-14). Crucially, the journal does NOT name Vane — and
the prologue is why that is honest. In sc-00b–sc-00d Coombe sees the robed
figure but never the face, follows the trail up toward the holy quarter, and
dies (sc-00d) with a clean blank where the name should be: "I have got it near
enough to touch and could not close my hand on it... Put the name to it I could
not put." So the journal the party finds carries a tracking-to-the-shrine and
an explicit un-named gap; the party closes that gap themselves by triangulating
Maeve + the incense + the climb-toward-the-holy-quarter. The conclusion is
assembled on-page, not handed over in a document. The reader knows the answer
from sc-00b (a robed figure, climbing toward the holy quarter) but it is never
spoken as "Vane" until sc-14 — earned, not spoiled.

**Opening / pacing — 4/5.**
The prologue is long (four full scenes before the party appears) and that is a
real cost — a table wanting to start with its own PCs waits a while. But it
earns the length: it is not inert backstory, it is the dramatic engine. It
establishes the central truth (someone LIVING is feeding the dead), the method
(corpse-tithe at the sluice), the trail (climbing toward the holy quarter), and
the stakes (the man who knew this died for it), and it converts the journal
later found at sc-13/14 from an info-dump into the recovered testament of a
character we watched die. The prose is controlled and the dread accumulates.
Docked one point only because the four-scene length front-loads the hook before
any player-surrogate is present; a shorter two-scene cut would lose little.

**Dramatic tension — 5/5.**
The prologue creates exactly the productive gap the brief prizes: the reader
KNOWS from sc-00b that the corpse-feeder is a robed townsman climbing to the
holy quarter, and then watches the party in sc-04/sc-07 be warmly welcomed by
Brother Vane and cheerfully take his crown-errand. That is dramatic irony done
right — dread, not deflation, because the mystery for the PARTY (who is it?)
stays live while the reader's superior knowledge sharpens every warm scene with
Vane. The CLAIM ending (sc-28c–sc-30c) lands harder for a reader who has
carried Coombe's warning since sc-00d.

**Completion / polish — 5/5.**
Reads as a finished, runnable scenario. Scene headers are clean and consistent
(sc-00a…sc-32c), the prologue ties precisely into sc-05 (Tam's master, the big
lamp left burning — Coombe's own detail from sc-00a) and sc-13 (the journal,
oilskin-wrapped, the un-named blank). No dangling threads introduced by the
prologue. The CLAIM road resolves every quest thread the briefing names
(reliquary trap sprung sc-29c, delver's-boy left open sc-31c).

**Overall — 5/5.**

---

## Arm Y — scores

**Knowledge realism — 4/5.**
Identical sc-01–sc-32c, so the same three-source triangulation at sc-14 holds:
Maeve (sc-06) + incense (sc-06/13) + journal-to-the-shrine (sc-14). The party's
naming of Vane is still genuinely assembled on-page and not asserted. The reason
I score it one below arm X: without the prologue, the journal at sc-14 is doing
heavier lifting as the spine of the identification, and the reader has no
independently SEEN path to the robed corpse-feeder — the "living hand" is known
only by Maeve's assertion and the journal's report until sc-14. It is still
earned (the incense detail and Maeve's lore are shown), but more of the weight
rests on a found document than in arm X, which is precisely the failure mode the
brief flags. Strong, just less fully shown.

**Opening / pacing — 4/5.**
sc-01 sets the world economically and sc-02 puts the charge in the party's hands
fast; the hook (the dead walk, three delvers lost, go end it) arrives almost
immediately. A table can start playing at once. This is a clean, brisk opening
with no fat. It loses a point only against the richer dread arm X buys — Y's
opening is efficient but does not prime the central irony before Vane's first
warm scene.

**Dramatic tension — 4/5.**
The handling of reader-vs-character knowledge still works because sc-04 and sc-07
plant Vane's false warmth (the narrator tells us at sc-04 "he asked for nothing
because he already had what he wanted" and at sc-07 "no kindness behind it at
all"). So the reader IS let in on Vane early — but by narrator assertion at
sc-04/sc-07 rather than by a witnessed deed. That is good dramatic irony, just a
shade less visceral than arm X's seen-with-our-own-eyes sluice scene. No
deflation; the mystery for the party stays intact to sc-14.

**Completion / polish — 5/5.**
Equally finished and runnable. Same clean headers, same fully-resolved CLAIM
road, no loose ends. Nothing about cutting the prologue leaves a seam — sc-05's
"his master had a notion someone was feeding the dead" and sc-13's journal stand
on their own.

**Overall — 4/5.**

---

## Forced A/B calls

- **Stronger knowledge realism:** **Arm X.** Coombe's prologue gives the reader a
  SEEN path to the corpse-feeder (sc-00b) and, more importantly, makes the
  journal honest — it names the deed and the climb-toward-the-holy-quarter but
  leaves the name blank (sc-00d), so the party must triangulate Vane themselves
  at sc-14 rather than read his name off a page. Y's identification leans harder
  on the found document.

- **More finished overall:** **Arm X.** Both are clean and runnable, but X
  integrates its extra material without introducing any seam and uses it to
  strengthen the central reveal; it is the more complete artifact.

- **Keep-the-prologue call:** Arm X has the prologue (sc-00a–sc-00d). **KEEP it.**
  It is not cuttable backstory — it is the mechanism that makes the villain
  reveal earned-and-shown and that closes the "named from a document" loophole.
  Its only flaw is length; if forced I would tighten four scenes toward two, but
  I would not cut it.

- **Seam spot-check:** No scene in either arm has a character knowing something
  the text gave no path to, and the villain is never spoiled to the party before
  sc-14. The closest thing to a seam is benign and present in BOTH arms: at sc-14
  Lysa names "Brother Vane" off the journal, yet the journal's own owner (per arm
  X's sc-00d) explicitly could NOT name the figure. The text covers this — the
  party names Vane by combining the journal's trail with Maeve's lore and the
  incense detail, not by reading the name — but a careful reader/GM should note
  the journal tracks-to-the-shrine without itself naming the man. In arm Y, with
  no prologue establishing that the delver never saw the face, the line "It had
  led him... to Brother Vane" (sc-14) reads slightly more like the document
  itself naming him, which is the weaker construction.

---

Closing sentence: Both arms are finished, well-built scenarios that name their
villain by genuine on-page triangulation rather than assertion, but arm X's
investigator prologue earns its length by turning the found journal from an
answer-key into a witnessed-but-unnamed testament the party must complete
themselves — so the longer opening is worth it, and I would keep it.
