## sc-00b — PROLOGUE — a robed figure tips the dead into the sluice by night

He found the right place by following the water. There was a sluice down a side-gallery, an old dwarrow work of channelled stone where the flood ran off into a deeper dark, and the fresh dead lay thickest where the channel began — so that was where a patient man set his back to the wet wall and shuttered his lamp to nothing and waited for night, if there was any telling night from day this far under. He waited a long while. Long enough that the cold got into the seams of him and the dark stopped being a thing he looked into and became a thing he was simply inside of.

And then, by night, somebody came down to the sluice.

Coombe heard it before he saw it — not the shuffle of the drowned dead, which he'd learned by now, that slow water-heavy drag, but a *step*. A walking step, careful, picking its footing on wet stone the way a living thing picks footing because a living thing can fall and break and does not want to. He held himself still as the rock he leaned on. A shape came along the channel-edge with a low covered light, and it was robed — a hooded shape, all in a long robe that took the dark and gave nothing back, the face of it lost in the hood and turned away besides. And it was *carrying* something. It was carrying a body, the way you'd carry a heavy sleeping child, awkward and close, and it brought the body to the lip of the sluice and it laid it down and it tipped it in.

The water took the dead thing with hardly a sound. The robed figure stood a moment over the place where it had gone, head bowed — at prayer, or only catching its breath, Coombe could not have said which from where he crouched — and then it turned to go back the way it had come, the covered light swinging.

There. That was it. That was the whole of what he'd come down for, given to him plain in one held breath: the hand behind the new tithes, the living somebody feeding the dead to the deep, doing it here, by night, with its own two arms. He had it. He had found it. The triumph of it went through him cold and sharp and then turned, just as fast, to a thing nearer fear — because he was a single man with a shuttered lamp in the dark and twenty feet off was the one who did *this*, calmly, like a chore.

He did not move on it. Every hunting bone in him said hold, *hold* — do not close, do not breathe, you have a knife and a notion and the other has whatever lets a body do this work alone in the deep and not run mad. So he kept to the shadow of his wall and marked it the only safe way, with his eyes. And the one thing he made sure of, watching that hooded shape lift and tip and turn, was this: it moved like a *person*. Not like the drowned. The drowned dragged; this one walked, and stooped, and balanced a weight, and bowed a living head. Whatever fed the water, it was no monster come up out of the deep to do it. It was someone with a back that tired and feet that minded the wet. Someone living. Someone, Coombe thought, that a man might pass in a lane up top and never know.
