## sc-00a — PROLOGUE — Coombe marks the fresh tithes in the floodwater

Coombe went down alone, and he went down ahead of everything — ahead of the party the reeve was still scraping the coin together to hire, ahead of the talk in the alehouse, ahead of his own better sense, which had told him plainly at the lantern-house door to wait for company and which he had left burning up top with the big lamp. A hunter did not wait. That was the long and short of why he was the last of them down in the dark with no light to climb back to but his own. He'd told the boy the third night. He'd half believed it himself.

He had a notion in his head and he had come down to run it to ground. It was the dead that had put it there — the fresh dead. Down in the lower galleries, where the floodwater stood black and old and had stood so a lifetime, he found bodies in it that were *not* a lifetime old. He knew the difference the way a man who skins game knows the difference between a kill of yesterday and a kill of last week. The long-drowned dwarrow of the hold were one thing, gone to the water and the dark until they were half the water themselves. These others were soft yet. Whole. Recent in a way that nothing down here had any right to be recent.

He crouched at the water's lip with his lamp shuttered to a slit and turned it over in his mind the way he'd turn a track over with his thumb. The old dead drowned when the cisterns burst, all in a night, a lifetime gone. So who were these? Nobody fell down here and drowned fresh — nobody came down here at all but the three delvers before him, and the three before him had not come back up to *be* found, let alone to lie new-drowned in the lower galleries where a man could trip on them. No. These had not fallen. These had been *brought*. Somebody living was adding to the water. Somebody was giving the floodwater new dead, and not long ago, and meaning to.

The town half-knew it already, if the town had cared to think. Old Maeve out past the wind knew it for certain, he'd wager. A tithe of the dead, the lore ran, given to the deep to wake a thing and set it keeping — and a tithe given fresh, given *now*. He hadn't believed the lore when he was warm and up top. He believed it now, kneeling over a drowned face that was younger than the ones around it, with the water lapping cold at the toes of his boots and the dark behind him full of the patient dead. New tithes, he thought, and the word sat ugly in him. New tithes, and a living hand to give them. That was the thing he'd come down to put a face to. So he shuttered the lamp the rest of the way and settled in to watch where the dark would do its work, because a thing like this got *done* somewhere, by somebody, and a hunter who waited in the right place long enough would see the hand that did it.
