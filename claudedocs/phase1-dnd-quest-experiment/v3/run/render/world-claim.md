## sc-00a — PROLOGUE — Coombe marks the fresh tithes in the floodwater

Coombe went down alone, and he went down ahead of everything — ahead of the party the reeve was still scraping the coin together to hire, ahead of the talk in the alehouse, ahead of his own better sense, which had told him plainly at the lantern-house door to wait for company and which he had left burning up top with the big lamp. A hunter did not wait. That was the long and short of why he was the last of them down in the dark with no light to climb back to but his own. He'd told the boy the third night. He'd half believed it himself.

He had a notion in his head and he had come down to run it to ground. It was the dead that had put it there — the fresh dead. Down in the lower galleries, where the floodwater stood black and old and had stood so a lifetime, he found bodies in it that were *not* a lifetime old. He knew the difference the way a man who skins game knows the difference between a kill of yesterday and a kill of last week. The long-drowned dwarrow of the hold were one thing, gone to the water and the dark until they were half the water themselves. These others were soft yet. Whole. Recent in a way that nothing down here had any right to be recent.

He crouched at the water's lip with his lamp shuttered to a slit and turned it over in his mind the way he'd turn a track over with his thumb. The old dead drowned when the cisterns burst, all in a night, a lifetime gone. So who were these? Nobody fell down here and drowned fresh — nobody came down here at all but the three delvers before him, and the three before him had not come back up to *be* found, let alone to lie new-drowned in the lower galleries where a man could trip on them. No. These had not fallen. These had been *brought*. Somebody living was adding to the water. Somebody was giving the floodwater new dead, and not long ago, and meaning to.

The town half-knew it already, if the town had cared to think. Old Maeve out past the wind knew it for certain, he'd wager. A tithe of the dead, the lore ran, given to the deep to wake a thing and set it keeping — and a tithe given fresh, given *now*. He hadn't believed the lore when he was warm and up top. He believed it now, kneeling over a drowned face that was younger than the ones around it, with the water lapping cold at the toes of his boots and the dark behind him full of the patient dead. New tithes, he thought, and the word sat ugly in him. New tithes, and a living hand to give them. That was the thing he'd come down to put a face to. So he shuttered the lamp the rest of the way and settled in to watch where the dark would do its work, because a thing like this got *done* somewhere, by somebody, and a hunter who waited in the right place long enough would see the hand that did it.

## sc-00b — PROLOGUE — a robed figure tips the dead into the sluice by night

He found the right place by following the water. There was a sluice down a side-gallery, an old dwarrow work of channelled stone where the flood ran off into a deeper dark, and the fresh dead lay thickest where the channel began — so that was where a patient man set his back to the wet wall and shuttered his lamp to nothing and waited for night, if there was any telling night from day this far under. He waited a long while. Long enough that the cold got into the seams of him and the dark stopped being a thing he looked into and became a thing he was simply inside of.

And then, by night, somebody came down to the sluice.

Coombe heard it before he saw it — not the shuffle of the drowned dead, which he'd learned by now, that slow water-heavy drag, but a *step*. A walking step, careful, picking its footing on wet stone the way a living thing picks footing because a living thing can fall and break and does not want to. He held himself still as the rock he leaned on. A shape came along the channel-edge with a low covered light, and it was robed — a hooded shape, all in a long robe that took the dark and gave nothing back, the face of it lost in the hood and turned away besides. And it was *carrying* something. It was carrying a body, the way you'd carry a heavy sleeping child, awkward and close, and it brought the body to the lip of the sluice and it laid it down and it tipped it in.

The water took the dead thing with hardly a sound. The robed figure stood a moment over the place where it had gone, head bowed — at prayer, or only catching its breath, Coombe could not have said which from where he crouched — and then it turned to go back the way it had come, the covered light swinging.

There. That was it. That was the whole of what he'd come down for, given to him plain in one held breath: the hand behind the new tithes, the living somebody feeding the dead to the deep, doing it here, by night, with its own two arms. He had it. He had found it. The triumph of it went through him cold and sharp and then turned, just as fast, to a thing nearer fear — because he was a single man with a shuttered lamp in the dark and twenty feet off was the one who did *this*, calmly, like a chore.

He did not move on it. Every hunting bone in him said hold, *hold* — do not close, do not breathe, you have a knife and a notion and the other has whatever lets a body do this work alone in the deep and not run mad. So he kept to the shadow of his wall and marked it the only safe way, with his eyes. And the one thing he made sure of, watching that hooded shape lift and tip and turn, was this: it moved like a *person*. Not like the drowned. The drowned dragged; this one walked, and stooped, and balanced a weight, and bowed a living head. Whatever fed the water, it was no monster come up out of the deep to do it. It was someone with a back that tired and feet that minded the wet. Someone living. Someone, Coombe thought, that a man might pass in a lane up top and never know.

## sc-00c — PROLOGUE — Coombe follows the trail toward the upper town

When its work at the sluice was done the robed figure did not go deeper. That told Coombe something on its own — a thing of the deep would have gone down to the deep, back to the black water it belonged in. This one climbed. It took up its covered light and went *up*, out of the lower galleries by a way that Coombe, for all his weeks down here, had not found — a way that bent its slow course toward the upper reaches of the hold and the town above them.

So he followed. That was the hunt and he knew how to do it; it was the one thing in all of this he was sure of his ground on. He gave the light a long lead and went after it soft, keeping a turning between them, reading the trail where he could not see the maker of it — a wet print fading on dry stone, a smear where a robe-hem had dragged a wet wall, the faint far swing of a covered lamp seen around two corners at once and then not. The way climbed. He felt it climbing in his knees and in the slow thinning of the water from his ankles to nothing, and he felt where it was *going* before he could have named the turnings. Up. Toward the upper ways. Toward the town's own roots, and above those, if a man kept on — the upper town, and the quarter where the ground was holy.

And somewhere on that climb the last of his doubt went out of him, and a colder certainty came in to take its place. This was no thing of the deep. He had known it at the sluice and he knew it past all arguing now, watching it climb toward the living world by a living road: the hand that fed the dead to the water was *someone of the town*. One of them. A neighbour. A face he very likely knew, that had nodded to him in the market or taken his coin or wished him well going down — going down, even, after the very work this one did by night. The thought turned his stomach worse than any drowned face had. Not a monster. A *man* of the Reach, or a woman, climbing home from feeding corpses to the flood as easy as climbing home from any night's labour.

He pushed it then — too hard, he'd think after, if he had the leisure to think after. The trail thinned where the ways branched and branched again near the top, old dwarrow passages crossing newer cellar-cuts, and the covered light went around one corner too many and did not show around the next. He cast for it. He went up one branch and back and up another. A dripping somewhere, a draught from somewhere, three ways that all climbed toward the upper town and no telling which one had taken his quarry up into the lamplit lanes above. He had the *deed*, whole and witnessed. He had where it climbed to. He had everything but the last thing — the turn of a face, the say of a name — and in the branching dark near the top of the under-ways, a hand's breadth from the having of it, the trail ran out under his feet and left him alone.

## sc-00d — PROLOGUE — cut off in the under-ways, the last entry

He sat down where the trail had failed him, in a dry-enough niche off the climbing way, and he got out the journal and the stub of pencil and he wrote it all down while it was whole in him, because a hunter who loses the track keeps the sign, so the next man can take it up. He wrote it careful and he wrote it plain. The fresh dead in the lower galleries, too new for the hold's old drowned. The sluice down the side-gallery where the new ones went in. The robed figure that came by night and carried the dead in its own arms and tipped them to the deep. The trail that climbed — that bent up and up toward the upper town, toward the holy quarter above, a living road for a living hand. He set down everything he was sure of, and the one thing he was *not* sure of he left a clean blank for, because he would not write down a guess as if it were a knowing. He could not name the hand. He had not seen the face. He wrote it as far as he had hunted it and no step further, and where the name should go he wrote only that the figure was someone of the town, and that he had lost it in the upper ways before he could put a face to it.

Done with the writing, he meant to climb. That was the whole of his plan and it was a good one — go up now, at once, while it was hot in him, and lay the tracking before the reeve and the town: that the rising was no curse blown up out of the deep but a thing *fed*, deliberately, by a living hand among them, and that the hand climbed home each night by the upper ways. Let the town hunt from there with more men than one. He wrapped the journal close in its oilskin and put it deep inside his coat, against his chest, the way you keep the one thing that matters dry, and he got his legs under him to climb.

He never climbed. Whether the water had risen behind him while he watched and waited and wrote, or whether the branching ways had turned him without his knowing toward the deep instead of the day, he came down off his niche into a passage that was filling — and then into one that was full — and the under-ways that had let him in did not let him back out. The flood was patient and he was only a man and the dark was very large. It cut him off low down, far from the lantern-light he'd promised himself, and it closed over him there, and the warning he'd meant to carry up went down with him into the cold instead. The journal stayed dry a while longer against his chest, the oilskin keeping its small promise after the man could keep none of his.

His last lines, written before ever he tried to climb, told where he reckoned his body would come to lie, so a finder might know the dead man for who he was. And the very last of them were not for himself at all. *I have got it near enough to touch and could not close my hand on it,* the writing ran, near enough. *Whoever finds this — finish the tracking I could not. The dead are fed by a living hand of this town. Climb where I climbed. Put the name to it I could not put. Name the hand that feeds the dead, and end it.* And there the careful writing stopped, with a clean space after it where a name was never set down, and the water came on.

## sc-01 — Karrow's Reach above the drowned hold

Karrow's Reach was a town that had learned to live on a cliff's lip and keep its back to the drop. It clung there to the broken rim of Vael Mooren, the great dwarrow undercity that had gone down into the dark a lifetime ago — sunk in a single black night when its cisterns burst and drowned the lower halls hall by hall, the way a man drowns who has stopped fighting the water. The old folk still pointed down past the scree to where the topmost gallery-windows showed under the flood like the eyes of something patient.

For a season now the drowning had stopped being only a story. The dead had begun to walk. Out of the floodwater in the lower tunnels they rose by night, the long-drowned dwarrow and the unlucky after them, and they came up the stairs a little further each darkness. Three delvers hired out of the Reach to go down and learn the cause had gone down. None of the three had come back up.

It was the Hollow Crown that did it — though no one in the town above could have named it so. Far below, in the deep vault at the hold's drowned heart, the relic of Vael Mooren's last warden held court over the water, and the warden's own shade kept it, and the dead answered the will that the crown bent to its keeping. That was the truth of the thing, sunk and silent under the Reach, waiting for someone with the nerve to go down and find it.

## sc-02 — The reeve's hall — the party is hired

Reeve Calder's hall was the only stone building in the Reach with a fire worth the name, and he kept the four of them standing in front of it while he made up his mind about them, the way a man counts coins twice he means to part with.

"You've heard it," he said at last. "The dead walk the lower tunnels. The water's coming up. I've sent three good delvers down already and bought myself three widows for the trouble." He had a face like a gate-post and he did not soften it. "I'm hiring the four of you to go down into Vael Mooren, find the thing that wakes the dead, and put an end to it. That's the whole of the charge. Find it. End it."

"A curse," Calder said, and he believed it, you could see he believed it, a curse blown up out of the deep hold the way damp comes up out of a cellar — and curses got ended by reaching whatever spun them and breaking it. That was as far as his thinking ran, and it ran straight.

Doran took the coin off the table and weighed it in his palm before it went into his belt. He read the job the way he read most jobs — no curse in it, only work. Get down. Find the thing. Put it down. Get the four of them back up the stairs breathing. "We'll end your rising," he said, "or we won't come back to tell you we couldn't." And that was the charge taken, the one the whole road would turn on.

## sc-03 — The dead walk the lower tunnels

They spent the first day taking the town's measure of the thing, and the town was frightened in the flat, tired way of people who had stopped sleeping. Drowned dead by night, the alewife said. Up the lower stairs, said the man who mended nets that no one fished any longer. And the water — everyone agreed on the water. It crept higher up the steps each week, patient as the dead themselves.

Mother Henna stood a long while at the lip of the nearest tunnel-mouth with her eyes closed, and what she felt turned her cold. The dead below were not the restless dead she knew, the ones that lingered out of grief or unfinished love and could be eased. These were *held*. Leashed. Something deep beneath had a grip on them, the way a hand has a grip on a dog's chain, and they did not wander — they were *kept*.

Doran read a different map than Henna read. He walked the tunnel-mouths and marked the footing: where the rubble would turn an ankle, where the passage pinched so two men could hold off twenty, where a wet step in the dark would send a fool down into the black water and not bring him up. He filed all of it away. He had buried friends who'd died for want of someone noticing the floor.

## sc-04 — The shrine — the keeper offers help

The shrine of the Reach stood a little above the town, and Brother Vane came down its steps to meet them with both hands out and a smile that warmed the whole grey morning. "Delvers," he said, as though they were the answer to a prayer he'd been wearing thin. "Bless you, bless every one of you for coming. Come up, come up — there's little enough I can give but you shall have all of it."

And he gave it freely. Blessings laid soft on each bowed head. The old lore of the hold, more than the reeve had, told in a low pleasant voice that made the dark below sound almost knowable. The run of his shrine, his fire, his bread. He was a brother of the cloth in every line of him, and he asked for nothing.

He asked for nothing because he already had what he wanted and meant to keep building it — but no line of his face showed that, and no word of it was spoken, not by him and not by anyone. Mother Henna took his welcome for exactly what it looked like: a brother of the cloth, a friend met in a hard place. She had a sense for falseness and she turned it on him the way she turned it on everyone, gently, by habit, and it found nothing. She liked him at once.

## sc-05 — The lantern-house — the boy's missing master

The lantern-house leaned at the edge of town where the road gave up and became the descent, and a boy of maybe twelve kept it lit. Tam, he said his name was, scrubbing at his nose with the back of a sooty hand. The lamps were his master's trade — were, he caught himself, and then said *are* very firmly, as if the word could hold a man up.

"He went down," Tam told them. "Last of all of them. Said he'd be back by the third night and I should keep the big lamp burning so he'd have a light to climb to." The boy's eyes went to the big lamp, burning still, and came back. "That was twelve nights ago."

And he had not gone down idle, the boy was quick to tell them, as if it might matter to the finding. His master had got a notion in his head these last weeks — about the dead going into the water. Fresh dead, new bodies, where there'd no business be any. "He said somebody was *putting* them down there. Feeding the flood. And he meant to learn who." Tam said it proudly and a little fearfully both, the way a child repeats a dangerous thing a grown man said. "He went down to follow where the new ones came from. Said he'd have a name for it when he climbed back up." The boy did not seem to hear what he'd told them — only that his master had gone down on a purpose and a purpose was a thing a man came back from.

He did not ask the way a beggar asks. He asked the way a child asks who has decided the asking will work because it has to. "You're going down. Find him. He's only lost — it's dark down there, anybody could get turned around in the dark. Just find him and bring me word." Pip, who had been half a step apart from the others the whole morning, found the boy looking straight at her, and took the errand. "We'll bring you word," she said. "On my hand, we will." The boy nodded as though that settled it.

## sc-06 — The wise-woman and the first tithe

Old Maeve lived furthest out of anyone, in a hut where the wind never stopped, and she kept the lore the shrine did not — the cistern-lore, the deep-lore, the things the dwarrow had let slip before the water took them. She did not get up when they came. She told it from her stool by the fire, in the long way, the way it was meant to be told.

"The water down there's not just water," she said. "It was *given* to. Long ago, when the hold first went under, someone gave it a tithe — a tithe of the dead, bodies down into the dark, to wake a thing and set it keeping. That's old. That's a lifetime old." She turned her head, slow, and her eyes were sharper than the rest of her. "But a tithe wakes nothing twice. If the dead are walking *now*, it's because someone living is feeding the water *now*. Somebody's giving it the dead again, fresh. You find that hand, you've found your rising."

Pip lingered when the others made for the door — she had a way of being the last out of any room — and so it was Pip alone who heard the old woman mutter to the fire, not meaning it for them at all: "New tithe's got a smell on it. Incense. Smells like shrine-work, this one does." Pip filed it where she filed everything, said nothing, and stepped out into the wind after the rest.

## sc-07 — The reliquary errand — bring me the crown

They came back up to the shrine before the descent, and Brother Vane met them as warm as before, and only at the last, almost shyly, did he ask his favour. "When you find the cause of it," he said — "and you may, you may find the relic itself, the old warden's crown, if the tales run true — bring it to me. Not to the reeve. To me." He laid a hand to his own breast. "I have a reliquary blessed and waiting. A thing like that should be *sealed*, by a brother's hands, so it can never wake another corpse. Will you bring it to me, when you have it?"

It was a kind thing to ask, the way he asked it, and it asked nothing but trust. Mother Henna gave it gladly — of course they would, of course a relic like that belonged sealed in holy keeping and not loose in a soldier's pack. She took the errand on for all of them and meant to keep it.

The hand he laid to his breast was over the place his heart should have been, and there was no kindness behind it at all. The reliquary was no seal. To hand him the crown would be to hand him the thing itself — the power he had bent a lifetime and a season of the drowned dead toward gathering into his own grasp. But not a word of that was in the room, and Henna walked down to the descent warm with the work of being trusted.

## sc-08 — Down into the upper galleries

The descent took the daylight away from them a flight at a time. By the upper galleries of the hold the world had become lamplight and dripping stone and the smell of old water that has stood too long in the dark. The flood was about their knees here and rising as they went — not fast, but always, so that a step that had been ankle-deep on the way in would be calf-deep on the way out. Doran went first with the lamp, and the four of them waded down into Vael Mooren together.

## sc-09 — First of the drowned dead

They came up out of a flooded gallery without a sound, which was the worst of it — no moan, no rattle, just the water breaking as drowned shoulders rose through it, and then the dead were wading at the party with the deliberate patience of things that did not need to hurry.

Doran read them in a heartbeat and did not waste the read. Slow, he saw — slow, but tireless, no breath in them to spend, no fear to break. You could not rout what could not be made afraid. So he did not try. He fell back to where the gallery pinched to a throat barely wide enough for two abreast, set his shield in it, and made the dead come to him one and two at a time where their numbers meant nothing.

And Mother Henna, striking one down at the throat-mouth, felt the strangest thing as it fell. The corpse did not simply *stop*. The moment its drowned weight let go, something in it pulled — downward, deepward, back toward the flood and the dark beneath the flood, the way a fish pulls on a line when the rod bends. Every one of them did it as it died. As if each were reeled in on a single thread that ran down out of sight.

## sc-10 — The rogue finds the flooded stair

When the last of the dead had gone slack and slid back into the water that bore them, Pip went looking, because the others fought and she found, and that was the shape of the four of them. Behind a fall of broken rubble at the gallery's far end — a fall that looked like nothing, like the ceiling had simply given way years past — she found that the rubble hid a stair. A flooded stair, going down, narrow and secret, the true throat of the hold.

She crouched at the top of it and read it the way she read a locked drawer. There were tools on the steps. Dropped tools, a pick and a snapped lantern-bracket, lying where the flood hadn't quite reached, not rusted to nothing the way a lifetime would rust them. *Recent.* Someone living had come this hidden way down, and not long ago, and gone on into the dark below. She marked it, and only she had marked it, and she said the next part out loud: "Somebody's been down here. Lately. Down *this* way."

## sc-11 — The rival sellsword in the dark

He was waiting where the stair bottomed out, sitting on a dry shelf of stone with his blade across his knees and a lamp of his own, and he did not startle when their light found him — he'd heard them coming a while. "Took you long enough," he said. Skell, he named himself, when Doran asked it at sword's length. A sellsword, like them, only down ahead of them, and he wanted the same thing in the dark that any sellsword wanted: the worth of whatever the warden's crown would fetch, and he meant to come up with it.

He offered to walk with them — four blades and his was five, and the dead didn't care how many you were — and his reasons were plain and his manners were not bad. He'd work beside them. Down here the living stood with the living.

Doran took his measure and kept it short. A fighting man, this one — competent, you could see it in how he held the blade and how he sat where he could see both ways. And a man who'd turn the moment the gold was actually on the table, sure as water runs down; Doran had soldiered beside enough Skells to read the type from the door. So he said aye, walk with us, and then he put Skell where he could see him — out in front, in the lamplight, never at his back. What Skell meant to do past the turning Doran couldn't have said. He only knew not to give him the chance to do it unwatched.

## sc-12 — The cleric reads the dead's leash

The deeper they went the louder the thing became to Henna, until down here she could read it plainly and no longer had to guess. The leash she had felt up top — the downward pull on every falling corpse — she could trace now to its source. Every walking dead thing in Vael Mooren was bound. Not loosed by a curse to wander, the way the reeve thought. *Held.* Bound by a single will, and that will was seated somewhere below them in the deep, at the bottom of the dark, holding all of them at once on one tether.

And a will, she said — half to the others and half to herself — a *will* was not like a curse. A curse you broke. A will you could break, yes. But a will could also be *spoken to*. There was someone down there, in the worst sense of someone. And someone, she thought, might be reasoned with where a curse never could.

## sc-13 — The last delver's body and journal

They found him in a side-cell off the passage, half out of the floodwater, one arm still hooked over a broken bench as if he'd dragged himself up to die a little drier. He'd been dead a while. The water had been kind enough to keep him whole and unkind enough to make sure no one would ever set the face right in a mother's memory.

It was Pip who waded in and went through him, gently, the way you go through the dead when you mean them no disrespect, only need what they're past needing. His journal was still on him, oilskin-wrapped, the wrapping done careful — a man's last care, keeping his words dry when he could no longer keep himself so. She lifted it free and held it a moment before she said anything, because she already knew, looking at the careful wrapping and the careless body, what it meant. This was Tam's master. This was the answer she'd sworn to bring the boy. He was not lost. He was dead, and the proof of it was in her hands, and so was whatever he'd died learning.

And there was a thing she'd been carrying since Maeve's door that she'd told no one — kept it the way she kept everything, close, until it had somewhere to sit. It had somewhere to sit now. She crouched there over the drowned man with the journal in her hand and she said it out at last, low, to the three of them. "The old woman. Maeve. When you'd all gone out I heard her talking to her fire. She said the new tithe had a smell on it." Pip looked from the careful oilskin to the careful body. "Incense, she said. Said it smelled like shrine-work." She let the word fall and lie there beside the dead man's last care, and did not say what it meant, because she didn't have to. "I should've said it sooner. I'm saying it now."

## sc-14 — The journal names the corpse-tithe

They dried their hands and crowded the lamp and Lysa read it out, because she read fastest and her voice didn't shake. The delver had been a careful man to the end, and the journal was the careful man's whole hunt set down day on day. He'd done what they were doing — followed the rising back toward its cause — only he'd got further, because he'd had longer and gone alone and been afraid the right amount. He'd marked where the fresh dead went into the water. He'd watched the floodwater take new bodies, night on night, and he'd not been content to know *that* it happened; he'd set himself to learn whose hand did it. Page on page he tracked it — a watch kept, a path followed, the source of the corpse-tithes run down at last to the one who fed them. And the last pages told where the tracking had led him, and the writing of it had got him killed.

It had led him to the shrine. To the shrine-keeper. To Brother Vane.

It sat among them in the lamplight like a fifth body. The delver had followed the fresh dead to the very hand that put them in the water — the warm brother with both hands out, the friend who'd blessed their bowed heads and asked only that they bring him the crown to keep it safe. He was the living hand behind the whole season of the drowned dead. The buried cause of it, that the reeve had called a curse and Maeve had called a hand and this dead man had hunted down to a name — surfaced, here, with the name on it in a dead man's careful script.

Lysa lowered the journal and set the rest of it together, fast and cold and certain, the way she set anything together. She laid the journal's hunt beside the two things they'd carried down into the dark without quite knowing what they were worth. Maeve's lore, first — *a tithe wakes nothing twice; if the dead are walking now, someone living is feeding the water now.* Someone living. And then the thing Pip had just laid over the body: that the new tithe smelled of incense, of shrine-work. Three pieces, and they were the one piece. The wise-woman's living hand, the rogue's shrine-smell, and a dead man's hunt run to ground at Brother Vane's own door. "It was never a curse," Lysa said. "There's no curse in any of it. It was him. It was Vane that woke the warden, and we've three witnesses to it now and one of them died getting it." She had no more doubt of it than she had of the runes on the wall.

## sc-15 — The wizard finds the warden's record

The runes on the wall, as it happened, were the next thing she found. The side-cell was older than the dead delver by a hundred lifetimes, and graven into the wet stone of it, in the close hard script of the dwarrow, was a record. Lysa wiped the silt from it and held the lamp close and began to read, and she was the only one of the four who could — the others saw scratches; she saw a hand and a voice.

It was the warden's own record. The man who had kept Vael Mooren when Vael Mooren went under. His name, she read, was Orrek. And he was no monster, whatever the dead he held had made the town believe. The record was a record of *duty* — of a keeper who had stayed at his post when the water came, who had taken up a burden and held it because there was no one else to hold it. A man bound to his charge. She read his name out softly to the others, Orrek, and found she said it with respect, the way you name a man and not a thing.

## sc-16 — The locked vault door

The passage gave at last onto the door, and the door ended everything for a while. The deep vault — they all knew it for the deep vault the moment the lamp found it, a slab of dwarrow-work taller than Doran and sealed so close the floodwater barely wept through the seam. Doran set his shoulder to it on principle and got nothing but a bruise for it. Skell tried a pry-bar at the seam and the bar bent first. No force was going to open this. The dwarrow had not built it to be forced.

It was Lysa, reading the lintel, who found the rest of the bad news and spoke it: the door knew only one key. The warden's own. Graven plain above the seam — *only the warden's key turns this lock* — and nothing else, no trick of leverage or learning, would admit them. The crown was on the far side of that slab, and the crown could not be reached, not by any of them, not at all, until someone found the key. So the errand changed shape in front of them: before anything, the key. Pip took it on without being asked, because finding was hers. The warden's key, wherever a lifetime of water had taken it. One way past the door, and one only.

## sc-17 — The warden's cell and the drowned key

Pip went back to the flooded stair, because the key of a man who fell a lifetime ago would be where the man fell, and the careful tools on the secret stair had told her the warden's own way down. Off that stair she found his cell — Orrek's, it had to be, you could feel the long stillness of it — drowned to the ceiling in one half, the silt undisturbed since the world above had a different shape.

The key was in the silt. It had lain there the whole lifetime, sunk where Orrek had fallen with it still on him, and the flood had buried it in the patient grey muck and forgotten it the way the water forgot everything. Pip went in to her chest in the cold black and felt for it with bare fingers and found it — a thing of dark dwarrow-iron, heavy, cold past cold — and lifted it dripping into the lamplight. The key. The one she'd sworn to find, found. She wrung the silt off it and put it deep in her coat where nothing would lift it back out, and she knew exactly what she was carrying: the only thing standing between four delvers and the deep vault.

## sc-18 — The crown's true nature, read in runes

With the warden's record read whole, Lysa could tell them at last what the crown actually *was*, and it was stranger than any of them had braced for. The Hollow Crown did not merely command the dead. It held *Orrek* — his shade, bound into the relic as surely as the dead were bound to him. The warden and the crown were one keeping, and to deal with the one was to deal with the other. There were three roads the relic allowed and no more, she told them, and she named them out plainly so the choice would stand clear when it came.

The first was to *unmake* it — to shatter the crown by an unmaking she alone had read in the runes and alone could speak, and so end the binding by ending the thing that bound. The second was to *claim* it — to take the crown up in living hands and pass its power to a new holder, the leash of the dead changing grip but not breaking. And the third — this was the one the runes had given up last, and the one that changed the colour of the other two — the third was the *oath*. For the record said Orrek kept the dead under *duty*, not malice; a keeper worn past the end of his charge, holding on only because no one had ever come to take the holding from him kindly. A shade like that would hear a fair oath fairly sworn. It could be bound by a swearing — the rising laid down, the dead set at rest, the crown neither broken nor seized. Three roads, she said again, so none of them would mistake it: unmake it, claim it, or bind it by an oath. To leave it on his brow and walk away was no road at all — that was only refusing to choose, and the water would go on rising while they refused.

Mother Henna heard the third road named and something in her settled and stood up at the same time. She understood, the moment Lysa said it, that the oath was a thing she herself could do — no shattering and no seizing in it at all. She could speak to him. She could swear to Orrek's shade and bind the dead by the swearing, and lay the whole rising down without ever breaking the crown or taking it. The thought of it steadied her hand the rest of the way down to the door.

## sc-19 — The key turns — the vault opens

Pip put the warden's key into the deep vault's lock and it went home with a sound like a stone settling in a grave, and then it would not turn for her — too stiff, too long sunk — so Doran put his hand over hers on the cold iron and set his shoulder to the slab at the same time, and between her turn and his weight the seal of a lifetime gave. It did not swing. It *ground*, slow, shedding silt and black water from the seam, opening by inches onto the deeper dark beyond, where the flood stood still and waited and was a great deal more than knee-deep.

## sc-20 — The deep vault and the warden's shade

The deep vault was a drowned hall and the water in it was black and unmoving as glass, and out of the middle of it the warden rose.

He came up the way the dead came up, without sound, the water sheeting off a shape that had once been a broad dwarrow in a keeper's mail, and on the drowned brow of him sat the Hollow Crown — a thin dark circlet that drank the lamplight rather than give any back. The dead of the hold stood ranked behind him in the black water, rank on rank, all the drowned of Vael Mooren, and not one of them moved, because he had not moved them. He held the crown and the crown held them and he was the still point all the long leash ran back to.

Orrek's shade knew what he was and what was on his brow. He knew the crown bound him to keep the hold's dead and that the binding had only three ends — its unmaking, its claiming, or an oath sworn that would loose the burden either way. He had known it a lifetime. He looked at the four small lights that had come down into his hall and he waited to learn which end they had brought him.

And Mother Henna, looking up into the worn drowned face of him, felt no wrath there at all. She had braced for wrath. What she felt was *weariness* — bottomless, a keeper worn down past the last edge of his duty, holding and holding because no one had ever come to take the holding away. Not a monster. A tired man who could not put a thing down.

## sc-21 — The crown between them — the choice

So the four of them stood at the water's edge before the warden, and the crown stood between them, and the three roads Lysa had named were all there in the dark to take. Shatter it — and end the binding by ending the crown. Claim it — and take its power into living hands. Parley — and swear the oath the shade would hear, and bind the dead by the swearing. Three roads, no fourth, and the choosing was theirs.

And there was a fifth body at the water's edge that had come this far at their heels. Skell. He'd walked behind them down every stair and held his peace and his blade, and now his eyes were on the crown and nothing but the crown, and you could see the whole of his reckoning in his face: whichever road these four took, he meant to come up out of Vael Mooren with the worth of that circlet in his hands. He said nothing yet. He only watched it drink the light, and waited.

## sc-22 — The fork at the deep vault

This was the turning the whole delve had bent toward — the one choice that everything down the stairs had been narrowing to. Shatter the crown. Claim the crown. Parley with the shade. Standing in the cold breath of the deep vault, with the warden waiting and the dead ranked behind him and Skell's eyes burning on the relic, the party made the choice the adventure turned on.

They chose to claim it.

## sc-23c — CLAIM — the party takes the crown's power

It was Doran who waded out, in the end, because someone had to and his were the steadiest hands, and Henna's oath died unspoken in her throat as she watched him go. The warden did not fight it. That was the thing none of them forgot after — Orrek did not fight. He bowed his drowned head, slow, almost as if some part of him were grateful, and let living hands lift the Hollow Crown off his brow and out of his keeping.

And the power came with it. Doran felt it come the moment the dark circlet left the shade and passed into his grip — a weight that had nothing to do with the weight of the iron, a cold authority running up his arm and into the back of his skull, the leash of all the drowned dead of Vael Mooren passing out of the warden's hold and into his own. The crown was theirs. It had gone from the warden to the four of them, and they held it now, and the holding had a price they had not yet had time to feel.

## sc-24c — The warden bound under a new will

Orrek's shade did not sink back into the black water. That was the first of the price. With the crown wrested from his brow he was not freed — the binding did not release him, it only changed hands. The same leash that had run from the dead to him now ran from the dead through the crown to the party, and Orrek hung on the end of it with the rest, bound under their will now in the place where his own duty had been. Neither loosed nor at rest. Henna watched the weary face and saw it understand that even this end, even being unseated, had not let him put the burden down. He had only handed the chain to a newer hand and stayed chained.

## sc-25c — The dead made to obey the crown's holder

But the dead obeyed. That was the other side of the cold coin, and it was undeniable, and for a moment it felt like winning. With the crown on living hands the drowned of Vael Mooren no longer rose wild and leashless toward the stairs — they bent. They turned their dead faces toward the one who held the crown and they *waited*, ranked and still and obedient, the way a kennel comes to heel. The wild rising was over. Doran had ended it the way the reeve had charged them to end it — only not by breaking the thing that woke the dead, but by taking its handle and mastering it. The charge was discharged. It was discharged in a dark coin, and they all felt the darkness of it even as the dead stood down.

## sc-26c — The rival kneels or is broken

Skell saw the dead come to heel and Skell was no fool. He had come down for the crown's worth and he had watched four delvers become the masters of a hall full of corpses, and he did the sum fast. A man could draw on four tired delvers. A man could not draw on four tired delvers and a standing army of the obedient drowned. So he laughed, short and ugly, and he knelt — not in worship, in arithmetic. "A share, then," he said. "Under the new power. I'll take a share and a grave a long way off." He threw in with the crown's holder, reckoning a place at the new table better than a place among the dead at it, and that was the whole of Skell, plain at last for any of them to see: a man who had only ever served the winning side, and now knew which side that was.

## sc-27c — The climb up with the Hollow Crown

They climbed up out of Vael Mooren the way they had come down, except that the dark came up with them. Doran bore the Hollow Crown — none of them had said aloud that it should be his, but none of them reached for it either — and at its call the obedient dead climbed in their wake, the drowned of the hold rising out of their flood at last to follow the living up the long stairs into the air. The party came up out of the drowned hold not as it had gone down. It had gone down four delvers with a charge. It came up bearing a crown and a tail of the walking dead, into the grey light of the Reach.

## sc-28c — The shrine-keeper's reliquary, hand it over

And there at the top, at the shrine above the town, waited Brother Vane, both hands out, warm as the first morning — and the party, the crown in their keeping at last, could finally do the kind thing he had asked of them. Henna remembered the errand even now, with the dead at their backs; she had taken it on gladly and she meant to keep her word. The relic should be *sealed*, he'd said, by a brother's hands, so it could never wake another corpse. Here was the relic. Here were the brother's hands, reaching for it. She put the Hollow Crown into Brother Vane's keeping and felt the small clean relief of a promise kept — the reliquary errand fulfilled, the dark thing passed into holy hands to be made safe. The crown left the party's hold and settled into the shrine-keeper's grasp at last: the crown he had schemed for, all along, through a lifetime and a season of the drowned dead, come home to the very hand that had woken them.

## sc-29c — The shrine-keeper's betrayal at the shrine

The warmth went out of him the instant his fingers closed on the iron.

It went out the way a lamp goes out — all at once, and then a different thing standing where the light had been. Brother Vane lifted the Hollow Crown and the obedient dead, that had followed the party up out of the dark at the crown's call, felt the call change hands. The leash had run from the dead through the crown to the four of them; now it ran to him. The drowned of Vael Mooren turned their dead faces from the delvers who had carried them up and fixed them on the brother who held the iron, and waited on his will instead.

He did not pretend any longer. He threw off the helpful brother like a wet cloak, and there was no friend left in his face, only the patient hunger that had been under it the whole time — under the blessings, under the bread, under the kind soft errand they had carried for him every step of the long climb. He had schemed a lifetime and a season of the drowned dead toward this, he told them, almost gently, the way you'd tell a slow child a thing it should have seen for itself. He had woken the warden. He had fed the water. He had asked them, so warmly, to bring the crown to no hand but his — and they had gone down and taken it up and climbed it all the way back to him and *set it in his palm*. He would rule the Reach now, he said, by the dead it commanded. And the dead the party had mastered waded toward the party at his word, because the party had handed away the one thing on the earth that ruled them.

## sc-30c — The crown changes hands — who rules the Reach

This was the end their grasping had bought them, and it had a name, and the name was Brother Vane. They had not been beaten in the deep vault. They had won there — taken the crown off the warden's brow, brought the wild dead to heel, mastered the very rising the reeve had charged them to end. And then they had carried their winning up the long stairs in their own hands and laid it, warm and trusting, into the one waiting palm that had wanted it all along. The crown ruled Karrow's Reach now, as it was always going to; the only thing the party had decided, down all those stairs, was *whose brow it ruled from*. They had reached for it. They had got it. And in the getting they had delivered it — and the Reach — to the schemer who had sent them down after it, the friend who had blessed their bowed heads and then turned their own dead on them the instant the iron was his. The living of the Reach were held by the dead, and the dead answered to Brother Vane, because four delvers had grasped at a crown and handed it to him.

Up in his stone hall Reeve Calder learned what his authority was worth now, which was nothing. His charge had been to end the rising, and the rising was ended — mastered, *kept*, the dead taught a new hand — and the new hand was not his, and would never be his. His hall held no power at all over the man who wore the iron and the corpses that came at its call. The Reach answered to the crown. The crown answered to Brother Vane. And Calder, who had hired four delvers to save his town and got it stolen out from under him by the brother on the hill, sat in a hall that no longer meant anything, in a Reach that had only changed masters, and learned the difference too late to spend it.

## sc-31c — The wise-woman and the boy under a new master

Old Maeve sat by her wind-shaken fire and named the bargain for what it was, the way she named everything, in the long plain way with no comfort in it. The rising was not ended, she said. The rising was *mastered*. That was a different word and a worse one. The dead still walked — they had only been taught a new hand to walk for. The Reach had not been saved from the drowned. The Reach had only changed which fist the drowned obeyed.

And in the lantern-house the big lamp still burned, because no one had ever told the boy to put it out. Tam kept it lit for a master who would not climb to its light, and no one came to tell him so. The crown had made rulers and then made other rulers, and in all the changing of hands not one of the new hands had a word to spare for a lantern-boy. He was left in the crown's long shadow with the lamp burning and the question still in him — *where is he, did you find him, you swore you'd bring me word* — and no one left who owed him the answer.

## sc-32c — CLAIM ending — a crowned Reach, and the cost

And so the Reach ended under the Hollow Crown. The dead commanded, ranked and obedient, walking now to a living will. The wise-woman grim at her fire, knowing the difference between *ended* and *kept* and made to live in it. The reeve hollow in his hall, a man of authority over a town that no longer answered to authority. And the lantern-boy, his big lamp burning down toward a master who would never come, never told what the dark had done to the man who went down into it — left holding a light for the dead, under the shadow of a crown, while the water below stood still and waited for whoever held the iron next.
