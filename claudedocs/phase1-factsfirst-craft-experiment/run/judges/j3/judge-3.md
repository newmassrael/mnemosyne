# Judge 3 — Verdict

Three matched world-lines of the same branching mystery ("The Vane Observatory"
/ death of Dr. Aurel Crane), each judged as two linear versions, A and B.

A general structural note that bears on every world-line: the two versions are
built on opposite narrative strategies. Version A opens by handing the reader
the entire solution in Scenes 1–2 (Pike's theft and forged telegram, the
undelivered wire, Junia on the gallery) in a cool omniscient register, and then
spends the rest of the story watching Hale read the same physical evidence
*backwards* before he catches up. Version B opens at the door with Hale, keeps a
tighter third-person-limited frame around him, and leaks the truth to the reader
through periodic narratorial asides ("The reader knows this. Hale does not,
yet."). Version B carries dialogue, a body examination, physical staging, and a
genuine confrontation scene; Version A is more essayistic and summary-driven.
This difference is consistent across all three world-lines and drives most of my
scoring.

---

## World-line 1 — confront / reveal (W1-confront-reveal)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A — 1 internal tension, no hard error.**
- The clock mechanism. A's whole spine (Scenes 5 and 15) is that *nothing*
  stopped the clock by hand — the fall rang the shared north pier and the
  brittle pendulum ceased; "The clock had recorded no lie and no alibi."
  This is internally consistent within A and is even re-confirmed in Scene 15.
  No contradiction inside A.
- Minor: Scene 17 has Junia describe Pike's intrusion as having *startled*
  Crane into the fall ("the sound of an intruder on the stair had startled
  him"), yet she also insists "Pike had not laid a hand on Crane." This is
  coherent but thin — she claims eyewitness certainty about causation
  (startle, not contact) from twelve feet below in the dark, which strains
  her knowledge slightly. I count this as a soft believability issue, not a
  continuity break (see item 5). No hard continuity errors in A.

**Version B — 1 genuine cross-version causality divergence, internally clean.**
- B gives a *different* clock mechanism: Pike grips the clockwork to save
  himself during the struggle and stops the pendulum with his hand, leaving a
  thread of sleeve in the works (Scene 18; the bruise on Crane's wrist is also
  paid off here). This contradicts A's "the fall rang the pier" mechanism — but
  the two versions need not agree with each other, and within B it is fully
  consistent: Scene 3 establishes "a clock stops because someone stops it," the
  wrist bruise is planted in Scene 2, and Scene 18 discharges both. This is
  *tighter* setup/payoff than A, not an error.
- One small knowledge wrinkle in B Scene 17: Junia says "Crane was already
  above me... he must have gone up before I did" — she infers, honestly flagged
  as inference ("I had not seen him come"). Clean.
- No hard continuity errors in B. **B = 0 errors; A = 0 hard errors, 1 soft
  knowledge strain.**

### 2. Prose quality (told story vs assembled facts)
Version A reads intelligently but *essayistically*: long summarizing paragraphs,
heavy use of aphorism ("a told clock meant a teller, and a teller meant
intent"), and the omniscient narrator explaining the meaning of each clue rather
than dramatizing it. It has a voice, but it is a *narrator's lecture* voice; very
few scenes contain a scene. Version B has the same authorial intelligence but
spends it on rendered moments — the body under the sheet, the developing trays,
the lamplight interrogation, Junia's account delivered as living testimony in
Scenes 17–18. B reads as a told *story*; A reads closer to an annotated solution.
**A = 3, B = 4.**

### 3. Stakes and tension
A pre-empts its own tension by giving away the answer in Scene 1 and then telling
us repeatedly that Hale is wrong; the suspense becomes "when will he catch up,"
which A itself keeps deflating with foreknowledge. B preserves dramatic pressure
through dialogue and the slow tightening on Pike, and the Scene 17–18 reveal
lands as a real climax with physical struggle. **A = 3, B = 4.**

### 4. Setup / payoff
Both pay off the core clues (clock, plate, key, two letters). B is materially
better: it *plants* the wrist bruise (Scene 2) and the body's heaviness, then
discharges them in the struggle (Scene 18), and it dramatizes Pike's "recall"
slip (Scene 10) as a setup that the study-drawer search pays off. A's payoffs are
real but pre-explained, so they satisfy less. **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
A's Hale sometimes knows-by-narrator: the omniscient voice repeatedly tells us
what Hale "would come back to" and what the objects "really" were before Hale
could know, which is narrator omniscience rather than character knowledge — a
craft choice, not a violation. The one soft strain is Junia's confident causal
account in Scene 17. B is cleaner: every fact Hale acts on, he has first
physically obtained, and Junia explicitly marks the limits of what she could see
and hear ("I could not hear all of it... the shutter sings"). **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 6. Forced choice for W1: **B**
B wins W1 decisively. It tells the story rather than annotating it, it preserves
tension that A spends in its first scene, and crucially its clue architecture is
*tighter*: the wrist bruise and the thread-in-the-clockwork give the struggle a
forensic spine A simply does not have, and the reveal is a dramatized human scene
rather than a summary. A is elegant and never wrong, but it reads like a
beautifully written explanation of a mystery rather than the mystery itself.

## World-line 2 — confront / burn (W2-confront-burn)

This is the branch where Junia *burns* the night-log rather than name the
climber, leaving Hale unable to close the death.

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A — 0 hard errors.** A re-uses Scenes 1–16 verbatim from its W1 spine
(same shared-pier clock theory) and diverges at Scene 17 (burn). The aftermath
(Scenes 17–18) is internally consistent: with the log gone, Hale can certify the
fraud and the hidden-key intrusion but not the death; he lets the policy pay as
misadventure for want of proof. Coherent. One note: A's Scene 18 says the burned
log was "the keystone" that anchored hour and hand together, yet A's own clock
(stopped by the fall, not a hand) and the blank plate survive the fire and still
fix the hour and break Junia's alibi — so the log was arguably *not* the
keystone, weakening A's stated stakes. Soft logic strain, not a continuity error.

**Version B — 0 hard errors, and it handles the same beat more rigorously.** B
diverges at Scene 17 (burn) after its longer 1–16 spine. Critically, B's ending
is internally *more* consistent with its own clue set: because B's clock was
stopped by a *hand* (thread in the works) rather than by the fall, the death in B
genuinely *is* underdetermined once the witness refuses to speak — Hale honestly
writes "cause of death by human agency, agent unestablished" (Scene 19). B also
correctly keeps the surviving physical evidence (drained account, forged recall,
stopped clock, key, unsent resignation, blank plates) and reasons about exactly
what each can and cannot prove. **Both 0 hard errors; B's bookkeeping of what
survives the fire is tighter than A's.**

### 2. Prose quality
Same A/B character as W1: A summarizes and aphorizes; B renders. B's burn scene
(Scene 17) is a dramatized act with Hale choosing not to intervene and reasoning,
in the moment, that he cannot take the *name* from her even if he could take the
book; A's burn is narrated more than enacted. B's closing Scene 19 ("the most
honest sentence he had ever signed, and the least use to anybody, and he carried
it down into the valley like a stone") is the strongest single line of prose in
either W2 file. **A = 3, B = 4.**

### 3. Stakes and tension
The burn branch is the higher-stakes branch for both versions, and both use it
well — the irreversible destruction of evidence is inherently tense. But A,
having front-loaded the solution, has less to lose dramatically; the burn reads
as a foregone melancholy. B earns the loss because the reader has watched Hale
build toward the name through dialogue and confrontation, so the fire *takes*
something the reader felt him reaching for. **A = 3, B = 4.**

### 4. Setup / payoff
Both pay off the burn as a deliberate, prepared act (Junia floats burning it in
Scene 15/239 in B; A reaches it by its own route). B's payoff is richer because
the destroyed log is genuinely load-bearing in B's evidentiary scheme (hand-stop
clock + log were the only links to a *person*), so its loss has real consequence;
in A the surviving fall-stopped clock and blank plate undercut the claimed
keystone status of the log. B's "agent unestablished" certificate is the cleaner
payoff of the whole branch premise. **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
Both clean. Neither Hale acts on something he cannot know; both versions are
careful that the *name* is precisely the one thing the evidence cannot supply
once the log burns — A states it, B dramatizes it. B's Scene 18 inventory of
"mute witnesses... that did not depend on Junia Frost's tongue" is a model of a
character reasoning strictly from what he holds. **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 6. Forced choice for W2: **B**
B again. The burn branch is fundamentally about *what is lost and what remains
provable*, and B's evidentiary machine is built so that the loss actually
matters and the surviving certificate ("something else, agent unestablished") is
the honest, devastating consequence. A reaches a similar place but on a clue set
where the log's destruction is less decisive than A claims, and it tells the
ending more than it stages it. B is the better-engineered and better-written
version of this branch.

## World-line 3 — quiet (W3-quiet)

This is the branch where Hale takes the *cold/quiet road*: he does **not**
confront Pike at the ledger, but stays silent and lets the guilty man move.

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A — 0 hard errors.** A diverges at Scene 9 ("Hale said nothing") and
solves the case entirely from objects, never extracting a confession. It is
internally consistent: silent ledger reconstruction (Scene 9), watching Pike hide
the duplicate ledgers and use the spare key by night (Scenes 10–11), the
fall-stopped clock re-derived (Scene 14), the undelivered wire found in the loose
records and traced to the post-box (Scene 15). Coherent throughout. Note: A's W3
reaches the *correct* solution (intrusion, not murder) by objects alone, and
notably the Junia eyewitness thread is never needed — A simply never gets her
testimony, which is consistent with the "quiet road" premise.

**Version B — 0 hard errors, and it is the most intricately consistent of all six
files.** B's quiet road keeps Hale from confronting Pike at the ledger (Scene 9
gives him a courteous goodnight instead) and then dramatizes Pike's nocturnal
*visiting* of the drawer — Pike does **not** destroy the telegram but reads it
and replaces it (Scene 10), which Hale reads as "not the fear of an embezzler...
larger, and older, and with a death in it." This is then paid off thematically in
Scene 19, where Junia explains *why* Pike keeps the paper ("it is the only place
the old man still is... the proof, to him, that there was a cause"). That is the
single best causal-emotional through-line in the entire comparison, and it is
seeded in Scene 10 and discharged in Scene 19 without a seam. No errors.

### 2. Prose quality
B is clearly the stronger telling here. A's quiet road is the most essayistic of
all three A-files — long stretches of "He laid it out for himself... as a
draughtsman lays out a plan" — competent but inert, a man narrating his own
deductions. B dramatizes the quiet road as genuine suspense (the watcher in the
doorway, the board that speaks in the passage, Pike pressing his hand on the
closed drawer "as a man presses on a wound") and then delivers the fullest, most
human reveal of any branch in Scenes 17–19. **A = 3, B = 4.**

### 3. Stakes and tension
A's quiet road has the *least* tension of all six files: with the solution given
in Scene 1 and Hale working alone and silently through paper, there is almost no
dramatic pressure — it is a procedural in summary. B converts the same quiet
premise into stalking tension (will Pike notice he is watched; what is in the
drawer he cannot destroy) and then a confession-free emotional climax. **A = 2,
B = 4.**

### 4. Setup / payoff
B's drawer-visiting setup (Scene 10) paying off into Junia's "it is the only
place the old man still is" (Scene 19) is the strongest setup/payoff in the whole
experiment — it converts a plot mechanic (where is the telegram) into character
tragedy. A pays off its clues correctly but flatly, and its quiet road
deliberately forgoes the human confrontation, so its payoffs are intellectual
rather than emotional. **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
Both clean on hard knowledge. A is scrupulous that Hale reaches the truth from
objects he physically obtained. B is equally scrupulous *and* richer: Hale's big
inference (that Pike's fear "has a death in it") is explicitly built from
something he *watched* (Scene 10), not asserted, and Junia again marks the limits
of her hearing in Scene 18. B's only faint reach is Junia's psychological reading
of Pike's motive in Scene 19, but it is framed as *her* interpretation, not as
fact. **A = 4, B = 5.**

### 6. Forced choice for W3: **B**
B wins W3 by the widest margin of the three. A's quiet road is where the
giveaway-everything-up-front strategy hurts most: a silent, solitary,
object-only deduction with the answer already known is close to a flat
procedural. B takes the identical branch and makes it the most affecting chapter
in either telling — the drawer Pike cannot destroy, and the explanation of why,
is genuinely moving and is engineered as a clean setup/payoff. This is B at its
best and A at its weakest.

---

## Overall forced choice across the whole comparison: **B**

B wins all three world-lines, and for the same compounding reasons. Version A is
intelligent, never factually self-contradictory, and has a real (if essayistic)
voice — but it makes a fatal craft wager: it hands the reader the complete
solution in its opening scene and then narrates Hale re-deriving it, which spends
its own suspense, flattens its climaxes into foregone melancholy, and pushes the
prose toward annotated explanation rather than dramatized story. Version B keeps a
tighter frame on Hale, carries dialogue and physical staging, and builds a clue
architecture that is measurably more rigorous — the wrist bruise and the
thread-in-the-clockwork give B a forensic spine A lacks, the burn branch loses
something that genuinely matters, and the quiet branch turns a mechanical
question ("where is the telegram") into the most affecting beat in the entire
experiment. On every rubric that distinguishes a *told story* from a list of true
statements — voice-in-scene, tension, setup/payoff, and earned emotional
consequence — B is the stronger telling. My overall forced choice is **B**.
