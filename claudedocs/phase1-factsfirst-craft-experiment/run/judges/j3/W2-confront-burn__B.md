## Scene 1

The road ended in snow a half-mile below the summit, and Lewin Hale walked the rest, valise in one gloved hand and the patron's letter buttoned against his chest. He had ridden such roads before — to burned mills, to flooded stores, to the smoking ruins of warehouses whose owners had insured them a little too lately and a little too well. He had learned to distrust a thing that arranged itself too neatly into an accident.

The Vane Observatory came up out of the white like a thing half-built and half-abandoned: a long low house of grey stone, and beyond it the dome, a pale ribbed shell that caught the last of the afternoon. The dome's shutter was open. A wound in the side of the sky, Hale thought, and could not say why the phrase came to him, except that it was very cold and the slit gaped at nothing.

A man met him at the door before he had knocked. Heavyset, soft-handed, a ledger-keeper's stoop and a smile arranged the way a clerk arranges figures. "Mr. Hale. We had your wire. I am Pike — Onslow Pike, the bursar. A terrible business. Terrible." He said it twice, and Hale noted the doubling, as he noted everything, without yet knowing what it meant.

Inside it was scarcely warmer. The fires were banked low; coal, Pike explained, was husbanded until the thaw. They went down a corridor hung with star-charts gone brown at the edges, and Pike talked — of the snow, of the patron's generosity, of the policy, of how glad they all were that the Society had sent so careful a man.

Hale said little. He was listening for the thing under the words. He did not yet hear it. But he knew, the way a man knows weather, that it was there.

---

## Scene 2

They had laid Dr. Crane in the cold storeroom off the kitchen, on a trestle, under a sheet stiffening in the chill. Pike drew the sheet back with the reluctance of a man uncovering an account he would rather not balance.

The director had been an old man, but not a frail one — Hale could see that even now, in the heavy shoulders, the strong blunt hands. There was a bruise across the temple, dark and wide, and another on the wrist, and the back of the skull, when Hale turned the head with two fingers, gave under his touch in a way that a fall might explain. Might. He had certified deaths that a fall explained, and deaths that a fall only wore like a borrowed coat.

"He fell," Pike said. "From the gallery, or from the stair. We think the stair. We found him at the foot of it, beneath the great glass, at first light."

"Who found him?"

"Miss Frost. The assistant. She came down at dawn from her observing and — there he was." Pike's hands worked at the hem of the sheet. "A dreadful thing for a young woman."

Hale looked at the bruise on the wrist. A man who falls puts out his hands; the bruises come on the heels of the palms, on the forearms. This was on the inner wrist, a band of it, as though something had closed there. He said nothing. He had learned that the surest way to be told a story was to let the silence ask for it.

"You'll want to see the dome," Pike said, too quickly, and drew the sheet back up.

---

## Scene 3

The dome was a single great drum of iron and timber, cold as the inside of a bell. The refractor rose through the middle of it, a long brass barrel pointed up through the open shutter at the white afternoon, and at its base the iron stair spiralled to the observing gallery thirty feet above. Frost had grown on the lower steps. At the foot of the stair the stone floor bore a stain, dark and dried, where the director had lain.

Above, where the eyepiece would be, stood the meridian clock in its tall case. Its pendulum hung dead. Its hands stood at fourteen minutes past three.

"It stopped," Pike said, "when he fell. The shock of it, perhaps."

Hale crossed to the case and opened it. The weights hung low — but not at the bottom. He drew them up a fraction and felt the resistance, the mechanism whole and willing. "A clock like this runs eight days on a winding," he said. "When was it wound?"

Pike hesitated. "Crane wound it himself. The evening before. It was his habit, before the night's work."

"Then it had run some six hours and stopped." Hale let the pendulum swing once, twice. It went on swinging. The clock was not broken. He shut the case. "A clock does not stop because a man falls thirty feet from it, Mr. Pike. A clock stops because someone stops it, or because the work is fouled. This work is not fouled."

Pike's smile had gone somewhere. "I am a man of figures, Mr. Hale, not of clocks."

"So am I," said Hale, "of figures." And he went on looking at the dead hands, at fourteen minutes past three, the hour written into the iron as though the night itself had signed it.

---

## Scene 4

Onslow Pike kept the observatory's accounts in a small iron-bound book in the bursar's room, and he kept his own accounts in his head, and the two had not agreed for a long time. The reader may as well know it plainly, since Hale does not: there had been a fund — the instrument account, money set by against the day the great refractor's lens should need its costly regrinding — and Pike had drained it, a little and a little, over three years, into debts of his own that need not be named here but were real and pressing and his.

In the autumn he had seen the reckoning coming. The patron meant to send an auditor in the spring. Crane, who signed for the account though he never read it, would be ruined alongside Pike, or would ruin Pike to save himself. So Pike had done a clever, fatal thing. He had forged a telegram, under the patron's name, recalling Crane to the city — recalling him, in plain words, to dismissal. He had meant the old man to leave before the spring, to be gone and disgraced and discredited, so that whatever the auditor found could be hung upon a director already cast out.

Crane never knew. The telegram lay in a drawer; Pike had lost his nerve and not yet delivered it. But Pike, who had lived with the lie so long, had come to believe it was already done — had come to believe, on the night Crane died, that Crane knew, that Crane had read his dismissal and his ruin in it, and that the old man on the iron stair at three in the morning was a man with nothing left to lose.

A belief is a kind of weather too. It moves men. Remember that the reader knows this, and Hale, three rooms away with a cold cup of tea, does not.

---

## Scene 5

Junia Frost was younger than Hale had expected, and steadier. She received him in the small library, her hands folded, her plate-camera and her note-books arranged on the table between them like a defence. She wore mourning grey, but her eyes were dry and very clear.

"I was at the spectroscope," she said, before he had asked. "From eleven until dawn. There is a star — a variable in Cygnus — I have been taking its spectrum these three winters. I was at the eyepiece the whole night. I heard nothing. When I came down at first light I found him."

"At the foot of the stair."

"At the foot of the stair."

Hale turned his hat in his hands. "The spectroscope is in the dome, then? On the gallery, near the clock?"

"In the side annexe. Through the door beneath the gallery. One cannot see the stair from there, nor the gallery. The walls are between." She said it smoothly, and it was smoothly true, and it was a lie all the same — for she had not been in the annexe at all. She had been above, on the dark gallery, in the freezing wind under the open shutter, and she had seen the whole of it. The reader knows this. Hale does not, yet.

"You took plates? Of the star?"

"All night." A small pause, no longer than a held breath. "The seeing was poor toward morning."

"I should like to see them," Hale said, "the plates," and watched her hands, and saw them not move at all, which told him more than a tremble would have.

---

## Scene 6

The plate-camera was a fine instrument, mahogany and brass, and its magazine held a dozen glass plates in their dark slides. Hale carried it to the little darkroom himself, Junia at his shoulder saying nothing.

He developed three plates by the red lamp, his sleeves rolled, the trays acrid in the cold air. He had done this once before, in another life, before insurance — he had a steady hand for it. The plates came up out of the developer the way a confession comes up out of a long silence: slowly, and then all at once.

They were blank. Not poorly exposed; not fogged; not faint with bad seeing. Blank — clear glass, the emulsion untouched by any light at all. He developed a fourth, and a fifth, and the last of the magazine. Every plate was unexposed. The camera had not photographed a star, nor anything, that night. It had not been opened to the sky.

Hale set the dripping plates in their rack and looked at them a while in the red light. Junia stood very still by the door.

"You said you took plates all night," he said. "Of your variable in Cygnus."

"The seeing was poor."

"There is no star here, Miss Frost. There is no anything. These plates never saw the dark, let alone the sky." He dried his hands slowly. "A spectrum is light written on glass. You have written nothing. Either your camera failed you for six hours and you did not notice — which I do not believe of a woman who has done this three winters — or you were not at your camera at all."

Her clear eyes did not waver. "The shutter jammed," she said. "I worked, and got nothing, and was ashamed of it, and said I had taken plates because I could not bear to say I had wasted the only clear night in a month." It was quick and it was good, and Hale almost believed it, which was the trouble with Junia Frost: she lied the way an honest woman tells the truth.

---

## Scene 7

That evening Hale sat with the iron-bound ledger Pike had, after some persuasion, laid open before him "for the policy's sake." Pike sat across the lamp, soft hands folded, watching the adjuster's pencil the way a man watches a surgeon's knife.

The instrument account ran down the right-hand pages. Hale read figures for a living, and these figures had been kept by a man who knew that someday another man would read them. They were neat. They were complete. They balanced. And they were, Hale grew slowly certain, false — false in the way that only careful figures can be false, each entry true and the sum of them a lie. Money had gone out under headings that meant nothing: *sundries, carriage, regrinding fund — provisional.* The provisional regrinding fund had been provided for, and provided for, and the provision was gone.

He did not say so. He turned back a page, and forward two, and let Pike sweat in the lamplight. The reader, who knows the fund is drained and the telegram forged and the old man's death some product of that lie, watches Hale circle the truth with his pencil and not yet land on it.

"A handsome account," Hale said at last, mildly. "The Society funds you well."

"The patron is generous."

"And the regrinding fund — provisional. Provisional against what?"

"Against the lens. Should it ever need — these instruments age, Mr. Hale." Pike's voice was steady; only the doubling had crept back. "Should it ever need, ever need regrinding."

Hale closed the ledger on his finger, keeping the page. He could press now — lay the figures bare, watch the bursar break or brazen it. Or he could hold his finger in the book and say nothing, and audit on, and let Pike believe himself uncaught a day longer, and see what an unfrightened guilty man does. He weighed the two, there in the lamplight, the way he had weighed everything since the snow.

---

## Scene 8

What decided him, in the end, was the smell of the room: lamp-oil and cold coal and, under it, fear — old fear, lived-in fear, the fear of a man who had been afraid so long he had stopped knowing it. Hale had smelled it in others. It changed how a man could be handled. A new fear you could spring like a trap. An old fear had grown a shell, and a trap sprung on a shell only warned the creature inside.

He took his finger from the ledger. Outside, the wind had got up, and the open shutter of the dome was singing somewhere above the house, one low note, on and on, as though the observatory were trying to remember the hour at which it had stopped.

Three secrets sat in the room with them, and Hale knew none of them. He did not know that the figures before him had killed a man as surely as the iron stair had. He did not know that the night-log for the missed observation was written in a hand that was not Crane's — he had not yet found it. He did not know that a spare dome key, reported lost a month past, lay even now where it should not lie. He did not know that somewhere in this cold house was a letter, never sent, in which Aurel Crane resigned his own post in his own hand, for reasons all his own.

He knew only that he had a guilty man across the lamp, and a lying woman down the hall, and a clock that had been stopped by a human hand at fourteen minutes past three.

"Mr. Pike," he said, and stopped, and chose.

## Scene 9

"Mr. Pike," Hale said, and opened the ledger flat, and turned it so the lamplight fell on the right-hand pages and the bursar could not look away from his own figures. "Tell me what regrinding the lens of a sixteen-inch refractor costs. To the pound. You will know, because you have provided for it. Provided for it for three years."

Pike's soft hands came apart and lay flat on the table, as though to steady it. "I — these are technical matters —"

"Eight hundred and forty pounds has left this account under that heading. The lens has not been touched; I have seen it tonight, whole and clean in its cell. So I will ask you plainly, since the snow has shut the door and there is no one here but us and a dead man in the cold room. Where is the money?"

The fear came up in Pike's face all at once, the old fear cracking its shell, and for a moment Hale thought he had broken him. But a man who has been afraid for three years has also rehearsed for three years. Pike wet his lips. "There were debts of the Society's. Old debts. I paid them as they came, out of what fund could bear it, meaning to make it good before the audit. It is a — an irregularity. Not a theft. The director knew. Crane knew, and approved it, and now he is dead and cannot say so." He spread his hands. "You will not find his hand against it, because such things are not written down. But he knew."

It was a lie laid over a truth laid over a worse truth, and Hale heard the layers in it. He had cornered the man, and the man had not confessed; he had thrown a dead director across the gap to bridge it. *Crane knew.* Hale filed the words away. He did not yet understand that he had just been handed, sideways, the shape of the whole thing — that Pike's whole defence was that Crane had known, when the reader knows that what Pike truly believed Crane knew was something else entirely, and worse.

---

## Scene 10

Hale pressed, and pressing, found the edge of it. "If Crane approved the drawing-down, why are you frightened, Mr. Pike? An approved irregularity is the patron's quarrel with his director, not yours. Yet you sweat. You have sweated since I came up the road. A man does not sweat over another man's irregularity."

Pike said nothing. The wind sang in the shutter overhead.

"I think," Hale went on, slowly, building it as he spoke, "that the money is yours and the debts are yours, and Crane did not know, and you were going to be found out in the spring. That is my working theory. I do not yet know what it has to do with a clock stopped at three. But a man does not generally die at the foot of a stair on the very winter that his bursar is about to be ruined." He let that sit. "Did he know? In the end — did Crane know what you had done?"

And here Pike made his single great mistake, the mistake of a guilty man asked the wrong question and answering the one in his own head. "He knew," Pike whispered. "He knew everything. The recall came, and he read it, and he knew he was finished, and that I had —" He stopped. His mouth worked.

Hale went very still. "What recall?"

"Nothing. A figure of speech." But the bursar's eyes had gone to the corridor, toward the director's study, toward a drawer; and Hale, who missed very little, marked the direction of a frightened man's glance and said no more that night. The reader knows there is a forged telegram in that drawer. Hale knows only that Pike looked toward something, and lied, and that the word *recall* had come out of him like a tooth.

---

## Scene 11

Hale did not sleep. He had a cell of a room, a candle, and a habit of walking a house while it slept. Toward two he went up to the dome alone.

It was a place of knives in the dark — the cold cut, the wind through the open slit cut, the brass barrel of the refractor a black column going up into a colder black. He found the observing gallery by feel and the meridian clock by the pale gleam of its dead face, three-fourteen, three-fourteen. On the small desk bolted to the gallery rail lay the night-log, the great ruled book in which every observation of the station was entered, ruled in years of Crane's small upright hand.

He brought the candle close. The last entry was dated the night of the death. And it was not in Crane's hand.

The earlier pages were all the director's — small, upright, the *t*'s crossed flat, the figures square. The final entry sprawled: a looser, larger hand, hurried, the ink unevenly pressed. It recorded an observation of a star in Cygnus — *the variable, spectra, eleven o'clock until* — and there it broke off, mid-word, mid-line, as though the writer had been interrupted, or had lost the nerve to go on, or had only ever meant it to be found.

Hale knew two hands in this house now. Crane's, square and dead. And this one. He set the candle on Junia Frost's note-books, open on the library table three rooms below in his memory, and saw the looser, larger hand that had written *the seeing was poor toward morning* in her own books. The same hand. Junia had written the night-log — had written Crane's observation, in her hand, for a night on which her own camera held no exposures at all. She had manufactured a record of work that the blank plates proved had never been done. Hale closed the log. He had her in two lies now, and did not yet know which lie was the one that mattered.

---

## Scene 12

Morning came grey and grudging. Hale found the spare dome key by accident, which is how he found most true things — the arranged things announced themselves, and the true things had to be tripped over.

He had asked Pike, the evening before, who held keys to the dome. "Two only," Pike had said. "Crane's, on his person. And the spare, on the board in the passage — though that one was lost, a month back, and never found. A nuisance; we have meant to have another cut." So there should have been one key in the house and one on a dead man.

But that morning, sweeping his candle along the base of the meridian clock's case — looking for he knew not what, a scratch, a scuff, the mark of the human hand that had stilled the pendulum — Hale's fingers found a key fallen down behind the case, against the cold iron of the gallery floor. He drew it out. It was the dome key; he tried it later in the lock and it turned. The spare, lost a month before, found now behind the very clock that a hand had stopped at three-fourteen.

Someone had carried the lost key up to this gallery. Someone had been here, by the clock, with a means of locking the dome that left no trace at the board below. Hale stood with the cold key in his palm and felt the case of the thing close another inch. A drained fund. A forged something in a drawer. A clock stopped by hand. A night-log in the assistant's hand for work she never did. And now a key that had been lost and was not. He did not yet have the picture. But he had, he thought, all its corners.

---

## Scene 13

He took the key to Junia Frost.

She was in the library again, the grey light flat on her clear face, the plate-camera before her like a thing she was guarding rather than using. Hale set the spare dome key on the table between them, in the place where, on the first day, her hands had not trembled.

"This was lost a month ago," he said. "I found it behind the meridian clock, on the gallery, this morning. The gallery where you say you were not — you say you were in the annexe, at the spectroscope, behind walls, all night. But your camera took no plate. The night-log for that night is in your hand, not Crane's. And the key that locks the dome was on the gallery, where a hand reached up and stopped the clock at three-fourteen." He kept his voice level, the voice he used for the last questions. "You were on the gallery, Miss Frost. You saw the director go up the stair, or come down it. You saw whatever happened at the clock. And you have lied to me in three ways to keep me from it."

For the first time her composure moved — not broke, but moved, the way deep water moves when something large turns over far beneath. "If I were on the gallery," she said, very low, "and if I saw — what would you do with it, Mr. Hale? You came to certify an accident or something else. Which would you rather it were?"

"I came to certify the truth."

"No man comes for that." But she was looking at the night-log under his arm, and at the key on the table, and Hale could see her weighing two roads as plainly as he had weighed his own in the lamplight two nights gone. She held the truth — she had seen who climbed the iron stair. And she held, still, the means to bury it. The reader knows she means to shield someone. Hale, watching her decide, does not yet know whom.

---

## Scene 14

Before she answered, Hale played the last card he did not yet know was a card.

"Crane's study," he said. "I will see it now. With or without your leave, Miss Frost, the snow having made me the nearest thing to law on this hill." And she, perhaps glad of the reprieve, perhaps calculating that he would find there what would turn him from her, rose and took him to it.

The director's study was a cold scholarly cell, books to the beam, a globe, a desk. Hale went through it as he went through everything, without haste and without mercy. In the shallow drawer where Pike's frightened glance had pointed two nights before, he found a telegram — but he set it aside half-read, for under it, in Crane's own square upright hand, lay a folded sheet that stopped him cold.

It was a letter of resignation. *To the Patron and the Society* — and below, in the dead director's hand, Aurel Crane resigned his post at the Vane Observatory, citing his age, his failing eyes, his wish to end his days in the south where the cold did not reach his bones. It was dated three days before his death. It was complete. It was signed. And it had never been sent.

Hale stood holding it in the grey light. Here was a man who, three days before he died at the foot of a stair, had quietly, of his own hand and his own reasons, given up the very post that — the telegram beneath, he now saw, glancing down — a wire under the patron's name was even then *recalling him from*. He did not yet understand what he held. He understood only that the dead director had wanted to leave, freely, before anyone made him; and that someone had not known it; and that a great deal of fear and forgery had been spent to push a man toward a door he had already opened himself.

---

## Scene 15

He came back to Junia Frost with the letter in one hand and the half-read telegram in the other, and laid them beside the key.

"Here is what I think happened," he said, and the room was very quiet. "Crane meant to resign — had written it, signed it, three days before. He wanted out, on his own terms, gently. He never sent it. Meanwhile someone forged a recall under the patron's name to drive him out in disgrace, for reasons that have to do with that ledger and a drained fund. The night he died, something brought him up the iron stair at three in the morning. The clock was stopped by hand at three-fourteen. The dome was locked with a key that had been hidden a month. And you, Miss Frost, were on the gallery, and saw it, and have built me a false night out of an empty camera and a borrowed hand to keep me from one fact." He set the letter down. "I do not think you killed him. I think you are shielding someone who was there. I am close enough now that I will have it within the day, with you or without. So I give you the choice that is left to you."

Her clear eyes had filled, at last, looking at the dead director's unsent letter — at the proof that the old man had wanted to go quietly, that the whole machinery of forgery and fear had been spent to force a door already unlocked. Whatever she had told herself to justify her silence, the letter had taken some prop from under it.

"You don't know what you'd be certifying," she whispered. "You think the truth is a clean thing. It is not. If I tell you who was on that stair, a man's whole life — and a woman's — go into your report, and out into the world, and nothing I saw will mean what it meant on the gallery in the dark." She put her hand flat on the night-log. "It would be kinder to burn it. All of it. Let the policy pay for an accident and let the dead rest."

---

## Scene 16

Hale did not answer at once. He looked at the four things on the table between them — the spare key, the unsent resignation, the forged recall, the night-log in her hand — and at the woman who held the last fact of all behind her clear and grieving eyes.

"You found him," he said. "At the foot of the stair, at dawn. That was true; the only true thing you have told me. You were on the gallery the whole night. You saw him climb. You saw who climbed after him, or who waited above. You have spent two days and three lies to keep me from one name." He leaned in. "I have given you every chance to let me reach it gently. Now I ask you straight, with the dead man's own letter on the table and the snow at the door: who was on the iron stair with Aurel Crane at fourteen minutes past three?"

The candle she had lit against the grey guttered in the draught from the eternal open shutter. Her hand was flat on the night-log. The hearth beside her held a small live fire.

She had come to the edge of it. She could give him the truth she had carried alone on the freezing gallery — speak the name, and let it fall where it would. Or she could end it the other way: feed the night-log to the fire, burn the false record and the looser hand and the last thread that tied her to that night, and swear there had never been anything to see.

The reader knows what Hale does not: that she shields someone real, that there is a name, that S2 is true and waiting. Junia's hand tightened on the book.

## Scene 17

She did not argue, and she did not weep. That was the terrible economy of it. Junia Frost looked at Lewin Hale across the table for a long moment — at the spare key, the forged recall, the unsent resignation, the false night-log in her own hand — and then she picked up the night-log, the great ruled book of three winters and one lie, and she leaned, and she fed it to the fire.

Hale could have stopped her. He was the nearer of the two to the grate, and quicker, and the snow had made him the nearest thing to law on the hill. He did not move. He had spent two days teaching himself to read this woman, and what he read now was that the fact behind her eyes was hers, had been bought at a price he had not paid, on a freezing gallery in the dark while he was warm in a city he could not remember; and that he could take the *book* from the fire but he could not take the *name* from her, not by force, not by law, not by the cleverest question he owned. The name lived in memory alone. The book was only its shadow.

The ruled pages caught and curled. The looser, larger hand — *the variable, spectra, eleven o'clock until* — browned and lifted and was gone. The square upright entries of three years of a dead man's nights went with it, and Hale watched them go with a grief that surprised him, for the log was the last writing of Aurel Crane in the world, and he had let it burn to learn that he could not have what he came for.

"There," Junia said, when it was ash. "Now there is nothing on the gallery to see. There never was. I was at the spectroscope, the shutter jammed, the plates failed, and I found him at the foot of the stair at dawn." She folded her hands. "Certify what the iron and the glass will tell you, Mr. Hale. They will not tell you a name. I have made sure of that."

---

## Scene 18

Hale did not sleep that night either, but it was a different sleeplessness — not the hunt's, but the surveyor's, taking the measure of a thing he now knew he would never wholly have.

He laid it out on the cell table one last time, the mute witnesses, the things that did not depend on Junia Frost's tongue. The instrument account, drained eight hundred and forty pounds under a regrinding fund that had reground nothing: that was real, and ink, and Pike's. The forged telegram of recall under the patron's name: real, and in a drawer, and the patron, wired in the spring, would disown it. The clock stopped by a human hand at three-fourteen, the thread of cloth caught in its works: real. The lost dome key found behind that clock: real. Crane's own unsent resignation, three days before his death: real, and square, and signed. The blank plates that broke the assistant's alibi to pieces: real.

And against all that, one hole exactly the size of a name. He knew a fund had been drained and a fraud was about to be found out. He knew a wire had been forged to drive the director out before the spring. He knew a hand had stopped the clock and a hidden key had locked the dome and the assistant had built a false night to bury something she had seen. He could draw the whole figure but the centre of it, the single human act on the upper rail, the *who* and the *how* — that, she had burned. The reader, who holds both secrets entire, sees the shape Hale cannot fill: that it was Pike on the stair, over a thing Crane never knew; that it was a panic and not a murder; that Junia burned a book to keep a broken man out of the dock. Hale sees only the hole, and that it is precisely the shape of a withheld truth.

A lesser adjuster, he thought, would write *accident* and go down the mountain. A dishonest one would write *murder* and name the likeliest man and let the courts sort it. He was neither, and the snow gave him until morning to decide which kind of honesty he could afford.

---

## Scene 19

In the end Hale wrote the only report a careful man could write with the night-log in ashes and the name unspoken, and it satisfied no one, which was how he knew it was true.

He did not certify *accident.* He had told Junia Frost he came to certify the truth, and the truth was not an accident: a clock does not stop itself by a human hand, a key does not lose itself for a month and find its way behind that clock, an account does not drain itself, a telegram does not forge itself. He set every one of those down — the drained instrument fund, the forged recall, the stopped clock and the thread in its works, the recovered spare key, the false night-log (destroyed, he wrote, in his presence, by the witness who had written it), the blank plates that gave that witness the lie. He set down, too, Crane's unsent resignation and the irony he could prove without a name: that a wire had been forged to drive out a man who had three days earlier resigned his own post and left the letter unsent — the whole apparatus of fraud aimed at a door the dead man had already, quietly, opened.

But he would not write the name he could not prove. He certified the death *not an accident within the meaning of the instrument policy* — *something else,* arising out of fraud and forgery upon the accounts — *cause of death by human agency, agent unestablished.* The policy would not pay; he would not let it pay for an accident the iron and the glass told him was none. And he would not hang a name on a hole. He recommended the patron set an auditor on the bursar's books in the spring, where the figures, at least, could not burn.

Going down the mountain, Hale thought of the woman on the freezing gallery with a name she would carry to her grave, of the bursar safe for a winter behind her silence, and of the dead director who had wanted only to go gently south and had died baffled at the foot of his own stair. *Something else, agent unestablished.* It was the most honest sentence he had ever signed, and the least use to anybody, and he carried it down into the valley like a stone.
