# Judge 2 — Verdict

Three matched world-lines of the same branching mystery ("The Meridian Vane" /
the Vane Observatory), each in an A and a B version. I read both linear
versions of each world-line, then scored. All scores are 1–5 (5 best).

A general note up front, because it bears on every world-line: the two versions
are written from opposite narrative postures. The **A** versions are told in a
detached, omniscient, almost essayistic register — most scenes pair Hale's
mistaken reading of an object with the narrator stepping in to tell us the true
reading ("He was looking at the right object and reading it the wrong way
round"). The **B** versions are told scene-by-scene, in close third on Hale,
with dialogue, physical staging, and dramatic irony withheld for effect. This
posture difference is the single biggest driver of the prose, stakes, and
believability scores below, so I note it once here rather than repeating it.

---

## World-line 1 — confront / reveal (`W1-confront-reveal`)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A:** I found **no hard continuity or causality errors.** The chain is
internally consistent: forged telegram never delivered (S1, S11), clock stopped
by the fall via the shared north pier (S5, S15), blank plate breaks Junia's
alibi (S4, S14), night-log is dictation not forgery (S3, S16–17), spare key is
Pike's means of unseen entry (S7, S18). The reveal in S17 (Junia saw Pike on the
stair; Crane startled and fell; not murder) is consistent with every planted
object. One **minor friction**, not an error: Scene 2 says the patron's man came
"two days later" and the patron's-man framing is omniscient, while later scenes
imply Hale spent "a fortnight" on the summit (S12, S13) — the elapsed-time
language is loose but not contradictory (two days to arrival, a fortnight of
investigation).

**Version B:** I found **one genuine causality wrinkle and one staging tension.**
(a) In B's reveal (S17–S18), the clock is stopped *by Pike's own gripping hand*
catching the works as he is pulled over the rail, and "a thread of his sleeve in
the works" is offered as proof. But in S3 Hale has already reasoned, correctly
for B's world, "A clock does not stop because a man falls thirty feet from it...
A clock stops because someone stops it" — and B's mechanism (a hand on the
works) is consistent with that. So B is internally consistent on the clock; the
A-world "shared pier vibration" mechanism is simply a different solution. Not an
error. (b) The **wrist bruise**: B plants it hard in S2 ("a band of it, as
though something had closed there") and pays it off in S18 (Pike gripping the
wrist). Good setup/payoff, no error. (c) The one I will count: in S11 Hale finds
the night-log "on the small desk bolted to the gallery rail," but in S5/S6 Junia
says the spectroscope is "in the side annexe... One cannot see the stair from
there." The night-log being on the *gallery* desk (not the annexe) is consistent
with Junia having been on the gallery — fine — but it means the forged-by-Junia
log sat in plain view on the gallery for a fortnight, which slightly undercuts
her otherwise careful concealment. Soft, arguably intentional. **Net: A = 0
hard errors; B = 0 hard errors, 1–2 soft frictions.** Both are clean.

### 2. Prose quality (told story vs assembled facts)

**A — 3/5.** A is fluent and often striking at the sentence level ("a thumb that
should not have been there," "the truest document in the building... told in the
wrong voice"). But its method is *expository*: nearly every scene is structured
as "here is the object → here is how Hale misread it → here is the truth the
narrator now hands you." It tells the solution alongside the mystery. This reads
less as a dramatized story than as a beautifully written *annotated solution*. It
has a voice, but the voice is the narrator's lecture, not the world's.

**B — 5/5.** B dramatizes. It opens with Hale walking up through snow, gives Pike
a body and a verbal tic ("Terrible. Terrible"), stages the clock argument as
live dialogue (S3), and earns its dramatic irony through scene-level withholding
rather than narrator asides. The reader-address ("The reader knows this. Hale
does not, yet") is a deliberate device, used sparingly. It reads as a told story
with a controlling voice.

### 3. Stakes and tension

**A — 3/5.** Because A keeps explaining the true reading immediately after each
false one, the engine of suspense (does Hale figure it out?) is partly spent in
advance; we mostly watch a foregone conclusion arrive on schedule. Stakes are
intellectual, not felt.

**B — 4/5.** B holds tension better: the wrist bruise, the doubling tic, the
"What recall?" hard stop (S10), the confrontation that builds across S9–S10. The
climactic reveal (S17–S19) is an actual *scene* with two old men struggling on a
frosted rail. Real tension.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

**A — 4/5.** Every planted object is paid off in order and the "murder is a
phantom" inversion lands cleanly. The undelivered-telegram irony (S12, S19) is
genuinely satisfying. Slightly undercut because A often pays things off *as it
plants them* (telling us the truth at planting time), which softens the
"aha."

**B — 5/5.** B plants quietly (wrist bruise, the *recall* "like a tooth," the
glance at the drawer) and pays off late and hard. The unsent resignation under
the forged telegram in the same drawer (S14, S20) is the strongest single
payoff in the whole comparison.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

**A — 4/5.** Mostly clean. Hale acts only on what he has found; the narrator's
omniscience is framed as narrator, not character, knowledge. One small strain:
in S9–S10 Pike confesses "I sent him to his death" with eyewitness certainty
about Crane *reading* the wire — but the text explicitly flags this as Pike
reporting an assumption, so it is handled, not a slip.

**B — 5/5.** B is careful about epistemics: it repeatedly marks what Hale does
*not* yet know, Pike's confession is grounded in his *belief* not knowledge, and
Junia's account in S17–S18 is hedged ("I could not hear all of it," "I cannot
say," "I'd wager") exactly where a real witness on a dark gallery would hedge.
Very believable.

### 6. Forced choice for W1

**Choice: B.** Both reach the same clean solution with no hard errors, but A
reads as an elegantly annotated answer key while B reads as a story. B
dramatizes the confrontation, earns its irony through withholding rather than
narration, plants and pays off the wrist bruise and the same-drawer letters with
real craft, and handles witness epistemics with care. A's relentless
"object-then-correction" structure spends its own suspense. B is the stronger
told story.

## World-line 2 — confront / burn (`W2-confront-burn`)

This is the variant where Junia *burns the night-log* rather than reveal the
name. A's S1–S16 are identical to W1-A; B's S1–S16 are identical to W1-B. The
divergence is entirely in the ending (A: S17–S18; B: S17–S19).

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A:** **No hard errors.** The burn ending is consistent: with the log
gone, Hale cannot prove the second hand innocent, cannot name the climber, and
so must let the policy pay as misadventure (S18) while still certifying the
fraud. The logic that "the burned page was the keystone that let the objects
testify in chorus" (S17) is sound within A's world. One **causality concern**:
A's S18 concludes Hale "could not certify the death... a death he could neither
acquit nor convict... the policy must be allowed to pay as misadventure." This
sits in tension with A's *own* earlier S5/S15 finding that the clock recorded
the fall innocently and S7/S18-of-W1 that the key implies unlawful intrusion —
in the burn-A world Hale arguably still has the key and the fraud, which B's
ending (below) actually uses to refuse the payout. So A's "policy pays clean"
conclusion is defensible but slightly *under-reasoned* given the evidence A
itself says survives the fire (the key, the fraud). Not a contradiction, but the
weakest causal link in either W2 ending. I count it as **1 soft causal
inconsistency in A.**

**Version B:** **No hard errors.** B's burn ending is more rigorously reasoned:
S18 enumerates exactly what survives the fire (drained account, forged telegram,
clock-with-thread, recovered key, unsent resignation, blank plates) and S19 has
Hale certify "*something else... cause of death by human agency, agent
unestablished*" and refuse the payout *because* the surviving physical evidence
proves it was not an accident. This is the more logically complete handling of
the same burn. **Net: A = 1 soft causal under-reasoning; B = 0.**

### 2. Prose quality

**A — 3/5.** Same expository register as W1-A. The burn scene (S17) is well
written ("there was nothing in his trade or his commission that gave him the
right to lay hands on a woman to save a book") but again narrates its own
meaning rather than dramatizing it.

**B — 5/5.** B's burn (S17) is the strongest scene in either W2 version: "She
did not argue, and she did not weep. That was the terrible economy of it." The
realization that Hale "could take the *book* from the fire but he could not take
the *name* from her" (S17) is dramatized through his choice not to move, not
explained at us. The closing image — "the most honest sentence he had ever
signed, and the least use to anybody, and he carried it down into the valley
like a stone" (S19) — is genuinely good prose.

### 3. Stakes and tension

**A — 3/5.** The burn is a strong dramatic beat, but A's habit of immediately
explaining its significance ("He understood, watching it burn, exactly what he
was losing") releases the tension as fast as it builds.

**B — 4/5.** B lets the burn *happen* before glossing it, and the stakes of the
choice (the policy will pay, Pike walks, the truth dies in the grate) are felt
through Hale's helpless stillness rather than told.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

**A — 3/5.** The burn pays off the "she shields someone" setup, but A's ending
leaves the case oddly *resolved-against-itself* (policy pays despite proven
fraud), which slightly cheats the payoff of all those carefully planted objects.

**B — 5/5.** B's S18–S19 explicitly cashes every surviving plant and turns the
burn into a *partial defeat that is still a refusal to lie* — the keystone is
gone, but the surviving objects still deny the payout. The "agent unestablished"
verdict is the most satisfying possible payoff of a burned-keystone ending.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

**A — 4/5.** Clean; Hale reasons only from what survives. The mild strain is the
under-reasoned payout conclusion noted above (he seems to *forget* evidence he
holds).

**B — 5/5.** Hale acts strictly on surviving physical evidence and explicitly
refuses to name what he cannot prove. Exemplary.

### 6. Forced choice for W2

**Choice: B.** Same divergence point, but B's burn ending is both better
dramatized (the "terrible economy," the choice not to lunge for the book) and
more rigorously reasoned (it enumerates surviving evidence and refuses the
payout on it, where A lets the policy pay clean in a way that under-uses the
evidence A itself says survives). B turns a downbeat ending into a coherent moral
and evidentiary stance; A narrates a loss. B wins clearly.

## World-line 3 — quiet (`W3-quiet`)

This is the "audit limb": Hale chooses *not* to confront Pike (S9 onward) and
solves the case by silent reconstruction and watching. A solves it entirely from
objects, never confronting anyone (ends S16). B's quiet road still arrives at the
*same reveal scene* as the confront-line (Junia names Pike, S17–S18) plus an
added Pike-grief thread, ending S20.

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**Version A:** I found **one notable causality strain.** A-quiet's whole premise
is that Hale solves it *from objects alone, never confronting anyone* (S16: "not
one soul on the summit had confessed a word of it to him"). But to do this A has
Hale deduce the dictation/kindness explanation of the night-log purely from
"kitchen talk he had let run on around him" that "Crane's fingers had stiffened
past fine writing" (S14). That overheard fact is doing very heavy lifting — it
is the single load-bearing input that converts the "forged log = murder" reading
into "kindness = innocent," and it arrives conveniently as ambient gossip. It is
not a contradiction, but it is a **causal convenience** that the confront-lines
earn from Junia's own mouth. Otherwise the chain is consistent. **Count: 1 soft
causal convenience.**

**Version B:** **No hard errors**, and B's quiet road is unusually well wired:
it threads in Pike's midnight *visiting* of the drawer (S9–S10) — not destroying
the telegram but keeping it — and pays that off in S19 as the emotional core
("it is the only place the old man still is"). One thing to flag, not an error: B
*reuses the confront-line reveal scenes (S17–S18) verbatim* on the quiet road,
which means Junia still narrates the struggle in the same words. That is
consistent (she saw the same thing), and B then *adds* the Pike-grief reading on
top, so it deepens rather than contradicts. **Net: A = 1 soft convenience; B =
0.**

### 2. Prose quality

**A — 4/5.** A-quiet is, interestingly, the strongest of the three A versions as
prose, because the "watch the bursar break cover" material (S9–S11) is more
*dramatized* than A's usual expository mode — Hale at a hide, Pike moving in the
dark with a hooded lantern, the key catching the lantern-light. It still narrates
its meaning, but it has more scene in it. Up a point from W1-A/W2-A.

**B — 5/5.** B-quiet adds genuinely new dramatic material (S9–S10: Pike visiting
the drawer and pressing his hand on it "as a man presses on a wound") and a
new thematic depth in S19 (Pike keeps the telegram because letting it go would
mean the death "was for nothing"). This is the most emotionally developed
version in the entire comparison.

### 3. Stakes and tension

**A — 4/5.** The "watcher at a hide" structure (S9–S11) gives A-quiet real
forward tension — will Pike break cover, what is in the coat — that A's confront
versions lack. Best of the three A's on tension.

**B — 4/5.** B-quiet trades some confrontation-tension for a slower, sadder
register, but the drawer-vigil (S10) and the final Pike-breaking scene (S20)
carry real weight. Roughly even with A here, both strong.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

**A — 4/5.** A-quiet pays off the key, the clock, the plate, and the telegram
from objects alone, which is a satisfying "cold method" payoff consistent with
its thesis. Slightly undercut by the convenient kitchen-talk input noted above.

**B — 5/5.** B-quiet's standout payoff is the *recontextualized telegram*: Hale
watches Pike keep-not-burn the wire in S10, names it to himself as "larger, and
older, and with a death in it," and pays it off in S19 when Junia explains *why*
he keeps it. That is a setup planted in new B-only material and paid off with the
best thematic line in the comparison.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

**A — 3/5.** This is A-quiet's weakest axis and the only place across all six
files where I think a character acts close to the edge of what they can know.
A-quiet has Hale reconstruct the *entire* fall — "a blind old man, startled on
the stair, had fallen; no deliberate hand had done it" (S15) — *from objects
alone, never having heard Junia's account.* The objects (key, clock, plate,
telegram) genuinely establish unlawful intrusion and that Crane didn't know of
the recall; but they do **not** establish that the fall was an accidental startle
rather than a deliberate push. A-quiet asserts the benign reading as proven when
the surviving evidence underdetermines it — Hale is, in effect, certifying a
*mental state* (no deliberate hand) he has no witness for. The confront-lines
earn this from Junia's eyewitness account; A-quiet does not, yet states it with
equal confidence. I mark this down.

**B — 5/5.** B-quiet *does* get the fall from Junia's eyewitness account
(S17–S18), so its "not a deliberate hand" conclusion is properly grounded, and
Hale's deductions about Pike's grief are explicitly tied to what he *watched*
(the drawer vigil). Believable throughout.

### 6. Forced choice for W3

**Choice: B.** A-quiet is the best of the three A versions and has real merits
(the hide/watcher tension, the cold-method thesis). But it overreaches on
character-knowledge: it certifies the *manner* of the fall (accidental startle,
no deliberate hand) from physical objects that cannot establish it, having never
heard a witness — the one place in the whole set where the solver claims more
than the evidence licenses. B-quiet earns the same conclusion through Junia's
testimony, and on top of that contributes the richest new material in the
comparison (Pike keeping the telegram because it proves the death "had a
reason"). B is both better grounded and more moving.

---

## Overall forced choice

**OVERALL choice: B.**

Across all three world-lines the verdict is the same, and consistently. The B
versions are written as told stories — close third on Hale, live dialogue,
physical staging, dramatic irony achieved by scene-level withholding — and they
plant and pay off their best devices (the wrist bruise, the *recall* spoken
"like a tooth," the two letters in one drawer, the kept-not-burned telegram) with
real craft. The A versions are fluent and frequently beautiful at the sentence
level, but they operate as elegantly *annotated solutions*: nearly every scene
pairs the clue with the narrator immediately supplying its true reading, which
spends the mystery's own suspense and reads as exposition rather than drama.
Beyond style, B was also the more rigorous on two endings that matter — the
burn-ending (W2) where B refuses the payout on surviving evidence while A lets
the policy pay clean, and the quiet-line (W3) where B grounds the manner-of-death
in a witness while A asserts an unprovable mental state ("no deliberate hand")
from objects alone. A reads like the key at the back of the book; B reads like
the book. B wins all three, and overall.
