# Judge 1 — Blind Verdict

Three matched world-lines of the same branching mystery ("The Meridian Vane"),
each in two versions (A and B). Judged one world-line at a time: read both linear
versions, then scored continuity, prose, stakes, payoff, and character-knowledge
believability, with a forced A/B choice per world-line and one overall.

A general note up front, true of all three pairs: the two versions tell the same
story by visibly different methods. The **A** versions open from inside the
guilty parties' heads (Pike in Scene 1, Junia in Scene 2) and run on an omniscient
narrator who repeatedly addresses what Hale "did not know" / "could not have
known," reading each clue twice — once as Hale misreads it, once as the narrator
corrects it. The **B** versions open with Hale arriving at the observatory and
keep much closer to his point of view, dramatizing discoveries in scene with
dialogue, while a lighter omniscient hand still occasionally tells the reader
what Hale does not know ("The reader knows this. Hale does not, yet."). This
distinction matters most for prose-voice and stakes scoring.

---

## World-line 1 — confront / reveal

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

I found **no hard continuity or causality contradictions** within either version.
Both are internally consistent: the drained account, the forged-but-undelivered
telegram, the unsent resignation, the spare key, the stopped clock, the blank
plate, and Junia's gallery vantage all interlock the same way and resolve to the
same truth (Pike's intrusion startles Crane into a fatal fall; no deliberate
murder).

Two softer notes, neither a true error:

- **The clock mechanism diverges between A and B, and the divergence is a genuine
  causal claim, not a stylistic one.** A (Scene 5, Scene 15) is emphatic and
  repeated: nothing touched the clock; the *fall itself* rang the shared north
  pier and stopped the brittle pendulum "to the minute." B instead has Junia
  testify (Scene 18) that *Pike's own hand* caught the clockwork as he was pulled
  half over the rail, leaving "a thread of his sleeve in the works." These are
  incompatible accounts of the same physical fact. Within each version it is
  consistent, so I do not count it as an internal error — but B's version is the
  more mechanically satisfying because it also explains the bruise-band on Crane's
  wrist (planted in B Scene 2) and the thread in the works (B Scene 18); A's
  "sympathetic vibration stopped a clock thirty feet of pier away" is the
  cleverer idea but the less physically convincing one, and A never plants a
  wrist-bruise to pay off.

- **B Scene 17 has a small internal stumble.** Junia says "Crane was already above
  me ... he must have gone up before I did," yet she also says she was on the
  gallery from eleven. For Crane to be above her she must mean the *upper* rail
  vs. her gallery position; it reads slightly muddled but is reconcilable
  (two-level gallery). Not counted as an error.

So: **A = 0 internal errors; B = 0 internal errors** (1 cosmetic ambiguity in B
S17).

### 2. Prose quality (told story vs. assembled facts)

- **A: 3/5.** Competent and often elegant at the sentence level, but the
  twice-told structure ("He did not know X. In fact X was..."; "He was looking at
  the right object and reading it the wrong way round") becomes a mechanical
  refrain. The narrator keeps *explaining the clue ledger* to the reader rather
  than letting scenes happen. It reads as a very well-written **annotation of a
  fact-set** more than a told story — every scene is a clue being correctly filed.
- **B: 4/5.** B has a stronger narrating *voice* and dramatizes: the wrist-bruise
  observed and silently noted (S2), the clock tested with a swinging pendulum and
  Pike's "I am a man of figures, not of clocks" (S3), the developing plates "the
  way a confession comes up out of a long silence" (S6). Junia's testimony in
  S17–S18 is genuinely staged, with overheard dialogue ("Read what, man? Known
  what?"). It reads as a story being *told*, not a fact-base being projected.

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 3/5.** The omniscient frame leaks tension: the reader is told the truth in
  Scene 1, and the narrator keeps confirming Hale is wrong, so suspense becomes
  dramatic irony only. Pike's false confession (S9–S12) is the strongest stretch.
- **B: 4/5.** Closer POV plus withheld physical detail (the wrist-band, the
  thread) keeps a live question alive longer; the confrontation with Junia builds.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 4/5.** Every clue is set up and paid off with near-diagrammatic
  thoroughness — almost *too* thoroughly; the payoffs are narrated rather than
  felt, but nothing dangles.
- **B: 4/5.** The wrist-bruise (S2) → wrist-grab (S18) and the thread-in-works
  (S18) are excellent concrete payoffs A lacks; B's clock-stop is *caused by a
  character act*, which pays off harder. One small softness: B leans on Pike's
  blurted "recall" (S10) doing a lot of the bridging work.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 4/5.** Carefully guarded — the narrator is explicit that Pike "reports with
  the conviction of an eyewitness a thing he had only assumed," which is the
  point, not an error; Junia acts only on what she saw from the gallery.
- **B: 4/5.** Equally careful; Hale acts only on what he has observed, Junia on
  what she witnessed. Believable throughout.

### 6. Forced choice for World-line 1: **B**

B wins this world-line. It tells the same mystery as a dramatized story with a
distinct voice instead of an annotated clue-ledger, and — decisively — it pays
off the death mechanically better: the wrist-bruise and the thread in the
clockwork are concrete, planted-and-redeemed details that make Crane's fall and
the stopped clock a single physical event a character caused, where A asks the
reader to accept a fall thirty feet away stopping a clock by sympathetic
vibration and never uses the wrist-bruise at all. A is more elegant sentence by
sentence and more exhaustively tidy, but its method keeps the reader outside the
events being catalogued.

## World-line 2 — confront / burn

This world-line shares A's and B's Scenes 1–16 with their W1 equivalents almost
verbatim (A is identical to W1-A through Scene 16; B is identical to W1-B through
Scene 16). The branch is the ending: Junia *burns* the night-log instead of
naming the climber.

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

- **A: 0 internal errors.** The burn ending is consistent: log destroyed (S17),
  Hale left with fraud + key but unable to prove the second hand innocent or name
  the climber (S18), death un-certifiable as either accident or murder, policy
  pays by default. Clean.
- **B: 0 internal errors.** Same branch, consistently executed (S17 burn, S18
  stock-taking, S19 "agent unestablished" report). B additionally and correctly
  reasons that the burn does *not* save the fraud case because the ledgers and
  invoices "would not burn" (B S18) — a nice causal honesty.

One cross-version note (not an error): A's burn ending lets the policy **pay out**
(A S18: "the Society would pay out, clean"), while B's burn ending has Hale refuse
to certify accident and write "*something else, agent unestablished*" so the
policy **does not pay** (B S19). These are two different, internally-valid
outcomes of the same act — B's is the more rigorous reading (a clock stopped by a
human hand, a forged recall, and a drained fund are all provable without the log,
so "accident" is unwriteable), and it makes Hale's integrity cost something. A's
outcome (burn → policy pays) is the bleaker irony but slightly less defensible,
since A's own Scene 18 still lists the proven key and fraud, which a careful
adjuster would not paper over with "accident."

### 2. Prose quality

- **A: 3/5.** Same strengths and the same twice-told mechanical refrain as W1-A.
  The burn scene (S17) is well-handled and restrained.
- **B: 4/5.** Same voice advantage as W1-B. B's burn scene is the standout of the
  whole comparison: "He could take the *book* from the fire but he could not take
  the *name* from her... The name lived in memory alone. The book was only its
  shadow." (S17) — and the "*something else, agent unestablished* ... carried it
  down into the valley like a stone" close (S19) is the best-written ending in the
  set.

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 3/5.** Same dramatic-irony ceiling.
- **B: 4/5.** The burn lands harder in B because Hale's *choice not to stop her*
  is dramatized as a live moral beat (B S17: "He did not move"), and the cost to
  his report is concrete.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 3/5.** The burn pays off the "she shields someone" setup, but because A
  never dramatized Junia's interiority as richly, the burn is more *reported* than
  felt. The policy-pays irony is set up adequately.
- **B: 4/5.** Stronger: B set up Junia as "she lied the way an honest woman tells
  the truth" (S6) and the "it would be kinder to burn it" line (S15) explicitly
  foreshadows the act, so the burn is a planted payoff, not a swerve.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 4/5.** Consistent; Hale certifies only what objects prove.
- **B: 5/5.** B is the more believable here precisely because Hale's final report
  refuses to claim what he cannot prove ("agent unestablished") — the version most
  scrupulous about the boundary between what a character knows and what he asserts.

### 6. Forced choice for World-line 2: **B**

B wins, more decisively than in W1. The burn is the emotional hinge of this
world-line, and B both foreshadows it ("kinder to burn it," S15) and dramatizes
it with the strongest prose in the comparison, then follows it with the most
intellectually honest ending — Hale refusing to write "accident" over a provable
human agency and signing "something else, agent unestablished." A's parallel
ending is competent but reports the burn and reaches the less defensible outcome
(policy pays clean despite a proven hidden key and forged recall sitting in
Hale's own notes).

## World-line 3 — quiet (audit road; Hale never confronts Pike)

Here the branch is earlier and deeper: at Scene 9 Hale chooses **silence** over
confrontation and reconstructs the theft alone, watching Pike incriminate himself.
The two versions then diverge structurally in their *endings*:

- **A** reaches the whole truth **from objects alone, with no confession at all**
  (Junia never speaks; Hale infers everything, S13–S16).
- **B** still routes to **Junia's spoken testimony** (S17–S18, the same narrated
  reveal as W1-B), but arrives there by the quiet road, and adds a distinctive
  psychological beat: Hale watches Pike *visit but not destroy* the telegram
  drawer (B S10), and Junia later explains Pike keeps it "because it is the only
  place the old man still is" (B S19).

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

- **A: 0 internal errors.** Impressively, A-quiet closes the entire case by
  inference (clock by shared-pier vibration, hand by kitchen-talk about Crane's
  stiffened fingers, climber by key + plate + undelivered wire) without anyone
  confessing. Internally consistent.
- **B: 1 soft tension, not a hard error.** B-quiet inherits the same clock account
  as W1-B (Pike's hand caught the works, thread of sleeve — B S18). But B-quiet
  also has Hale find the thread *himself* in Scene 12 ("a thread of cloth caught
  on the rough cast iron... a hand had reached in to grip and still the swinging
  weight") *before* Junia's testimony — which is fine and actually good
  foreshadowing. No contradiction. I count **0 hard errors** for B as well; the
  thread is consistently a human hand's in B throughout.

  One genuinely soft spot in **A**: A-quiet's clock is still stopped by *vibration
  from the fall* (S14), yet A-quiet's Pike "patting at something smaller and
  harder through the lining of his coat" and the whole reconstruction never
  accounts for the wrist-bruise that A's own opening does not plant (A never has
  the bruise; A's B-counterpart does). So A is self-consistent but thinner on
  physical corroboration than B.

So: **A = 0 internal errors; B = 0 internal errors.**

### 2. Prose quality

- **A: 3/5.** Same competent-but-annotative voice. The "quiet road" framing
  ("found without a single voice raised," "the objects... had never once tried to
  tell him a lie," S16) is thematically strong, and A-quiet is arguably A's best
  of the three because the all-inference ending suits the omniscient-ledger method
  — the method and the plot finally agree.
- **B: 4/5.** Same voice advantage. B-quiet's added Pike-at-the-drawer material
  (S10, S19) is the most psychologically rich writing in either column: "He was
  not hiding evidence... He was visiting it. ...he could not part with it and
  could not bear it."

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 3/5.** The watch-and-wait surveillance (S9–S11) generates some hunter's
  tension, but the omniscient frame still pre-spoils the truth.
- **B: 4/5.** B-quiet has the richest tension of the set: the silent night-watch,
  the drawer Pike "could neither destroy nor bear," and the kindness-as-cruelty
  confrontation with Junia ("kindness is the cruelest way to ask," S15).

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 4/5.** The "let the objects speak" thesis set up across the whole story
  pays off cleanly in an all-inference solve — the most thematically unified
  payoff A achieves. Slight cost: no human catharsis; the case closes coldly by
  design, which A acknowledges ("a lonelier way than he had expected," S16).
- **B: 5/5.** B-quiet has the best setup/payoff in the entire comparison: the
  drawer Pike *visits but won't destroy* (planted S10) is paid off by Junia's
  reading of it (S19, "the only place the old man still is"), turning a clue into
  the story's emotional thesis. That is a told-story payoff, not a filed clue.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 5/5.** A-quiet is the single most rigorous version on this axis: Hale
  reaches every conclusion strictly from things he could observe or overhear, and
  the narrative is scrupulous that he infers (clock from bolts, hand from kitchen
  talk) rather than being told. Nobody acts on what they cannot know.
- **B: 4/5.** Also strong, but B leans on Junia's eyewitness testimony for the
  final mechanism, and on Hale's inference from watching Pike's face — slightly
  more interpretive leaps than A's pure-inference solve, though all believable.

### 6. Forced choice for World-line 3: **B**

The closest of the three. A-quiet is genuinely excellent on its own terms: it is
the one version where A's omniscient-ledger method and the plot align, producing
the most knowledge-rigorous solve in the set (5/5 believability, all from
objects, no confession). If I were scoring *method-purity* A would take this
world-line. But on the brief's keystone — told story vs. list of facts — B-quiet
is the richer told story: the Pike-at-the-drawer thread (visit-don't-destroy →
"the only place the old man still is") is the best single piece of
character-writing in the whole comparison, it transforms a clue into the theme,
and B's stakes and setup/payoff are stronger. A-quiet wins the puzzle; B-quiet
wins the story. By the brief's emphasis on voice and told-story quality, B takes
this world-line too — narrowly.

---

## Overall forced choice across the whole comparison: **B**

Across all three world-lines, B is the stronger work as a *told story with a
voice*, which is the comparison's stated keystone. The B versions dramatize
discovery in scene, carry concrete planted-and-redeemed physical details (the
wrist-band → wrist-grab → thread-in-the-clockwork chain that makes Crane's fall
and the stopped clock one character-caused event), and produce the best writing
in the set (the burn passage in W2-B, the drawer passage in W3-B). The A versions
are frequently elegant sentence by sentence and are *more exhaustively tidy* about
their clue-ledger — and in W3 (quiet) A achieves a genuinely superior, fully
knowledge-rigorous all-inference solve that I rate 5/5 on character-knowledge
believability. But A's recurring method — narrate the clue, narrate Hale's
misreading, narrate the correction — keeps the reader outside the events being
catalogued; it reads more often as a well-annotated fact-set than as a told story.
B has the occasional looser seam (more reliance on Pike's blurted "recall," a
faintly muddled "above me" in W1-B S17) and one weaker clue-mechanism choice is
actually A's, not B's. Neither version shows hard internal continuity errors in
any world-line. On balance, and especially against the told-story-vs-list rubric,
B is the better of the two across the comparison.
