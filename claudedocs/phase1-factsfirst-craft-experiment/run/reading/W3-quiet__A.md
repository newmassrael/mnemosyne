## Scene 1

Onslow Pike had not meant to be the last living man to see the director alive, and he told himself, in the grey hour before dawn, that he had not been.

He had drawn the money out of the instrument account in careful spoonfuls across two years — a guinea here against a lens that was never ground, a freight charge for a crate that never came — and he had covered the slow wound with paper. When the auditors named their winter visit, he had done the simplest, worst thing a frightened bookkeeper can do: he had forged a telegram in the patron's name, recalling Dr. Aurel Crane to the city to answer for the Society's affairs, so that the director would be gone before the ledgers were opened and the loss laid at the only door above his own.

Pike believed the telegram had reached Crane. He believed the director had read it that very evening, had understood that he was to be dismissed in disgrace, and had climbed to the dome in the small hours because a ruined man cannot sleep. Pike believed this so entirely that it shaped everything he did after.

He was wrong on the single fact the whole night turned upon. The forged telegram had never come up the hill at all. It lay in the iron post-box at the foot of the snow-cut road, where Pike's own clerk had left it against the thaw, undelivered. Crane had never seen it. Crane never knew, to the last second of his life, that any man wished him gone.

At the foot of the iron stair, beneath the great refractor, the director lay where he had fallen from the dark top of it. The shutter stood frozen open to the freezing sky. Pike looked at the body a long moment, then went down the way he had come.

---

## Scene 2

Junia Frost stood on the dome gallery in the cold and watched a man climb the iron stair, and for the rest of her life she would tell people she had been at the spectroscope.

She had been on the gallery. She had heard the careful boots on the rungs at a little past three, had seen the shape of the climber against the open shutter, and she had known him. When they found Crane in the morning she had a choice between the truth and a lie, and she chose the lie without hesitating, because the truth would hang a man she could not bear to see hanged. She said she had worked at the spectroscope until dawn. She would say it to anyone who asked.

The patron's man came up the last passable road two days later, when the worst of the drift had been beaten down by the post-cart — Lewin Hale, an insurance adjuster, methodical as a tide-table, sent to certify whether the Society's instrument policy paid for an accident or for something else.

Hale's working theory, formed before his boots were dry, was the plainest one the scene offered. An old man rises in the night. The stair is iron, the rail is glazed with frost, the dome is dark. He slips; he falls. There is nothing here a careful policy need not cover — a death by misadventure, regrettable and clean. Hale wrote *accidental* at the head of his first page and meant to spend three days proving it.

He did not know that the woman who poured his tea had stood twelve feet above the dead man and watched the living one go down the stair. He had no reason yet to doubt a single word she said.

---

## Scene 3

The first thing that did not fit was the handwriting.

Hale found the night-log open on the desk under the refractor, the entry for the observation due that night set down in a small, even hand — and it was not Crane's hand. Hale had the director's signature from the policy file, a cramped and backward-leaning scrawl, and the log was nothing like it. The log was patient. The log was *legible*.

He turned the page under the lamp and felt the case shift around him. A second hand in the dome meant a second person in the dome. A forged log meant a forger. To a man whose trade was the careful reading of small discrepancies, the wrong hand on the page was the print of a thumb that should not have been there — and a thumb that should not have been there, on the night a man died, was the first cold suggestion that the death had been arranged.

He did not know what the page in fact was. The hand was Junia's. Crane's fingers, that bitter winter, had stiffened past the writing of anything finer than his own ruined signature, and for a month she had taken down his observations at his dictation, sitting at his elbow in the lamplight while he read the eyepiece aloud. The log was the truest document in the building. It was only the truth told in the wrong voice.

But Hale had the policy to think of, and a death, and a hand that was not the dead man's, and he set the three together the way his trade taught him to. *Someone else was here,* he wrote in his own book, and underlined it twice.

---

## Scene 4

He went looking for the someone else, and the building gave him Junia Frost.

She received him in the cold workroom with her hands folded and her story already smooth: she had been at the spectroscope through the whole of the night, drawing the slow plate of a star whose name Hale wrote down and could not pronounce, and so she had heard nothing on the stair, seen nothing, known nothing until the morning brought the director home in pieces. She said it well. She was a steady woman and her eyes did not move while she said it, and Hale, who had heard a thousand careful lies and prided himself on the ear for them, heard none in her.

He took her at her word. He set her in his book as a witness — the closest thing to an honest one the summit held — and turned the page on her. It was the cleanest mistake he made.

For the spectroscope told the other story, if he had pressed it, and he had not yet learned to press a thing that agreed with him. The plate-camera below the eyepiece held its glass exactly as it had been racked the evening before, unmarked, undeveloped, blank as the snow outside. No star had been drawn upon it. No exposure had been made the whole night through. A woman who had stood at that instrument until dawn would have left her hours written in silver on the plate, and the plate was empty.

Hale would come back to the empty plate. He always came back to things. But he came back to them in his own order, and on the day he met Junia Frost he believed her, and the plate kept its contradiction to itself a little longer.

---

## Scene 5

The meridian clock had stopped at fourteen minutes past three, and a stopped clock is a gift to a methodical man, because it hands him a time.

It stood against the north pier, a tall regulator with a seconds-beating pendulum and a face Crane had trusted to a tenth of a second for twenty years. Its hands held 3:14 as if posed. Hale checked it against his own watch, against the railway timetable, against the kitchen clock in the warm room below, and confirmed what the dial said: the great clock had died at three-fourteen and not moved since.

And here was the small, hard fact that would not lie down. The maintenance book showed the clock wound at midnight — Pike's own initials against the entry, the bursar keeping the one chore the staff still shared. A clock of that quality, freshly wound, does not stop of itself in three hours. It runs eight days on a winding. Something had stopped it.

Hale stood with his watch in his palm and let the possibilities sort themselves. A clock stopped by a hand is a clock stopped to *say* something — to fix an hour, to build an alibi, to plant a time in the record that the records would then obey. He did not like it. He liked a clock that ran or a clock that was broken; he did not like a clock that had been *told* to stop, because a told clock meant a teller, and a teller meant intent.

He was looking at the right object and reading it the wrong way round. Nothing had told the clock the hour. The fall had. The iron stair and the clock's tall case were bolted to the same north pier, and when a heavy man went down the stair in the dark the whole pier had rung like a struck rail, and the old pendulum, true to a tenth of a second and brittle as everything that is finely made, had simply ceased. The clock had not recorded a lie. It had recorded, to the minute, the instant Crane fell.

---

## Scene 6

In the bottom drawer of the director's desk, under the spare ribbons and the dead man's spectacles, Hale found a letter, and the letter complicated everything.

It was in Crane's own backward hand, unfinished and unsent, and it was a resignation. The director laid down his post in it with a plain and private grief — his eyes were failing, he wrote, the fine doubled stars had blurred to single smears these two years, and a man who can no longer see the sky has no honest business keeping the key to it. There was no scandal in the letter. There was no enemy in it. There was only an old astronomer going blind, deciding quietly to stop.

Hale read it twice and could not make it agree with the rest. A man planning his own quiet exit does not seem like a man who is pushed. The letter pulled toward accident; the wrong-handed log and the stopped clock pulled toward design; and the two would not be reconciled by any theory he had yet.

He did not know, could not have known, the cruelty folded into the timing. Down in the cold counting-room, Onslow Pike carried a different letter in his head — the telegram he believed Crane had read, the dismissal he believed Crane had understood. Pike was certain the director had climbed the stair a disgraced man, certain the confrontation he had set in motion was the thing that put Crane at the rail in the dark. He moved through the days like a man who has half-killed someone, burying what he could bury, because he believed himself the author of the fall.

Two letters, then, and only one of them ever sent — and the unsent one true, and the sent one a lie that never arrived, and the man who wrote the lie convinced past argument that it had landed. Hale held the resignation to the lamp and felt, for the first time, that the summit was lying to him in more directions than one.

---

## Scene 7

There were two keys to the dome, and Hale could account for one.

The director's key hung on the dead man's ring, recovered from the body, cold and present. The second key — the spare — appeared in the inventory book with a line through it: *reported lost,* a month before the death, in Pike's careful column-hand. A lost key is an ordinary thing on a remote summit; things slide into snow and are not seen till spring. Hale noted it and would have passed on, except that he was a man who counted, and a thing reported lost that then turns up is no longer lost. It is *hidden.*

For the spare key was not at the bottom of any drift. It was warm in the lining of Onslow Pike's own coat, where it had ridden since the night he last used it, and it was the quiet hinge the whole crime swung upon. With it, a man could let himself into the dome at any hour, by the lower door, unseen by the resident staff and unrecorded by any book — could climb to the safe where Crane kept the duplicate ledgers, could go up the iron stair in the dark and come down again, and leave the morning to find a director who seemed to have died alone.

Hale did not have the key yet, and did not know whose coat held it. But he had the hole where it should have been, and the date it went missing, and the cold arithmetic of a lost thing that someone had taken care to call lost. Around that hole the accident theory began, very slowly, to come apart in his hands. An accident does not need a hidden key. Only a visitor needs a hidden key — a visitor who came when he should not have, and left before he could be seen.

---

## Scene 8

It was the account that finally turned the lock.

Hale had asked for the instrument ledgers as a formality — every policy claim wants the books — and he had not expected them to bleed. But the figures, set against the freight receipts and the optician's invoices he had wired down the valley to confirm, would not close. There was a sum gone out of the instrument account that had bought no instrument. There was a year of small subtractions, each defensible alone, that together came to a wound. The motive he had not been looking for lay open on the page in front of him, in the bursar's own neat hand, and once he had seen it he could not un-see it.

A drained account. A director recalled, the staff said, by the patron's wire — though Hale had not yet found the wire. A spare key called lost. A clock that stopped to the minute of the fall. A log in a hand that was not the dead man's. Each had been, alone, a thing he could explain away. Set in a row beside the missing money, they stopped being a row of accidents and became a shape — and the shape had a man-sized hole in the middle of it, and the man whose hand had drained the account was the man who kept the keys to everything.

Onslow Pike was in the counting-room with the snow at the window, and Hale stood in the doorway with the ledger under his arm and the discrepancy ringing in him like the stopped clock's last beat. He could feel the whole investigation gather to a point. He had a choice to make, and he knew it, and he stood in the door a long moment making it.

---

## Scene 9

Hale said nothing.

He had stood in the doorway with the discrepancy ringing in him, and he had made the colder choice: he closed the ledger, smiled the small grey smile of a man satisfied by tidy books, and asked only — as a formality, he said — for the rest of the records, the freight files, the optician's correspondence, the year's loose receipts. Pike gave them gladly, the way a guilty man gives what he thinks is harmless, and Hale carried the whole armful back to the cold room he had been given and shut the door and began, in silence, to take the theft apart.

It came apart cleanly under patience. Every spooned guinea against a lens never ground; every freight charge for a crate that never came up the road; every defensible small subtraction that, laid end to end, made the wound. He matched each phantom outlay to the gap it left and built the theft back up in his own neat columns until he had the whole shape of it — two years long, careful, and damning. He did not write Pike's name at the head of it. He did not need to. The hand in the ledgers was the hand that kept the keys.

And because he had said nothing, Pike believed himself unsuspected. That was the thing Hale had bought with his silence. A confronted man defends himself and goes still; an unsuspected man, who believes himself a near-murderer with much to hide, *moves.* Hale meant to let him move. He sat with the reconstructed theft locked in his case and waited, like a man at a hide, for the frightened thing to break cover.

---

## Scene 10

Pike broke cover on the second night.

Hale had taken to walking the cold galleries after the staff were abed, on the pretext of a bad chest that wanted air, and on the second night he saw the bursar moving where a bursar had no business at that hour — down the service passage toward the lower dome, a dark lantern hooded to a coin of light, a bundle under his coat. Hale stood still in a doorway and let him pass and did not breathe.

He could not see all of what Pike did in the dark, but he could read the shape of it. The man was hiding things. He carried the duplicate ledgers — the second set, the true set, that would have damned the false columns at a glance — and he carried something smaller and harder that he kept patting at through the lining of his coat, the way a man checks a thing he is afraid to lose. Pike moved like a man burying a body in pieces: furtive, methodical, sick with the work. He believed himself a murderer covering a murder. Every thread he hid, he hid against a hanging.

Hale watched and understood the half of it that was true and could not yet see the half that was false. He saw a guilty man destroying evidence in the dark, and read it, reasonably, as the act of the man who had also put Crane at the foot of the stair. He did not know — could not know, from a doorway — that the guilt driving Pike was guilt for a killing that had never happened, that the bundle of ledgers was real theft and the dread was phantom murder. He saw a man hiding things, and waited to see *what.*

---

## Scene 11

On the third night Hale climbed ahead of him, and waited in the dark of the stair-head, and learned what the coat had been hiding.

He had guessed Pike would come to the dome itself — a man hiding the true ledgers would hide them where the staff never went, in the safe beneath the refractor that only Crane had used. So Hale went up first, in stocking feet, and folded himself into the black recess at the top of the iron stair, and waited in a cold that bit through to the bone, and after an hour he heard the careful boots on the rungs below.

Pike came up not by the gallery door, which the staff could open, but by the lower dome door — and to open the lower dome door he drew from the lining of his coat a key. Hale watched the small hard thing catch the lantern-light. He knew it at once for what the inventory book had mourned: the spare dome key, *reported lost* a month before the death in Pike's own column-hand, that had never been lost at all. It had ridden warm against the bursar's ribs the whole time, the quiet hinge the man's nights swung upon — the key that let him into the dome at any hour, unseen by the resident staff, unrecorded by any book.

There it was, then: the hidden key in the proven hand. A man who had drained the account, forged a recall, and kept a secret key to the dome — and who came to that dome in the dark, alone, to bury what would hang him. Hale's theory firmed from suspicion into intrusion. The bursar had been in this dome unseen, by this door, with this key. The accident he had been sent to certify was no accident at all.

---

## Scene 12

By the grey of the third dawn Hale had the whole shape of the theft, and a man-sized hole in the middle of the night still unfilled.

He laid it out for himself in the cold room as a draughtsman lays out a plan: the drained account, two years deep, in Pike's hand; the forged recall meant to clear Crane out before the audit; the spare key called lost and kept; the bursar abroad in the dome at unholy hours with a key no honest errand needed. Every line of the theft was firm. He could certify the fraud tomorrow and the patron would believe him and the policy would not pay a penny it owed to a fraud.

But the death would not close. That was the thing he kept circling back to, as he always circled back to things, in his own order. He had a thief proven past doubt and a dome the thief could enter unseen, and the easy reading — the reading his trade would have applauded — was that the thief had also been the killer, had climbed the iron stair in the dark and sent the old man over the rail. It fit. It nearly fit. And yet the unsent resignation in the drawer would not lie down beside it, and the stopped clock said a thing he had not yet made it say, and the woman who swore she had been at the spectroscope had a stillness in her eyes he had taken, too quickly, for honesty.

Hale had reconstructed the theft in silence and watched the frightened man run, and the running had told him everything about the money and nothing certain about the fall. To learn how Crane went down the stair, he would have to stop watching the bursar and start pressing the instrument the assistant swore she had worked all night — the one that, alone in all the building, could not lie to him.

---

## Scene 13

On the audit limb Hale never confronted the bursar, and so the truth came to him the cold way, out of objects and never out of a man's mouth.

He had reconstructed the whole theft in silence and watched Pike run by night, and the running had told him everything about the money and nothing certain about the fall. So he came back, in his own order, to the one instrument that could not learn a story and could not be persuaded to repeat one — the spectroscope Junia swore she had worked until dawn, and the plate racked beneath its eyepiece. He developed it himself, alone, in the stinking little dark closet by the red lamp, with his sleeves rolled and his commission forgotten, and he watched the glass come up out of the bath.

Nothing came up. The plate surfaced exactly as it had gone in: clear, even, unmarked, blank as the snow on the dome. No star had been drawn upon it. No exposure had been made the whole night through. A woman who had stood at that instrument from dusk to dawn would have laid down her hours in silver, and the silver was not there.

He carried the cold wet glass out into the daylight and understood that Junia Frost had lied to him from the first afternoon. She had not been at the spectroscope. Her steady, smooth, unmoving account — the account he had taken for the one honest testimony on the summit and turned the page on — was the lie he had failed to hear, and the plate had held its contradiction quietly the whole fortnight, waiting for him to think to ask it. He did not go to her with it. On this limb he had learned to let the objects speak and to trust them over any face; and the object had spoken, and it said she had been somewhere else.

---

## Scene 14

He climbed to the dome with a lamp and settled, at last, the two things he had read wrong from the start: the clock, and the hand.

The clock first. He got down on the cold floor and traced the bolts, and found the clock's tall case and the foot of the iron stair fastened, both of them, to the same great north pier the whole dome hung upon. He set his palm flat on the masonry and struck the bottom stair-tread, and felt the pier ring under his hand like a struck rail and the tremor run straight up into the case where the old pendulum hung true to a tenth of a second and brittle as everything finely made. Nothing had told the clock to stop. The fall had. When a heavy man went down that stair in the dark the pier had rung and the pendulum had simply ceased — not at an hour chosen by any hand, but at the precise instant Crane struck the iron. The clock had recorded no alibi and no lie. It had recorded, to the minute, the moment the director fell: fourteen minutes past three.

The hand next, and this he reasoned without forcing a word from anyone. He had the director's ruined backward signature from the policy file, and he had heard, in the kitchen talk he had let run on around him, that Crane's fingers had stiffened past fine writing two winters back. A man whose hand can no longer form fine letters does not stop observing; he dictates, and someone writes at his elbow. The patient legible log in the wrong hand was no forger's mark and no second intruder's calling-card. It was a kindness, taken down at the eyepiece by the assistant who sat beside him — the truest page in the building, told in another's voice, and he had spent a week reading murder in it.

Two false trails closed, and not a confession spent to close them.

---

## Scene 15

What remained was to name how Crane went down the stair, and Hale built the answer the way he built everything on this limb — out of paper, in silence, alone.

He went back through the loose records Pike had handed over so gladly, the freight files and the year's correspondence, and in among them he found the thing the bursar had been too frightened to think to hold back: a copy-book draft, in Pike's own forging hand, of a telegram in the patron's name recalling Crane to the city to answer for the Society's affairs. Hale traced it. He went down to the iron post-box at the foot of the snow-cut road, where the post-cart left what it could not carry up against the drift, and there the wire was — sealed, addressed, and undelivered, lain a fortnight, never carried the last mile, never read. Crane had never seen it. The dismissal Pike had plainly built some private dread upon had never reached the man it named.

Against the undelivered wire Hale set the unsent letter he had found in the desk drawer — Crane's own resignation, in his own backward hand, laying down his post for his failing eyes, a private grief and no enemy in it. Read together they made one quiet sense: Crane had meant to leave of his own accord, in his own time, pushed by no one. He had been a man walking slowly toward a door he intended to open himself.

So Hale assembled the climber without ever seeing him. The clock fixed the hour. The blank plate put the one possible witness off her sworn post. The spare key — lost, and in the proven thief's keeping — gave an unseen road into the dome by the lower door. The wire proved Crane died not knowing any man wished him gone. A thief had come in the dark with a kept key to take the ledgers from the safe; a blind old man, startled on the stair, had fallen; no deliberate hand had done it. He had reached, from objects alone, the whole truth — and not one soul on the summit had confessed a word of it to him.

---

## Scene 16

Hale went down the last passable road with the case built entire and not a confession in it anywhere, which was, he thought, the way he preferred to work and a lonelier way than he had expected.

He certified the fraud without hesitation. The drained instrument account stood proven to the guinea, the forged recall stood proven in the bursar's own copy-book hand, the kept key stood proven by the hole in the inventory and the man it pointed to. Pike would answer to the law for the theft and the forgery both, on evidence no confession had been needed to gather and no confession could have made firmer.

The death he certified as it truly was, and not as the easy reading would have had it. Not a clean accident the policy would pay without a murmur — for a man had entered that dome unlawfully, by a stolen key, in the dark, and a frail director startled on the stair had fallen because of it. Not a murder either — for no deliberate hand had put Crane over the rail, and Hale would not write what he could not prove and did not believe. He wrote it precisely: a death occasioned by unlawful intrusion upon a blameless man, the trespasser being the same hand that drained the account. The policy would not pay clean for that, and the patron would understand exactly why.

It troubled him a little, riding down, that he had never once gone to Pike with the open ledger, never watched the floor go out from under the man, never heard him break. He had chosen the cold method and the cold method had served — every line proven, no theatre, no broken confession to mislead him into a murder that never happened. He had been spared, by his own silence, the bursar's certainty that a dismissal Crane never read had killed him. Hale knew better than that certainty. The objects had told him so, and the objects, alone of everything on that summit, had never once tried to tell him a lie.
