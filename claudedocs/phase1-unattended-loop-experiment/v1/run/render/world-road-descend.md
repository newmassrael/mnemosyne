## sc-01-long-climb

The road gave out a mile below the ridge, the way it always did, and Wren Calloway left the truck nosed in against a deadfall and went up on foot with the light already going amber behind her. Late in the season for this climb. Late enough that the manzanita had gone the color of old rope and the whole slope smelled of dust and heated resin — of a summer that had held its breath too long and was only now, grudgingly, thinking about letting go.

She knew the trail by heart: the switchback under the leaning snag, the long traverse where the ridge fell away on both sides. She took it at the steady, unhurried pace of someone who had a whole night in front of her and meant to arrive with something still in the legs. The Ban Tower stood up out of the last of the daylight, a small box on its stilts catching the dusk in its glass. One more turn of the watch before the rains came. She'd drawn the tail end of it. Somebody had to.

By the time she reached the base stairs the valley behind her had gone blue and depthless, and the first stars were coming up hard and cold over a country that had not seen rain in a hundred days.

## sc-02-empty-cab

The cab door was unlatched.

That was the first wrong thing, and she stood on the top stair a moment with her hand flat against it, listening. A lookout latched the door. You latched it against the wind that came up the draws at night, and against the animals, and against the plain habit of a person who lived a hundred feet in the air. She pushed it open, and the hinges spoke into a silence that had no one in it.

"Tomas?"

The cab was cold. Not cooling — cold, the flat unbreathing cold of a room the stove had gone out in hours ago. His mug stood on the map table with a skin on the coffee. The bunk was made. The logbook lay open under the west window where the light died, the fire-finder trained on some bearing out past the glass, and of Tomas Hale, who ought to have been standing right here to hand her the watch and grumble about the drive up — of Tomas there was nothing at all.

She said his name once more, softer, the way you do when you already know the room won't answer. Then she set her pack down slow, so as not to add any more noise to a quiet that was starting to feel like it meant something.

## sc-03-torn-page

She lit the lamp before she did anything else, because the dark up here was a thing you got ahead of or it got ahead of you.

The logbook was Tomas's hand all down the page — the small square print she'd know anywhere. Times, weather, the wind out of the northeast, the humidity dropping through the afternoon like a stone down a well. Ordinary. The watch as it had been kept ten thousand nights running. She turned toward the end to find where he'd left off, where she'd take it up.

The last page was gone.

Not lost — torn. A clean stub ran down against the binding, the paper parted in one hard pull, and the page that should have carried the close of his day and the start of her night simply wasn't there. She ran her thumb along the ragged edge. Whoever tore it out had wanted it gone in a hurry, or wanted it gone badly, and either way it had been his own book and his own hand — and now it was a book with its last word missing.

## sc-04-last-words

But not the whole last word.

The radio kept its own record, a slip beside the set, and that Tomas hadn't touched. His last call was there in his print, gone hasty at the end, timed to the evening before. *Smoke*, it said. *West draw* — and then a bearing, and the fire-finder still sat locked to it, that thin sighting-hair laid across the dark shoulder of the mountain where the west draw fell away toward country no road reached. He'd called it down to the valley. And in the margin, copied out from the dispatcher's reply in his own hand: *unconfirmed*. No column read from below. Nothing on their instruments, where he swore he had seen smoke rise.

She stood with the slip in her hand and the cold cab around her, and understood — the way you understand a thing before you have decided anything about it — what the night was going to ask of her. A smoke that no one else could find. A man who had gone toward it and not come back. The radio net would close before long and the window with it, and after that she'd be alone up here with a question and no one to put it to. Confirm the column. Find out what had become of him. Do it before the valley went dark to her.

She folded the slip into her breast pocket like something she had signed her name to. Nobody had handed her this watch. She took it up anyway.

## sc-05-no-answer

The hand set took cranking. She worked the little dynamo until the needle woke and the valley came up thin and far out of the static — a woman's voice she knew, Marla Vane, down at the district desk where it was warm and lit and there were other people in the room.

"Ban Tower," Wren said. "Calloway. I've relieved the post." She let that sit a second. "Give me your read on the west draw. Tomas logged a column there last night."

The static breathed. Then: "Ban Tower, we've got nothing in the west draw. Never did. We ran it twice — the valley cameras, the far tower — and it reads dead ground, Wren. Cold as a stone. There's no fire there." A pause, and the voice came softer, careful. "Where's Tomas?"

"That," Wren said, "is my other question."

The silence that came back had a weight to it. She heard the scratch of Marla's pencil, or imagined she did, the night going down into the record the way nights did: *Ban Tower reports a column; dispatch cannot confirm; dead ground.* The instruments were good. The instruments said nothing was burning where Tomas had sworn he'd seen smoke, and where Tomas himself had now gone. *Missing*, she made herself think. Only missing. She had not earned any darker word than that, and she was not going to spend one early.

## sc-06-reading-the-room

She put the radio down and read the room the way he would have wanted her to, because a lookout's cab was a kind of sentence and everything in it was a word.

His boots were gone from under the bunk — the good stiff-soled ones he wore off the tower and nowhere else, because up here you went in wool socks or camp shoes and saved your feet. Gone. And the coil of climbing rope that lived on the iron hook by the door: she had reached to hang her own jacket toward that hook out of habit and found it bare, a paler ring worn into the wall where forty feet of rope had hung and swung and hung again for years. Gone too.

She stood in the middle of it and let the cab finish its sentence. He had seen a smoke the valley couldn't find. He had called it down and been told there was nothing there. And a man who logs dead ground and then takes his boots and his rope and goes off into it in the last light — that is not a man who thinks he saw a fire. You don't bring rope to a wildfire. You bring rope to a person. He had thought there was somebody down that draw. He had thought it hard enough to go alone, in the dark — because he'd understood, as well as she did now, what the answer from the valley meant: that no one else was coming, that if it was going to be anyone it would have to be him.

## sc-07-window-narrows

Full dark now, and the mountain gone to shapes. Out the west glass the draw was a blackness folded into other blacknesses, no light in it, no smoke that anyone could see.

The net would close soon. That was the shape of the pressure building in the room. Somewhere down in the district office the last check-ins were coming in tower by tower, and when the net closed for the night a manned post was meant to stay a manned post — that was Ossory's rule, the district ranger's, black ink in the drought protocol: in a season this dry the towers held, all night, every night, eyes on the country until the sun came back. Dell Ossory would expect the Ban Tower to answer at dawn from right here, a hundred feet up, watching. He had built his whole thin margin of safety on the towers staying put.

And she was the tower now. If she went down the draw she took the district's only eyes down with her, into a ravine where they'd see nothing but the six feet her headlamp reached. Hold the post and keep the faith the protocol was built on — and leave whatever was below to the dark. Or go, and answer the other thing the night was asking, the thing that had already taken Tomas.

She had until the net closed to decide. And the net was closing.

## sc-08-west-draw

She took the lamp out onto the catwalk, to the west rail, where the overlook gave straight down into the draw.

Behind her the cab held its small dutiful glow — the post, the protocol, the rule that kept a whole district together on nights like this, everything she had climbed up here to be. Ahead of her and below, past the rail, the draw fell away into a dark that had swallowed a smoke and a man and would not give up either. She couldn't see a fire. She never had. All she had was a torn page, an empty pair of hooks, a dead read from the valley, and the shape her own mind kept making out of them: that a person was down there. That Tomas had believed it enough to go. That belief was the only light she carried, and it pointed one way, and the rule pointed the other, and she stood at the rail with her hand on the cold iron and felt the whole night narrow to the width of a single step.

Then she went back inside for her rope.

## sc-09d-down-the-scree

She left the cab lit behind her — let it burn, let it say to anyone watching that the Ban Tower was still awake even if no one now stood in it — and went off the end of the ridge on foot, down toward where the ground broke away into the west draw.

The scree began where the trees quit. Loose rock over steeper rock, the kind of slope that shifted under you if you asked too much of it, and she went into it sideways and careful, the headlamp throwing her own hunched shadow down the fall ahead of her. Somewhere below, the draw. Somewhere below, the thing she had come down to find.

Her lamp caught the rope before she understood what she was seeing.

It came up out of the dark at the lip of the scree — a climbing line made fast around the base of a wind-twisted pine, the knot dressed and snugged the way a careful man ties a knot he means to trust his weight to. It ran off over the edge and down into the black, taut, waiting. Someone had rigged a handline here. Someone had stood where she stood, in the dark, and looked at the same drop, and decided to go down it, and left a rope behind to say so. She closed her hand around it. It held. She followed it down.

## sc-10d-cold-ring

The rope ran out onto the floor of the draw, and there, where it let her down at last onto flat ground, her lamp found the fire.

Not a fire — the ghost of one. A ring of blackened stones on the bare gravel, ash gone gray and cold in the middle of it, a little cairn of burnt sticks fallen in on themselves. She crouched and put her bare hand flat to the ash and felt nothing, no heat, only the night's chill come all the way down into it. Dead hours ago. But real — a real fire, laid by real hands in a ring of gathered stones, exactly where Tomas had swung the fire-finder and called his smoke down to a valley that swore there was nothing there. The valley had been wrong. He had not imagined it. Somebody had lit this.

And then, past the edge of the lamplight, a sound — a voice, cracked and small and human, saying something that might have been a word or might only have been the shape a person makes when the dark finally answers back.

She was up against the far bank, out of the wind, where she'd dragged herself after the fall took her leg. Sella. Two days she had been down here, the woman would tell her later — feeding that fire with everything she could reach, sending up the only smoke she had against a mountain that would not look at her, two days signalling to be seen, and on the whole ridge only one man had seen. Now there was Wren, kneeling in the cold ash with her lamp on a face gone gaunt and joyful and streaked, saying it's all right, I've got you, I'm here, I came. Alive. Hurt, and half-frozen, and alive.

## sc-11d-on-the-scree

She found him on the way to getting Sella out.

The rope she had come down by — his rope, she understood now, the forty feet off the bare hook in the cab — ran on past the fire ring toward the head of the draw and ended at a broken ledge, a lip of rock sheared away raw and pale where the rest of it had let go. And below the ledge, where the ledge had dropped him, she found Tomas Hale.

He lay at the foot of the scree the way a man lies who has fallen a long way in the dark and not got up, and he had lain there since the evening before she had ever set her hand to the door of his cab — since before she climbed the ridge, before the cold coffee, before any of it. He had gone down to the smoke he believed in. He had rigged his line and worked along the ledge to reach her, and the ledge had given way under him, and that had been the end of the watch he was keeping. The rope simply stopped where the rock had.

She knelt in the scree beside him a while. In the breast of his coat, folded small, was the last page of the log — the page he had torn from his own book and carried down with him, the record of the smoke he would not let the valley talk him out of, the one word the cab had been missing all night. He had been right. She read it by the lamp, then folded it again and put it away beside the slip already over her own heart, and told him, quietly, that she had found her. That the woman he'd gone down for was alive. That he had not been wrong, and he had not been alone in it, not at the end.

## sc-12d-carry-out

Dawn came gray up the draw before she'd got Sella warm enough to move, and with the first of it Wren dug the hand set out of her pack and cranked it there on the floor of the ravine, the little dynamo whirring under cold fingers until the valley woke.

"Ban Tower," she said, though she was a thousand feet below it now. "Calloway. I'm on the floor of the west draw, and I need you to write this down."

Marla's voice came back fast, no sleep left in it. Wren gave her the draw. Gave her the fire ring for a landmark and the broken ledge above it. One survivor, she said — a hiker, alive, a leg she couldn't walk on and two days with no food; bring the litter, and bring it up the north approach, the scree won't take a stretcher. And one recovery. She gave them Tomas, too, and where, and how, and heard the pencil stop on the far end and the small silence that meant Marla had understood.

There had been no confirming any of it from a hundred feet up. No instrument in the valley had ever read this ground as anything but dead. It had wanted a person to come down into the dark and put a hand to the ash and find out — and one had, the evening before, and paid for it; and then another had, and had not had to pay, and was going home. Wren stayed on the crank in the growing light, giving the valley everything the night had cost, until she heard the far-off engines start.
