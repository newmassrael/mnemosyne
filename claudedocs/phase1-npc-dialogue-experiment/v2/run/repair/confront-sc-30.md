## sc-30

But it was not the whole of it, and Ysolt Marran knew it was not.

She came forward with her chest — the chest she had slept across, that she had let no soul near — and set it down on the wet shingle in front of them all, and took a key from a cord at her own throat (a second key, cut against the loss of the first) and opened it before the Holm.

"There's no coin in it," she said. "No plate. No treasure worth a wrecking." She drew out the papers. "A deed. A contested deed, and my inheritance under it, that I sailed to lodge ashore and make law. And this one against it, that would break my claim." She did not hold them up for the gathering. She turned, with them in her hand, and went straight at the mate. "The man who carried the deed against me — who carried the key to this chest — was my own kin. The supercargo. The man you say the sea took out of your hand."

"It did," Cass said. "I told you — I had his coat in my fist and the sea — "

"Where is the key, then." Quiet, and it cut straight through him. "He carried it on a cord, like mine. The sea takes a man, mate. It doesn't unknot a cord from a dead man's neck and leave you the key." She came a step nearer. "Where is the lading-bill. You went into that water with him. You came out of it with what he carried. A man does not get a paper off a drowning kinsman's coat by *grieving* him."

"You don't know what's in the water," Cass said, but it came thin. "You weren't in it. You don't know what a man — "

And Halsa, the lantern still in her hand, the deed in front of her eyes, saw the bottom of the whole black thing at last, and said it slow, to be sure of it as she said it. "You held him under."

"I never — "

"You held him under." She turned full on him before the Holm. "In the surf, in the dark — for the bill, for what it was worth to you. That's why you alone came off her living. You made sure of it." She had not known it an hour since. She knew it now, watching his face come apart.

Cass looked at the deed, and at the ring of the Holm closed round him on the bar with the tide on the make and nowhere to run, and the survivor's grief he had worn all week slid off him like a wet coat.

"He'd have had it *all*." It broke out of him ugly, sideways, not to the woman, not to anyone, only to get it out from where it had been stopping his throat a week. "The cargo, the claim, every cask — walked off with the lot and left me, the only living officer off her, with *nothing*. So when she went over and we were both in the black water together I — " He stopped. His mouth worked. "Aye. I had hold of him. And I didn't let go to save him." Lower. "I held on the other way. Till he went quiet. And I took the bill off him and let the sea have the rest, and I told you he slipped my grip." His face was wet and it was not the sea now. "He slipped nothing. I drowned him for the paper. There. *There.* Is that — is that what you wanted off me."

Pell Garrow, who had stood there counting casks a week, heard the mate own a killing and felt the ground of his own grievance fall away.

"A *killing*," the factor said, low, into the quiet. "I came onto this rock for one short cask of brandy. One cask. And I'd have hanged this man for *that.*" He looked at Cass with something near horror. "My short cask. God forgive me — it's the least wrong done on this shore. The very least of it."
