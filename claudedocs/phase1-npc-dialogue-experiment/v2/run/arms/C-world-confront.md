## sc-01

The tide-bell did not ring.

Halsa knew it before she was rightly awake — the way you know a tooth gone in the night, by the shape of the absence. The bell hung in the Holm's little belfry over the boat-noost, and it rang the turn of the tide whether a soul wanted it or no; it had rung her whole life, under her mother's life, under everyone's. And now it had not.

She lay in the dark and listened to the gale go over the roof like a cart of stones, and she listened past it for the bell, and there was no bell. There was only the sea, very loud, very close, where the sea should not have been so close.

She went to the window. The night was black as a closed eye. And out across the Sneck, where the Holm light should have stood up steady and yellow on its stair to mark the channel and the bar — there was nothing. No light. The Holm light was dark.

"The light's out," she said aloud, to no one, and the saying of it frightened her more than the silence had. She could not say why it had failed. In her whole life she had never once seen it fail.

—

Higher up the island, in the lee of the chapel wall, Orne Veck stood with his back to the wind and watched the Sneck and said nothing at all. He had a thing under his coat, against his ribs, that he meant no living soul should ever see. He knew the Holm light was dark. He knew the hour. And he knew, as he stood there with the gale tearing at his coat-skirts, that whatever came up out of the water before morning, no one on the Holm must ever learn that the light had been *made* dark.

Out on the channel a great groaning came, timber and the sea taking it, the long terrible grind of a hull finding the Sneck. Orne did not move. He had carried the lantern to the Ness that night, and shown what he had shown, and he stood now in the chapel's lee with the thing against his ribs and let the sea do the rest.

—

On the light-stair, in the dark, Bryde Crewe was climbing.

The keeper had seen the light go out — seen it from below, the great lamp swallowed as if a hand had closed over it — and had gone up the stair two at a time in the black with the gale shouldering the tower, climbing to relight what should never have gone out. The stair was wet. The fourth turn was always wet. Bryde's foot went, and the leg under it, and Bryde went down hard against the stone with a crack that the wind ate whole, and lay there with the light still dark above and the sea taking something out on the Sneck below, and could not climb, and could not call, and the night closed over the Holm like water over a stone.

## sc-02

By grey morning the wind had dropped to a sulk, and the shore was a ruin of timber.

Halsa stood at the head of the strand and could not for a moment make sense of it — the whole beach planked over with broken ship, spars and stove barrel-staves and a litter of dark sodden things rolling in the wash. The Sneck had taken a vessel in the night. The *Marran*, they'd have her name by noon, off a board or a man. And the Drang — the bar of shingle that was the Holm's one road to the mainland at low water — the Drang was drowned under, the tide standing high and grey over the whole of it.

She understood the second thing before she had words for the first. No one was leaving. No one was coming. Until the long ebb bared the Drang again, the Holm was shut in with whatever the night had washed up onto it.

Eda the curer came down past her, wiping her hands on her apron, and stood looking at the wreckage with the flat sorrow of someone who had seen it before.

"The Sneck," Eda said. "It's a wicked piece of water and always was. Honest weather, a black night, and no light to steer by when she needed one — " She crossed herself, brief and practical. "God keep them. There'll be men under all that. There always are."

The officer was further down, where the water turned the timber, his Crown coat dark with spray, and he was looking not at the wreck but at the drowned Drang.

"No soul leaves the Holm," he said, more to himself than to anyone. "Not till the ebb bares the road. There's no help to send for and none can come." He looked back up the strand at the shut-in island as if measuring it. "So whatever there is to find here, it's here. It can't get off."

## sc-03

Bryde lay abed in the keeper's room with the leg splinted between two oar-blades, and would not be moved, and could not have been.

"You'll keep the tally," Bryde said. "While I'm down. Someone has to keep the Holm's account or there's no account at all. You can read my hand."

So Halsa took the keeper's tally-book, the long ledger where every tide and every lamp-filling and every barrel landed was logged in Bryde's square, careful hand, going back years. She took it to the table by the window for the light, and she turned to the last page, to bring it up to the day.

And there she stopped.

The last line, in Bryde's own hand, written she could not say when in the night, read: *Light out.* And the hour beside it. And she had only to set that hour against the hour the *Marran* had struck — everyone knew that hour, the whole Holm had heard the grinding — to see that they were the same. The Holm light had gone out at the very hour the ship found the bar.

She sat very still with the book.

"Bryde," she said. "How did the light come to be out?"

There was a long quiet from the bed.

"I'll not say," said Bryde at last, flat and tired. "I'll say one thing only, and you can write it: I did not douse it. Write that. *Bryde Crewe did not douse the Holm light.* And then leave it."

## sc-04

The boy came up from the shore at a dead run, white in the face.

"Wick," Halsa said, catching him by the shoulder. "What is it. What did you find."

"A man," the boy gasped. "A drowned man, on the bar — "

"Where? Show me."

But Wick's eyes slid away. "No — a cask. It were a cask, washed up, I — "

"Which, Wick. A man or a cask."

And the boy's whole face crumpled and shut at once, the way a child's does over a thing too big for it. "A lantern," he whispered. "It were only a lantern. I don't — I don't know which it were. Leave me be." And he twisted out from under her hand and was gone, up toward the net-loft, faster than she could follow.

Senna had watched the whole of it from the wall, the girl's clever face very still.

"He's lying," Senna said, when Halsa looked at her. She said it without heat, the way you'd report the weather. "He found a real thing. He's got it hid in the loft, under the straw, and he's that feared of it he can't keep one telling in his mouth." She tipped her head. "I'd not press him too hard. Whatever it is, it frighted him bad."

Halsa stood and marked it: three tellings out of one boy, and under all three a real thing, and a fright. *Something he found frightens him into lying.* She did not yet know what.

## sc-05

The officer set up his count on a plank laid over two barrels above the tide-line, and worked through the morning with a Crown ledger and a stub of pencil, and made a tally of every cask and bale and broken crate the sea brought up out of the *Marran*.

"It's the Crown's business, a wreck," he said, to anyone who stood near enough to hear. "Every stave of it. What the sea throws up off a lost ship is accounted to the Crown, and I'm the Crown on this rock until the cutter comes. So it's counted." He licked the pencil. "What did you land, and where, and when. I take your word for it — I've no other word to take — but I take it written."

He did not yet know the count was short. He had no way to know. He wrote down what the islanders carried to him and called to him, in good faith, and the number at the foot of his column was the number they gave him.

Pell Garrow stood a little apart with his arms folded and watched the casks go onto the tally one by one, and his lips moved over the count.

"Brandy," he said, when the officer glanced up. "My master's owed brandy off this bottom — proper brandy, bonded, and I've the lading-bill to say how much. I'll have what's his when your count's done, officer. To the cask." He had the air of a man who counted for his living and trusted nothing he had not counted himself.

## sc-06

They pulled a living man out of the surf at the turn of the morning tide, half-drowned and blue and coughing brine, and carried him up to the curing-shed and laid him by the fire, and when he could speak he gave his name as Cass Pellow, mate of the *Marran*.

"The only one," he said, when the warmth had got into him enough to talk. "The only living soul off her, I think. God." He pressed the heels of his hands to his eyes. "We struck in the black — no light, no light anywhere where the light should be — and she went over and broke her back, and I was in the water. I had hold of the supercargo a while. Had his very coat in my fist." His voice cracked, well-placed. "And the sea took him out of my hand. I felt him go. Right out of my hand, into the dark, and under. There was nothing — there was nothing a man could do."

Halsa, standing in the shed door with the tally-book under her arm, believed him. Why would she not? It was an honest, terrible thing, and he told it like a man who had lived it. A ship lost to a black night and a bad bar, and a drowned man the sea would not give back. She believed every word of it that morning, and was sorry for him.

## sc-07

The other one the sea had spared was a passenger, a woman, brought off the wreck living with a sea-chest she would not let go of.

She gave her name as Ysolt Marran — *of* the Marran, she said, with a small dry stress, as though the ship and she shared more than a wreck — and she kept the chest under her own hand and her own eye, in the corner of the headman's house, and slept across it.

"It is mine," she said, when Halsa asked, kindly enough, was there aught in it that wanted drying, aught the salt had got into. "That is all you need to know of it. It is mine, it is locked, and the mate — " her eyes went hard " — the mate is not to have it. Whatever he says, whatever he claims. Keep that man from my chest."

She would not say what was in it. She did not say — though she knew it, holding it close as the chest itself — that the man the sea had taken, the supercargo Cass Pellow mourned so well by the fire, was her own kin; nor that he had carried the chest's key on his body, and carried with it a thing that stood against her, a paper that would unmake her claim. She said only: it is mine, and the mate must not have it. The rest she kept locked, like the chest.

## sc-08

Dunna Quick had been first down at the ebb, before the officer was rightly up, before the count was set — first down, as she was always first down, with her skirts kilted and her creel on her back, because the salt-wife who is not first to the wrack goes home empty.

And the sea, that black morning, had been generous.

"A gift," she told her own hearth, later, with the one cask snug behind the salt-sacks where no Crown pencil would find it. "A plain gift of the sea, and what the sea gives the Holm keeps, and always has, since before there was a Crown to count it." She thumped the cask-head, comfortable. "Brandy. Real brandy. And us with nothing in the house but salt fish since the spring."

She had carried it up before the officer ever wrote his first line, and she felt no great wrong in it. The headman himself had bid them — quiet, the night the timber came in — *take what the sea gives, and say nothing to the Crown's man of what came up before he was counting.* Dunna had a clear conscience and a full cask. She thought the wreck a gift of the sea, plain and simple. It never crossed her mind that anything but weather had brought the *Marran* to the bar.

## sc-09

Maon Skerry came down to the strand on the second day, slow on the shingle the way the old go slow, and stood a long while reading the wreck the way another man would read a letter.

"Mark where the timber lies," he said. Halsa had come to stand by him; people did, with Maon, to hear what the water told him. "Mark it. The big stuff — the keel-stuff, the heavy spars — it's come up *there*, off the Ness end. Now a ship lost honest on the Sneck in that wind, she fetches up *here*." He pointed a horn-nailed finger down-strand. "The set of that tide, the lie of that bar, she comes up *here*. Always has. I've read fifty wrecks off this bar and they all come up *here*."

"And this one?"

"This one was steering," said the old pilot. "This one had her head laid for a light. Out on the Ness." He was quiet a moment, his eyes on the headland where no light had any business being. "Where no light belongs. There's no mark out there, no leading-light, nothing a master should steer to. And she steered to it." He worked his jaw. "The new buoys took the channel off me, years gone — the Crown's iron buoys, painted and numbered, that any fool can follow without a pilot. Took my living clean away. But I'll tell you what I never lost: I can still read what a light on the Ness would do to a vessel in this channel on a black night. It would draw her. It would draw her right onto the Sneck like a moth to a window."

He did not name a hand. He looked at the wreck, and at the Ness, and at the shut Holm behind them, and he had shown ships off the bar himself in the old days, in the old way, and he held his tongue about whose hand it might have been. "I'll not say a name," he said. "I'll say only: a light was shown that should not have been. The rest is not mine to say."

Halsa heard him. *A ship steered to a wrong light.* She did not yet know whose.

## sc-10

The headman called the Holm together in the chapel, out of the wind, and Orne Veck stood up before them and spoke for the island, as he always had.

"We've a wreck, and we've the Crown on us, and we've the road drowned till the ebb." He let that sit. He chose his words the way a careful man lays stones in a wall, each one bedded. "Now. A wreck on the Sneck is a sorrow and it's an old sorrow and there's no shame in it. Weather and a bad bar take ships; they took ships before any of us, they'll take them after. We give the officer a plain account. A ship lost to the dark. A man drowned, God rest him. Whatever the sea brought up, brought up — and what's ours by the old right is ours, and the Crown need not have the whole of our business." His eye went round them, slow. "We keep the Holm's own counsel. As we always have. The Holm looks to itself first. It must, on a rock like this, or it doesn't last."

It was good sense and it was kindly said and the Holm took it. Orne believed, as he said it, that the boy had found only a drowned body on the bar; he did not know — could not have dreamed — that Wick had taken up off the bar the very lantern Orne himself had carried to the Ness, and hidden it in the straw.

Halsa marked something else. She stood at the back with the tally-book and she watched the headman steer the whole Holm, gently, surely, toward one telling and one only — and she asked herself, for the first time, *why.* Why a man so careful, over a wreck the sea brought in honest.

## sc-11

"Short," said Pell Garrow. "Your count is short, officer, against my bill, and I'll know why."

He had his master's copy of the lading-bill spread on the plank, weighted at the corners with pebbles against the wind, and his finger on the line.

"So many casks of brandy laded at the far port. So many. I can read, and I can count, and I've done both, and what's come ashore is one short of what this bill says was aboard. One cask of brandy, gone." He folded the bill. "Now I'll tell you my mind. The sea doesn't drink brandy. A cask doesn't walk. Someone's lifted it — some islander, down at the ebb before honest folk were up, has had a cask away up the rocks." His mouth twisted. "Or — " and here his eye went to the curing-shed " — or the one man off her that lived. The mate. He'd know what she carried better than any. I'd look hard at that mate before I'd hang it on any of you."

Cass Pellow, by the fire, heard the suspicion fall on the islanders and was well content to let it lie there. Let the factor blame the Holm; let the Holm and the factor wrangle over a cask. It kept every eye off the thing Cass kept hidden against his own body — the dead man's lading-bill — and off the dead man's claim, which Cass meant to make his own.

## sc-12

Halsa went back up to the keeper's room, to the bed, to the one who knew and would not say.

"Bryde. The tally says the light was out at the hour she struck. The pilot says she steered to a false light on the Ness. The boy found something that frightens him witless. And you'll say only that you did not douse it." She sat on the stool by the bed. "Then tell me this, and I'll press no further. When you climbed the stair — was the light already out? Or did it fail under your hand?"

Bryde looked at the ceiling a long time.

"Already out," Bryde said. "When I started up, it was already gone. That's God's truth. The lamp doesn't fail like that — not all at once, not on a full reservoir, which it had, I'd filled it myself at sundown. A lamp dies slow, gutters, smokes, you smell it. This went out *at once*. Like a hand on it." Bryde's jaw worked. "A hand doused that light. I climbed to relight what a hand put out. I never saw the hand — it was black as a bag up there, and I was down off the stair before I'd got to the lamp at all." A bitter breath. "Whose hand, I cannot tell you. I can tell you only that it was a hand, and not the weather."

Halsa went down the stair slowly. She believed him now. The dark light was no accident. And the wreck on the bar and the doused light over it were not two matters. They were one.

## sc-13

She found the boy in the net-loft, in the brown loft-dark with the smell of tar and old fish, sitting on the straw with his knees up and his face in them, and Senna beside him with a hand on his back.

"Wick," Halsa said, and sat down on the straw across from him, slow, the way you'd come up on a hurt thing. "No one's going to harm you. I only want the true telling. Not three. One. The true one. What did you find on the bar?"

The boy shook, and would not look up, and at last Senna spoke low into his ear — "Tell her, Wick. She'll not let them rope you. Tell her" — and the boy lifted his face.

"A lantern," Wick said, and this time it held; this time it was the truth and it stayed. "A lantern, up high on the bar, dry as a bone. Not wet at all. Shuttered up. I never found no man — I said a man because — because a lantern's worse, somehow, a dry lantern up there's worse than a man, and I didn't — " He swallowed. "I hid it. Under the straw. I hid it because I was feared, and I been feared ever since."

"Where in the straw," Halsa said gently.

"There." Senna pointed, plain and steady, where Wick could not. "Under there. By the third post. I'll show you. He'd not have hid it if he'd not known it were a wrong thing to find."

## sc-14

Halsa dug it out of the straw with her own hands, and held it up to the loft's one grey window, and went cold.

It was a lantern, right enough. But it was not ship's gear. She knew Holm-work when she held it — she had grown up with it — the local make, the way the Holm smith turned a handle, the cut of the horn panes. This was off the island. And it was dry, dry through, with a crust of burnt oil black on the wick and the reservoir near empty: it had been *lit*, and lit long, and burned till the oil was near gone, and then shuttered. A lantern off the Holm, carried out and burned dry and shuttered and left high on the bar where no wave had touched it.

She stood in the loft with it in her two hands and the whole shape of the thing came up out of the dark at her at once.

A Holm lantern, burned out on the bar. The Holm light doused by a hand at the very hour. A false light on the Ness, the pilot said, where no light belongs. And down on the shore the factor's count one cask short — the Holm itself, then, had taken from the wreck; Dunna's cask, or another's, it came to the same: *the Holm had its hand in the sea's pocket.*

It was not weather. It was not an honest loss. Someone had shown a false light off the Ness to draw the *Marran* onto the bar, and doused the true light so she'd not be saved, and the starving Holm had gone down at the ebb to take what the lie washed up. One purpose. One hand steering it. She did not yet know the hand. But she knew, standing in the loft with the dry lantern, that there had been a hand.

## sc-15

The ebb was making. Halsa could see it from the loft window: the grey water drawing off the Drang, the first black spine of the shingle road showing through, and beyond it, far out, the topsail of the Crown cutter standing in toward the Sneck with the falling tide. By the next low water the Drang would be bare, and the cutter would be over it, and the Holm would be open to the law again.

And she had it all in her two hands, near enough. The false light. The doused light. The short cask. The lantern. She knew the *Marran* had been made to wreck, and the Holm had drunk of it. She did not yet know — she had no way yet to know — that the supercargo had not simply drowned; that a hand had held him under in the surf to take what he carried. That was hidden still, deeper than the rest.

But she knew enough to be made to choose.

She could carry it to the officer — the lantern, the tally, all of it — and let the Crown's law come down on the Holm. She could bury it, give the officer the plain wreck the headman wanted, and keep the island whole and keep her own hands dirty forever. Or she could call the Holm itself down to the shore and make it name its own wrong, out loud, before the law could.

Three roads, and the water falling on all of them.

—

Orne Veck, too, watched the Drang begin to bare, from the chapel wall where he'd stood the wreck-night. And Orne was not weighing his soul. Orne was reckoning the tide. The cutter was coming; the count would be made; and somewhere on his island was a lantern he had thought safe under the sea and was not. He meant to have it back before the Crown could lay a hand on it, and the cargo away, and the whole made thing buried under the plain face of an honest wreck. He had time, he judged, if he moved. The ebb was his clock now, the same as hers.

## sc-28

Halsa called the Holm to the shore.

She did it herself, going hut to hut at the ebb while the Drang bared, the lantern wrapped under her arm — *down to the bar, all of you, before the cutter's over the road* — and they came, the whole shut island down onto the bared shingle in the grey low-water light: the salt-wife and the curer and the factor's man, the boy and the girl, the passenger with her chest, the men, and last and unhurried the headman, who came because a headman cannot let the Holm gather without him at the head of it.

"I'll not carry this to the Crown behind your backs," Halsa told them, standing where the whole island could see her, the bar wide and wet around them and the cutter's topsail growing off the Sneck. "And I'll not bury it under a salt-house floor either. Whatever was done the wreck-night, it was done by the Holm and to the Holm, and the Holm will hear it named — by us, here, before the law comes and names it for us. I'd rather we judged ourselves than have the Crown do it over our heads."

Orne Veck came down the shingle to the front of them, and he came to put it down — to take the gathering in hand the way he took everything in hand, to say *the girl's overwrought, it was weather, go home.* He did not know that the thing wrapped under her arm was the very lantern he had carried to the Ness. He thought it talk. He came to quiet talk.

But Maon Skerry climbed up onto the high shoulder of the bar beside Halsa, the old pilot, and turned to the gathered Holm, and the talk went out of them.

"You'll hear me," Maon said, "for there's not a soul here knows this water as I do, and I'll tell you plain what I told the keeper's girl. A light on the Ness draws a ship onto the Sneck. There's no mark out there — none — and a master in the dark takes a light for a leading-mark and lays his head for it and she's on the bar before he knows the lie of it." He let his old eyes go round them all. "The *Marran* steered to a light on the Ness. I read it in the lie of her timber. A light was shown out there the wreck-night. That much the water itself will tell you, and I'm only the one left who can read it."

## sc-29

Then Halsa held up the lantern.

She unwound the sacking and held it up high over her head where the whole Holm could see it against the grey morning — the dry Holm-work lantern, the horn panes, the black-crusted wick — and her voice carried flat and clear over the bar.

"This is the false light," she said. "It is not off any ship. It is *Holm-work* — every one of you knows our own smith's hand when you see it. It was burned dry and carried out and left high on the bar where no wave touched it. This is the light that was shown from the Ness to bring the *Marran* onto the Sneck. This. From this island. By one of us."

The Holm went very still on the wet shingle.

"I found it." It was the boy. Wick stepped out from the crowd, white and shaking but on his own two feet, and he said it to the whole island's face, the thing he had hidden a whole week and told three ways. "I found it on the bar at first light, dry, shuttered. I hid it in the net-loft straw because I was feared. I told it a man, I told it a cask — it was *that*. That lantern. I found it and now I've said it true and I'll not say it any way but true again." And Senna came out and stood at his shoulder, close, so that he should not stand and say it alone, the girl's chin up and her hand at his back.

Every eye went, then, to the headman. There was nowhere else for them to go. A Holm-work lantern, a false light off the Ness, the doused light, the whole island's gaze — and Orne Veck stood on the shingle with the proof of it held up over his head before the people he had led his whole life, and he saw that there was no wall left to lay a stone in.

He was a long time silent. The water ran in the runnels of the bar.

"Aye," Orne said at last. His voice was not the chapel voice now; it was older and lower and it had the sea in it. "I carried it. I lit it on the Ness and I doused our own light so she'd not be answered, and I drew her onto the Sneck." He looked not at Halsa but at the Holm, at the salt-wife and the curer and the boy. "We were *starving.* Another winter like the last and there'd not have been a Holm to keep a light at all. I did it for the island — to bring the island what the sea wouldn't give it honest." His jaw set. "I'll not say it was right. It was never right. But I'll say to your faces it was *mine*, and done for you, and I'll stand to it. I doused the light. I showed the false one. The *Marran*'s on the bar because of my hand and no weather." He had carried that secret meaning to take it to his grave, and he laid it down on the shingle before the whole Holm at last.

## sc-30

But it was not the whole of it, and Ysolt Marran knew it was not.

She came forward onto the bar with her chest — the chest she had slept across, that she had let no soul near — and she set it down on the wet shingle in front of them all, and she took a key from a cord at her own throat (a second key, cut against the loss of the first, the prudence of a careful woman) and she opened it before the Holm, the thing she had guarded with her life.

"There is no coin in it," she said. "No plate. No treasure worth a wrecking." She drew out the papers and held them up as Halsa had held the lantern. "A deed. A contested deed, and the papers of my inheritance, that I sailed to lodge ashore and make law. And *this* — " a second paper " — the deed they'd got up against me, that would break my claim." Her voice did not shake; it had gone past shaking. "The man who carried that paper against me, and carried the key to this chest — he was my own kin. The supercargo. The man your mate says the sea took out of his hand." She turned and looked full at Cass Pellow. "A man does not get a paper off a drowning kinsman's coat by *grieving* him. Where is the key, mate? Where is the lading-bill? You went into the water with him and you came out with what he carried."

And Halsa, the lantern still in her hand, the deed now before her eyes, saw the bottom of the whole black thing at last.

"The supercargo did not drown," she said, slow, turning to face the mate before the Holm. "Not honest. You held him under. In the surf, in the dark — you held the man under till he was dead and took the bill off his body, for what it was worth to you. That's why you alone came living off her. You made sure of it." She had not known it an hour since. She knew it now, with the deed in front of her and the mate's face going to pieces in front of the whole island.

Cass Pellow looked at the deed, and at the lantern, and at the ring of the Holm closed round him on the bar with the tide on the make and nowhere to run, and the survivor's grief he had worn all week fell off him like a wet coat.

"He'd have had it all," the mate said, hoarse. "The cargo, the claim, every cask — the supercargo'd have walked off with the lot and left me nothing, the wreck's only living officer and *nothing.* So I — aye." He could not look at the woman. "Aye. I held him under. In the surf. He went quiet and I took the bill off him and let the sea have the rest of him, and I told you he slipped my hand. He slipped nothing. I drowned him for the paper." It came out of him all at once and ugly, the truth he'd never meant to say, wrung out of him on the bar before them all.

Pell Garrow, who had stood there counting casks a week, heard the mate own a murder and felt the ground of his own grievance fall away.

"A *killing*," the factor said, low, into the silence. "I came onto this rock to chase one short cask of brandy. One cask. And I'd have hanged this man for *that.*" He looked at the mate with something like horror. "My short cask. God forgive me, it's the least wrong done on this shore. The very least of it."

## sc-31

So the reckoning was held on the bar, with the tide making and the cutter standing in, the Holm judging its own before the law could.

Dunna Quick went up the rocks to her own salt-house and came back down the shingle with the short cask on her shoulder, breathing hard, and she set it down among the recovered casks of the wreck with her own two hands, in front of the factor, in front of them all.

"There," she said, and stood back from it, red-faced. "I took it. First down at the ebb, like always, and the headman bid us take what the sea gave, and I thought it the sea's plain gift — I never knew of any light, I'll swear that to God and the Crown both. But there it is, set back, the count's whole by my own hand." She wiped her palms on her apron. "I'll not have it said the salt-wife hid a cask while the headman owned a wreck. There's my cask. Count it."

And Halsa held the reckoning over them, the lantern in her hand, the deed beside her, the mate and the headman both owned before the island.

"This is ours to have named, and we've named it," she said. "The headman's wreck and the mate's killing, owned on the Holm's own shore, by the Holm's own people, before ever the Crown set foot on the bar. We did it. Not the cutter. Us." Her voice was steady but it cost her. "Whatever the law does now, it does to men this island has already judged."

Maon Skerry stood cleared at last, the old pilot, the false light named and owned to another hand than his.

"Forty year I've held my tongue about the old trade," he said, "shown ships off this bar myself in the bad days and never answered for it, and I thought I'd carry the knowing of what a Ness light does into the ground with me. But it's named now, and owned, and it wasn't my hand this time — and I'll say it before you all, glad as I've been of anything: my hands are clean of the *Marran*, and the silence is done." There was a kind of ease in the old man, a weight set down.

And they carried Bryde down to the shore on a hurdle — the keeper would be carried down, broke leg and all, to be there when it was named — and Bryde lifted up on one elbow and told the Holm the last small true thing.

"I climbed the stair to relight what his hand put out," Bryde said, the voice thin but clear, nodding at the headman. "The light was dark when I started up, and the stair was wet, and I went down on the fourth turn and broke this leg of mine. So you'll know — the doused light didn't only drown a ship. It broke your own keeper, climbing in the dark to set right what Orne Veck put wrong. That's in the tally true, every word, and now it's said out loud as well."

## sc-32

The officer came down to the shore last of all, drawn by the whole island gone to the bar, and found it already done.

He stood at the edge of the gathering with his Crown ledger forgotten under his arm and listened to the end of it — the headman owned, the mate owned, the cask set back, the deed shown, the keeper's word — and he understood that the inquiry he had thought to open had been held without him, by the people who were its subject, on the bar, at the ebb.

"The Holm has judged its own," he said, slowly, "before I could. Well. There's the headman owns a made wreck, and the mate owns a killing, and a count made whole by the hand that broke it." He looked at Halsa. "I'll take the two of them for the cutter, and the truth goes with them — but the truth was told here first, by you, and I'll set that down. The island named it. That's not nothing. That's not nothing at all."

And Halsa answered the cutter for the whole Holm. When it came over the bared Drang she met it at the tide-line with the officer beside her, and she gave up the mate for the supercargo's death and the headman for the made wreck — but she gave them up with the telling already told, the island's own, not wrung out of a frightened people by the Crown.

Ysolt Marran went down to the boat with her deed shown and her chest open at last, her claim spoken before witnesses and her kin's death named on the bar where it happened.

"On the mainland they'd have wrung this out of you in a Crown court over months," she said to Halsa, gathering her papers, "and called it justice. You did it in a morning on a beach, and called it the Holm looking to itself. I came off that wreck thinking I'd landed among thieves." She inclined her head, the mainland nicety, but it meant something now. "I was wrong. I'll lodge my deed and I'll not forget where it was first heard."

Pell Garrow went to the same boat with his count whole and a thing he had not had when he came.

"Made whole by the very woman who lifted it," he said, looking at the cask set back among the rest. "And a wrecker and a murderer owned to my face before I could lay a wrong charge. I came for brandy and I'm leaving with — " he shook his head " — with a respect I didn't think to find on a starving rock. The island judged itself. I've not seen the like, mainland or island. I'll tell my master the count came right, and I'll tell him *how*, and he'll not believe the how."

Eda the curer stood among her spoiled fish with her brine-red hands and did not, this time, call it the sea's honest work.

"I salted fish through a dozen wrecks on the Sneck and called every one of them the weather," she said, low, to no one. "Honest losses, all. And this one I'd have called the same — would have, to my dying day." She watched the cutter take the headman. "And it was Orne. Orne, that I've known forty year. I'll never again look at a wreck on this bar and call it the sea's plain work without I think of this morning. The Holm named its own. God help us, but it named its own." And she crossed herself, and did not call it accident, then or ever after.

Wick kept the bar with Senna while the cutter pulled away.

The boy who had told it three ways stood at the high shoulder of the bar where he'd first found the lantern, and Senna beside him, and the two of them watched the boat carry the headman and the mate out over the Drang into the grey. He did not hide his face this time. He had told it true at the last, before the whole Holm, and the stone was out of him.

"Is it right, what we did?" he asked Senna, watching the cutter go.

"It's *true,*" Senna said. "That's better than right. Right you can argue. True you can't." And she kept the bar with him while the tide came back.

And Halsa stood at the head of the strand and watched the cutter take the Holm's headman and the *Marran*'s mate away over the road, and behind her the island was whole — shut, and shamed, and grieving its headman, but whole, and owned, and not lying. She had kept the Holm together not by hiding its wrong nor by handing it meekly to the Crown, but by making it stand on its own shore and name what it had done, out loud, before the law could come and name it for them. The light burned true that evening on its stair, the tide-bell rang the turn, and the Holm lived on — having judged its own, before the law could, on the bar at the ebb.
