# A/B packet — four scenes, two versions each

## Scene 1 (sc-16)

**Version 1:**

Halsa carried it to the officer.

She did it at the turn, while the Drang was still half under and the cutter still standing off, so that there was no leaving it half-done — once said, it could not be unsaid. She found him at his plank-table with his Crown ledger and she set the dry Holm lantern down on it, on top of his neat columns, and she set the keeper's tally beside it open to the last line.

"That lantern is Holm-work," she said. "It is not off the ship. The boy found it dry, high on the bar, where no sea reached. It was carried there and burned there. And this — " her finger on the tally " — is the keeper's own hand. *Light out.* And the hour beside it is the hour the *Marran* struck. Our light was put out by a hand, the night she wrecked. And the old pilot will tell you a ship steered to a false light on the Ness, where no light should ever be." She kept her voice level, though her heart went like the bell that hadn't rung. "I think someone showed a false light off the Ness to bring her onto the Sneck, and doused our true light so she'd not be saved. I think the wreck was made."

The officer did not touch the lantern at once. He looked at it a long moment, and at her, and at the shut island around them.

"You understand what you're telling me," he said. "You're telling me this wasn't weather. You're telling me a wreck was made — and that's not a short cask and a fine. That's a hanging matter, and it's not one stranger's neck, it's the Holm's." He drew the ledger toward him and turned to a clean page. "If you're right, I'll find it in the open, before the cutter, with everyone still shut on this rock and unable to get clear. If you're wrong, you've put a rope-shadow over your own folk for nothing." He picked up the pencil. "Are you sure?"

"No," Halsa said. "But I'm sure enough to bring it to you, and let you be the one to find out. I'll not be the Holm's law. I'll only not be its lie."

"Then I'll open it," the officer said, and wrote at the head of the clean page, and the inquiry was begun.

She found him at his plank-table with his Crown ledger and she set the dry Holm lantern down on it, on top of his neat columns, and she set the keeper's tally beside it open to the last line.

Her finger on the tally. "*Light out.* And the hour beside it is the hour the *Marran* struck." She had meant, walking down, to say the whole of it. At the table she found she could not lay it on him in words, and so she laid the things instead, and let them lie there between them, and waited.

The officer did not touch the lantern. He looked at it where it sat on his columns, and at the hour on the tally, and he was a while looking. He knew what a ship's lantern was. This was not one.

"A dry lantern," he said. "High on a bar, in a gale that planked the whole strand with timber. And our light dark at the very hour she struck." He turned the tally a quarter-turn toward himself with one finger, not lifting it, and watched her over it. "I could write that as a thing the boy mislaid and a lamp that failed in a hard blow. I've written stranger and slept sound. Is that what you fetched it to me to write?"

"No," Halsa said.

"Then you'd best say what you fetched it for." But he said it low, and did not reach for the pencil, and she understood he was not pressing her so much as leaving her the room — and the rope — to be the one who said it.

She would not be drawn the whole way, and neither, she saw, would he. "I'll say what's in front of you. A light put out by a hand. A light shown where no light belongs — ask the old pilot what a light on the Ness does to a ship in this channel. I'll say the wreck was *made*." She stopped. "I'll not say by whom. I don't know it sure, and I'll not be the one to fit a name to a rope."

"No," the officer agreed, very quiet, and there it was at last, the word neither of them had used, standing in the *rope* she'd said and the *name* she wouldn't. "You'll leave the fitting to me." He looked round the shut island, the topsail growing off the Sneck. "You know what you've handed me. It's not a short cask, and it's not a fine." He let her measure it, and would not name the size of it either, the size being the same in both their mouths and wanting no saying. "And you've handed it me with every soul still on this rock and none able to get clear. Once I open it, it opens on whoever it opens on. There's no half of this."

"I know what I've handed you."

"Then I'll ask you the once." He picked up the pencil, and held it. "Are you sure enough to have me open a thing that won't shut again?"

"No. I'm only past being able to carry it shut." She put her hand flat on the tally. "I'll not be the Holm's law. I'll only not be its lie. What you do with it is the Crown's, not mine."

The officer looked at her a moment longer than was comfortable, and seemed to find in her face the thing he'd been testing for, that was not spite and was not fear. Then he drew the clean page to him and wrote at the head of it, and the inquiry was begun.

## Scene 2 (sc-29)

**Version 1:**

Then Halsa held up the lantern.

She unwound the sacking and held it up high over her head where the whole Holm could see it against the grey morning — the dry Holm-work lantern, the horn panes, the black-crusted wick — and her voice carried flat and clear over the bar.

"This is the false light," she said. "It is not off any ship. It is *Holm-work* — every one of you knows our own smith's hand when you see it. It was burned dry and carried out and left high on the bar where no wave touched it. This is the light that was shown from the Ness to bring the *Marran* onto the Sneck. This. From this island. By one of us."

The Holm went very still on the wet shingle.

"I found it." It was the boy. Wick stepped out from the crowd, white and shaking but on his own two feet, and he said it to the whole island's face, the thing he had hidden a whole week and told three ways. "I found it on the bar at first light, dry, shuttered. I hid it in the net-loft straw because I was feared. I told it a man, I told it a cask — it was *that*. That lantern. I found it and now I've said it true and I'll not say it any way but true again." And Senna came out and stood at his shoulder, close, so that he should not stand and say it alone, the girl's chin up and her hand at his back.

Every eye went, then, to the headman. There was nowhere else for them to go. A Holm-work lantern, a false light off the Ness, the doused light, the whole island's gaze — and Orne Veck stood on the shingle with the proof of it held up over his head before the people he had led his whole life, and he saw that there was no wall left to lay a stone in.

He was a long time silent. The water ran in the runnels of the bar.

"Aye," Orne said at last. His voice was not the chapel voice now; it was older and lower and it had the sea in it. "I carried it. I lit it on the Ness and I doused our own light so she'd not be answered, and I drew her onto the Sneck." He looked not at Halsa but at the Holm, at the salt-wife and the curer and the boy. "We were *starving.* Another winter like the last and there'd not have been a Holm to keep a light at all. I did it for the island — to bring the island what the sea wouldn't give it honest." His jaw set. "I'll not say it was right. It was never right. But I'll say to your faces it was *mine*, and done for you, and I'll stand to it. I doused the light. I showed the false one. The *Marran*'s on the bar because of my hand and no weather." He had carried that secret meaning to take it to his grave, and he laid it down on the shingle before the whole Holm at last.

Orne Veck came down the front of them as he always came down, to take it in hand, to lay the careful stone — and his eye fell on the thing held up over the boy's head, and the stone would not lay. He looked at the lantern. He looked at it the way you look at a face you knew dead and find living. His mouth opened on the chapel voice and the chapel voice was not there.

"Put it down," he said. Not to Halsa. To the lantern, almost — low, rough, an old man's voice with the sea in it and none of the wall-builder left. "Put it — " He stopped. He looked at Eda, who had known him forty year, and could not hold her eye, and looked instead at the wet shingle between his own feet. "Aye. Aye, it's mine. I'll not stand here and let the boy carry it. It's mine."

No one spoke. The water ran in the runnels of the bar.

"We were starving." It came out of him small, not pitched to the gathering at all, pitched to the woman nearest him as if he were explaining a thing across a hearth. "Eda. We were *starving*. The spring catch failed and the autumn one after it, and there was salt fish and salt fish and then there was the half of that, and I sat in that chapel and counted the winter and the winter didn't *count*." His hands worked at nothing. "So I — there was a ship in the offing, and a black night coming, and I knew what a light on the Ness does, every Holm man past sixty knows it — I carried it out. I lit it on the Ness. I doused our own so she'd not be answered. And I came back to the chapel wall and I stood and I let the sea do the rest of it." His voice went. "I told myself it was the island. I told myself I never laid a hand on a man, only on a lamp — "

"Orne," Eda said, just his name, and it stopped him worse than a shout.

"It was never right," he said, to the shingle, to her shoes. "I knew it stood up dark the whole night through. I'm not — I'll not tell you it was right. I'll tell you it was mine, and I did it for the lot of you, and the doing of it for you doesn't make it any less the thing it is." He lifted his head at last, but not to make a speech of it; he looked from one of them to the next, the boy, the salt-wife, the curer, the way a man looks who is counting what he has cost. "There. It's said. The light was mine. The *Marran*'s mine. Do with me what you'll do."

## Scene 3 (sc-30)

**Version 1:**

But it was not the whole of it, and Ysolt Marran knew it was not.

She came forward onto the bar with her chest — the chest she had slept across, that she had let no soul near — and she set it down on the wet shingle in front of them all, and she took a key from a cord at her own throat (a second key, cut against the loss of the first, the prudence of a careful woman) and she opened it before the Holm, the thing she had guarded with her life.

"There is no coin in it," she said. "No plate. No treasure worth a wrecking." She drew out the papers and held them up as Halsa had held the lantern. "A deed. A contested deed, and the papers of my inheritance, that I sailed to lodge ashore and make law. And *this* — " a second paper " — the deed they'd got up against me, that would break my claim." Her voice did not shake; it had gone past shaking. "The man who carried that paper against me, and carried the key to this chest — he was my own kin. The supercargo. The man your mate says the sea took out of his hand." She turned and looked full at Cass Pellow. "A man does not get a paper off a drowning kinsman's coat by *grieving* him. Where is the key, mate? Where is the lading-bill? You went into the water with him and you came out with what he carried."

And Halsa, the lantern still in her hand, the deed now before her eyes, saw the bottom of the whole black thing at last.

"The supercargo did not drown," she said, slow, turning to face the mate before the Holm. "Not honest. You held him under. In the surf, in the dark — you held the man under till he was dead and took the bill off his body, for what it was worth to you. That's why you alone came living off her. You made sure of it." She had not known it an hour since. She knew it now, with the deed in front of her and the mate's face going to pieces in front of the whole island.

Cass Pellow looked at the deed, and at the lantern, and at the ring of the Holm closed round him on the bar with the tide on the make and nowhere to run, and the survivor's grief he had worn all week fell off him like a wet coat.

"He'd have had it all," the mate said, hoarse. "The cargo, the claim, every cask — the supercargo'd have walked off with the lot and left me nothing, the wreck's only living officer and *nothing.* So I — aye." He could not look at the woman. "Aye. I held him under. In the surf. He went quiet and I took the bill off him and let the sea have the rest of him, and I told you he slipped my hand. He slipped nothing. I drowned him for the paper." It came out of him all at once and ugly, the truth he'd never meant to say, wrung out of him on the bar before them all.

Pell Garrow, who had stood there counting casks a week, heard the mate own a murder and felt the ground of his own grievance fall away.

"A *killing*," the factor said, low, into the silence. "I came onto this rock to chase one short cask of brandy. One cask. And I'd have hanged this man for *that.*" He looked at the mate with something like horror. "My short cask. God forgive me, it's the least wrong done on this shore. The very least of it."

## Scene 4 (sc-27)

**Version 1:**

The cutter gone, the Drang drowned again behind it, the Holm settled back into its shut life — but it was not the same Holm.

Orne Veck ruled it as before. He stood up in the chapel and spoke for the island as he always had, and the island did as he said, as it always had. But it was different now and they both knew it — he and the Holm — for the whole hamlet held his secret over him, the way you hold a knife you've agreed not to use. He had bought the island's life with a lie and the island had paid in silence, and now every soul on it knew what the headman was, and the headman knew they knew, and he ruled a place that would never again be quite his.

Wick grew quiet. The boy who had told it three ways on the shore, all fear and flutter, went still and apart. He did not run with the other children the way he had. He kept to the high rocks and the net-loft, and the lantern's truth sat in him like a stone in a fish's belly, and slowly it changed the shape of him, and he was no longer the boy who could not keep one telling in his mouth — he was a boy who kept one telling, the false one, and would keep it all his life.

Ysolt Marran did not leave. The cutter went and she stayed — her chest unopened, her claim unsettled, her deed unread and unlodged — bound to the Holm by the very bargain that had saved her lock. She had her chest and no way to make it law, and a kinsman dead and unavenged, and an island full of people who knew her for the mainland woman who'd traded silence for secrecy. She walked the strand in the evenings, a stranger settled among strangers, and was as shut in as the Holm itself.

Eda the curer noticed only that the Holm had gone quiet.

"It's a quiet island this year," she said to Halsa, salting the last of the spring's catch, honestly puzzled. "Since the wreck. Folk don't talk the way they did. You'd think they'd lost their own off the *Marran*, the way they go about — and it was strangers drowned, poor souls, an honest weather-loss like a dozen I've salted fish through." She shook her head, knowing nothing, believing it the sea's plain work to the last. "Strange, how a wreck of strangers can hush a whole island. The Sneck takes more than ships, I think."

And Bryde Crewe, mending slowly, the leg knitting crooked, kept a tally of the truth in secret.

"I'll not say it out," Bryde told Halsa, low, from the bed, "since you've chosen and I'll not undo your choosing. But I'll write it down, every line of it true — the light doused by a hand, the hour, the false light the pilot read, the lantern the boy found — and I'll keep it where no Crown finds it, against the day the Holm should *need* the truth and not the silence. A keeper keeps the true account, Halsa. Even the account no one's to read. *Especially* that one." And the keeper wrote it, in the square careful hand, and hid the true tally away.

Halsa kept the Holm whole and the light true. She filled the great lamp at sundown and set it burning steady and yellow over the Sneck, the honest light over the bar where the false one had drawn the *Marran* in, and the tide-bell rang the turn of the tide as it always had. The island lasted. The children would have children. And Halsa carried the wreck-night's silence in her like the keeper's hidden tally, the price she had paid for the life of the only place she knew — a whole island bought, and one drowned stranger's truth the coin, and her own peace gone forever into the cold sand under the salt-house floor.

**Version 2:**

The cutter gone, the Drang drowned again behind it, the Holm settled back into its shut life — and that first evening with the road shut and no Crown on the rock, they gathered at the curing-shed fire the way the Holm gathered after any hard sea, because that was the shape grief took here whether it was named or not.

Orne Veck sat in the headman's place. No one had taken it from him. But he sat in it quiet, and took the cup Eda passed him with a nod and did not speak for the room the way he once would have, and the room felt the difference and said nothing of it.

Dunna Quick talked, because the silence frightened her. "A mercy the road's shut again," she said, to the fire. "A body can breathe. All that counting, those Crown coats — and the count came right in the end, the factor off content, no harm to a soul of us. The sea gives and the sea takes, and there's an end." No one took it up. She heard the no-one, and her words ran down, and she looked at the gap behind the salt-sacks she had filled with nothing, and went quiet too.

Eda salted on, her hands red, knowing the least of them all and so the only one easy. "Hard week. Poor souls under all that timber, and not one of ours among them — that's the grace in it. Strangers drowned, God keep them, and the Holm whole." She crossed herself. "We've buried our own off this bar before now. To lose only strangers is light, as the Sneck goes." And because she believed every word of it, hers were the only words in the shed without a second floor under them.

Ysolt Marran sat apart, the mainland woman who had not gone home, where the firelight reached her and the cold did not. She held her cup and did not drink, and when Eda spoke of strangers drowned and a grace in it, her eyes went once to the fire and stayed. She said nothing. She had made a bargain of saying nothing, and she kept it, and the keeping sat on her like the chest she still slept across.

Bryde Crewe had been carried down, the bad leg out on a stool, and watched the headman in his place and kept the true account behind a still face — as it would be kept now, written and hidden, against a day that might never come.

Senna sat against the wall with Wick beside her, outside the ring of grown talk, her shoulder against his. Neither had said much all evening. It was Wick the silence had changed the most, and the others, glancing at him, saw it and looked away — the chattering boy gone quiet.

"It gets smaller," Senna said to him, low, under the grown voices. "The knowing. It's big now. It gets smaller the longer you don't say it. Like a stone in the sea — it stays, but it wears."

Wick looked at the fire a long while. Then he said his one thing, low, only to her: "I keep feared I'll forget which telling's the true one." He turned his cup in his hands the way the grown ones did. "And then I think — maybe that's the mending of it. When I can't mind anymore which one was true." Senna put her arm through his and said nothing, and held the silence with him, the two of them keeping it at the cold edge of the warm room.

And over them all the fire settled and the talk thinned to nothing and the Holm sat in its own quiet — a quiet that was not peace and was not yet grief, a held-breath quiet, every soul in the shed carrying the same unsaid weight and not one of them setting it down. Outside, the great lamp burned true and yellow over the Sneck, the honest light over the bar, and the tide-bell rang the turn as it always had, and the Holm — whole, and shut, and lying still — went on into the dark with its silence kept.
