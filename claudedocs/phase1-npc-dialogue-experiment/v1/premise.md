# Premise — "Wreckfall at Cawdy Holm"

This is the ONLY story input you are given. Everything else — the events, each
person's secrets, what is really happening, the scene sequence, the branches, the
causality, WHO KNOWS WHAT, and (in the render stage) HOW EACH PERSON SPEAKS — is
yours to invent.

## Setting

Cawdy Holm is a tidal island off a hard coast: a fishing hamlet of a few dozen
souls, joined to the mainland only by the Drang — a cobbled causeway that the sea
covers twice a day and bares twice a day. At spring tides the Drang stays under for
most of two days; nobody crosses, nobody leaves. The Holm lives off the fishing, a
little salt-boiling, and what the sea brings up on the bar — the long shoal called
the Sneck, where ships that mistake the channel go aground.

One night near the spring low, in weather, a coasting vessel struck the Sneck and
broke up in the dark. By the grey morning her timbers and cargo were strewn along
the Holm's shore, and the tide had risen over the Drang again — so the wreck, the
survivors, and the islanders are shut in together until the next long ebb bares the
causeway and the mainland can be reached. A revenue cutter was due at the Holm that
week; she has not yet come, but she will.

## The cast (you may rename, reshape, or add — but keep it BROAD)

A whole shut-in community plus the people the sea threw onto it. These are seeds;
make each a real person with their own knowledge, their own reason to be where they
are, their own stake — and, for the render stage, their own way of speaking. At
minimum these, and add more:

- **Halsa Crewe** — the keeper's child, young, left tending the Holm's light and
  tide-bell while the old keeper lies abed with a broken leg. The point-of-view
  character. Earnest, watchful, not yet sure of their own authority.
- **the old keeper (Halsa's parent)** — abed, injured the night of the wreck; how
  they came to break that leg matters.
- **a revenue riding-officer** — Crown, lodged on the Holm to wait for the cutter,
  with a duty to account for every cask and bale the sea brings up.
- **the wrecked ship's surviving mate** — pulled from the surf, the only crew left.
- **a passenger off the wreck** — a woman travelling with a locked sea-chest and few
  words about what is in it or where she was bound.
- **the Holm's headman / net-master** — speaks for the islanders, decides what the
  Holm admits to.
- **a salt-wife / fisher-widow** — first down to the shore at the ebb; gathers what
  the sea gives.
- **an old pilot** — knows the Sneck and the channel lights better than anyone; once
  guided ships off the bar for a fee.
- **a boy of the Holm** — found the first thing on the shore (a body? a cask? a
  lantern?) and has been telling it differently each time.
- **a mainland factor's man** — stranded on the Holm by the tide, waiting on a cargo
  that was aboard the wreck.
- **a fish-curer / a boat-builder / a girl betrothed to the boy** — fill the hamlet
  out.

## The inciting event

In the dark of the wreck-night, something happened on the Holm that should not have
— and it is NOT simply that a ship was lost. Choose what is really true and make it
true in the facts: was the wreck a true accident of weather, or was the ship lured
onto the Sneck by a false light shown from the shore (wrecking — a hanging matter)?
Did the one dead man drown, or was he helped to drown for what he carried? Is the
cargo the manifest names all ashore, or has some of it already gone quietly up the
Holm? The night is witnessed in fragments — the boy saw one thing, the salt-wife
another, the pilot knows what a light on that headland would do, the mate knows what
orders the dead man gave, the passenger knows what was in the chest. None of them
holds all of it; some of them are lying about their piece.

## The central dramatic question

Halsa, left to keep the Holm, must work out what truly happened on the wreck-night —
from a shut-in houseful of islanders and castaways who each hold a fragment and a
reason to shade it — and decide what to do before the long ebb bares the Drang and
the revenue cutter comes. When the causeway opens, Halsa can:

- **REPORT** — lay what Halsa has come to understand before the riding-officer (and
  so the Crown), and let the law and the revenue take it where they will;
- **SHELTER** — keep the Holm's part of it quiet (to protect the island, or one
  person, or out of honest doubt), and let the wreck stand as the sea's accident;
- **CONFRONT** — settle it on the Holm before the cutter comes, Halsa's own way —
  name the thing to the people who did it, and force a reckoning on the shore.

What is really behind the wreck-night, what each person actually knows and believes,
and where each of these three roads leads — all yours to decide and make true. The
three roads need not lead to the same place, and the Holm and its people must still
be there, living it out, at the end of every road.

## Structural constraints (requirements, not suggestions)

- A BROAD CAST is the essence — many distinct people, each knowing and believing
  different things, several wrong about what the others know. Not one truth and a
  crowd of bystanders.
- Roughly 30–34 scenes. Enough to give a real cast room AND to leave each road a
  real AFTERMATH — but this is NOT a test of length. Keep the spine tight; spend the
  room on the PEOPLE and on what each road's ending costs them.
- EXACTLY ONE primary branch point (the fork: REPORT / SHELTER / CONFRONT) at
  roughly the 40–50% mark — NOT at the very end. Each of the 3 world-lines must then
  run a real TAIL of its own (its aftermath: how that road plays out on the Holm,
  who ends where, what is finally known, done, or lost) before its distinct ending.
  The aftermath is where the choice is paid for.
- THE WHOLE HOLM MUST STILL BE THERE AT EACH ENDING. The islanders and castaways who
  populated the early scenes do not vanish once the principals take over the
  aftermath — the salt-wife, the boy, the pilot, the widow, the factor's man and the
  rest are still present, still knowing their fragment, still acting on each road to
  its close. A road whose final scenes shrink to two or three principals talking has
  let the house empty out; keep the Holm peopled to the last scene of every road.
- At least two genuine SETUPS that PAY OFF later (planted details that return and
  matter — at least one a piece of knowledge only one person held).
- Decide the ground truth, and let several people BELIEVE things that differ from it
  and from each other — each acting on what THEY believe and what THEY have had a
  chance to learn, not on what you the author know.

Tell the best, most coherent shut-in coastal mystery you can. Make every consequence
trace to a placed cause, make each person know exactly their own road through the
wreck-night and the days after — no more, no less — and keep the Holm alive and
peopled all the way to the end of every road.
