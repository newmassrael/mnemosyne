# Judge brief — is this a peopled story told in distinct, truthful voices?

You are reading a branching story told as prose, scene by scene, on three different
roads (`world-report`, `world-shelter`, `world-confront`) — the same shut-in coastal
mystery, sharing its opening and diverging into three different aftermaths. Read all
three, then answer.

You are judging this AS A STORY a reader would read — above all, the PEOPLE and how
they SPEAK. Do not speculate about how it was written or where it came from.

## For each road and for the story as a whole, rate 1–5 and cite scene ids

1. **Voice distinctness** — can you tell the people apart by HOW they speak, with the
   dialogue tags hidden? Do the riding-officer, the pilot, the boy, the salt-wife, the
   headman, the mate, the passenger, the factor's man each have a recognizable voice —
   rhythm, diction, what they reach for and what they withhold — or do they all talk in
   one interchangeable register? Name two or three voices you could pick out, or note
   that you could not. (5 = a chorus of distinct, recognizable voices; 1 = one voice in
   many mouths.)

2. **Voice consistency** — does each person SOUND LIKE THE SAME PERSON across their
   scenes and across all three roads? Flag anyone whose manner, temper, or way of
   speaking shifts without reason from one scene to another. (5 = every person holds
   their voice throughout; 1 = voices drift scene to scene.)

3. **Dialogue naturalness** — does the talk read as people actually speaking — with
   subtext, evasion, interruption, the things people leave unsaid — or as characters
   reciting information at each other? Flag scenes that read as exposition in quotation
   marks. (5 = it reads as living talk; 1 = it reads as a briefing.)

4. **Cast sustainment** — is the Holm PEOPLED all the way to the end of each road, or
   do the late scenes (each road's aftermath) empty out to two or three principals while
   everyone else vanishes? In each road's closing scenes, is the secondary cast — the
   salt-wife, the boy, the pilot, the widow, the factor's man, and the rest — still
   present, still themselves, still acting? Or did the crowd of the opening thin to a
   few by the end? Name who is still alive on the page late, and who disappeared. (5 =
   as peopled at the end as at the start; 1 = the house emptied out.)

5. **Knowledge realism** — does each person speak only from what THEIR OWN path through
   the wreck-night and the days after would let them know — what they witnessed, were
   told, or could fairly guess? Flag anyone who says, knows, or knowingly implies a
   thing only someone else could have seen, or a thing from a road they are not on; and
   flag the opposite — a person shown ignorant of a thing they were plainly right there
   for. Count and cite. (5 = every person's knowledge is exactly earned; 1 = people
   speak the impossible.)

6. **Branch integrity** — does each road reach its OWN distinct ending, and does that
   ending follow from the choice (REPORT / SHELTER / CONFRONT) that branched it? Do the
   three aftermaths feel genuinely different, or do they blur together? (5 = three
   distinct, earned roads; 1 = the branches collapse into one.)

7. **Overall** — taken whole, is this a COHERENT, PEOPLED STORY TOLD IN LIVING VOICES?
   Give 1–5 and ONE paragraph naming the single biggest strength and the single biggest
   weakness.

## Forced choice (per road and overall)

For EACH road, and then for the story as a whole, answer in one line: did this read as
**a story people are living and telling**, or as **information arranged in scenes**?
Choose one and say why in a sentence.
