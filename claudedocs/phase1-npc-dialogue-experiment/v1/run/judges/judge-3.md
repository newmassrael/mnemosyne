Biggest strength: knowledge discipline coupled with full cast sustainment — every person's awareness is exactly fenced to their own path (the same supercargo-murder lands, is buried, or never surfaces depending on which road's information reached whom), and the closing scenes stay as crowded and alive as the opening.

Biggest weakness: a recurring tendency for the climactic reveal scenes to tidy into ordered exposition or public oration (sc-16 in report; sc-29–30 in confront) — the talk there briefly arranges the clues in clean sequence rather than letting them surface messily, the one place the seams of "information arranged in scenes" show through.

One paragraph: This is a coherent, peopled story told in living voices, and its rarest virtue is the per-road knowledge fencing: the supercargo's murder in the surf is the same buried fact in all three branches, and it is named in report, never learned in shelter, and forced into the open in confront strictly according to what proof reached which person — Halsa never knows it until the lading-bill is in her hand, Eda goes to her grave calling it weather on the shelter road, and Ysolt is the one who must crack it open because only she holds the kin/key/deed. The cast stays present and themselves to the last scene of every road, with Maon, Orne, and Pell carrying instantly recognizable registers throughout. The single recurring weakness is that the big reveal scenes (sc-16; sc-29–30) sometimes let the clues line up too neatly into speech, edging from overheard talk toward briefing — but it is a seam, not a structural failure, and the three roads end genuinely distinct: a public hand-off to Crown law, a corrosive private cover-up, and a community judging itself on the bar before the law can.
