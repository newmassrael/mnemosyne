OVERALL: voice-distinct 4/5, voice-consistent 5/5, natural 4/5, sustainment 5/5, knowledge 5/5, branch-integrity 5/5, overall 5/5

Voices you could pick out (2–3, named): Maon Skerry the pilot — gnomic, weather-reading, the channel-as-letter and "Buried's not undone" cadence, with the standing grievance of the buoys that took his living. Dunna Quick the salt-wife — defensive, self-justifying, "what the sea gives the Holm keeps," tumbling into exculpation when cornered ("I'll not swing for the rest of it"). Orne Veck the headman — careful stone-laying chapel-prose that "beds each word like a stone in a wall," which he holds in denial (report/shelter) and then visibly sheds for an older, sea-deep register when he confesses (confront sc-29). Senna the girl is a fourth easy pick — cool, unsentimental, "It's true. That's better than right."

Knowledge breaks: none. Knowledge is scrupulously path-gated throughout: the officer never knows the count is short until shown (sc-05, sc-26); Dunna never suspects more than weather (sc-08, sc-18, sc-25, sc-31); Eda believes weather to the last unless confronted on the bar (sc-20, sc-27, sc-32); the murder is withheld from Halsa until the deed surfaces and is then deduced, never simply known (report sc-17→sc-19, shelter sc-24 left unknown, confront sc-30); Bryde knows only "a hand, not the weather," never the hand (sc-12). The shelter road correctly leaves Halsa permanently ignorant of the murder (sc-24, sc-27), which is the hardest knowledge-discipline of the three and it holds.

Where the cast thinned, if anywhere: nowhere materially. All three closers (report sc-20–sc-21, shelter sc-26–sc-27, confront sc-31–sc-32) deliberately re-poll the secondary cast — pilot, curer, salt-wife, factor, passenger, boy, girl, keeper — and give each a final beat in voice. If forced to name the thinnest, report sc-19 narrows hard to Cass/Wick/Senna for the chase, but sc-20–sc-21 immediately re-people the close.

Biggest strength: The cast is sustained and individuated all the way to each road's last scene — the late aftermaths re-poll the whole island rather than collapsing to two principals, and every person speaks from exactly what their own path would let them know, including the genuinely difficult choice to keep Halsa ignorant of the murder on the shelter road.

Biggest weakness: A recurring writerly polish in the climactic dialogue — the aphoristic, reader-facing line ("It's true. That's better than right," "Buried's not undone," "That's not nothing at all," the officer's stakes-speech in report sc-16) — which is admirable but occasionally tips living talk toward staged epigram, most in the public set-pieces of confront (sc-28–sc-32) and the bargains of shelter (sc-24).

One paragraph: This is a coherent, peopled story told in living voices, and it earns a high mark on the terms the brief sets. The shared trunk (sc-01–sc-15) builds a genuine ensemble — pilot, keeper, headman, salt-wife, boy, girl, mate, passenger, factor, curer, officer — each with a recognizable rhythm and a private interest, and the three aftermaths neither blur nor empty out: report hands the Holm to the law, shelter buries the truth at the cost of everyone's silence, confront makes the island indict itself, and these are three materially different moral worlds reached from the same choice-point, each keeping its secondary cast alive and acting to the final scene. Knowledge is the standout discipline — no one speaks across a road they are not on, the murder is consistently withheld and then deduced rather than known, and the shelter road's permanent ignorance is held with real nerve. The single recurring weakness is that the most quotable lines are sometimes too quotable: in the climaxes, especially confront's public reckoning, a few characters orate truths at the reader where people would evade, trail off, or leave things unsaid — the craft is high enough that its own epigrammatic gift is the thing that, now and then, breaks the spell of overheard rather than authored talk.
