# Premise — The Backfire at Mara Ridge

A fresh, original premise. Invent everything else — names, details, the exact beats.

## Setting

Mara Ridge stands above the town of Coldwater Bend. It is the night a wildfire, two
weeks burning, finally crests the ridge. The fire will be driven down onto the town
at dawn, when the valley wind reverses and pushes downslope — the **turn of the
wind** is a hard, known deadline every old hand in town can feel coming. There is
only one play before it: starve the fire at the ridge by **cutting a firebreak along
the saddle** and **lighting a controlled backfire** to burn the fuel out ahead of
the main fire, so the two fires meet at the cut line and die for want of anything
left to burn. If it works, the town wakes to ash on the ridge and nothing more. If
it fails, Coldwater Bend is gone by full light.

## The two crews (invent the rest)

Through this one night, two crews work the ridge AT THE SAME TIME:

- **The cut crew** — under **Bo Aldercott**, the town's road-warden — saws and clears
  the firebreak line along the saddle, working east to west.
- **The burn crew** — under **Wrenna Syle**, who has set backfires before and is the
  only one who reads fire well enough to walk one down a slope — lights the backfire
  high and walks its edge down toward the cut, so it arrives at the saddle as the
  break is finished.

Both crews are on the ridge through the same hours. Neither can do the other's job.
The town's survival needs BOTH done, and done in a way that fits together, before the
wind turns.

## What makes the night nearly impossible (the heart of the story)

Three hard truths bind the two crews together through the night — they are not two
separate jobs, they are one problem two crews are inside of at once:

1. **The backfire cannot reach the saddle before the cut does.** Wrenna's fire and
   Bo's cut are walking toward the same place from two directions. If the backfire
   arrives at the saddle while the break is still open, it does not stop — it crosses
   the unfinished line and runs down into Coldwater Bend itself. So how fast Wrenna
   may walk her fire depends, minute by minute, on how far Bo's cut has actually got.
   Neither can see the other in the dark and smoke; they work on trust and on the few
   runners who cross between them.
2. **There is one water cart.** A single mule-drawn bowser is all the water on the
   ridge. The cut crew needs it to keep the saw-teams working in the heat; the burn
   crew needs it to hold the backfire's lower edge from jumping. It can be in only one
   place at a time, and the night is a constant, unresolved argument over where it
   belongs — every hour it spends with one crew is an hour the other works dry.
3. **Dawn is the wall.** When the wind turns, whatever is done is done. The town lives
   only if, by then, the cut is complete AND the backfire has met the line AND neither
   crew has failed for want of water at the wrong moment. It is not enough for each
   crew to "finish" on its own — the night only wins if both finish in a way that fits.
