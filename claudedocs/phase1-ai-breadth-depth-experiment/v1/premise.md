# Premise — "The Ember Winter at Grayloam"

This is the ONLY story input you are given. Everything else — the events, each
person's secrets, what is really happening, the scene sequence, the branches, the
causality, and (above all) WHO KNOWS WHAT — is yours to invent.

## Setting

Grayloam is an upland iron settlement at the dead end of a single mountain road — a
charcoal-burner's and bloom-smelter's village around an old furnace and a company
charter, set in a high valley that the first deep snows seal off from the lowlands
until the spring thaw. For one whole winter — four, five months — nobody comes up the
pass and nobody goes down it. The settlement lives the winter on the stores it laid
in: charcoal stacked in the long sheds, salt meat and meal in the company cellar, ore
already dug. Some forty souls overwinter here: the company's people, the families who
work the furnace and the coaling-hearths, and the handful of travellers the closing
pass caught and stranded.

This winter the snows closed early, and in the same week the settlement's old
ironmaster — who held the company's charter, kept the only accounts, controlled the
cellar key, and knew everyone's debts and secrets — was found dead (or vanished: YOU
decide which, and make it true in the facts). The pass is shut. The law, the company's
seat, the coroner — all of them are a thaw away. Whatever is to be done about it must
be done HERE, by the people snowed in, over a long dark winter, and answered for when
the road opens.

## The point-of-view

**Coll Brand** — young, the ironmaster's undermaster (deputy), left holding the
charter, the cellar key, and an authority no one quite agreed to. Coll is the POV: not
a detective, just the person everyone now turns to, out of their depth, with a whole
winter to get through and a houseful of people who each know a piece of what happened
and want different things. (You may rename Coll, but keep the role.)

## The cast (you may rename, reshape, or add — but keep it BROAD)

A whole settlement snowed in for a winter is the point. These are SEEDS; make each one
a real person with their own knowledge, their own reason for being at Grayloam, and
their own stake in the ironmaster's death and the winter ahead. At minimum a cast on
this order, and add more:

- **the company factor** — the firm's agent, wintering over, who wants the charter and
  the accounts to pass cleanly to the company.
- **the ironmaster's widow** — who kept the house and knows what was said in it.
- **the assay-clerk** — kept the books and the weights; knows the debts.
- **the master collier** — runs the charcoal hearths; the village's real authority.
- **the furnace-keeper** — tends the bloom; the furnace is failing this winter.
- **the herbwife / physician** — tends the sick; was called the night it happened.
- **the carter** — last over the pass before it closed; brought someone or something up.
- **a discharged soldier** — overwintering, walking home from a war.
- **two ore-prospectors / itinerants** — strangers the pass stranded, with their own
  business.
- **the chaplain** — keeps the small chapel; hears what people confess.
- **the smith**, **the cook**, **a bonded servant** of the company, **a child** who
  sees what adults miss — add as many distinct minds as the story wants.

## The inciting event

The ironmaster's death (or disappearance) on the first nights of the flood of snow —
a fall, a poisoning, a blow, a fire, a body taken by the cold, a faked death, a debt
called in: YOU choose what REALLY happened and make it true in the ground-truth facts.
It was witnessed by some and not others; it is understood differently by nearly
everyone. Several of the snowed-in know a fragment of what happened; none knows all of
it; some are lying about what they know, and some are wrong.

## The central dramatic question — and a WINTER of consequential choices

Coll, left in charge, must get the settlement through the winter AND work out what
happened to the ironmaster, from a community that each holds a different fragment and a
different agenda. This is a LONG arc: the choices come one after another across the
dark months, and each one turns the winter down a different road. For instance (invent
your own spine — these are only the shape):

- early: whether to open the ironmaster's locked accounts and cellar by the dead
  master's ledger, or to ration by need as the master collier demands;
- midwinter: when the furnace falters and the stores thin and someone falls sick or
  dies, whether to trust the company factor's plan or the village's;
- as the truth surfaces in fragments: whether to believe a confession, accuse the one
  the evidence points to, or keep doubting;
- at the thaw, when the pass opens and the company / the law / the outside returns:
  what Coll finally DOES about the ironmaster — lay it before the law, bury it to
  protect the settlement, or settle it at Grayloam before anyone goes down.

The roads these choices open need NOT lead to the same place. Build at least THREE
distinct terminal endings, reached by at least three consequential decision points
(the forks may come one after another — an early choice opening two roads, a later
choice splitting one of them again — not necessarily one big fork). What is really
behind the death, what each person actually knows and believes, and where each road
leads — all of it is yours to decide and make true in the fact base.

## Structural constraints (these are requirements, not suggestions)

- DEPTH **and** BREADTH, at once, are the essence of this story.
  - DEPTH: a genuinely long winter — roughly **60 or more scenes** — with **at least
    three consequential decision points** producing **at least three distinct terminal
    world-lines**, each reaching its OWN motivated ending. Sequential / nested forks are
    welcome (this is a season, not a single night). Make the branches genuinely diverge.
  - BREADTH: **at least ten distinct people, each with their own knowledge-frame**
    holding what THAT person knows and believes — several of them wrong about what the
    others know — sustained across the whole long arc. Not one truth and a crowd of
    bystanders, and not a big cast at the start who fade into scenery by midwinter.
- At least **three** genuine SETUPS that PAY OFF later (planted details that return and
  matter — at least one a piece of knowledge that only one person held).
- Decide what is actually true behind the death (the ground truth), and let several
  characters BELIEVE things that differ from the truth and from each other — their
  actions should follow from what THEY believe and what THEY have had a chance to learn
  across the winter, not from what you the author know.
- If two roads genuinely rejoin at a shared later event (say, the thaw arrives the same
  way down two different roads), you MAY declare that shared continuation once as a
  convergence instead of duplicating the scenes — but only if your plot naturally wants
  it. It is an ordinary tool, not a requirement.

Tell the best, most coherent deep-and-crowded winter story you can. Make it hang
together: every consequence should trace to a cause already present earlier on the
SAME road, every world-line should reach a real distinct ending, and every person
should know exactly what their own winter at Grayloam would let them know — no more,
no less.
