# Withering-Age future (planted road) — three generations on

## sc-27p

Three generations on, the same hollow lies under a long drought. Brae Hollow has gone grey and quiet. The slopes that ran loud with water are bare and cracked, the riverbed mostly stone, the air full of a fine dust that gets into everything. Up at the head of the valley the spring is down to a thread, and folk speak of it carefully, the way you speak of someone failing. It is the last water in the valley near enough, and everyone here knows the count of it. The young loud hollow is gone past remembering. What's left is dry ground and the people minding the little that holds.

## sc-28p

At the Cistern Steps a man named Marn keeps the cistern, and what he keeps is precious past saying — the last good water in the valley. He climbs the worn steps at first light and last, clears the lip, watches the level, rations what comes off it to the folk below who line up with their pails. He treats the basin like a sick thing that mustn't be jostled. "Easy now," he murmurs to it, to the stone, to no one. Every other spring in the valley has soured or gone dry. This one, his one, still gives. He guards it with his life because it is the valley's life.

## sc-29p

What Marn cannot tell you — what gnaws at him on the bad nights — is why. Why this cistern, and no other. He has watched springs he drank from as a boy turn foul and quit; he has seen catch-basins no different from his own go bad in a season. His runs clean and cold while the whole valley's water dies around it, and he does not know the reason. He has half a dozen guesses and trusts none of them. Sometimes he thinks it luck and is afraid, because luck runs out. He keeps it because it gives. The why of it is a thing his age has lost, and he has made a kind of peace with not knowing, the way you make peace with weather.

## sc-30p

You come up the Steps with the wend-pipe gone quiet at your chest — crossed over, the young hollow folded away behind a single note, and this dry one risen in its place around you. You know these Steps. You stood on them an age ago and a morning ago both. And there at the basin's lower vent, under the grey light, you stop: you know that vent. You watched it packed and sealed three generations gone, on a bright morning, by a keeper's careful hands. It is the same vent. The same stone, the same seam, older now and dust-greyed but yours to recognise. Marn doesn't see what you see. To him it is only the place the water runs back into the rock. To you it is a thing remembered from the other side of the pipe.

## sc-31p

And the cistern, here at the last of the valley, runs clean. That is the fact of it, plain and enormous: three generations into the withering, with every other spring soured or dead, the water that comes off these Steps comes cold and clear and good. Marn draws it and it is sweet. The valley drinks it and lives a little longer on it. You crouch at the lower vent where the overflow runs back, and in the old grey stone you find a mark — a small cut, three strokes and a notch, worn but plain to a hand that goes looking. A keeper's mark, set here by someone the late age does not remember. Marn has never noticed it; if he had, it would mean nothing to him, just an old scratch in old rock. You touch it, and you say nothing to Marn, because the thing you half-know is not yours to hand him here, and would only be a story to a man with a basin to mind. The water runs clean. The mark is in the stone. That much is true and present and held.

## sc-32p

Down at the Loom Quarter the row has gone silent. Where there were vats and shuttles and shouting there is dust and shut doors and the wind moving through empty frames. One loom still works. Pell the weaver sits at it, the last of the trade, throwing a shuttle through a thin warp, making cloth for a hollow that has little to trade for it. The clatter that filled this lane is down to one small knocking, Pell's alone. They work because it is what they do and what their parents did, on the dead craft-row, keeping a last loom going past all reason for it.

## sc-33p

Ask Pell about the old days and they shrug, not unkindly. There was a dispute once, long back, over the crossings and the carts — that much is a half-remembered word, a thing a grandparent might once have mentioned. Who settled it, who cut any stone, which trade was in the right — Pell knows nothing of that. "Before my grandmother's grandmother," they say, throwing the shuttle. "Names like that don't come down. The cloth comes down. The trade comes down. The reasons —" they shake their head — "those wash out." Pell knows the loom and the thread in front of them. The rest is gone, and Pell does not grieve a thing they never had.

## sc-34p

Down at the Wend Fields it is Ode who keeps the crossing now — Ode the field-warden, charged with getting the Withering folk over the channel to whatever ground will still grow them something. The fields are mostly dust. The river is a thin brown line where it runs at all. Ode walks the bank reading what's left of it, working out where folk and what few beasts remain can cross dry-shod to the far ground. It is hard, grudging work, the crossing different every season as the channel shifts in its dying bed. Ode keeps at it because the folk have to eat and the green that's left is on the other side.

## sc-35p

Working the shifted channel one morning, Ode's boot turns up an edge of cut stone half-buried in the silt. They dig it out with their hands — a boundary-stone, old, the channel having wandered away and left it stranded in the dry. Ode brushes the grime from its face and reads the cutting on it: a mark of the firm ford, a crossing named to hold. Ode crouches over it a long moment. They don't know who set it or when; the cutting is in an old hand and says nothing of its maker. But Ode knows their river, and reading the stone against the lie of the channel, they see it points true — here, where the bed is bone under the silt, is a crossing that holds. "Somebody knew this water," Ode says, half to the stone. That is as far as the knowing goes.

## sc-36p

So the Withering folk cross. On the firm ford the old stone marks, where the bed holds bone-hard under the shifting silt, they bring the folk and the last beasts over dry-shod to the last green field — the one stretch of ground across the channel that still grows. The crossing holds while the river dies around it. Ode stands at the head of it, on the stone's word, and waves them across, season on season, and the ground under their feet does not give. They cross because a stone in an old hand told Ode where the firm ford lay — set there by someone in an age none of them remember, for reasons washed out long ago. Ode does not know the why and does not need it. The ford holds. The folk get over. The stone keeps its one plain word, and the late age takes it on faith, the way it takes the clean water — a gift with the giver forgotten.

## sc-37p

And across the channel, spanning the dry gorge that once cut the way to the far field, there is a bridge-tree. It grew there — a slow gorge-sapling that threw a root each side of the cleft and over the long years grew itself into the gap, until it is no longer a tree but a way across, a living span you can walk dry over the raw dark of the gorge. The Withering folk cross it without much thought, the way you cross a thing that has always been there. To them it is simply the bridge-tree, the old crossing to the far ground, and they are glad of it because without it the last green field would be cut off and the gorge uncrossable.

## sc-38p

It is Tamsin who works the far field now — Tamsin the far-field settler, who goes over the bridge-tree each day to the one good ground left and brings back what it grows. Across the living span, in the field the gorge once cut off entirely, Tamsin coaxes a crop from soil that still has some give in it, the runoff and the shade pooling life in that one far corner while the rest of the hollow dries. "This field carries us," Tamsin says, walking back over the bridge-tree with a load on their back. "Hollow'd have starved without the far ground, and the far ground's no good without the bridge." They cross and recross it, settled into the small daily miracle of it, and do not ask where the tree came from — only thank it, in their way, by working the ground it reaches.

## sc-39p

You climb to the Steps and play the wend-pipe, and the dry age folds away and the bright young one rises around you — and this time you go to see. You stand at the Cistern Steps in the Founding morning and you watch the keeper, Sera, find the creeping rot at the lower vent and pack it tight, course on course, sealing it away; and cut her mark beside it for a later hand. This is why the cistern runs clean three generations on. This is the seal under all that good water. And down at the crossing you watch the boundary-mason, Halden, read the dying river before it dies and judge the one ford that will stay firm, and cut his stone and bed it at its head — for a cart-dispute, never dreaming of drought. This is why the ford holds. The thing the late age lost — the cause Marn could not name and Ode could only guess at — you see it now with your own eyes, done plainly by two people on an ordinary morning, never knowing what they saved. The seal and the stone. Now you know the why, and you carry it across.

## sc-40p

In the end Brae Hollow withers and the far field carries it. The hollow itself is grey and going, the loud young place worn down to dust and a thread of spring — but it does not die, because the things that hold, hold. The water holds, off the Steps, clean to the last. The ford holds, on the old stone's word, dry-shod across the dying river. And the bridge-tree holds, spanning the gorge to the one green field that feeds them. Withered, rationed, three generations into the drought, the hollow goes on — carried by a far field across a living bridge, drinking the last good water, crossing the one firm ford. What was done on a bright forgotten morning holds the dry age up. The hollow withers, and is not lost.
