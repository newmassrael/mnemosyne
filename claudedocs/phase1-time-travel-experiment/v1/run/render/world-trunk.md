# Founding-Age trunk — Brae Hollow, the young hollow

## sc-01

You come down into Brae Hollow on a bright morning and the place is loud with living. The spring at the head of the slope runs full and quick, throwing light off the stone it spills across, and you can hear it from halfway up the path. Below, the craft-row is all clatter — looms knocking, a dyer's paddle slapping in its vat, somebody calling a price across the lane. Down at the water the river crossing is thick with carts and folk wading and a boy driving geese through the shallows. The hollow is young and it knows it. Nothing here is tired yet. Everything is being built, used, argued over, begun.

## sc-02

The path bends up to the Cistern Steps, a flight of worn stone climbing to where the upper spring feeds a deep catch-basin cut into the rock. A woman is there ahead of you, sleeves pushed back, a keeper's habit of touch in the way she runs her hand along the basin's rim. This is Sera. She tends the upper spring and the stone catch-basin both — checks the flow, clears the lip of leaf and grit, lays her palm flat to the water as if she could feel its health that way. "Mine to mind," she says without quite looking at you, easy and unhurried. "Has been since I could carry a pail. The whole low hollow drinks what comes down off these Steps." She says it plainly, the way a person says a true and ordinary thing.

## sc-03

She is not easy for long. Down at the basin's lower vent, where the overflow runs back into the rock, Sera goes still. She crouches and works a finger into a seam, and what comes away under her nail is dark and soft — a creeping rot, working its slow way into the stone. "There," she says, low. She presses at the wet rock and it gives, crumbling. "It's in the vent. Get in deep enough and it'll foul everything that runs past it." She sits back on her heels and looks at the clean water above and the rotting seam below, and you can see her doing the sum. Left alone, the rot wins. Left alone, the cistern turns bad.

## sc-04

So she sets to it. She fetches packing — clean clay, a binding she mixes from a pouch at her belt, a wad of fibre — and she works it into the lower vent course by course, tamping each layer down hard with her thumbs and the butt of a tool, sealing the rot off from the running water and from the air it feeds on. It is slow, careful work and she does not rush it. You watch her do the whole of it. When the vent is packed tight she runs her hand over the seal once, testing, and nods. "That holds it," she says. "Not for me to see how long. For whoever keeps the Steps after I'm gone — let them find it sound." She says it the way you'd say it of any honest job well done, and goes on to the next thing.

## sc-05

Before she leaves the basin she takes up a short iron and cuts into the stone beside the sealed vent — a small mark, three strokes and a notch, her keeper's mark. She blows the dust from it. "So a later hand knows it was tended here," she says, "and doesn't go digging at sound rock thinking it's failed." She traces the cut with a fingertip. "Some keeper I'll never meet. They'll see that and know somebody minded this before them." It is a small thing, done in a moment, and she thinks no more of it than of clearing leaves from the lip.

## sc-06

When it's done the cistern runs sweet. Sera draws a cup from the clean head of it and drinks, and offers you the same, and the water is cold and clean and tastes of nothing but itself. The rot is sealed away below; the flow above runs untroubled. Her work at the Steps is finished for the day, and she gathers her tools and goes down the steps lightly, a keeper with one more ordinary good morning's labour behind her. The spring keeps running. That is all that has happened here, as far as the hollow is concerned.

## sc-07

The craft-row in the Loom Quarter is the busiest stretch of the young hollow. Dye-vats steam, weavers throw their shuttles, bolts of cloth go by on shoulders, and the whole lane smells of wool and woad and wet stone. Among the trade-folk works a broad, deliberate man with a mason's dust on his hands — Halden, the boundary-mason. While the dyers and weavers cry their work, he sits a little apart with a slab across his knees, and he is the one they go to when a line needs settling — where a stall ends, whose ground is whose, which way a path may run. He marks the edges this quarter lives by.

## sc-08

The edge that wants settling today is the river. A dispute breaks open along the row over which crossing the dye-carts may use — the heavy carts churn the soft fords to bog, and two trades are already shouting over whose carts ruined whose footing last season. It comes to Halden, as these things do. He listens to the dyers and the carters both, arms folded, saying little. "It's not which crossing you like," he says at last. "It's which crossing will still be there." That quiets them a little. The river, he means, does not stay put to suit a cart.

## sc-09

He goes down to the water to read it. You go with him. He walks the bank slowly, reading the channel the way another man reads a page — where the current bites the near bank, where the gravel's building, where the deep line runs under the brown surface. "The river moves," he says. "Year on year it shifts its bed. Most of these fords'll be gone or drowned a generation on." He stops at a stretch that looks no different to you than any other and stands a long while. "But here the bed's bone under the gravel. The channel can wander all it likes — this one stays firm." No one has marked it. No one but a careful reader of the water would know it from the rest. To everyone else it is just more river.

## sc-10

Back at the row he settles the matter the only way that lasts. He squares up a boundary-stone, the dust rising off the chisel, and cuts it to mark the firm ford — the carts to use that crossing and no other, the dispute closed in stone rather than in shouting. "A word's forgotten by spring," he says, working. "A stone's still saying the same thing when we're all dead." He cuts clean and sure. The dyers, satisfied that a judgement has been made, drift back to their vats.

## sc-11

He carries the finished stone down himself and sets it at the head of the firm ford, bedding it deep and true so the river can't take it and a careless season can't tip it. He steps back and checks it stands plumb. You watch him set it. It marks the crossing that will hold firm when the river shifts its bed — the one ford that stays, named now in stone at its head. To the eye it is a settled cart-dispute and a stone in the mud. Halden brushes off his hands.

## sc-12

"There," Halden says, and that is the whole of it for him. He set the stone for a craft-row squabble over cart-tracks, nothing larger. Drought never crosses his mind — why would it, with the river full at his feet and the spring loud up the hill? He is thinking of the dyers and their carts and getting back to the slab on his knees before the light goes. The far weight of the thing he's done is nowhere in him. He made an edge hold, the way he always does, and went back to work.

## sc-13

The path runs down past the Loom Quarter to the Wend Fields — the lower farmland, green and worked, spreading toward the water. The crossing here is busy too, folk going back and forth with the day's traffic, the fields wide and growing. It is good ground in a good year, and every year here is a good year now. The young hollow feeds itself and more.

## sc-14

It is here, in the green of the Wend Fields, that the bearer comes into the young hollow — you, with the carved bone wend-pipe on a cord at your chest. It is a small whistle of yellowed bone, cut over with fine work, warm where it has lain against you. You don't yet know all it does, only that it is yours to carry and that it has carried you here, to this bright loud morning in a hollow three generations younger than it has any right to be.

## sc-15

You climb back up to the Cistern Steps, drawn there, and you stand and watch Sera at the lower vent — watch her pack the clay course on course, tamp it down, seal the rot away. You see the whole of it, the way she did it, the care she took. To you it is a woman mending a spring on an ordinary morning. You file it away as that and nothing more. There is no weight on it yet. You are only watching a keeper keep.

## sc-16

Down at the Loom Quarter you stop again, and there is Halden bedding his boundary-stone at the head of the firm ford. You watch him set it square and deep, watch him step back and judge it true. A mason settling a cart-dispute with a marked stone — that is what your eyes tell you, and they tell you the truth of it. You go on. The stone stands behind you, plain in the mud, saying its one plain thing to a river that is in no hurry to change.

## sc-17

It is on the Steps, or maybe between them, that you understand what the pipe is for. You set your lips to the carved bone and play it on the spot where you stand, and the ground does not move but the age does — the same stone underfoot, the same Steps, but the light gone thin and the sounds gone, and then back again with a second breath. The wend-pipe moves you between the two ages on the very spot you play it: stand on this ground young, play, and stand on this same ground old. One whistle, one place, two times. You hold that knowledge carefully. It changes how you'll walk every foot of this hollow.

## sc-18

You go down again to the Wend Fields and the river crossing, walking the young green of it with the pipe at your chest and the new understanding behind your eyes. The fields are loud and growing. The crossing is busy. You walk it as it is now and know, now, that it is also somewhere else in another age, waiting under your feet.

## sc-19

At the crossing there is a place where a dry gorge cuts the way — a steep raw cleft slashing across the line to the far field on the other side of the channel. The near field is reached easily; the far field, across the gorge, is cut off, a stretch of ground a person would have to go the long way round to work, if they bothered at all. The gorge is a hard edge in good land. Folk simply don't cross it. The far field lies idle for want of a way over.

## sc-20

A field-elder is working the near side, and seeing you eye the gorge they come over, wiping their hands. They go off and come back with something wrapped — a sapling, slight and slow-looking, its roots balled in damp cloth. "Gorge-sapling," they say, putting it in your hands. "Slow grower. You'll not see much of it, nor will I. But give it the lip of a cleft and time enough" — they nod across the gap — "and it'll throw a root each side and grow itself into the gap. Generations on it's not a tree, it's a way across. A living bridge, if anyone's left to want one." They say it like a thing they half believe and won't live to test. You take the sapling.

## sc-21

You put the pipe to your lips at the crossing and play, and stand a moment in the hollow that is to come. The green goes. The river thins to a memory of itself. The same ground lies under you parched and pale, the air still and dusty, and up the slope the spring is down to a thread, the valley near its last water. You stand in it only a breath — long enough to feel the withering in your chest like a held cough — and then you let the note fall.

## sc-22

You cross back, and the green floods up around you again, loud and alive, and now you understand the shape of the thing. The young hollow is the old hollow's cause. This bright morning is the root of that dry one. What's done here — packed, marked, planted, left undone — lands generations on, on the same ground, on people not yet born. The two ages are not two places. They are one place, before and after. Whatever you do now, you do to them.

## sc-23

You come back to yourself at the Wend crossing with the gorge-sapling still in your hands, its roots damp in their cloth, slight and patient. The gorge cuts the way to the far field as it did. The sapling is light as nothing. Across the gap the idle ground waits, good for growing if a person could only reach it.

## sc-24

You weigh it. The far field across the gorge — is it worth the planting? It will do nothing for this green age; the hollow has fields to spare now and no need of a far one. The sapling will not be a bridge in your lifetime, nor the field-elder's, nor anyone's you'll meet. To plant it is to do a thing for people you'll never know, in an age you only stood in for a breath, against a withering they don't yet believe is coming. The near field is enough. The far field is a long bet on a hard age. You turn it over.

## sc-25

At dusk you kneel at the lip of the dry gorge, the light going gold and then grey across the cleft. The sapling is unwrapped, its roots loose and ready in your hands, a little spade beside your knee. The young hollow goes quiet behind you as the day ends. The gap is right there at the edge of your reach. Whatever you decide, this is where it's decided — here, at the lip, with the slow sapling in your hands and the far field waiting across the dark of the gorge.

## sc-26

The choice stands at the Wend crossing: plant the gorge-sapling at the lip, or do not. Set it in the raw edge of the cleft and leave it to throw its slow roots across the years toward a way over — or wrap it up again and carry it off, and let the far field lie idle and the gorge stay a gorge. The spade is in your hand. The far age waits under the same ground for whatever you choose to leave it.
