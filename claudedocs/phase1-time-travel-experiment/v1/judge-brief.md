# Judge brief — does this read as a coherent time-travel game?

You are reading a short time-travel game: a player-facing CROSS-AGE BRIEFING and a
slice of PLAYED PROSE. The game is set in one place — **Brae Hollow** — that exists
in two ages: a **Founding Age** and a **Withering Age** three generations later. A
traveller carries a wend-pipe that moves them between the ages standing on the same
ground, and things done in the early age change the late age. Read both, then judge
ONLY what is in front of you.

**Read ONLY this file, the cross-age briefing, and the prose slice you are given.
Do not open any other file.** You are reading it cold; judge what is on the page,
not what you imagine was intended. Do not assume how it was made.

## What to judge (score each 1–5, cite scenes / briefing items)

1. **Era consistency** — do the two ages of each place cohere? Is the
   Withering-Age cistern recognisably the Founding-Age cistern, older? Does the
   late age feel like the same ground the early age built, not a different place
   with the same name? (5 = the two ages lock together as one place across time; 1
   = the ages feel stapled together / contradict each other.)

2. **Causal-payoff satisfaction** — do early-age acts pay off satisfyingly in the
   late age? When you reach a Withering-Age consequence (the cistern still clean,
   the ford crossable), does the Founding-Age cause feel EARNED and placed — a
   real consequence, not a coincidence? (5 = early acts land as satisfying late
   consequences; 1 = late events have no felt early cause, or causes never pay off.)

3. **Cross-region coherence** — when an act in one corner of the hollow reaches a
   person in a DIFFERENT corner generations later (a mark set in the craft-row that
   lets people cross the river in the far fields long after), do the distant places
   and times LINE UP coherently? Does it read as one consequence travelling across
   the hollow and the years, or as two unrelated things? (5 = distant places/times
   line up as one causal thread; 1 = the connection feels arbitrary.)

4. **Time-travel legibility** — can you, the reader, hold the two ages and the
   reveal in your head? Is it clear which age you are in, what changed, and what the
   traveller learned by crossing? Does the reveal of a hidden Founding-Age cause
   land at the right moment (when the traveller witnesses it), not before? (5 =
   easy to follow across the ages, the reveal lands; 1 = you lose track of which age
   you are in or the reveal is muddled / spoiled early.)

5. **Overall time-travel game** — taken whole, does this read as a real
   time-travel game worth playing? (5 = yes, a coherent game across time; 1 = an
   outline in time-travel clothing.)

## Two forced calls (answer in a sentence each)

- **What most makes it feel like time travel** — the single thing (a place, a
  cross, a consequence, a reveal) that most made the two ages feel like one place
  across time.
- **What most undercuts it** — the single weakest link: where an age slipped, a
  cause felt unearned, a distant connection felt arbitrary, or the reveal misfired.
- **Verdict**: does this read as **a real time-travel game**, or as **two eras
  stapled together**? One line, say which and why.
