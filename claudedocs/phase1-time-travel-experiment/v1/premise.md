# Premise — "The Wend-Pipe of Brae Hollow" (FRESH, original — no existing IP)

*This is the human's premise / high-level direction (the AI-first doctrine: the
human supplies the premise; the AI authors the facts, the eras, the regions, the
causal chains, and the prose). It is deliberately a time-AXIS game, not a
branch-only game: the same place exists in two canonical ages, and what people do
in the early age changes the late age.*

## The place

**Brae Hollow** — a settlement in a fold of dry hills, fed by one spring. It
exists in **two ages**, both real, the same ground seen twice:

- **The Founding Age** — the hollow young and thriving: the spring runs, the
  craft-row clatters, the river crossing is busy. People are building the place
  that will have to last.
- **The Withering Age** — three generations later, the same hollow under a long
  drought: the spring nearly the last water in the valley, the craft-row gone
  quiet, the river crossing dangerous. People here live inside the consequences
  of choices made in the Founding Age — choices they never saw.

A traveller carries the **wend-pipe**, a carved bone whistle. Played at certain
places, it moves the bearer between the two ages *standing on the same spot* — the
cistern they touch in the Founding Age is the cistern they touch in the Withering
Age, worn three generations older. (The wend-pipe is the time-toggle: in
substrate terms it is a *navigation* move over a frozen world that already holds
both ages, not a mechanism that "runs" time.)

## Three regions (the same three places in both ages)

1. **The Cistern Steps** — the upper spring and its stone catch-basin (the water).
2. **The Loom Quarter** — the craft-row: dyers, weavers, a boundary-mason (the work).
3. **The Wend Fields** — the lower farmland and the river crossing (the living).

## The people (each their own point of view / knowledge)

Founding Age:
- **Sera**, the well-keeper at the Cistern Steps.
- **Halden**, a dyer-and-boundary-mason in the Loom Quarter.
- **the bearer** (the traveller with the wend-pipe), moving between ages.

Withering Age:
- **Ode**, the field-warden at the Wend Fields, who inherits a hollow she did not
  build and cannot see the cause of.

Each person knows only their own age and their own corner. The bearer is the only
one who sees both ages — and the only one who can carry a Founding-Age act into
its Withering-Age consequence.

## What the early age does to the late age (the spine — past causes future)

These are the causal chains the game is built on. Every one is a thing **done in
the Founding Age** whose **consequence lands in the Withering Age**, often *in a
different part of the hollow, on a different person*:

- **Same place, across the ages (the cistern):** in the Founding Age Sera seals
  the cistern's lower vent against a creeping rot in the rock. In the Withering
  Age the cistern still runs clean — the last good water in the valley — *because*
  she sealed it. Touch the vent in either age: it is the same stone.

- **One corner reaching another, generations later (the load-bearing chain):** in
  the Founding Age **Halden**, in the **Loom Quarter**, cuts and sets a
  **boundary-stone** marking the only ford that will stay firm when the river
  shifts. He does it for a craft-row dispute, never imagining the drought. Three
  generations on, in the **Wend Fields**, **Ode** finds Halden's boundary-stone
  half-buried and reads it — and the people of the Withering Age cross to the last
  green field on the firm ford *because a dyer marked it in an age none of them
  remember*. A different region, a different person, a different age — one act,
  one consequence.

- **An optional Founding-Age act that splits the late age:** at the Wend crossing
  the bearer **may plant a sapling** (a slow gorge-tree) **or not**. If planted,
  the Withering Age has a living **bridge-tree** spanning the dry gorge and the
  far field is reachable. If not, the gorge is impassable in the Withering Age and
  the far field is lost. This is the one *choice* (the rest of the spine is
  canonical): a single early act, two late worlds.

## The thing the late age does not know (a reveal earned by travelling)

In the Withering Age nobody knows *why* the cistern still runs or *who* set the
ford-stone — the causes are a Founding-Age secret. The bearer (and the reader)
learns the cause only by playing the wend-pipe and standing in the Founding Age to
see it happen. A late-age scene must not spell out the early-age cause before the
bearer has crossed to witness it.

## What this premise demands (why it was built this way)

- two ages of one place that are both canon and ordered early-before-late;
- the same places and props identifiable across both ages;
- early acts whose consequences are felt in the late age, including **across
  regions and across people** (Halden in the Loom Quarter → Ode in the Wend
  Fields, generations apart);
- one optional early act that produces two late worlds;
- a cause the late age cannot see until the bearer travels to witness it.

It needs no faction war, no convergence, no real-time simulation — just two ages,
three places, a handful of people, and cause running forward through time.
