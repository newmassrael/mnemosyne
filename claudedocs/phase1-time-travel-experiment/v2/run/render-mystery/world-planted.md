## sc-27p

Three generations of dust have settled on Brae Hollow, and the people who live there have never known it any other way. Their grandparents spoke of a green time, but their grandparents are gone, and what they handed down was the *word* green, not the thing. The drought lies over the valley the way a lid lies over a pot — close, total, and patient.

Every spring in the valley has gone. The old folk could once name a dozen: the seep under Tinder Rock, the cold well at Marrow Lane, the fast little run that fed the lower mill. One by one they slowed to a thread, then to a damp stain on the stone, then to nothing. Children are taken to see the dry places the way other children are taken to see graves. *This one ran in your great-grandmother's day,* they are told, and they stare at the cracked mud and try to imagine water there, and cannot.

Only one source still gives. It rises near the head of the hollow, the last living water in all the valley, and the whole of Brae Hollow bends toward it the way a sick man bends toward warmth. No one in the hollow can tell you why this one and no other. They only know that it holds, and that holding is the thread their lives hang on.

## sc-28p

Marn keeps it. He is the well-tender, as his mother was before him, and he holds the cistern at the Cistern Steps the way you would hold a child in deep water — both hands, all attention, no looking away.

The Steps are old worked stone, a broad flight cut down into the hillside, and at the foot of them the cistern lies dark and still and brimming. It is the last good water in the valley, and Marn measures it out. He knows the level by the wet line on the third step from the bottom; he knows the taste of it on his tongue at first light, before anyone has drawn; he knows the small green smell it has when the day is hot and how that is the smell of *clean* and not the other thing.

He is there before the others wake and there after they sleep. He skims what the wind brings down. He keeps the children from the brink and the cattle from the channel. The hollow trusts him because the water is good, and the water is good because Marn keeps it — that much they can all see. What keeps the *source* good, under all his keeping, is another question, and it is not one Marn has ever been able to answer.

## sc-29p

It troubles him, on the bad nights.

Every other spring in the valley failed. He has walked to all the dead ones — it is a thing the well-tenders do, a kind of pilgrimage of dread — and he has crouched by each ruined seep and looked for what was different about his own, and found nothing. The rock is the same rock. The hill is the same hill. The drought that strangled them all should have strangled this one too, and harder, for it gives the most.

But it runs. Year on year, through the worst summers his mother named and worse besides, it rises clear into the cistern and never fouls and never fails. Marn does not understand it. He has no story for it that satisfies him. He has tried the easy ones — that the spring is deeper, that the rock is luckier, that the hollow is owed one mercy — and none of them hold when he says them aloud in the dark.

So he keeps it without knowing it. He tends a thing whose reason he cannot find, and he has made his peace, mostly, with not finding it. The water comes clean. He does not know why. He guards the not-knowing along with the water, because to lose either would be the end of the hollow, and a man can carry a mystery a long way if it is the mystery that keeps him alive.

## sc-30p

A traveller came down the Cistern Steps one grey morning while Marn was skimming, and stood a long while looking at the cistern with an expression Marn could not place — not thirst, not wonder exactly, something nearer to recognition, as though the stranger had expected this and found it true.

The traveller carried a pipe at the belt, slim and worn, a wend-pipe, though Marn did not know the word for it. They went down to the lower courses of the stonework, where the cistern's wall met the living rock, and they crouched there and put a hand against a particular place — a low seam in the stone, packed tight and old, where the wall ran into the hill.

"You know this," the traveller said. It was not quite a question.

Marn did not know it. He had skimmed and tended above the waterline all his life and never thought about the dark joint down where the wall went into the earth. He came and crouched beside the stranger and looked where the stranger looked, and he saw only old stone, very tightly fitted, the seam of it firm under the traveller's palm. The traveller pressed it as a person presses something familiar, returned to. Marn pressed it too and felt nothing he could name. Just stone. Just a place where the water did not get in. He could not have said what the stranger saw there, only that they saw *something*, and that whatever it was, it had been waiting in that seam since before any living memory in the hollow.

## sc-31p

The traveller stayed by the lower seam, and after a while ran a thumb along a patch of the stone beside it, and went still.

Cut into the rock, weather-softened almost to nothing, was a mark. Marn had never seen it — it sat below the wet line, in the part of the Steps no one had reason to go. It was a small worked figure, deliberate, a maker's sign of some kind, the strokes shallow now but sure once. A keeper's mark, the traveller called it, fingertips in the grooves. Marn knelt and traced it himself. It fit no name he knew and told him nothing. Someone had cut it. He could not say who, or when, or why a hand would trouble to sign this hidden place.

But there beside the old worn sign the water rose, as it always rose — up through the dark joint of the wall and into the cistern, clear and cold and good. Three generations of drought above it and it did not foul; it did not slow; it gave and gave. Marn looked at the unfailing water and the unreadable mark together and felt the size of his not-knowing open under him like the cistern's dark.

It ran clean. It was the last good water in the valley. *Why* it ran clean when all the rest had died — that he could not tell, no more than he could read the name in the stone. He only knew the two things sat together: an old hand had been here once, and the water had never failed. Whether the one had anything to do with the other, Marn could not say. He had no way to know. He filled his jug and climbed back up the Steps into the dry morning, and the mystery climbed with him.

## sc-32p

The Loom Quarter has gone quiet.

It was the loud heart of Brae Hollow once — the craft-row, where the shuttles clattered dawn to dusk and the dyers' vats steamed and the carts came and went so thick you could not cross the lane without minding your feet. Pell can remember a thread of it from childhood: the noise, mostly, and the smell of wet wool, and being shooed out of the way of grown people in a hurry.

Now Pell works the last loom on the dead row. The other frames stand stripped or broken in the shut houses; the dye-yards are dry pits; the carts are firewood, long since. Pell weaves alone in the quiet, throwing the shuttle to a rhythm there is no one left to answer, and the sound of it goes out into the empty quarter and comes back with nothing on it. The wool is poor and thin — there is little grazing now — but Pell weaves it because weaving is what the hand knows, and a hollow with one working loom is not yet a hollow with none.

## sc-33p

People come and go through the Loom Quarter on their way to the fields, and sometimes one of them, looking at the shut craft-houses, will say there was trouble here once, in the old days. A quarrel. Something about the row, before it died.

Pell knows nothing of it. Whatever dispute the craft-row once held — whoever fell out with whom, over carts or boundaries or the right of a lane — none of it came down to Pell. There were no stories at the knee about it, no names kept, no grudge handed on. If a stone was ever cut to settle the matter, Pell never heard of it and would not know it from any other stone. The quarrel, if there was one, is as dead as the looms around it. Pell weaves over the top of a silence and does not even know it is a silence that was once a noise. To Pell the dead row has only ever been dead; what killed it, and what was argued before it died, are not even questions. They are simply the country of the forgotten, which is most of the past.

## sc-34p

Down past the dead row the land falls toward the Wend Fields, and there the river runs — or what is left of it. The great water is a memory; what crosses the valley now is a shrunk, brown, changeable thing that wanders its wide stony bed and is never twice in the same place. Ode is the field-warden, and Ode keeps the crossing.

It is the only crossing that matters, for the last green field lies on the far bank, and the last green field is half of what feeds the hollow. Twice a day the Withering folk go down to cross — to weed, to water, to bring back what the field gives — and twice a day they trust their footing to the warden's word. Ode knows the river's moods, where it runs deep after a freshet and where the soft mud will take a leg to the knee. Ode goes first and goes last and counts heads at the bank. Keeping the crossing is keeping the field, and keeping the field is keeping the hollow on its feet a little longer; Ode has never thought of it as anything grander than that, and never needed to.

## sc-35p

The channel shifted again after the spring melt, the way it does, and Ode went down to find the firm line of the ford anew. It is patient work — wading and testing, a staff probing ahead, learning where the bed has gone soft and where it still bears.

That morning the staff struck something that was not river-stone. Ode dug at it with a heel and then with both hands, and turned up out of the silt a block of cut stone, squared by a tool, half-buried at the very place the wading had said was firm. There were marks on its weathered face. Ode washed them with river water and looked a long time.

It was a boundary-stone — that much Ode could read in the shape of it and the squareness, a thing set on purpose to *mark* a place. The lines on its face Ode could not read at all; they were some older hand's, worn shallow. But the meaning of where it stood was plain enough to a warden: someone, in some age past all the hollow's remembering, had set this stone exactly where the crossing held firm. Ode could not say who, or when, or how they had known. The stone said nothing of that. It only said *here* — here is the firm line — and the river, for all its wandering, had never carried the firm line away from it. Ode took it for the mark of the true ford, set it back where it sat, and trusted it the more for being old.

## sc-36p

After that the folk crossed by the stone.

Ode lined the morning crossing on it and the evening crossing on it, and where the stone said firm, the bed bore them, even when the brown water had shifted overnight and the eye would have chosen wrong. Children learned to look for it before they stepped. *Mind the stone,* the mothers said, the way you say a thing that has always been said, and the children minded it and went over dry to the last green field and came back the same way with the field's green in their arms.

None of them knew its origin. None of them needed to. It was older than the oldest of them; it had been there before the warden before this warden, and the firm crossing it marked had carried the hollow across to its food through years no one alive could number. Why a stone should sit just there, and why the river — which respected nothing — should leave that one line firm beneath it season on season, the Withering folk did not ask and could not have answered. They trusted it the way you trust the ground: completely, and without a story. It held. It had always held. That was reason enough for people with little time to spare for reasons, and so they crossed, and lived, and called it luck when they called it anything at all.

## sc-37p

There is one more thing in Brae Hollow that should not be, and it stands at the dry gorge.

The gorge cuts the eastern arm of the valley, deep and sheer and waterless, a wound the old river left when it shrank. Nothing should bridge it; nothing was built to. Yet a tree grows there — has grown there — root-fast in the near wall and crown-fast in the far, its great trunk laid across the gap as though some hand had felled it there on purpose, except that it is *living*, leafed, putting out shade over the drop. It was a sapling once, by the look of the old growth; now it is a bridge-tree, broad enough to walk, spanning the dry gorge clean across.

The Withering folk use it without remark, as they use everything that holds. They walk the living trunk over the deep air to the ground beyond, and the tree bears them, year on year, and gives no sign of why a tree should have chosen to grow exactly there, or how it came to lie so true across the one place a crossing was needed. It simply stands, and spans, and goes on living, and the people go over.

## sc-38p

Beyond the bridge-tree there is land, and Tamsin works it.

Tamsin is the far-field settler — the only soul who lives across the gorge, in a low stone house at the edge of the ground the bridge-tree reaches. It is hard ground, and lonely, but it is ground, and in a withering hollow ground that will still take a seed is worth a life of loneliness. Each day Tamsin crosses the living trunk to tend it, and crosses back at dusk, and the far field gives — not much, but steadily — corn and roots and a little grazing that the home fields can no longer spare.

Tamsin does not wonder much about the tree anymore. The first season it was a marvel and a terror, that long walk over the drop on a thing that breathed; now it is the way to work, worn familiar under the feet. The far field carries more of the hollow each year as the near land fails. Tamsin works it the way the others work theirs — head down, trusting the things that hold, asking nothing of them but that they go on holding. They have not failed yet. That, to a settler at the world's dry edge, is as much as can be hoped, and more than the old springs gave.

## sc-39p

It was the bearer who learned the reason — the traveller of the wend-pipe, who had stood at the cistern and pressed the old seam and read the keeper's mark and known no more than Marn what it meant.

At the lip of the dry gorge the bearer raised the wend-pipe and played it. The notes went out thin over the drop, and the dry valley *thinned* with them — the dust pulling back like breath drawn in, the brown trickle swelling, the dead row crowding suddenly with carts and noise — and the bearer crossed, not the gorge, but the years. They came out blinking into the Founding Age, into a Brae Hollow green and loud and whole, and there, for the first time, they saw the why of everything they had walked through in the ruined age to come.

At the Cistern Steps — younger, sharp-cut, the water leaping — a woman knelt at the lower seam where the wall met the rock. Sera, the others called her, the keeper of the water. The bearer watched her find it: a creeping rot working its way into the stone at the cistern's lower vent, a soft dark spreading in the rock, the kind of thing that fouls a spring slow and sure if it is left. Sera knew it for what it was at once — the bearer saw the knowledge cross her face — knew it would taint the water if it reached the flow. So she packed the vent. She worked stone and seal into the lower joint, tight and patient, sealing the rot out and the good water in, and when it was done she sat back on her heels and pressed it once to test it, the very gesture the bearer's own hand had unknowingly repeated three ages on. *She did not do it for herself.* The bearer understood that, watching — she sealed it trusting the cistern to hold for whoever should keep it after her, hands she would never see. And then she took her tool and cut, in the stone beside the sealed vent, her keeper's mark — small, deliberate, sure-stroked — a sign she set there for some later keeper she would never meet, that a hand should know one day this place was tended. The same mark, the bearer saw, weather-worn almost to nothing, that Marn had traced and could not read.

And down at the river — running full and broad in this green age — a second figure waded and tested as Ode would test three generations on. Halden, the dyer, in a season of trouble: the craft-row in dispute, carts and dye and right-of-way, a quarrel the bearer had heard the last dead echo of in Pell's silent quarter. Halden was settling it. But the bearer watched him do more than settle a quarrel — watched him read the river itself, judging not where it ran *now* but where it would run in the years to come, which ford would stay firm when the channel shifted, which the water would betray. There was only one that would hold true through all the river's wandering, and it was unmarked, unknown, a line no one would find by chance once the channel moved. Halden read it out of the water by sheer careful attention, and then he cut a boundary-stone, settling the dye-cart dispute in stone as the row demanded — and set it, squared and sure, at the head of the one firm ford, marking the crossing that would stay firm when the river shifted in the ages after him. The same stone, half-buried and weather-blind, that Ode had turned up out of the silt and trusted without knowing whose hand had set it, or that the hand had been looking, across all those years, straight at her.

The bearer stood in the green and the noise of the Founding Age and saw it whole at last. Not luck. Not mercy owed. Two people, long ago, in an age the late folk could not even remember — a keeper who sealed the rot from the water, a dyer who read the river and marked the firm ford — had reached forward across three generations of drought and held the dying hollow up.

## sc-40p

Brae Hollow withers, and will go on withering; the green time does not come back, and the bearer, returning to the dry age, finds it as dry as it was left. But it holds. The water at the Cistern Steps still rises clean into Marn's keeping; the firm ford still bears Ode's folk over to the last green field; the bridge-tree still spans the dry gorge for Tamsin to cross at dawn. The near land fails and the far field carries the hollow, and the three things that should have died with the rest do not die. The water holds. The ford holds. The bridge-tree holds. On those three the withering hollow stands, and goes on standing, into the long dry years to come.
