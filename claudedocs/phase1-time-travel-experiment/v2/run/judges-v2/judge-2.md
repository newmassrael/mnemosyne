# Judge 2 — verdict (A/B, two tellings of the time-travel game)

## arm-1 — four scores

1. **Reveal impact — 5/5.** arm-1 withholds the Founding causes entirely until
   sc-39p. For thirteen scenes the *why* is held open as an active wound, not a
   placeholder: Marn "guards the not-knowing along with the water" (sc-29p), Ode
   "could not say who, or when, or how they had known" (sc-35p), Pell weaves "over
   the top of a silence and does not even know it is a silence" (sc-33p). When the
   bearer crosses at sc-39p and watches Sera find "a creeping rot working its way
   into the stone" and pack the vent, and Halden "read the river itself, judging not
   where it ran *now* but where it would run," the cause arrives as genuine
   discovery — the reader learns it the instant the bearer does. The closing line,
   "Two people, long ago... had reached forward across three generations of drought
   and held the dying hollow up" (sc-39p), lands as earned, not restated, because
   nothing on the page had spent it.

2. **Era texture — 3/5.** The *late* age is dense and living (Marn's cistern, Pell's
   one knocking loom, Ode's wading staff). But the rubric asks for the FOUNDING Age
   as a living place — and arm-1 has almost no Founding present. It exists only
   inside the bearer's single backward glimpse at sc-39p: Sera and Halden appear as
   figures-of-cause ("a woman knelt at the lower seam"; "Halden, the dyer, in a
   season of trouble"), seen through the lens of what they will mean later, not lived
   in their own loud morning. The craft-row dispute is a "last dead echo," not a
   scene. The Founding Age is vivid as *revelation* but thin as *place*.

3. **Time-travel coherence — 5/5.** The two ages lock cleanly on one ground. sc-39p
   makes the crossing physical and meaningful — "the bearer crossed, not the gorge,
   but the years," the dust pulling back, the dead row "crowding suddenly with carts
   and noise" — and every late-age artifact (the seam Marn cannot read, the mark Ode
   trusts) is paid by a Founding act the bearer alone can see. The cistern, ford, and
   bridge-tree all close. Crossing is the *only* way the causes connect, which is
   exactly what the briefing demands ("Only the bearer... can connect them").

4. **Overall time-travel game — 5/5.** This is the structure a time-travel reveal
   wants: live in the consequence, not knowing the cause, then cross back and *find*
   it. The asymmetry of knowledge — the late folk who "called it luck when they
   called it anything at all" (sc-36p) versus the bearer who "saw it whole at last"
   (sc-39p) — is the whole engine, and arm-1 runs it at full force.

## arm-2 — four scores

1. **Reveal impact — 2/5.** arm-2 opens with the entire Founding trunk lived in real
   time. The reader watches Sera find the rot — "what comes away under her nail is
   dark and soft — a creeping rot" (sc-03) — and pack the vent (sc-04) and cut her
   mark (sc-05); watches Halden read the river, "this one stays firm" (sc-09), and
   set the stone (sc-10–11). So when the late-age mystery is posed ("why this
   cistern, and no other," sc-29p) the reader already holds the answer, and the
   sc-39p "reveal" only restates what sc-03–sc-11 showed: "you watch the keeper, Sera,
   find the creeping rot... and pack it tight... This is why the cistern runs clean."
   It is confirmation, not discovery. The bearer even narrates the foreknowledge at
   sc-30p — "you know that vent... You watched it packed and sealed three generations
   gone" — so the late age plays as dramatic irony, not mystery.

2. **Era texture — 5/5.** This is arm-2's real strength. The Founding Age is a living
   place: sc-01 ("the hollow is young and it knows it. Nothing here is tired yet"),
   the craft-row "all clatter — looms knocking, a dyer's paddle slapping" (sc-01,
   sc-07), the actual cart-dispute argued out with two trades "shouting over whose
   carts ruined whose footing" (sc-08), Sera offering the bearer a cup of clean water
   (sc-06), the field-elder pressing the gorge-sapling into the bearer's hands and
   half-believing it (sc-20). The people have a present, work, and voices, not just a
   causal function.

3. **Time-travel coherence — 4/5.** The mechanics are clear and well-built — sc-17
   teaches the pipe ("One whistle, one place, two times"), sc-22 states the thesis
   ("The young hollow is the old hollow's cause"), and the planting fork (sc-24–sc-26)
   is genuinely posed as a choice for "people you'll never know." The ages lock on one
   ground. It loses a point only because the coherence is *told* up front (sc-22
   spells out the causal rule before any consequence is felt), which flattens the
   crossing into illustration of an already-stated principle rather than a discovered
   link.

4. **Overall time-travel game — 3/5.** As a layout of a time-travel *world* it is
   fuller and more grounded than arm-1, and the planting fork adds an interactive
   stake arm-1 lacks. But as a time-travel *game* — whose payoff is the reveal — it
   spends its own ammunition: by lacing the late age with the bearer's foreknowledge
   (sc-30p) and front-loading every cause, it converts its best card (the reveal)
   into a recap.

## The forced A/B call

**Winner: arm-1.**

What makes arm-1 work: it lives the consequence *before* the cause, so the late age
is a real mystery and the past arrives as a discovery. The reader sits inside Marn's
not-knowing, Ode's faith-without-a-story, Pell's silence-she-can't-hear — and only
then crosses back to *find* Sera's seal and Halden's stone. The reveal at sc-39p
delivers information the page has deliberately starved the reader of, so it lands.
arm-2 lives the past in full and the late-age folk don't know it — which is a fine
dramatic-irony shape, but it is the *opposite* of a reveal: the reader knows
everything and merely watches the bearer re-confirm it. For a game whose core
question (per the rubric and briefing) is the earned discovery of *why the late age
survives*, arm-1's ordering is the one that makes that question pay.

**What the loser (arm-2) does better:** Era texture and the lived Founding present.
arm-2's young hollow is a genuine place — the loud craft-row, the cart-dispute
actually argued, Sera handing over a cup, the field-elder's sapling — where arm-1's
Founding Age exists only as a backward glimpse of cause. arm-2 also gives the bearer
real agency the reveal-first telling cannot: the gorge-sapling fork (sc-24–sc-26) is
a true choice made for unborn strangers, which is arguably the most *interactive*
beat in either telling, and the explicit teaching of the pipe and the causal thesis
(sc-17, sc-22) make the mechanics unmistakable.
