# Judge 1 — verdict (A/B)

## What the two arms are

Both arms render the SAME planted-road future, sc-27p–sc-40p, almost
line-for-line in event content (Marn/cistern, Pell/loom, Ode/ford,
Tamsin/bridge-tree, the sc-39p crossing-back). The structural difference is
total:

- **arm-1** opens with the WHOLE Founding-Age trunk first (sc-01–sc-26):
  you watch Sera find the rot, pack the vent (sc-04) and cut her mark
  (sc-05); you watch Halden read the river and bed the boundary-stone
  (sc-09–sc-12). THEN the dry age (sc-27p–sc-38p). THEN the bearer crosses
  back at sc-39p to re-witness the same two acts.
- **arm-2** opens COLD in the dry age (sc-27p) — no Founding trunk at all.
  The cause of the clean water and the firm ford is withheld until the
  bearer plays the pipe and crosses back at sc-39p, where Sera's seal and
  Halden's stone are seen for the first time.

This is the axis the brief names: *the past arriving as a discovery* (arm-2)
vs. *the past lived in full while the late folk don't know it* (arm-1).

---

## arm-1 — scores

1. **Reveal impact — 2/5.** The sc-39p crossing-back ("This is why the
   cistern runs clean three generations on… This is why the ford holds")
   only RESTATES what the reader watched in full at sc-04/sc-05 and
   sc-09–sc-12. By the time arm-1 reaches the dry age, the reader already
   knows the seal and the stone are there; Marn's "why" (sc-29p) and Ode's
   "Somebody knew this water" (sc-35p) cannot land as mysteries to the
   reader because the answer was handed over 25 scenes earlier. The reveal
   restates rather than discovers.
2. **Era texture — 5/5.** This is arm-1's commanding strength. The Founding
   Age is a living, present place: sc-01's "loud with living," looms
   knocking, a dyer's paddle, a boy driving geese; sc-07's craft-row
   "wool and woad and wet stone"; sc-08's actual cart-dispute with two
   trades shouting. Sera (sc-02 "Mine to mind") and Halden (sc-12 "He set
   the stone for a craft-row squabble, nothing larger") are full people
   doing present work, not knowing what they save.
3. **Time-travel coherence — 5/5.** The crossing mechanic is taught
   explicitly and cleanly: sc-17 "stand on this ground young, play, and
   stand on this same ground old," and sc-22 "The two ages are not two
   places. They are one place, before and after." Same-spot crossings
   (Steps young → Steps old) make the lock between ages unmistakable, and
   sc-21/sc-26 set up the planting fork. The two ages lock together with
   total clarity.
4. **Overall time-travel game — 4/5.** As a *world* it is the richer,
   fuller artifact and teaches its own rules best — but as a *time-travel
   game* its core pleasure (learning why the late age survives) is spent
   before the dry age even begins, so the back half is confirmation, not
   suspense.

## arm-2 — scores

1. **Reveal impact — 5/5.** Because the Founding trunk is absent, the
   sc-39p crossing-back is a genuine discovery. The reader sits inside
   Marn's not-knowing (sc-29p "He has no story for it that satisfies him")
   and Ode's guess (sc-35p "someone… had set this stone exactly where the
   crossing held firm… could not say who, or when, or how they had known")
   with no answer in hand — so when the bearer crosses and watches Sera
   seal the rot and Halden read the river (sc-39p: "Not luck. Not mercy
   owed. Two people, long ago… had reached forward across three
   generations of drought and held the dying hollow up"), the answer
   arrives as earned payoff. The keeper's-mark callback ("the same
   gesture the bearer's own hand had unknowingly repeated three ages on")
   is genuinely surprising. This is the rubric's "earned discovery."
2. **Era texture — 2/5.** The Founding Age appears only inside sc-39p, as
   reported recollection ("at the Cistern Steps — younger, sharp-cut, the
   water leaping"). It is evocative but compressed into one scene; the
   young hollow is never inhabited as a present, living place. We get the
   dry age vividly (sc-32p's empty loom-row, sc-27p's "lid… over a pot")
   but the Founding Age stays thin — a flashback, not a world.
3. **Time-travel coherence — 4/5.** The lock is clear and the
   cross-generational causal chain is forceful (sc-39p: Halden's hand
   "looking, across all those years, straight at her" [Ode]). But the
   mechanic is taught only implicitly at sc-39p ("crossed, not the gorge,
   but the years"), with no prior establishing of the same-spot rule; a
   cold reader infers the wend-pipe's working rather than being shown it.
   Slightly less explicit than arm-1, hence 4.
4. **Overall time-travel game — 5/5.** As a time-travel game it does the
   one thing the genre lives on: it makes the late age's survival a
   mystery the reader feels, then pays it off by crossing time. The
   withheld past + the single converging reveal is the stronger game shape.

---

## A/B winner: **arm-2**

arm-2 is the better TIME-TRAVEL game. The genre's central pleasure here is
the question the briefing names — *why does the late age survive, and who
did it?* — and arm-2 is the only telling that keeps that question OPEN long
enough to pay off. By opening cold in the dry age and withholding the
Founding causes, it lets the reader live inside the late folk's
not-knowing: Marn's gnawing "why this one and no other" (sc-29p), Ode
trusting a stone whose maker is "past all the hollow's remembering"
(sc-35p), Tamsin crossing a tree that "should not be" (sc-37p). When the
bearer finally crosses time at sc-39p and sees Sera seal the rot and Halden
read the river, the past *arrives as a discovery* — and the discovery is
that two ordinary people reached three generations forward without knowing
it. The time-crossing is what delivers the answer, which is exactly what a
time-travel reveal should do.

arm-1, for all its richer world, defeats its own reveal: it shows the seal
(sc-04) and the stone (sc-11) in full BEFORE the dry age, so the sc-39p
crossing-back can only restate a thing the reader already watched. The
mystery never exists for the reader, so the payoff is confirmation, not
discovery.

## What the loser (arm-1) does better

arm-1's Founding Age is a genuinely **lived, present world**, and that is a
real strength arm-2 simply lacks. arm-1 inhabits the young hollow as its own
time: the loud craft-row (sc-01, sc-07), the actual cart-dispute that drives
Halden to the stone (sc-08–sc-10), Sera as a working keeper with a habit of
touch (sc-02), the field-elder pressing the gorge-sapling into the bearer's
hands (sc-20), and the weighed planting choice (sc-24–sc-26). arm-1 also
teaches the time-travel MECHANIC far more clearly — sc-17's same-spot rule
and sc-22's "one place, before and after" — where arm-2 leaves it implicit.
Read as a *two-age world*, arm-1 is the fuller and more explicit artifact;
it just spends its mystery too early to win as a time-travel *game*.
