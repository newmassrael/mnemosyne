# Judge 3 — verdict (A/B, two tellings of the time-travel game)

## Structural note (what I see on the page)

- **arm-1** is the *full* sequence: the Founding-Age trunk lived in present tense first
  (sc-01–sc-26 — Sera sealing the rot, Halden reading the river and bedding the stone,
  the bearer learning the pipe, the planting fork), *then* the Withering-Age future
  (sc-27p–sc-40p).
- **arm-2** contains *only* the Withering-Age future (sc-27p–sc-40p). The Founding Age
  is never lived in its own present; it appears only inside the sc-39p crossing-back,
  as a thing the bearer goes to *discover*.

I judge only what is on each page.

---

## arm-1 — four scores

1. **Reveal impact — 2/5.** By the time the bearer crosses back in sc-39p to "see why,"
   the page has already shown the reader the whole cause twice over. We watched Sera pack
   the vent course on course and cut "three strokes and a notch" (sc-04, sc-05), and we
   watched Halden read the firm ford and bed the stone (sc-09–sc-11) — and then the bearer
   *re-watched both* in sc-15/sc-16. So sc-39p ("This is why the cistern runs clean...
   This is the seal under all that good water") only re-states what the reader has now
   seen three times. The line "Now you know the why" rings false because *we* never lost
   it. It restates, it doesn't reveal.

2. **Era texture — 5/5.** This is arm-1's clear strength. The Founding Age is a living
   present: sc-01's "looms knocking, a dyer's paddle slapping in its vat, somebody calling
   a price," the goose-boy in the shallows; Sera's "Mine to mind... since I could carry a
   pail" (sc-02); Halden's "A word's forgotten by spring. A stone's still saying the same
   thing when we're all dead" (sc-10); the field-elder handing over the gorge-sapling,
   "a living bridge, if anyone's left to want one" (sc-20). The young hollow is fully
   inhabited, with work, dispute, and people who own their craft.

3. **Time-travel coherence — 5/5.** Crossing is mechanically clear and thematically
   tight. sc-17 establishes the rule cleanly ("stand on this ground young, play, and stand
   on this same ground old"); sc-21–sc-22 walk the bearer across and back and name the
   thesis ("The young hollow is the old hollow's cause... they are one place, before and
   after"). Each Founding act is later locked to its Withering consequence on the same
   ground (vent→clean water, stone→firm ford, sapling→bridge-tree). The two ages lock
   together with no slippage.

4. **Overall time-travel game — 3/5.** A complete, coherent, vividly-built game — but the
   ordering spends its own payoff. Because the Founding causes are shown in full *before*
   the late age, the bearer's climactic return (sc-39p) and the late-age people's not-
   knowing (Marn in sc-29p, Ode in sc-35p) carry far less charge for the reader, who is
   never in the dark. Strong world, blunted discovery.

---

## arm-2 — four scores

1. **Reveal impact — 5/5.** This is arm-2's engine. We meet the cistern as an unexplained
   mystery — Marn "does not understand it... has no story for it that satisfies him"
   (sc-29p); the bearer presses the seam and reads the keeper's mark but "knew no more
   than Marn what it meant" (sc-31p, sc-39p). The reader is held in that not-knowing right
   up to sc-39p, when the crossing-back finally *shows* Sera find the rot and seal it, and
   Halden read the river and cut the stone — "Not luck. Not mercy owed. Two people, long
   ago... had reached forward across three generations of drought and held the dying
   hollow up." Because the cause was withheld, the reveal lands as an earned discovery,
   not a restatement. (One small note: arm-2 calls Halden a "dyer" in sc-39p where arm-1
   calls him a mason — a minor wobble, but it does not blunt the reveal.)

2. **Era texture — 3/5.** The Founding Age is only glimpsed inside the sc-39p flashback —
   Sera at the seam, Halden reading the river, "a Brae Hollow green and loud and whole" —
   vivid for its length but compressed into one paragraph. We never live in it. By
   contrast the *Withering* Age is richly textured (the dead springs named in sc-27p:
   "the seep under Tinder Rock, the cold well at Marrow Lane"; Pell weaving "over the top
   of a silence," sc-33p; "Mind the stone, the mothers said," sc-36p). On the rubric's
   axis — vividness of the *Founding* Age as a living present — arm-2 is necessarily thin,
   so a 3.

3. **Time-travel coherence — 5/5.** The single crossing is deployed for maximum effect
   and reads cleanly: sc-39p's "the dust pulling back like breath drawn in... they crossed,
   not the gorge, but the years." The two ages lock — the very gestures rhyme ("the very
   gesture the bearer's own hand had unknowingly repeated three ages on"; Halden's stone
   "the hand had been looking, across all those years, straight at her," sc-39p). Cause
   and consequence are pinned to the same ground with real craft.

4. **Overall time-travel game — 5/5.** Taken whole, this is the stronger *time-travel*
   game. It uses the structure of time travel — a present that cannot see its own past,
   and a single crossing that recovers it — as the actual machine of the story. The late
   age's blindness is dramatized (Marn, Pell, Ode, Tamsin each not-knowing), and the
   bearer's crossing pays it off. The withheld cause is the point.

---

## A/B winner: arm-2

**Why.** A time-travel game lives or dies on the gap between what one age can see and what
another can. arm-2 *keeps that gap closed for the reader* until the crossing-back opens
it: we sit inside the Withering Age's not-knowing (Marn's "I do not know why," sc-29p; the
unreadable keeper's mark, sc-31p; Ode trusting a stone "set there by someone in an age
none of them remember," sc-36p), and only at sc-39p do we cross and *discover* Sera's seal
and Halden's ford as the bearer does. The reveal is earned because the cause was withheld.
arm-1 inverts this: it shows the Founding causes in full first (sc-04/sc-05, sc-09–sc-11),
so by sc-39p the "reveal" only re-states what we watched happen, and the late age's
mystery is no mystery to the reader. The past arriving *as a discovery* is what makes
arm-2 the better time-travel game; the past lived in full up front is what undercuts
arm-1.

## What the loser (arm-1) does better

**Era texture — the lived Founding present.** arm-1 actually inhabits the young hollow as
a place with its own day, work, and people: the loud opening (sc-01), Sera's keeper's
pride (sc-02–sc-06), Halden settling a real cart-dispute with the line "A stone's still
saying the same thing when we're all dead" (sc-10), the field-elder's gift of the
gorge-sapling (sc-20). arm-2 only ever glimpses the Founding Age in one compressed
flashback paragraph; arm-1 lets the reader *live* there. arm-1 also carries the planting
fork (sc-24–sc-26) as a genuine player choice, which arm-2 presents only as an
accomplished fact (the bridge-tree already grown, sc-37p). For Founding-Age vividness and
for showing the agency behind the bridge, arm-1 is the stronger telling.
