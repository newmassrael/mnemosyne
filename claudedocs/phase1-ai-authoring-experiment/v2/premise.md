# Premise — "Cold Signal at Vantor Pass"

This is the ONLY story input you are given. Everything else — the events, the
characters' secrets, what is really happening, the scene sequence, the branches,
the causality — is yours to invent. This is a LARGE story (see the scope at the
bottom): plan it top-down before you write any detail.

## Setting

A railway junction station high in a mountain pass, in the early age of steam and
the electric telegraph. The junction is the only crossing through the range; in
winter a single bore (the Vantor tunnel) carries the line, and the telegraph wire
strung along the track is the pass's one voice to the valley towns below. A relief
train climbs from the valley once a day at most; when the weather closes, the
junction is cut off for days and the wire is everything.

Three nights ago a rockslide came down across the cut just outside the tunnel
mouth during a blasting operation, burying a track-gang and closing the bore. The
pass is now snowed in. A relief train — carrying a company inspector and the
families' last hope of reaching the trapped — is expected at first light.

## Characters (you may add more)

- **Wren Calloway** — the young night telegraph operator, newly posted to the
  junction, still learning the company's codes and the older hands' shorthand. The
  point-of-view character.
- **Stationmaster Auld Halloran** — who has kept the junction for thirty winters,
  trusted up and down the line. Wren's master. He countersigns every order that
  goes out on the wire.
- **Margaret Voss** — from the valley town below, whose brother was in the buried
  track-gang; she came up on the last train before the snow and cannot get back
  down. The community's link to the outside.

## The inciting mystery

Working the night log, Wren finds that the blasting order the night of the slide
bears a timestamp that cannot be right — it was logged as *authorized from the
valley office* at an hour when the wire was down for ice, so no message could have
crossed it. And the warning the track-gang's foreman swears he sent up the wire —
to hold the blast until the cut was clear — is nowhere in the log at all, as if it
was never received, or unrecorded. Someone ordered that blast without the
authorization the record claims, and the warning that would have stopped it is
missing.

## The central dramatic question

What really happened behind the altered telegram — and what should Wren do about
it? Halloran will not explain the timestamp. At dawn the relief train arrives with
a company inspector who will take the log as evidence, and the wire is the only way
to send word down the pass before then. Wren can SEND THE TRUTH down the wire
(expose it before the inspector can bury it), HOLD THE LINE (protect Halloran / the
junction / let the company's account stand), or ACT ALONE (take the evidence — or
the wire itself — into Wren's own hands). What is really behind the order is for
YOU to decide and make true in the fact base, and the responses need not lead to
the same place. Let what each character BELIEVES drive them, even where it differs
from the truth you set.

## Structural constraints (these are requirements, not suggestions)

- Roughly **60–72 scenes total** — a full novella-scale branching story, not a
  sketch.
- **At least TWO branch points (forks).** One PRIMARY fork (Wren's central choice)
  producing distinct world-lines, AND at least one further DOWNSTREAM fork on one
  of those lines (a fork off a fork is supported — a later choice that splits a path
  again). Aim for **4–6 distinct terminal world-lines** in total, each reaching its
  OWN distinct ending that follows from the choices that branched it.
- **At least FOUR genuine setups that PAY OFF later** — planted details that return
  and matter. At least two of them should be LONG-RANGE (the setup early, the payoff
  many scenes later, across the fork), so the story has to hold together over
  distance.
- **At least TWO characters who BELIEVE something different from the truth** at some
  point, acting on the belief rather than on what you the author know. (Use separate
  belief frames.) The valley town and the company will likely each believe a
  different false account of the slide; the truth is yours to set and is held by
  neither.
- When a later scene REFERS BACK to an earlier event (a callback, "the warning he
  swore he sent", "the night the wire was down"), that reference must be
  STRUCTURAL — point at the establishing scene — not a bare mention. (The
  author-brief explains exactly how.)

Tell the best, most coherent branching story you can at this scale. Make it hang
together: every consequence should have a cause already placed earlier in the same
world-line, every world-line should reach a real ending, nothing important should
dangle, and the load-bearing turns should be unmistakable among the detail.
