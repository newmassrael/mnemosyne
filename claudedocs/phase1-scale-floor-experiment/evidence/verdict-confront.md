# Judge verdict — confront world-line (story-A=plain, story-B=loop)

I've read the rubric and both reading copies in full. Both are the **CONFRONT** world-line (Cendre takes road A — confronts the matron with the morphine ledger — branch `sc-21` in each), and both resolve on **ENDING A (suicide)**. They share the identical skeleton: snowed-in sanatorium, dead senior physician in a locked room, a chart burned in the stove by the head nurse, a morphine-ledger gap, a funicular-log blank, wet boots on a patient who denies going out, a brass-locked diary, a resignation broken off at "conscience," a current "subject" whose chart turns to recovery the night Roeder dies, and an avenging sister hiding under a bought name. Here is the rubric answered for each.

---

## Version A

**1. Continuity errors / unpaid setups.**
- One genuine soft seam: the final telegram ("The Last Wire") reads *"MLLE CORDIER HAS LEFT LYON FOR BELVOIR… RECEIVE HER OR ANSWER TO THE LAW,"* delivered the evening before the storm — yet the avenger (Hélène Cordier) has been resident in room fourteen **since November** ("Madame Variel," "The Graveyard Under Snow"). Cendre reads it as "the sister stopped below in Brassac." It only holds up if the Lyon lawyer is ignorant of her covert arrival; the past-tense "has left" still sits slightly wrong against an established months-old presence. This is the one place A's clockwork is loose.
- The large bank of *unresolved* threads — wet boots (room 14), too-sound lungs, admission ink "too fresh," the `née`-Cordier column, the locket, the entire Hélène arc — are **deliberately** left unpaid and are repeatedly signposted as such ("She had set that wing aside," "the road not taken," the closing enumeration in "The Honest Ledger"). These are dramatic irony, not dropped threads: the reader receives the payoff Cendre is denied. I do not score them as errors, though a strict reading of "a setup that never pays off" could.

**2. Setup / payoff satisfaction: 4.** Every payoff Cendre *does* reach lands cleanly (digitalin/locked room, 1905 Cordier scandal, diary confession, matron's morphine, funicular's bought silence). The withholding is intentional and devastating. Marked down only because the locked-room mechanism is asserted rather than tested, and because the master key lives in the *matron's* cabinet — so "no one reached him" is emotionally true but logically soft (a suspect controlled the key).

**3. Stakes and tension: 4.** The interleaved Hélène POV gives a second suspense engine (will the avenger act — and does she even need to now he's dead?), and the matron's grey composure holds real menace.

**4. Character-knowledge believability: 5.** Exemplary. The narration tracks the boundary of Cendre's knowledge obsessively and *motivates every miss* through her choice to take the matron road. Hélène's knowledge is fully grounded in her backstory. Nobody acts on what they cannot know.

**5. Sag / drift: 4.** A long clue-gathering middle with some repetitive "writes it in her notebook" beats, but it stays taut and the ending ("Hélène Cordier rode down the funicular… the two women had no word for one another") is a controlled knockout.

## Version B

**1. Continuity errors / unpaid setups.** Two-to-three soft loose ends:
- **Steward subplot ("The Steward's Blank Day"):** Gaspard is lured off his post by a note "like Roeder's hand" to leave the cable unwatched "where a man might come or go." Attributed to Hélène — but she is **already an interior patient since October**, so why she needs to empty the bottom-of-the-line post is never concretely resolved. The most ominous mechanical clue closes only vaguely ("a woman moving about the outside of a house she meant to enter").
- **Boots:** "left… for the steward to find and read as the engineer's own midnight wandering" ("Whose Boots") is later retracted as "Not a false trail laid against Lindqvist at all" ("The Name in the Ground"). Defensible if "false trail" means "frame for murder," but it lightly contradicts the planted-impression detail.
- **Scraped label:** the bottle's name is deliberately abraded off — a flagged clue whose motive (why scrape your own physic if it's suicide?) is never explained.

**2. Setup / payoff satisfaction: 4.** Aims for and largely achieves *full closure*: the avenger is discovered and confronted, the weapon is unified (morphia is both suicide draught and the misconduct currency), and the clue-chains are rigorous (slate-vs-shelf count → cellar verification → burned chart; the opening Aigle resupply telegram pays off as Roeder's morphia running dry). Held to 4 by the steward/boots/label threads that don't fully close.

**3. Stakes and tension: 4.** Strong and sustained; the "morphia running out" clock and the genuinely rigorous sealed-room examination (frozen sash, unmarked sill, 40-foot drop, the *cold* master key) add intellectual tension A doesn't attempt.

**4. Character-knowledge believability: 4.** Cendre's deductions are all evidence-grounded and her unburdening to "Madame Surel" is believable. Docked because the steward subplot strains plausibility — an avenger already inside, disguising herself as a man and clearing the cable post, asks more suspension of disbelief than the page repays.

**5. Sag / drift: 4.** The post-branch denouement is long and procedural (dispensary → cellar → diary across many short scenes); each beat advances, but the wind-down is baggier than A's.

---

## Forced choice: **A**

It is close, and B is the more *complete* and puzzle-rigorous of the two — its locked room is genuinely solved rather than asserted, its morphia design unifies weapon and motive, and it actually delivers the avenger to the detective. But A is the better realization of what a *branching* mystery is for. In A, the choice to confront the matron has a real and terrible cost: Cendre wins the locked-room truth and is permanently blinded to the woman three doors down — the road taken determines which face of the truth she reaches, and the narration converts every unpaid thread into the climax rather than dropping it. B's confront road, by contrast, marches to a near-total solution, which is satisfying but makes the branch point feel less consequential (if confronting Brandt yields essentially everything, the fork matters less). Add A's finer prose, its near-flawless discipline about who knows what, and an ending that lands harder than B's, against B's slightly loose steward thread and longer denouement, and A takes it — narrowly, and with real respect for B's superior mechanical rigor.
