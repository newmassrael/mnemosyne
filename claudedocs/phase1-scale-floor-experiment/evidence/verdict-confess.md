## Rubric

### 1. Continuity errors

**Version A — 1 minor contradiction + 2 under-resolved setups.**
- *Minor timing wrinkle.* In **The Graveyard Under Snow** Hélène goes out to the grave "before the storm broke fully… while the wire still hummed on its poles"; in her confession (**The Night She Went to the Grave**) she frames the same walk as happening when "the line was down." The wire-snap's position relative to her walk is fuzzy. Reconcilable (she's describing the night as a whole), but a soft seam.
- *Unresolved setup.* The funicular log shows a **recent** bought-silence run "a night not three weeks before the storm" (**The Caretaker's Book**), confirmed in **The Caretaker's Silence** as someone who "came up after dark and gone down before the light… muffled to the eyes." This recent intruder is never identified or explained — Hélène's confession only covers the grave-walk the night of the death. A genuine dangling thread (the *historical* 1905 gaps do pay off as the protocol cover-up; the recent one does not).
- *Under-resolved setup.* The morphine-ledger discrepancy is built up hard (**The Ledger**, **The Matron's Account**) but its resolution is vague: it's folded into "her morphine" the matron must "answer for," without ever stating where the grains went. A's method is a separate "serum," so morphine is left as loose business.

**Version B — 1 significant contradiction + 1 broken thread + 1 minor loose end.**
- *Significant, load-bearing contradiction.* **The Patient Who Watches** calls the avenger's name false in two ways — "a name that was not her name" and "**Surel** was a borrowed coat." But the confession (**The Name Laid Down** / **Six Years' Patience**) has her declare "**Surel is my own name** — it was mine before it was ever his, and his before it was the stone's," with "every line of [the papers] forged but the one that matters." Surel cannot be both a "borrowed coat" and her true birth-name. This is the clue the whole mystery turns on (the repeated name in the living register and the burial book), so the contradiction sits at the load-bearing joint. The same early passage is also garbled on identity ("somebody Roeder had once written into a ledger and then crossed out" — she was never his patient; her *brother* was).
- *Broken/dangling thread.* The telegram. **The Line Goes Dead** has the evening's "last telegram" be a routine Aigle supply order, which Cendre reads herself and Gaspard calls "the last of it." Yet **The Steward's Two Logs** later produces a telegram "addressed to the Doctor… the evening before the storm… she could not yet place its weight," and **What Hélène Also Did** has Hélène watch "the last telegram come up and lie unanswered." Either there are two contradictory "last" telegrams, or the supply order Cendre already read is being re-introduced as an unrecognized mystery. Worse, this ominously-framed telegram is **never paid off** — its contents are never revealed and it drives nothing.
- *Minor loose end.* The bottle's label is "scraped away… deliberately," underlined as a clue against suicide ("A man does not abrade the name off his own physic," **The Bottle's Name**) — but when the diary confirms Roeder filled the bottle himself, why *he* scraped it is never addressed.

To B's credit, two potential "errors" are actually deliberate and well-handled: the burnt chart is openly flagged as the one secret this world-line's gentle solving never reaches (**The Chart No One Names**), and the morphia *is* the method, so B's morphine thread closes more tightly than A's.

Net: A's continuity problems are minor/soft; B carries one serious self-contradiction at the center of its own clue plus a fully broken telegram thread.

### 2. Setup / payoff satisfaction
- **A — 4.** Nearly everything lands, and one payoff is genuinely excellent: the telegram Cendre has carried, unopened, in her own pocket since the storm (**The Line Goes Down**) turns out to be the lawyer's warning that the sister has *already left for Belvoir* (**The Wire That Came Too Late**) — the avenger was at his table while he died unaware, and the warning that might have armed him rode up the hill in the investigator's coat. Diary, digitalin→suicide, née-column "Cordier," boots→grave, the Sève cipher→true recovery all resolve. Dock for the loose morphine and the unexplained recent funicular run.
- **B — 3.** Strong craft in places — the paper-packed boots (**Whose Boots**), the forged note that empties the steward's post (**The Steward's Blank Day** → **What Hélène Also Did**), morphia-as-method, and the elegant choice to leave the burning forever outside Cendre's knowing. But the central identity reveal is undercut by the Surel contradiction, the heavily-foreshadowed telegram pays off nothing, and the scraped label is dropped.

### 3. Stakes and tension
- **A — 4.** Same snowbound premise, plus live jeopardy: the Sève girl may be in the serum's "borrowed fortnight" before collapse, and the carried telegram is a lit fuse the reader watches.
- **B — 4.** Equivalent premise; Lindqvist's draining "borrowed summer" is poignant. Slightly less live jeopardy (his decline is expected, not a mystery), but the murderer-among-us pressure holds.

### 4. Character-knowledge believability
- **A — 5.** Meticulous gating; the narrator repeatedly fences what Cendre does *not* yet know (the burnt chart, the matched telegrams, the grave). No one acts on unknowable information.
- **B — 4.** Also carefully gated for Cendre's deductions and Hélène's six-year knowledge — but the Surel "borrowed coat / my own name" contradiction muddies the believability of the identity logic the cast is reasoning over.

### 5. Sag / drift over length
- **A — 4.** Long, with some repeated notebook-"constellation" and Hélène-deliberation beats, but each re-tread adds new material (the Sève motive deepening) and escalates.
- **B — 3.** Noticeably re-treads its own conclusion: **The Vial in the Candlelight**, **The Key Cold on the Board**, **The Word He Stopped On**, **What She Has and Has Not** each re-arrive at the identical "I have the misconduct but not the death — it cuts both ways." Thematically deliberate (the sealed room's ambiguity), but it flattens the back half into a plateau.

### 6. Forced choice — **A**

Both are accomplished, atmospheric, and tonally controlled on the same matched world-line, and B has real strengths (a more rigorously worked sealed room, the paper-packed boots, morphia integrated *as* the method, the burning deliberately left unreached). But A wins on the axes that matter most for a mystery. Its continuity is clean where B's is not: B contradicts itself at the exact hinge of its plot — whether "Surel" is the avenger's false name or her true one — which damages both the central clue and the confession, and it dangles an ominously-framed telegram that resolves into nothing. A, by contrast, lands its threads (the unopened-telegram reveal is the single best payoff in either file, turning the locked-room solution into genuine tragic irony), gates character knowledge airtightly, and sustains tension with live jeopardy instead of re-stating its ambiguity. A's flaws (a soft wire-timing seam, a loose morphine thread, one unexplained funicular run) are minor next to a load-bearing contradiction and a broken setup. A.
