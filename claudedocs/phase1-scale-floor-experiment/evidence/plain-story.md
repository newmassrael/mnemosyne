# Belvoir

*A branching mystery. Sanatorium Belvoir, formerly the Grand Hôtel Belvoir, in the Valais Alps. Winter, 1909.*

Scenes carry stable ids (`sc-01`, `sc-02`, …). Scenes `sc-01`–`sc-20` are the shared trunk, common to every world-line. Fork points are marked with a `CHOICE:` line naming each option and the scene id it continues to. Endings are marked `ENDING`.

Two registers run through the book. Scenes in **Cendre's** view hold only what she has reasoned her way to — her *working theory*. Scenes marked with another mind (Sister Ottilie, Hélène) hold the *ground truth* the reader is given and Cendre is not.

---

## sc-01 — The Line Goes Down

By morning the storm had made an island of them.

Dr. Irène Cendre stood at the tall, frost-feathered window of the old reading room and watched the world end at the terrace balustrade. Beyond it was nothing — a white nothing, sky and slope and gorge all dissolved into the same blank wool. Somewhere under it lay the funicular track that was the only road down to Brassac in the valley, and somewhere along the ridge lay the telegraph wire. The avalanche had taken both in the night, the wire snapping with a sound she had mistaken, half-asleep, for a rifle shot.

Belvoir had been a grand hotel once, in the years when consumption was a thing wealthy people went to the mountains to outlive. The gilt still flaked from the cornices; the dining room where patients now took their cure of milk and cold air still wore its chandeliers like a dowager wearing her jewels to breakfast. Forty rooms, and this last winter of the season they held nine patients and a handful of staff, all of them now sealed in together until the thaw. Three weeks, Bochat the caretaker had said, spitting. Three weeks at the least before a man could get down that slope alive.

In the pocket of her grey wool jacket Cendre still carried the last telegram the wire had brought up before it died — handed to her at the office window the evening before the storm, addressed to Dr. Roeder, marked *private*. She had meant to give it to him at supper. He had not come to supper. She would take it to him after rounds, she decided, and turned from the white window to begin her day.

It was Sister Ottilie who met her on the stair, very pale, to say that Dr. Roeder did not answer his door.

## sc-02 — The Locked Room

The consulting room was on the first floor, at the quiet end, where the corridor lost its carpet and the cold came up through the boards. Cendre knocked, and the silence behind the door had a particular weight to it, the weight of a room that holds something still.

"He locks it at night," said Sister Ottilie Brandt, the matron, her hands folded against her apron. "Always. He keeps the chartes here, the — the confidential papers." She did not look at the door. "I have sent Berthe for the master key."

There was, then, more than one key. Roeder carried his own; the second, the master, lived in the matron's office in a locked cabinet, and it was this that young Nurse Berthe brought up at a run, breathless, and surrendered into Cendre's hand rather than the matron's. The lock turned heavily. The door swung.

He was at his desk, fallen a little sideways in the chair, one arm hung down. Augustin Roeder, fifty-eight years old, senior physician of Belvoir, with his silver head tipped back and his mouth slightly open and his face — Cendre crossed to him quickly — his face quite peaceful, quite without alarm. The skin was cold. He had been dead some hours. On the blotter before him his own key lay where he had set it down. The stove in the corner ticked with a low heat.

"His heart," murmured the matron from the doorway. "He had warnings of it. He told me himself."

Cendre did not answer at once. A physician learns the look of the heart's failure, and this was near enough to it. But near enough was a phrase she distrusted. She noted, without yet knowing why she noted them: a small brown medicine vial fallen on its side by his right hand; a black diary, shut, with a brass lock; the door that had been locked from a room a dead man could not have left.

## sc-03 — The Stove

They left her there a moment — Cendre gone down the corridor to send Berthe for paper and the death-certificate book, the matron to fetch a sheet to cover him. So Sister Ottilie Brandt was alone in the consulting room when she did the thing the reader will remember and the doctor will never know.

She went first to the door and listened. Then to the long cabinet behind Roeder's desk, where the old charts were kept that he allowed no one else to touch, and she did not fumble; she knew exactly the one she wanted. It was among the oldest, the paper gone soft and yellow at the folds, a name inked across its tab in Roeder's own younger hand. She did not need to read it. She had written half of it herself, four years ago, and had not slept a clean night since.

The stove door opened on a breath of heat. The chart went in folded, and she held it down with the poker while it caught, while the name browned and curled and the four-year-old ink of it flared once, bright, and was gone. She watched until there was nothing on the grate but a black leaf of ash that broke when she touched it.

Then she closed the stove, and set the poker straight, and folded her hands, and was standing so — grave, composed, a nurse beside the dead — when Cendre came back into the room with the certificate book under her arm and smelled, without remarking it, the faint sweetish reek of burnt paper, and thought only that the matron had built up the fire against the cold.

## sc-04 — What the Doctor Saw

Cendre worked the way she had been taught to work, which was slowly and on paper. She set her clinical notebook open on the corner of the desk and began to write down the room.

*Augustin Roeder. Found locked within. No mark of violence. Face composed. Lividity consistent with some hours since death, the body cold. Apparent cardiac failure —* and here her pen paused, because the word *apparent* had a habit of becoming *certain* in tired hands, and she would not let it.

She took up the small brown vial. The label, in the dispensary's copperplate, read *Tinct. Digitalin.* — the heart's own medicine, given in drops to steady a failing pulse, and given in not very many more drops a poison that stops the heart and leaves it looking, to the cursory eye, as though the heart had simply stopped of itself. Two fingers of the stuff were gone from the bottle. She corked it, and wrote its name, and slipped it into her pocket; from this moment the vial was in her keeping and no one else's.

The black diary would not open. The brass hasp held a lock no larger than a thumbnail and there was no key on the desk, nor in the dead man's waistcoat, where she made herself look. She set it aside.

The stove she did glance at. The grate held a soft grey ash, more than a few coals would leave, and a smell hung over it she could not place and did not pursue. *Fire lit,* she wrote, *room warm.* She underlined nothing. She did not know yet that the most important fact in the room had already gone up the chimney while her back was turned, and that she had smelled it, and let it pass.

## sc-05 — Madame Variel

In the south wing, in the white iron bed of room fourteen, the patient registered as Madame Hélène Variel heard the news brought up by a maid and turned her face to the wall so that no one should see what was in it.

It was not grief. She had not come to Belvoir to grieve for Augustin Roeder. It was something colder and more bewildering — the sensation of a long-drawn breath let out into empty air, a blow swung at a man who is no longer standing where you aimed. For two years she had arranged herself toward this house: the false name on the admission papers, the rehearsed cough, the small fortune spent to buy a bed in a dying season under a widow's identity that did not exist. She had a thing to say to Roeder, and other things she had let herself imagine doing, and now the storm had locked her in with him and his heart had stopped in the night and cheated her of every one of them.

Or had it. She lay very still and did not let herself finish the thought.

Under her pillow was a locket she had not worn openly since the valley. She did not open it now either; she knew the face inside it by heart, a young man's face, her own brow and chin in it, and the true name that went with the face and with hers — the name that was not Variel and that she meant no one in this house to learn until she chose. When the maid put her head round the door to ask did Madame need anything, the woman in the bed had arranged a sick smile over all of it, and said no, no, only that it was a terrible thing, a doctor dying so.

## sc-06 — The Last of the Season

By afternoon the house had organized itself around its new fact, the way a body organizes itself around a wound. And the fact carried with it an awkward corollary that no one said aloud until Monsieur Pernet said it, because old men say everything: with Roeder dead, the only physician left above the snowline was the young woman they had all been so politely surprised to receive in November.

"So we are in your hands, mademoiselle," said Pernet — seventy, asthmatic, a permanent resident who had outlived three head physicians and intended to outlive a fourth — and he said *mademoiselle* and not *docteur*, and smiled to take the sting from it, and did not take the sting from it.

Cendre let it go. She had spent a career letting it go. She walked the house instead and counted what it held: the matron Ottilie Brandt, iron and silent; Nurse Berthe, young and frightened of something Cendre could not yet name; Bochat in his lodge with the dead funicular; a cook, two maids; and the patients — nine of them, taking their cure on the glassed verandah in fur rugs, breathing the prescribed cold. Among them, in a wheeled chair too large for her, a girl of perhaps eighteen with a translucent face and a chart Cendre had not yet read. Marguerite Sève. *Margot,* the others called her, fondly, the way people are fond of the very ill.

Three weeks until the thaw. Three weeks until anyone could be summoned, or sent for, or sent away. Whatever had happened in the locked room, Cendre understood, would have to be understood here, by her, before the snow let go of them. She found she was glad of the work. It is easier to be the only doctor than the least one.

## sc-07 — The Ledger

The dispensary was a converted linen room, narrow, smelling of carbolic and camphor, and it was here, while Cendre took stock of what medicines the house could call on for three weeks, that Nurse Berthe stopped being frightened of her and started being frightened *to* her.

"I should have told Dr. Roeder," she said. "I had meant to tell him. This week." She had the morphine ledger open on the shelf, the long-columned book in which every grain of the drug was signed out against a patient and a date, and her finger was shaking over it. "The stores, mademoiselle. They do not agree with the book. The book says we have given less than the cupboard has lost. Going back — I do not know how far back. Weeks. The matron keeps the key to the cupboard and the key to the book both, and when I asked her she said I had miscounted, and I had not miscounted."

Cendre looked at the columns. A physician's signature, repeated; a careful, even hand; doses entered like prayers. And, when you held the figures up against the half-empty bottles in the cupboard, a gap. Morphine that the book swore had never been given, gone from the shelf all the same.

"Who signs these?" she asked.

"Dr. Roeder. And the matron, for him, when his hand was bad." Berthe shut the book as though shutting it could unsay it. "You will not say I told you."

"No," said Cendre, and meant it, and wrote in her own notebook, that night, under the locked room and the vial of digitalin, a third line that she did not yet know how to connect to the others: *The morphine does not balance. Custody of the ledger: the matron.*

## sc-08 — His Papers

As acting physician she had every right to his desk, and she told herself so twice before she sat down at it.

Roeder's papers were the papers of an orderly man with one disorder in him somewhere. Correspondence squared, accounts current, a drawer of patient files in alphabetical calm. And then, beneath the blotter, where a man puts the thing he means to finish and cannot, a single sheet in his own hand, unsent, undated past the year:

*To the Cantonal Medical Board — Gentlemen, it is with — * and a struck-out line, and then: *I beg to tender my resignation from the post of senior physician at the Sanatorium Belvoir, for reasons of health and of conscience, the latter being —* and there it stopped, the *being* trailing into white paper, the pen lifted and never set down again.

Conscience. She read it three times. A man does not resign his life's post for conscience and then leave the sentence open unless the rest of it frightened him.

The last telegram was there too, the one still in her own pocket — no, this was another, an earlier one, kept. It read, in the wire's flat capitals: *MONSIEUR — THE FAMILY OF J. CORDIER HAS INSTRUCTED ME IN THE MATTER OF 1905 — CORRESPONDENCE WILL FOLLOW BEFORE THE SPRING — VEILLOZ AVOCAT LYON.* Cendre turned it over. The name Cordier meant nothing to her; the year 1905 meant nothing; a lawyer in Lyon meant only that a dead man had had business, as the dead do. She set it with the resignation letter, in the drawer she now thought of as the drawer of unfinished things, and beside them the black diary with its little brass lock that she still could not open.

The reader may set the two telegrams side by side. Cendre could not; she carried one and had read the other and did not yet know they were the same conversation.

## sc-09 — The Graveyard Under Snow

There is a place above Belvoir that the brochures of the grand hotel never showed: a walled half-acre on the shoulder of the slope where the sanatorium buries those of its guests the mountain keeps. The cure does not always cure. When the valley is closed there is nowhere to send a body but up, and so Belvoir keeps its own ground, and its own book of the dead — a chapel register in the matron's care, names and dates in a column going quietly down the years.

Hélène Variel knew the place though she had been at Belvoir only since November. She had known of it before she ever came. In the worst hour of the night, before the storm broke fully, while the house slept and the wire still hummed on its poles, she had gone out into the first of the snow in her boots and her dark cloak and stood at one stone among the newer stones, where the carving still held its edges:

JONAS CORDIER · 1886–1905 · *requiescat*

Nineteen years old. Her brother. Admitted to Belvoir in the spring of 1905 with a shadow on one lung and a good chance, the valley doctor had said, a very good chance at his age — and dead by the autumn of that year, of *the natural progress of the disease*, the certificate read, signed Augustin Roeder. It had taken her two years and most of what their father left to learn what the certificate did not say: that her brother had not been left to the natural progress of anything, that there had been a treatment, an experiment, *Roeder's protocol*, tried on the young and the friendless and the cheap, and that the book of the dead up here held more than one name that the protocol had written into it.

She had come to make Roeder answer for it. She knelt in the snow at the stone, and the snow soaked black up the leather of her boots, and she made the dead boy a promise she did not yet know how she would keep.

## sc-10 — A Working Theory

That night Cendre sat with her notebook and the lamp and tried to make a single shape of what she had.

She wrote her facts in a column, the way she had been taught: *A locked room. A peaceful body. A vial of digitalin, two doses short, fallen at his hand. A resignation unfinished for conscience. A diary I cannot open. A ledger of morphine that does not balance. A house that cannot be left for three weeks.* She looked at the column a long time.

Then, below it, she wrote her theory, and underlined the word *theory* so that she would not forget what it was: *A man under some private pressure, his heart already weak, takes — by accident or design — a fatal measure of his own heart-medicine, and is found locked in the room where he keeps his secrets. If this is all, it is sad and it is finished.* She did not believe it was all. The morphine sat wrong. The matron's smooth face sat wrong. A man does not break off a sentence at the word *conscience.*

She had, she would later see, reasoned her way exactly to the edge of the truth and stopped at the wrong landmark. She did not write *Madame Variel*, because Madame Variel was only an invalid in the south wing she had not yet examined. She did not write *the stove*, because the stove had been the matron's housekeeping. She did not know there had been a chart, or whose, or that it was ash; she did not know that a woman under a borrowed name had stood at a grave in the snow that same night with murder folded up inside her grief.

What she knew was that the morphine ledger was the loose thread nearest her hand. In the morning, she decided, she would pull it. She would go to Sister Ottilie Brandt and ask her, plainly, to explain the book.

She closed the notebook. Outside, the snow that sealed them in went on falling in the dark, patient as an assize, and would not melt for three weeks.

## sc-11 — The Matron's Account

She had meant to ask plainly. She had written it so, the night before, in the clean resolved hand one uses for resolutions one will not keep.

But morning is a colder counsel than lamplight, and crossing the hall to the matron's office Cendre understood two things at once: that she had only Berthe's word and a column of figures, and that Sister Ottilie Brandt was not a woman one accused on a column of figures. *You will not say I told you.* The girl's fright was a debt Cendre meant to honour. So she came at it sidelong.

"The stores," she said. "With the house shut for three weeks I must know what we hold. The morphine especially — for Monsieur Pernet's nights, and the girl Sève, and whoever else may want easing before the thaw."

"You may rely on the cupboard." Ottilie did not rise. Her hands lay folded on the desk as they had lain folded on her apron at the locked door, and her face had the same composed grey calm. "Dr. Roeder prescribed it. I dispensed it. When his hand was bad I signed the book for him, in my own, with his leave. It is all written down."

*It is all written down.* It was, Cendre thought, precisely the trouble: it was all written down, and the writing did not agree with the shelf. But to say so was to spend Berthe, and to spend her too soon, before she knew what the matron's calm was made of.

"Of course," she said instead. "I only wished to know the house was provided."

"The house is provided," said Ottilie, and held her eyes a moment longer than the words required, so that Cendre went out with the sense of having been the one examined.

## sc-12 — The Caretaker's Book

Bochat's lodge stood apart, by the head of the funicular, and smelled of oil and pipe-smoke and wet wool. He did not ask her to sit. The machine was dead, he told her before she had asked: the track lay under a house's weight of snow at the second pylon, and would stay so until the thaw took it, and no, mademoiselle, there was no other way down — not for a goat, not for a fool.

She believed him. But on the wall by the winch hung a book on a string, oilcloth-covered, and when she asked what it was he was a breath too slow to answer. The maintenance log, he said then. The runs, the brakes, the cable greased; every day a line. She turned the pages while he watched. A patient hand, years of it, the funicular's whole dull life set down nightly — and gaps in it. Nights left blank. Not many; a scattering down the years, here a single night, there two together, the ruled lines empty where every other night had its dutiful entry. One such blank was recent, a night not three weeks before the storm.

"You missed these," she said.

"A man forgets. A man sleeps." He took the book from her hands, gently enough, and hung it back on its string. "Weather, most like. When it blows you don't go out to the cable to write down that you didn't run it."

It was a reasonable thing to say. She might have believed it, too, but for the care with which he did not meet her eye, and the way the older blanks, when she had turned to them, seemed to fall in clusters — as if forgetting, in Bochat, kept its own seasons. She thanked him and went out into the cold, and added the book to the list she carried in her head of things in this house that were almost, but not quite, an account of themselves.

## sc-13 — Marguerite Sève

As acting physician she read the charts, all nine, in an evening — the slow declines and the hopeful plateaus, the temperatures plotted like coastlines. Eight of them told the kind of story she knew. The ninth was the girl.

Marguerite Sève, eighteen, admitted the winter before with both lungs gravely involved and a prognosis Roeder had set down in the cautious Latin of a man not expecting to be proved wrong. And then the story of the chart changed its hand. The temperature curve, which had climbed and guttered like the rest, broke its own pattern: a sharp descent, a steadying, a girl mending by the numbers where no mending had been looked for. Beside the change, in Roeder's column, were notations Cendre could not read — not Latin, not the dispensary's shorthand, but a private cipher of letters and figures, dated, regular, like doses of something that had no name in any book she knew.

She sat with it a long time. A patient may rally; the disease has its remissions, its cruel false dawns. But a rally does not come with a cipher beside it. Someone had been *doing* something to this girl — something he wrote in a hand meant for no other eyes — and the doing had begun before the turn of the year and run through the winter in a steady cadence that stopped, she saw, turning the last leaves, on a date she now knew by heart. The cipher ended the night Roeder died. After it the chart was only Berthe's round plain hand: temperature, pulse, the figures gone silent.

She did not yet know what had been given, or why, or whether it had healed the girl or merely held her. She wrote in her notebook: *Sève — abrupt change. R.'s private notation. Ceased the night he died.* And beneath it, because it was true: *Ask the girl nothing yet.*

## sc-14 — What Hélène Knew

In room fourteen Hélène Variel kept her own chart, in her head, of the girl in the wheeled chair.

She had marked Margot Sève within a week of coming — not because the child was the frailest (she was not) but because of who came to her. Roeder himself, in the evenings, when the verandah emptied; Roeder with his soft step and his black bag and the particular gentleness men keep for what they are using. Hélène had seen that gentleness before, worn by the same man, in another year, bent over another bed. She had not been there to see it then. She had spent two years learning to recognize it now.

She knew the shape of the thing. A young patient, poor — Margot had no people that anyone spoke of — and friendless enough that no letters would be written if the cure went strangely. A treatment given in the evening, off the chart, or on a chart no one but the doctor read. It was *his protocol*, running again under this same roof, on this same kind of girl, while Hélène lay three doors off with a brother in the ground above the house and a locket beneath her pillow and her hands, she had thought, tied by the simple fact that the man was already dead.

Already dead. She turned the words over. Roeder's evenings with Margot had stopped — of course they had; the dead keep no appointments — and the girl, these last days, had a little more colour, as though some weight had lifted off her chest the night he died.

It eased nothing in Hélène. If anything it sharpened the old grief to a point. She had come to make one man answer, and someone, it seemed, had closed his account before she could reach him. She lay still in the dim room and found that she needed, very badly, to know who.

## sc-15 — Room Fourteen

She came to room fourteen on her rounds, the chart under her arm, and saw only what the chart told her to see: Madame Hélène Variel, widow, of Lyon, admitted in the autumn, consumptive, room kept dim.

The woman in the bed was perhaps five-and-thirty, dark, still, with a stillness Cendre took at first for the patience of the long sick. She submitted to the examination without complaint. And it was the examination that did not sit right. Cendre warmed the stethoscope and listened, and listened again, moving the bell across the woman's back while she breathed on command — and what she heard was not what two years of the disease, as the papers swore, should have left behind. There were old scars in the sound, yes. But the breath went down full on both sides, easy, very nearly clean. Cendre had listened to the chests of the truly consumptive every day for a decade. This was not one of the worst of them. It was hardly one of them at all.

"You are stronger than your file, madame," she said lightly, straightening.

"The mountain air." The woman's smile was prompt and faint and gave nothing away. "Dr. Roeder always said I should be a wonder of the mountain air."

Cendre let it pass. People mend; people are misfiled; a hopeful physician writes a hopeful page. But back at the desk she drew out the admission papers and looked at them properly for the first time, and a second small thing nagged at her — the hand of them, the very ink, a shade too fresh and too even for a document two years and three offices old, as though some part of the record of Madame Variel had been written all at one sitting, lately, by someone with the leisure to be careful.

She noted it. She did not know what it meant. She set room fourteen, gently, a little apart in her mind.

## sc-16 — The Book of the Dead

To understand the morphine she had to understand the dying, and to understand the dying she asked the matron for the registers.

Ottilie gave them up without a flicker — the great ruled admission register, and beside it, bound in black, the chapel book in which Belvoir set down those it could not send home: the book of the dead, the matron's own to keep, names and dates going quietly down the years to the walled ground above the house. Two books, two columns of the same people, the living register feeding the dead one a name at a time. Anyone might lay them side by side, Cendre saw, and read the whole traffic of the house between the door and the stone.

She read for the morphine — whose last weeks had wanted easing, whether the cupboard's losses kept company with the names in the black book — and the figures half-confirmed her and half did not, and she frowned over them and did not look up.

So she did not study the column the admission register kept for each patient's people: next of kin, and for the married women a maiden name set down in the clerk's old formula, *née.* She ran her eye down it once, names and more names, forty seasons of somebody's daughter, and not one of them stayed with her — because the single name in that column that should have stopped her hand meant, as yet, nothing to her: a name she had read once, in a lawyer's telegram, in a dead man's drawer, and filed as the ordinary business of the dead. The reader, who has stood at the grave in the snow, knows the name. Cendre turned the page.

She closed the books and gave them back. *The matron keeps the dead as she keeps the morphine and the keys,* she wrote that night. *Everything in this house passes through her hands.*

## sc-17 — The Wet Boots

It was Berthe who brought it, in the lowered voice she now kept for Cendre alone.

One of the maids, turning out the south wing, had found a pair of women's boots set to dry behind the stove in the service passage, and had mentioned them only because of their state: soaked through, the leather dark and stiffening, salt-rimed at the welt as boots are when snow has melted into them and dried and melted again. Soaked as though their wearer had stood out in deep snow, and not briefly. They had been found the morning the doctor was found — and they belonged, the maid was nearly certain, to room fourteen.

Cendre went and asked, lightly, making it nothing at all. Had Madame been out of doors before the storm? In the cold, with her chest?

Madame Variel looked at her from the pillow with mild, tired surprise. Out of doors? In February, in the night, in her condition? She had not left the wing, she said, since the snow began; the doctor — the late doctor — would never have allowed it. She had kept to her bed. Where else would she have been?

It was said simply, and well. But the boots had been wet, and someone had worn them through deep snow on or about the night Roeder died, and the woman who slept three doors from the matron's office said she had not stirred. Cendre thought of the consulting room at the quiet end of the corridor, and of how short a walk it was from the south wing, and made herself write down only what she knew: *Boots, room 14, wet through. Owner denies going out.* She did not know that there was anywhere to walk to in that snow but the consulting room. She did not know about the grave.

## sc-18 — The Matron's Watch

Sister Ottilie Brandt had not slept a whole night through in four years, and did not expect to begin now.

She knew what the young doctor was doing. She had watched physicians work for thirty years and could read an inquiry the way Cendre read a chest: the questions about the stores that were not really about the stores; the registers asked for; the careful rounds in the south wing; the Sève girl's chart drawn and studied. The mademoiselle was patient, and she was not a fool, and she was pulling, gently, at every thread the house hung by. One of those threads, drawn far enough, came back to a stove and a folded chart and a name that should have stayed in the ground with the boy it belonged to.

That, at least, was done, and safely; ash keeps no secrets. Ottilie did not let herself regret the burning. She had carried Jonas Cordier's chart in her chest like a stone for four years, and to put it in the fire had been — God forgive her — the first easy breath she had drawn since the morning they found the doctor. Other things she did regret. She did not name them, even to herself, even at three in the morning; she had made herself expert in not naming them.

She thought of the morphine, and of what she would say when the question came at last plainly, as it would. She thought of the master key, and of mornings, and of how much a composed face could carry across a room if it never once let itself be surprised. She had brought this house through worse than a dead doctor. She folded her hands in the dark and resolved, very simply, to go on protecting it — from the thaw, from the families, from the law, and from the clever, decent young woman who kept writing in her little book.

## sc-19 — Two Roads

The diary still would not open.

She had tried it again that evening — the brass hasp, the lock no larger than a thumbnail, the penknife she had not, in the end, the will to use, because forcing it felt like conceding where the case had brought her. Roeder's private record lay shut between her hands, keeping whatever a man keeps under so small a lock, and its key was somewhere in a house she could not leave.

She turned instead to her notebook and read back the week. *Locked room. Digitalin, two doses short. A resignation broken off at the word conscience. Morphine that does not balance — custody, the matron. A funicular log with nights gone out of it. The Sève girl mended under a cipher that stopped the night he died. Wet boots, and a denial. A widow whose lungs are too sound for her papers, and papers too fresh for their dates.* It made a constellation, and not yet a figure. But every line of it, she saw, ran one of two ways.

One way ran to the matron. ## sc-21 — The Ledger on the Desk

## sc-22 — What the Cupboard Was Short

## sc-30 — The Constellation Turns

## sc-34 — The Forced Lock

The diary had defeated her for three weeks — a small clasp of brass, no bigger than a thumbnail, and behind it whatever a guilty man writes when he is sure no one will read. ## sc-35 — Begun Again

The winter pages were the worst of all, because they were not remorse. They were work.

He had taken the serum up again this season — *the preparation nearer right than before,* he wrote, *the subject well chosen* — and beside the notes ran his private cipher, the same locked shorthand she had found once already and could not read. ## sc-36 — The Master Key, and the Other Hand

She made herself, that night, reason the locked room again — coldly, as if it were another woman's case.

## sc-37 — The Matron's Hands

Sister Ottilie Brandt had kept houses of the dying for thirty years, and she knew the sound of an inquiry changing its mind.

For a week the young physician had haunted the east corridor, the stores, the books — her own country, where she had nothing left to fear, the one chart that could have damned them all being long since clean grey ash in the consulting-room stove. ## sc-38 — Sitting With Her Again

## sc-45 — The Reckoning

She did not sleep, and toward morning she made her reckoning the way she made everything — in the notebook, by columns.

She wrote it at the dead man's desk, by his own lamp, the last clear night before the thaw.

In the cause of death she set down what was true: that Augustin Roeder had died of digitalin, self-administered — the vial short by the two doses his own hand had measured, the door locked from within on his own key, no other able to reach him in the night. *By his own act,* she wrote, *under apprehension of exposure.* A cause of death is a small line; the truth was large. On the pages the magistrate would read after, she wrote the rest. The serum he had devised, and the poor, friendless subjects. Jonas Cordier, dead in 1905 beneath a false certificate, his name still in the chapel's book of the dead. The lawyer's wire from Lyon. The resignation broken off at the word *conscience.* The matron's complicity, and the oldest chart turned to ash in the stove. The funicular's bought silence. And the diary, in his own hand, that confessed and sentenced him in one breath. She left nothing in the ground. Belvoir would go down the mountain disgraced, and Sister Brandt would answer to the law for all she had not refused to do; that was the price of an honest ledger, and Cendre paid it.

She had reached, by the hard road, every truth the house contained but one.

For the reader, who has stood at the grave in the snow and read the name under the locket, knows the one gap in her complete account. She never weighed the too-sound lungs against the file, nor the admission ink laid too fresh at one sitting, nor the wet boots dried behind the south-wing stove; she never set her eye a second time on the *née* column where the clerk had written *Cordier;* she pictured the avenging sister stopped below in Brassac and never counted the widow in room fourteen, three doors from the girl she had left unwatched. Hélène Cordier rode down the funicular when the thaw came, in the same car as the physician who carried the whole truth of his death in her bag — and the two women had no word for one another, having never, in all that sealed white month, been introduced.

## sc-57 — What Mercy Fits on a Form

The thaw was a week off, and she had spent three nights of it learning the difference between what was true and what she would write — which she had been taught at her lessons were the same thing, and had found, above the snowline, were not.

The death itself she would not lie about; she had not lied yet and would not begin over Roeder. Digitalin, self-administered; the door locked upon his own key; the vial short by his own measure — a suicide, under the apprehension of a past coming up the line. That much would stand on the certificate plain, and beneath it the protocol entire, for the magistrate: the serum, the boy Cordier, the false grave, the matron's part. Sister Brandt would answer for her concealment and her morphine and her old complicity — a great deal to answer for, and none of it murder, for there had been no murder here.

And there had been no murderer. That was the thing the certificate did not need to know. A woman had come up the mountain under a bought name with a wish in her bag, and had carried the wish to a grave in the snow and left it there, and the man had cheated her by dying of his own accord while she wept over her brother's stone. *Intent,* the law would call it, and want her for it — want the forged papers and the locket and the confessed knife of a purpose she had never once raised. The law liked a thing it could hold.

Cendre held the pen and would not give it one. She wrote the suicide, and she wrote the protocol, and she did not write the name of Hélène Cordier anywhere upon the page. There was no line on the form for a grief that had meant murder and done nothing; and where the form was silent, she found, the physician might be merciful.

## sc-58 — Down the Mountain

**ENDING B — "Mercy"  (WL-B1 · QUIET-CONFESS · SUICIDE, sister shielded)**

When the thaw broke the track at last and the funicular ground down its cable for the first time in a month, they went down the mountain in twos and threes, the last of the season — the cook, the maids, old Pernet wheezing at the rail, the Sève girl wrapped to the eyes and pink and alive, Berthe with the certificate-book sealed under the physician's frank for the magistrate at Brassac.

Hélène Cordier went down among them, ill in no register, named in none. She wore the widow's grey she had bought in Lyon and the locket beneath it, and she carried her brother's true name out of the house that had killed him and lied of it, and no hand was laid upon her arm. At the lower station she turned once and looked up at Belvoir — the grand white wreck of it, the chapel, the dark ground above where the snow was already going — and then she went on into the valley and out of the account, which was where Cendre had decided she should go.

The physician watched her into the crowd and felt no ease, only the weight of a thing chosen. She had certified the truth of the death, and laid the doctor's crime bare for the law, and shielded from the law the one soul in the house whom the law would have taken and called the taking justice. Whether it was justice or only mercy she did not know; she suspected, by now, that above a certain altitude the two could not always be told apart, and that the certificate book had no column for either. She had learned, in the end, exactly what mercy could be made to fit upon a printed form: a silence, in the place where a name would have gone.

## sc-59 — The Last Draught

"Which of us the law should want," the matron had said, and Cendre had had no answer that the certificate book would hold. She had one by morning. The law would want them both — the doctor for the boy, the matron for the doctor — but the doctor was past wanting, and so, by the time Cendre came up with it, was the matron.

Sister Brandt had not waited for the thaw. She had gone about her last evening as she had gone about thirty years of them — the medicine-round, the lamps, the cupboard locked — and somewhere in the grey middle of the night she had poured herself the draught she knew best, the same digitalin to a grain, and lain down in her narrow room off the office and not woken to the snow-light either. There was no letter. Cendre had not looked for one. The woman had said all she meant to say across the desk — *I poured him his medicine the same as I have every night since you were a child at your lessons* — and had poured herself the last of it sooner than ride the cog-rail down to a courtroom in the spring.

Cendre certified it as she had certified him: digitalin, by her own hand. And on the pages beneath she wrote the murder out whole — the evening draught stirred for a man who would then go and lock his own door upon it and die inside it seeming innocent, to stop his mouth before he could set down the serum and the boy and her own name beside his; the oldest chart burned not for the families' grief but for the matron's neck; the morphine; the long concealment; the years. The locked room that had looked like his alibi had been hers all along. She left it entire for the magistrate, in a hand that did not shake.

## sc-60 — The Cost of Going Quietly

**ENDING C — "The Active Evil"  (WL-B2 · QUIET-ESCALATE · MURDER)**

When the funicular ran again it carried three deaths down the mountain in its book: the doctor, poisoned in his locked room by the hand that fed him; the matron, poisoned by her own at the last; and between them, the death that had broken the case open and weighed heaviest of the three — Marguerite Sève, eighteen, dead of a serum begun again in the new year by a man who had not lived to watch it finish its work. Cendre had certified the girl plainly: the protocol, the borrowed fortnight, the fall. The second of Roeder's children, laid in the ground above the house beside the first.

That was the reckoning she could write on no form, and so wrote only in her own notebook, on the last night. She had gone quietly. She had set the morphine ledger back on its shelf and chosen the slow road — the verandah, the patient questions, the gentle turning-over of a careful woman — and the slow road had been honest, and had been wrong, because while she turned the matter over so gently the matter had killed a child three doors from her bed. Had she gone the other way that first morning, hard and direct, to the grey woman who poured the medicines — she would not let herself finish the thought. There was no certificate for a road not taken.

Hélène Cordier went down with the rest, cleared and unnamed: the sister whose intent was no crime and whose hand had touched no one, who had come to kill Roeder and instead stood by for the burial of a second of his children, and carried her locket down the mountain with nothing in it settled. Cendre let her go. She had a murderer already, and the murderer was dead, and the active evil of the house had in the end consumed itself — but not before it had spent, on its way out, the one life in Belvoir that no one had ever climbed the mountain to take.
