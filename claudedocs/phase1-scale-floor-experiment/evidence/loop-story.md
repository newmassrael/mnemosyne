# Belvoir

*A branching mystery. Sanatorium Belvoir, winter 1909.*

## sc-01 — The Line Goes Dead

The Sanatorium Belvoir stood where the Hôtel Belvoir had stood before it, eight
thousand feet above the valley and its God, and in winter the cloud closed under
the terrace like a second, lower country. Dr. Cendre had been two weeks in the
post and had not yet learned to trust the silence of so much snow.

The storm came down off the Diablerets a little after four. By five the wind had
the shutters speaking. At half past, Gaspard the steward carried the evening's
last telegram up from the cable office in the basement and laid it, damp at one
corner, on the hall salver: a supply order acknowledged from Aigle, the apothecary
promising morphia and quinine on the first clear funicular. Cendre read it over
his shoulder because there was nothing else to read.

"That will be the last of it," Gaspard said, "if the wind keeps on." He was not a
man who said things twice, and he did not. At a quarter to six the funicular made
its final ascent of the season — they heard the cable groan and stop — and
somewhere below the lip of the cirque a great weight of snow let go of the
mountain. The sound was not loud. It was only very large, and then it was over,
and the brass telegraph key in the basement, which had been clicking idly with
Aigle's good night, fell still and stayed still.

Nobody understood at once that the line was cut. They understood the cold. The
matron lit the second stove in the salon; the patients were counted up to bed;
and above them all Dr. Augustin Roeder kept to his consulting room and did not
come down to dinner. The thaw, Gaspard said, would not open the pass for three
weeks. He was right about that, too.

## sc-02 — Roeder Locks His Door

Cendre met him once more alive, on the gallery landing at ten, where the
consulting-room corridor ran off toward the old hotel's east wing. Roeder was a
tall, grey, exact man who wore his authority the way other men wore a watch
chain. He had a candle in one hand and his diary in the other — a small black
book with a brass lock, of the kind a man keeps when he does not trust his own
desk.

"You're prowling, Doctor," he said, not unkindly.

"I couldn't sleep for the quiet."

"You'll learn to." He turned the consulting-room key in its lock — a heavy door,
walnut, with the hotel's own brass furniture — and tried the handle twice to be
sure, as was his habit. "Whatever the night brings, it can wait for daylight.
Sound principle. Write it down." The morphia and the ledgers and the dangerous
little bottles all lived behind that door with him; he kept the only working key
of it on his own ring, and the house master key, he reminded her, hung where it
always hung, on the warden's board in Gaspard's office. "In case I should ever
lock myself in and die of my own caution," he said, and almost smiled, and did
not.

She thought afterward that he had looked like a man rehearsing a decision. The
diary's brass lock caught the candlelight when he tucked it into his coat. He
bid her good night, and the corridor took him, and she heard the bolt of his
private cabinet go home behind the walnut door. It was the last she saw of him on
his feet: an exact grey man, locking the night out, or himself in.

## sc-03 — The Door That Would Not Answer

Nurse Brandt knocked first, at seven, with his shaving water as she had done for
nine winters. Cendre learned this later; what she heard, from the floor below, was
the second knocking — harder, and then the flat of a hand — and Brandt's voice
losing its starch.

The head nurse was a square, capable woman with hands reddened by carbolic and a
face she had trained to show nothing. She was not showing nothing now. "He does
not answer, and the door is fast," she said, when Cendre came up. "He never bolts
against me."

The walnut door stood shut. The private key was in the lock on the inside — they
could feel it stop the keyhole — so Roeder's own ring was beyond reach within.
Cendre called his name to the panel and got the particular silence a room gives
back when it is occupied and will not speak. She sent Gaspard for the master key.
He came back too slowly and out of breath, and the heavy old key, when he brought
it, was cold from the board exactly as a key should be that had hung untouched all
night.

They turned it. The inner key, dislodged, fell to the boards inside with a small
domestic sound. The door swung. The cold of the room came out to meet them like
a held breath at last let go.

## sc-04 — Dead at the Desk

Roeder sat at his desk as though he had paused over his work and forgotten to
resume. His head was tipped a little, his right hand open on the blotter, his
spectacles folded beside an inkwell with the stopper out. The fire was long dead.
The cold had kept him as the cold keeps everything at Belvoir, exactly and
without mercy, so that he might have died at midnight or at six and the room would
not say.

Cendre did what she had been trained to do before she let herself feel anything.
No pulse, none for hours; the jaw and the hands already set; the lips and nails
gone the dusk colour of a stopped heart. On the blotter, near the open hand, stood
a small brown medicine bottle, its label turned away, perhaps a third gone. She
left it where it stood and noted that she had left it.

"His heart," Brandt said, from the doorway, not coming in. "He has had warnings of
it for a year. He would not rest." Her voice had recovered its starch.

It was the obvious reading, and Cendre, who was twenty-nine and tired and the only
physician now within three weeks of any other, was glad of an obvious reading. A
strong man of sixty, a bad heart, a hard winter, a door he had bolted himself
against a night that brought him nothing. She would write *cardiac failure* on the
certificate and she would be right to. She believed it as she thought it, and the
believing settled something in her chest that had no business being unsettled.

She did not yet think to ask why a careful man dies with the stopper out of his
ink and a bottle turned to hide its name.

## sc-05 — The Stove

Cendre went down to fetch her bag and a sheet, and for the length of that errand
the consulting room held only Nurse Brandt and the dead.

Brandt did not look at Roeder. She went to the shelf of current charts — the
buff folders of the living, kept at his elbow so he might reach them without
rising — and she drew one out by its number, which she knew without reading, and
she did not open it. The consulting-room stove was small and cold, but a stove at
Belvoir is never let go entirely out; a red eye still lived in its grate. She knelt,
and worked the iron door, and fed the folder in.

It did not want to burn. Card stock never does. She held it down with the poker
while the corner caught and the name on the tab browned and curled — a surname, and
above it an admission number lower than the season's, an older number, a number
that should not have belonged to anyone now drawing breath in the house. The
reader may see it brown and lift and go to ash. Cendre, on the stair with a folded
sheet over her arm, saw nothing, and would have sworn to a court that the records
of the dead man's room had not been touched.

Brandt riddled the grate until the card was grey flake among the coal ash, indistinguishable, and shut the iron door, and stood, and smoothed her apron with hands that did not shake. When Cendre came back she found the head nurse drawing the curtains against the snow-light, composed as Sunday, and thanked her for it.

## sc-06 — The Patient Who Watches

On the floor below, the patient registered as Madame Hélène Surel heard the
house change pitch — the running feet, the held voices — and understood it
completely, the way one understands a thing one has been waiting a long time to
hear.

She rose and stood at her window, where the snow came sideways, and she was very
calm. In the writing-case on her knees lay the admission papers that had brought
her up the mountain in October: a letter of referral from a Lausanne physician who
did not exist, a name that was not her name, a history of the lungs that was not
her history. She had paid well for the papers and they were good. *Surel* was a
borrowed coat. Under it she was somebody Roeder had once written into a ledger and
then crossed out, the sister of a man who had come to Belvoir to be cured and had
gone instead into the sanatorium's own ground, in a season the official histories
preferred to skip.

She had come up the mountain in October to look at the man's face while she told
him whose she was. The avalanche had spoiled that; one cannot settle a score with
the dead. She felt the loss of it as a real grief, oddly — she had rehearsed the
telling for six years. The reader knows her for what she is. Below her in the cold
the new young doctor was already, she supposed, deciding it was a heart. Let her.
The snow would hold them all in the same jar for three weeks, and there were other
truths in the house than the one in the consulting room, and Hélène Surel — that
borrowed, excellent name — had time now, and nothing but time.

## sc-07 — The Only Physician Left

By noon the arithmetic of it had reached everyone: the line dead, the funicular
stopped, the pass shut for three weeks, and on the mountain exactly one person
licensed to sign for a life or against it. Dr. Cendre, twenty-nine, was Belvoir's
whole medical estate.

She gathered the household in the salon and told them plainly what they already
felt. There were eleven patients wintering and nine of staff. She named the ones
who would matter to her later without yet knowing they would: Brandt, who held the
keys of the sickroom and the discipline of it; Gaspard, who held the keys of
everything else; and among the patients, Herr Edvard Lindqvist, a Stockholm
engineer six months consumptive, whose chart Roeder had carried about with him
like a worry. Hélène Surel sat at the back with her hands folded and asked one
quiet question about the burial of the doctor, which Cendre thought decent of her.

The dangerous stores were the first care. From the dead man's room Cendre took
the long green morphine ledger that had lived behind his door — every grain of
the house's morphia entered out and entered back, signed for in Roeder's small
upright hand — and Brandt surrendered, with reddened reluctant fingers, the
dispensary keys that belonged with it. Custody passed now from Roeder's authority
into Cendre's keeping, and she signed the foot of the last page to mark the
change, as the regulations of the canton required.
She did not read up the column. There was too much to do, and she trusted the
book the way one trusts a thing that has never yet lied. The green ledger went
into the drawer of her own room, and she kept the little key on the chain at her
waist, beside the watch that had been her father's.

## sc-08 — The Diary and the Letter

In the afternoon she went back into the consulting room to make it decent and to
take an inventory, which was a physician's duty and also, she admitted to herself,
an excuse to be alone with the puzzle of him.

The brass-locked diary lay in the breast of his coat where he had put it the night
before; she could not open it, and would not force it, and set it aside on the
desk to be opened properly when there was time and a small file. Locked, it
reproached her simply by existing — a careful man's most private book, and she had
no leave to read it and every reason to want to.

The unsent letter she found under the blotter, and that she did read, because it
was not sealed and her name was halfway down it. It was a draft, much struck
through, addressed to the Sanatorium's Board of Governors at Berne. *I have the
honour to tender,* it began, and the honour was his resignation, effective the
spring, on grounds of health — but the health was crossed out twice, and over it,
in the same upright hand pressed harder, he had written *and of conscience,* and
struck that through as well, and not found a third word before he stopped. It was
dated the day of the storm.

A man does not draft his resignation on grounds of conscience and then die
conveniently of his heart the same night without inviting a question, even from a
doctor who wishes very much not to ask one. Cendre stood in the cold room with the
half-finished letter in her hand and felt the obvious reading lose, very slightly,
its grip.

## sc-09 — The Log and the Boots

She took her questions to Gaspard in the steward's office, where the warden's
board hung with its rows of keys and the master key back on its hook, cold and
innocent. He answered what he could. The funicular had made its last run at a
quarter to six; he had logged it, as he logged everything, in the maintenance
book chained to the desk.

But the maintenance book had a gap. Cendre turned its pages and found the daily
inspection signed every dawn through the autumn in Gaspard's careful block hand —
*cable, brake, hauling-gear, sound* — and then, across the very day before the
storm, nothing at all. No entry, no signature, no line drawn to mark the omission.
The morning of the storm resumed as if the blank day had never been owed. Gaspard
looked at the gap a long moment and said, slowly, that he did not remember missing
it, and that he had never missed it in nine years, which was not the same as saying
he had not.

And there were the boots. By the office stove, set to dry, stood a single pair of
gentleman's boots, soaked through and rimmed white with melted snow-salt to the
ankle. They were Lindqvist's; the steward had found them outside the sick-wing
door at first light and brought them in to dry as a kindness. But Lindqvist was a
bed-patient under orders not to cross his own threshold, and would say, when she
asked him gently that evening, that he had not set foot out of doors in a fortnight
and could not think how his boots came to be full of the mountain.

## sc-10 — The Ledger and the Grave

That night, when the house was down, Cendre lit a second candle and at last read
up the green column of the morphine ledger, and the book that had never lied to
her told her a small, flat, terrible thing. The grains entered out did not match
the grains entered back. Across the autumn the discrepancy gathered quietly,
drachm by drachm — morphia signed out for patients in Roeder's upright hand,
against doses the patients' own charts did not record receiving. Somewhere a great
deal of the house's morphia had walked out of the book and not come home, and the
hand that signed it out was the hand now folded on a blotter upstairs.

She fetched the patient register to steady herself with names, and the register
undid her further. She had meant only to confirm the living against the book. But
the admission column carried, among the autumn's entries, one older surname she
half-knew, and when she crossed the corridor to the leather-bound burial record
that the old hotel had kept since it turned hospital — Belvoir buried its own when
the pass was shut — she found the same surname there, cut into the ground of the
sanatorium six years before. A name on the register of the living that answered, exactly, a name in the book of the dead.

And there was Lindqvist, whose chart she read last, by then past flinching: a man
six months declining, whose every weekly entry charted the slow defeat of a lung —
until a fortnight since, when in Roeder's hand the curve simply turned, and a dying
engineer was abruptly written well. Cendre sat among her three open books in the
small hours with the snow pressing the glass, and understood that she was not, after
all, going to be permitted the obvious reading; and that she had three weeks, one
corpse, and a houseful of the snowbound to find the true one in, before the thaw
came down and let the guilty walk to Aigle.

## sc-11 — The Bottle's Name

In the grey of the second morning Cendre went back into the cold room that was
hers to keep now, and took up the small brown bottle she had been careful to
leave standing. She had noted leaving it; she noted lifting it. The label,
turned to the wall on the blotter, was no label at all when she brought it to
the light — only a paper ghost, the printing scraped away to fibre, deliberately,
with something not sharp. A man does not abrade the name off his own physic.

She drew the stopper. The smell came up bitter and flat, with the far sweetness
of the poppy under it that she would have known blindfold. In the dispensary she
had the reagents and the patience; she spent an hour of the morning on a rough
assay, and the bottle answered for what her nose had already sworn — morphia in
solution, and not the timid solution one spoons to a wakeful patient. This was
strong enough that a mouthful would lay a strong heart down inside the hour and
leave the lips and the nail-beds the dusk colour she had read off Roeder with
her own eyes.

A third of it was gone.

She corked it, and wound it in lint, and carried it down to the locked drawer of
her own room where the green ledger already lay, and she set the two side by
side because in her mind they had begun to lie side by side: morphia missing from
the book, morphia standing strong at the dead man's open hand. She would not yet
say the word aloud, even to the snow. But she had stopped being able to think the
other one. Not *heart*. The cold thing the room had been trying to tell her all
along was simpler and worse, and it began with the letter that *heart* did not.

## sc-12 — The Sealed Room

If it was the bottle, then the bottle had to have a road into a room that had let
nothing else in. She gave the morning's second hour to the room itself, on her
knees and on a chair, reading it the way she would read a chest.

The single window gave on the north drop and the weather. Its sash was frozen to
the frame in a continuous seam of old ice, unbroken; she worked at it with the
flat of her hand and it would not so much as complain. Outside on the stone sill
the snow lay in an unmarked drift to the depth of a hand, its crust whole, not a
print nor a scrape nor the dark of a thumb in it. Forty feet of sheer wall fell
away beneath. No one had come or gone by the glass since the storm laid that snow,
and the storm had laid it the night he died.

The chimney was a hand's breadth at the throat. The door she already knew:
bolted, his own key stopping the keyhole from within, and the house master key —
Gaspard had sworn it, and the cold board bore him out — hung untouched on its
hook the whole night through. She stood in the middle of the floor and made
herself say the conclusion plainly, because a conclusion unsaid is a conclusion
half-believed. The room had been shut from the inside, and stayed shut, and no
living person had been inside it with Roeder between the turning of his bolt and
the turning of the master key at seven. Whatever had killed him had been in the
room already, or in the man already, before he locked the door. The poison did
not need a murderer in the room. It only needed to have arrived before the bolt.

## sc-13 — A Gap on the Shelf

She catalogued the consulting room as a physician must, and as an excuse to stay
among its secrets, and it was the cataloguing that turned up the small wrong
thing. Roeder had kept, in his upright way, a slate index of the buff folders on
the shelf at his elbow — the current charts, the living, numbered and ticked. She
ran the slate against the shelf to be tidy, and the shelf was one folder short of
the slate. A number was written and ticked as present that no folder answered to.

She carried the discrepancy to Nurse Brandt, because Brandt had kept these
shelves nine winters and would know their habits.

Brandt looked at the number on the slate without haste, and her red carbolic
hands stayed where they were folded. "That one," she said. "An old discharge.
The Doctor would not have an empty slot reproach him, so he left the line ticked
and sent the folder down to the archive boxes in the cellar when the patient went
out, last spring. Before your time, Doctor. Before the season." She said it the
way one reads off a familiar dose, and turned the talk to the cold, and the
matter closed itself like a drawer.

Cendre wrote *one folder, archived, to verify* in her own book and let it lie. It
was a small honest housekeeping wrongness and a hundred true ones could explain
it. She did not think — she had no reason yet to think — that a folder *ticked as
living* might have an admission number older than the season, nor that the cellar
boxes were a long cold walk for a busy woman to send her to while the grate in
this very room held its grey particular ash. The reader has watched that folder
brown and lift. Cendre marked it *to verify* and went down to her rounds.

## sc-14 — Madame Surel Is Kind

On the lower floor the patient called Hélène Surel kept her room well, and
received the new doctor's visit with the grave courtesy of a woman raised to it.
She had a good colour for a consumptive and a stillness that Cendre, tired and
glad of stillness, took for the composure of the genuinely sick who have made
their peace.

They spoke of Roeder. Madame Surel asked, lightly, whether the poor Doctor would
lie in the house ground until the thaw, as the others did — she had heard the
sanatorium buried its own — and whether it was true the old hotel had kept its
register of the dead since the first winter. Cendre, who that morning had begun to
find the room and the bottle unbearable to carry alone, heard herself say more
than she meant to: that she was no longer easy in her mind about how the Doctor
had died, that there were small things that would not sit, that she might have to
ask hard questions of the house before the pass opened. She said it for the
relief of saying it to a calm face.

The calm face received it. Madame Surel said only that she was sorry, that a
doctor's death in a doctor's care must weigh doubly, and that Cendre must spare
herself nothing in the asking — the truth was owed to the dead. It was decently
said and Cendre was grateful and went up lighter. She did not see — the reader
sees — that she had just told the one person in the house who had crossed an
ocean of years to learn exactly that: that the inquiry had begun, and what shape
it was taking, and how little the young physician yet knew.

## sc-15 — The Same Name, Twice

The burial register the house kept she would not read in the corridor where it
lived. She carried the heavy leather book across to her own desk under the lamp,
beside the patient register she had fetched the night before, and she set the
living against the dead column by column to be sure she had not dreamed the thing
that had undone her in the small hours.

She had not dreamed it. There, six years back in the burial hand, cut into the
ground of the sanatorium in a March the official histories skipped over: a man's
Christian name she did not know, and a surname she now did. *Surel.* And there in
the living register, admitted in October of the present season under a Lausanne
physician's referral: *Madame Hélène Surel,* the grave and grateful woman one
floor down who had this morning asked her, so lightly, about the register of the
dead.

A name will repeat. Cousins come to the same cure; whole valleys carry three
surnames between them. She told herself so and did not believe herself, because
the woman had asked. She wrote, under the morning's note about the archived
folder: *Surel — living, register Oct; Surel — buried, six yrs. Ask her of the
connection — gently.* She did not yet let the two facts touch the third, the
crossed-out ledger entry and the experiments nobody named; she had not the shape
for it. But she had circled, without knowing it, the very wound the woman had
climbed eight thousand feet to keep open, and written *gently* beside it.

## sc-16 — The Steward's Blank Day

Gaspard she found in his office among his keys, and she put the maintenance
log's blank day to him a second time, gently, because the first time had only
frightened him.

He was steadier now, and worse. He had remembered. On the afternoon before the
storm a boy had brought him a written word — he had taken it for the Doctor's
hand, or near enough, and had not kept it — calling him down to the cable-house
in the cirque to look at a fault on the hauling-gear that, when he climbed all
that cold way to it, was no fault at all. The brake was sound; the cable sound;
nothing wanted him there. He had come back up in the failing light cross and
puzzled and behind his evening, and the dawn inspection of the gear — the one
entry in nine years he had let fall — had fallen because he had spent the daylight
he kept it by on an errand to nowhere.

"Someone wished me off my post a few hours, Doctor," he said, slow, looking at
his own block hand in the book and the white silence where the next line should
have stood. "And down at the bottom of the line, where a man might come or go by
the gear if he knew it. I cannot make it mean anything good." Cendre could not
either. She left the log chained where it hung — it was the house's, not hers —
and carried away only the shape of it: that the day before Roeder died, a hand
that could pass for his had emptied the one office from which the mountain's one
road was watched.

## sc-17 — Whose Boots

The boots had stood drying by the steward's stove for two days, and the salt-rime
had dried to a white tide-line at the ankle. She asked Gaspard's leave and carried
them up to the light, because a thing found where its owner swears he never went
is either a lie or a loan, and she had begun to mistrust easy lies.

They were Lindqvist's; his name was inked on the tongue of each in a steward's
careful hand, the way the house marked what it dried and returned. But Lindqvist
was six months wasting, and a wasting man's foot grows thin and small inside its
own skin, and these boots had been worn out into deep snow by a foot that did not
fill them. At the toe of each, crushed down hard against the cap, she drew out a
wad of folded paper — packing, to take up the room a smaller foot left. Whoever
had crossed the snow in Edvard Lindqvist's boots had borrowed both the boots and
the size of the man, and left them at the sick-wing door at first light for the
steward to find and read as the engineer's own midnight wandering.

She sat with the cold leather in her lap and the paper wads beside it and made the
plain reading she was getting better at making. Lindqvist had not gone out. Someone
had gone out *as* Lindqvist — had wanted a track in the snow that pointed at a
bed-bound man written, a fortnight since and against all his lungs, abruptly well.
She did not unfold the paper yet. She set the boots in her own room with the rest
of it: the bottle, the ledger, the two registers, and now a pair of boots that had
walked somewhere their owner had not.

## sc-18 — Health, and of Conscience

That night she gathered the dead man's papers into her own keeping, for they had
no business lying about a room any of the house could enter. Two she took up the
stair: the brass-locked diary, which she still would not force — it lay in her
drawer as sealed and reproachful as the day she set it on his desk, a careful
man's last witness behind a lock she had no key to and no leave for — and the
unsent letter, which needed no key.

She read the resignation again by the one candle. *I have the honour to tender.*
The honour was his resignation to the Board at Berne, effective the spring, on
grounds of — and the word *health* stood struck through twice, and over it,
graven harder in the same upright hand, *and of conscience,* and that struck
through as well, and after it no third word at all, only the white of a man who
could not finish. Dated the day of the storm.

She let the pieces stand together at last and saw a shape in them she did not
like. Morphia walking out of a ledger in his own hand. A dying engineer written
well overnight in that same hand. A name crossed out of a book six years gone and
buried in the house ground. A resignation halted on the word *conscience.* It
made a physician who had done something to his patients he could no longer carry —
treatments off the register, paid for in the house's morphia and in at least one
grave — and who had sat down at last to give up his post for it, and had died
that same night with a scraped bottle at his hand. Whether the dying was his own
last act of conscience or someone else's first act of fear she could not yet say.
But she was done suspecting an accident. She let the working name of it in her
mind harden from *foul play* to a thing with edges: *Roeder's own misconduct, and
a death that closed the books on it.*

## sc-19 — Two Ways to Read a Ledger

It came back, as everything came back that week, to the green ledger and its
quiet subtraction. Morphia signed out, drachm on drachm across the autumn, in
Roeder's upright hand — against doses the patients' own charts never recorded
taking. The grains had walked. The book said only that they had walked under his
signature.

But a signature is not a witness to a dose; it is a witness to a pen. The morphia
lived behind the consulting-room door, true, and Roeder held that door's only key.
Yet the daily discipline of the dispensary — the drawing, the measuring, the
carrying of it to the wards — had been Brandt's nine winters together, Brandt who
had surrendered the dispensary keys to Cendre with such reddened reluctance, Brandt
whose hands did not shake. The discrepancy looked two ways at once and would not
choose. Either Roeder had drawn the morphia himself, signed for it himself, and
spent it on the treatments his conscience could not name — or the hand that
measured the daily doses had been quietly drawing more than the wards received,
and his signature at the foot of the column had been the roof over it, witting or
blind.

Cendre laid the ledger flat on her desk and understood that the book could not
settle its own meaning. Only two parties on the mountain knew where the missing
grains had truly gone: the woman who measured them out, and the patients into
whose arms they were, or were not, put. She could ask either. She could not, she
thought, ask both — not without the asking of one warning the other.

## sc-20 — The Two Roads from the Ledger

She sat a long time over the flat green book while the stove ticked and the snow
went on with its endless soft arithmetic against the glass. Three weeks. One
corpse. A houseful of the snowbound, and somewhere among them a hand that had
emptied a steward's post, packed a dead size into a sick man's boots, and drawn
strong morphia out of a book that would not say where it went.

Two roads lay open from the ledger, and she could walk only one of them first,
and the first would shape all the rest.

She could go to Brandt. Lay the book open on the dispensary table, set her finger
on the subtraction, and ask the head nurse of nine winters to her face to account
for the grains the wards never had — the keeper of the doses, the burner of no
chart Cendre yet knew of, the one woman whose composure had begun to feel to
Cendre like a held breath. A confrontation would force the issue into the open in

## sc-21 — The Book on the Dispensary Table

She chose the harder road, and carried the green ledger down to the dispensary
before her courage could cool. Brandt kept her kingdom there among the labelled
drawers and the carbolic and the cold clean smell of order, and she did not look
up at once when Cendre laid the book open on the deal table between them and set
one finger on the autumn's long subtraction.

"I have read it both ways," Cendre said, "and it reads the same both ways.
Morphia signed out of this house under the Doctor's hand, drachm on drachm,
against doses the patients' own charts never took. I am asking you, who measured
them, where the grains went."

Brandt read the column the way she would read a familiar dose, without haste, her
red hands folded on the table's edge. She did not deny it. She did not flinch from
the finger or the figure. "They went where he sent them," she said at last. "He
signed for what he ordered, and I carried what he bade me carry. If they did not
reach the wards, Doctor, it was because he kept cases of his own — off the books
you have been reading — and those I carried too."

So there it was, in the open, in an hour, as she had known it would be: cases off
the wards' books, and a keeper who would say so to her face. But Cendre felt the
cold underside of the bargain even as she heard it. She had asked, and so she had
told. Whatever Brandt was, she now knew exactly how much the young doctor knew,
and the long snow lay all around them, and there were three weeks yet in which a
steady hand might make any book agree with any story.

## sc-22 — What Brandt Carried

"What cases," Cendre said. "On whom."

"His own." Brandt's composure did not crack so much as set, harder, like water
going to ice. "Treatments he gave himself, behind his own door, of an evening — a

## sc-23 — The Cold Boxes in the Cellar

One thing Brandt had told her could be tested without Brandt, and Cendre went down
to test it that same afternoon, a candle in her hand and the cellar cold striking
up through her soles. Months back — in a season she had not been here to see — the
head nurse had accounted for that same missing folder another way: an old
discharge, she had said, sent down to the archive boxes when the patient went out

## sc-24 — An Old Number, Burned

It was the number that would not let her sleep. A chart is ticked living or it is
not; the slate had ticked this one living, current, a patient of the house — and
yet no living patient answered to it on any round she had walked, and the archive

patient who was in no bed. She sat with her own notebook and worked it the only
way it would go.

The admission number was an old one. She had the register by heart enough now to
feel the wrongness of it — a number from years before this season, before her,
before half the present staff, kept ticked at the head of the current slate as
though its owner still took soup at the end of the east corridor. Roeder had
carried one old case forward, year on year, alive on paper long after — she made
herself write the word — long after he was not. And when the Doctor died, the one
hand that knew which folder it was had taken it out of the world before the new
doctor could read the name on its tab.

## sc-25 — What the Bolt Still Keeps

And still it did not tell her how he died.

could lay out the misconduct now plank by plank — the diverted grains, the evening
injections, the old case kept alive on paper and burned to ash — and Brandt's own

## sc-26 — Bed to Bed

She chose the other road, and told no one she had chosen it. The ledger went back
into the locked drawer; her face she kept as mild as a morning round, and she went
bed to bed through the wards with nothing in her hands and a single soft question
folded inside a dozen kind ones. *What did the Doctor give you for the pain. How
much. How often.* She asked it of the woman with the wasted wrists and the boy from
Vevey and the old curé who coughed his rosary, and she carried no slate where they
could see it, and wrote nothing down until she was alone.

The answers, gathered, made a column of their own, and the column did not match
the book. Drops, they told her. A spoonful on the worst nights. The good Doctor
was sparing — too sparing, one or two said, with the mild grievance of the long
sick — and held his strong physic close. Bed after bed told her the same: that the
morphia signed out of the house in such quantity under Roeder's hand had not, in
any quantity worth the name, come to rest in these arms. The grains the ledger
mourned had never reached the wards at all.

She kept her counsel, and her gentleness, and went on. Down the corridor the
dispensary kept its order undisturbed, its keeper unwatched and unwarned, measuring
the day's small honest doses while the young doctor built, bed by quiet bed, the
proof that the great dishonest quantity had gone somewhere else entirely. It was
slower than a confrontation. It was also a thing no clever hand could hurry to
forestall, because no clever hand yet knew that it had begun.

## sc-27 — Herr Lindqvist's New Strength

Herr Lindqvist she kept for last and came to gently, for his was the chart that had
turned, and turned in a hand she had begun to mistrust. He was propped grey against
his bolster, the great frame of the Stockholm engineer gone to slats inside his
nightshirt, glad of company and a little ashamed of something, and the shame came
out before the gladness was done.

The Doctor had been good to him, he wanted her to know. These last weeks
especially — a new treatment, of an evening, that the Doctor gave him himself
behind the consulting-room door: a needle, and then an hour, sometimes two, in
which the cold went out of his chest and the warmth came up like a remembered
summer and he felt, God forgive him the lie of it, *strong.* Well, even. As though
the lung had quietly mended. He had let himself believe it; the chart, she knew,
had believed it with him — the dying engineer written abruptly toward recovery in
Roeder's upright hand.

It was the morphia. She did not say so to him. She read his "strength" for what it
was — the poppy's warm hour, dressed in a chart as a cure — and knew now where a
fair share of the missing grains had gone: not to ease the wards, but into this one
borrowed euphoria behind a private door. And because she was gentle, and patient,
she asked him too, lightly, whether he had been out — out at all, into the snow,
even once. He almost laughed. He could not cross his own room to the window,
Doctor; he had not stood unhelped in a month. Someone, then, had stood in his boots
and crossed the snow for him. She thanked him, and drew the blanket to his chin,
and carried both new certainties out quietly under her plain mild face.

## sc-28 — The Mouths That Remember

The wards held longer memories than their charts, and the quiet road let her walk
into them. Among the season's sick was one who had wintered at Belvoir before — a
spinal case, years on her board, who knew the house the way Brandt knew the
drawers, and who took the new doctor's gentle company as a gift and paid for it in
talk.

Cendre led her, softly, to the Doctor's special kindnesses, and the woman's face
did a complicated thing. Oh, the evening treatments were not new, she said. The
Doctor had always had his favourites — a man here, a woman there, taken under his
particular care, given the needle and the hour the others did not get. It was
thought an honour, in its way. But the favoured ones, now she counted them back
across the winters — and she counted them on her thin fingers, a name, another, a
third — the favoured ones had a way of not lasting. Of going downhill in the
spring, when the season turned, the way the merely sick somehow did not. She had
wondered of it, idly, the way one wonders of the sick.

Cendre wondered of it less idly. The method behind the locked door was not a thing

and the favour had a grave at the end of it more often than a cure. Whatever Roeder
had been doing to the bodies in his particular care, he had been doing it long

## sc-29 — Madame Surel, Gently

## sc-30 — The Net and the Knife

## sc-31 — The Vial in the Candlelight

She had carried the unlabelled bottle up to her own room weeks since, and weeks
since had run out of ways to make it speak. Tonight she tried again, because the
quiet road had given her everything but the death, and the death was in the glass
if it was anywhere. She set the candle close and turned the brown vial in its
light, tilting the thin amber skin of residue down its inner wall, and put the

did not have.

They told her too much and too little, both at once. The dreg was a physician's
dreg — something compounded, something with the sweetish under-reek she had learned
to know — and it could as easily have been a draught Roeder mixed for his own arm
in the privacy of his shame as a dose another hand had set ready on his table days

same grains either way, and would swear to nothing.

That was the whole cruelty of the thing. A self-murderer and a murderer leave the
identical sediment in the identical glass, and the glass had no memory of the hand.
She corked it and set it back in the drawer beside the green ledger and the sealed
brass book, three witnesses she possessed and could not cross-examine, and blew the
candle out, and lay a long while in the dark adding what she had: a method, a
motive, a sealed room, and a residue that pointed every way at once.

## sc-32 — The Steward's Two Logs

In the morning she went to Gaspard, who kept the house's going-out and coming-in
as Brandt kept its doses. His office was warm and orderly and smelled of lamp-oil
and ledger-glue, and he was glad, in his stiff way, to be asked anything by a
doctor at last.

The funicular's maintenance log hung chained to his desk, and she read it leaf by
leaf. The last entry in the running-gear book stood several days back of the storm

simply not made in the week the avalanche came down. He coloured a little at it.
The weather, he said; his own rounds broken by it; a slackness he was not proud
of. Whether the slackness had been his ordinary winter slackness or something a
quieter will had counted on, neither of them could say from the page.

Then she asked after the last word from the world below, and he gave her the
telegram from the salver where it had lain unclaimed since the line went dead —
addressed to the Doctor, taken down the evening before the storm, and never
answered, for by the next night Roeder was past answering anything. A single line
of someone else's business; she could not yet place its weight. She thanked him and

of snow-wet boots whose owner swore he never walked, a week of unwatched cable, a
last message that came in and met no reply. Someone, she thought, had been moving
about outside this shut house near the hour it shut. She did not yet know who, or
toward what.

## sc-33 — The Key Cold on the Board

The master key she came to last in Gaspard's room, because it was the one thing
whose answer she thought she already knew, and wished undone.

It hung on the warden's board where it had always hung, third peg from the window,
and the board about it was undisturbed — the faint grime of the wall unsmudged
behind it, the key itself cold and dull with disuse. Gaspard swore it had not come
off that peg the night the Doctor died; he had locked the office himself and found
it untouched at dawn. She believed him, the more so because the board agreed with
him without being asked. And Roeder's own key, the private one off his ring, had
lain inside the bolted room all the while, on the floor where it fell.

So the room had been shut from the inside. No house key had gone to it; no spare
had let a visitor in and out and locked the dead man up behind a lie. Whoever or
whatever had reached Augustin Roeder had reached him before he drew the bolt
himself, or had never been there at all and left him only his own company and his
own glass.

She stood with that a moment in the warm oil-smelling office. It cut precisely both
ways, as everything in this house cut both ways. A man alone behind his own bolt is
the perfect picture of a suicide. He is also the perfect victim of a dose laid
ready hours before, by a hand he never suspected, that had only to wait for him to
lock his own door upon it.

## sc-34 — The Name That Repeats

She had the patient register by her now, and the burial book beside it, and on a
clear cold afternoon she laid them open together at last and let the two columns
look at one another.

There it was, six years deep in the ground-book: a Surel, a man, set into Belvoir's

## sc-35 — The Word He Stopped On

That evening she took out the resignation again, the unsent one, the draft she had
lifted from his blotter the first week and had not been able to put down since.

It was a few lines only, in his upright hand, and it broke off. He had written to
the directors of his own house that after long reflection he found he could no
longer in good — and there the pen had stopped, on the half-word, and the next word,
when she set it in herself the only way it would go, was *conscience.* He had not
dated it. He had not named the thing his conscience would no longer hold. He had
got as far as the wall of it and no farther, and then he had died, or been died,
with the sentence open on his desk.

She read it, as she read everything now, twice and against itself. A man who could
not finish resigning over his own conscience was a man already half over the rail —
shame is a steep enough physic, and the glass was in the room, and the bolt was his
own to draw. There was a whole quiet suicide written in that broken line.

But an undated, unfinished confession of a nameless wrong was also exactly the sort
of paper that, found by the wrong reader before its writer could send or burn it,
makes a motive in another breast entirely. Someone who feared what the finished
sentence would say had as much in this halted line as the dead man did. It cut both
ways. By now she would have been astonished by a thing that did not.

## sc-36 — Lindqvist's Borrowed Summer

She went up to Lindqvist meaning only to sit with him, and found the summer going
out of him while she watched.

The evening needle had died with the Doctor who gave it; no one now came to the
engineer's bedside of a night with the small warm mercy that had written him, these
last weeks, into a man on the mend. Without it the borrowed strength drained back
the way a tide goes, and what it bared was the true coast: the great Stockholm frame
gone to kindling, the breath a wet rag drawn over stones, the bright recovered
colour of his chart a lie the man's own face would not any longer support. The chart
said *improving* in Roeder's upright hand. The man under it was sinking, and they
both knew it now, and only one of them would say so.

She held his grey hand and asked him again, lightly, the thing she had asked before,
because she could not let it go: had he been out, ever, into the snow. And again,
with the ghost of the old laugh, he told her no — that he had not crossed his own
floor unhelped in a month, let alone the drifts; that whoever had got the cold into
those boots of his had not been Edvard Lindqvist, who could barely get himself to
the window.

She drew the blanket up and went out with her fear plain in her. The poppy's warm
hour had been dressed in his chart as a cure, and now the cure was withdrawn and the
truth was coming up fast through the man, and she did not think, looking at him, that
he would see the thaw. And still, somewhere, a pair of his boots had walked in snow
he never touched.

## sc-37 — The Brass Lock

The diary she kept apart from the rest, in its own corner of the drawer, because it
asked a question of her she had not liked any of her answers to.

It was a handsome small book, brass-cornered, brass-clasped, the clasp held by a
lock no bigger than a thumbnail and a key she did not have. Somewhere a man on the
confronting road — a harder, faster Cendre she would never meet — had already
decided that a dead man's last confidence was hers to break, and had gone looking
for a blade thin enough and a will strong enough to spring it. This Cendre, the one
the quiet road had made, could not bring herself to it. To force the lock was to
say she had the right to his private hours by main strength, and she found she had
not got that right in her, not yet, not while another way might still be tried.

So she tried the other way, and it failed her. The little brass key was on neither
ring she could find — not the private ring off his own hand, not the house ring on
Gaspard's board. It was in no drawer of his desk, no pocket of the coats still
hanging in his press, no fold of the blotter. Either he had hidden it past a
searcher's finding, or a hand had taken it as another hand had taken a chart, and
either way the book stayed shut. She set it back, sealed as she had found it, its
witness locked inside its brass, and went on without the one voice in the house that
might have spoken plainly.

## sc-38 — The Slate and the Shelf

It nagged at her, the count, the way a sum that will not balance nags. She went back
to the consulting room with her slate one grey forenoon to be sure of it.

The living charts stood ranked on the shelf in their buff folders, and the slate at
the door tallied the house's current souls, and the two would not agree. The shelf
held one folder fewer than the slate's living count — one charted patient whose
paper was simply not among the papers, a gap on the boards as plain as a missing
tooth to anyone who troubled to count both. She counted both. She counted them
twice.

A misfiling, she told herself; a folder carried off to a bedside and not returned, a
discharge slid into the wrong year's box, the small entropy of any working ward. She
had been gently taught, weeks back, that the house sent its done-with charts down to
the cellar archive, and she half-meant to go down again and look. The records of
this room, she still believed, were as Roeder and his nurse had kept them: honest,
if guilty; complete, if damning. It did not once cross her mind that the missing
folder was not mislaid anywhere a candle could find it — that its want on the shelf
was the simple arithmetic of fire.

For the reader has watched that one chart brown and curl and lift up the flue on the
first grey morning, while the new doctor slept; and the gap she frowns at now, and
files in her mind under carelessness, is the exact shape of the thing that was
burned to keep her from it.

## sc-39 — What She Has and Has Not

She sat that night with the notebook full and totted the long account, and it came
out the same way it had come out every night that week: rich on the one side, and on
the other a blank she could not write into.

On the rich side, the misconduct, whole now in its outline if not in its every name.
The morphia signed out and never reaching the wards. The warm hour behind the
private door. The favoured cases that went down each spring while the merely sick
hung on. The old patient kept alive on a slate years after the ground had him, and
the older Surel sleeping in that ground, a subject of the method. A letter halted on
*conscience.* She could have stood any of it up before a coroner and made it stay
standing.

On the blank side, the death. Suicide and murder lay across the sealed room like two
transparencies of the same drawing, every line of one falling exactly on a line of

## sc-40 — The Edge of the Bolt

## sc-41 — The Name Laid Down

She came down in the morning as she had resolved, the Surel name ready on her lips
and her notebook under her arm, and knocked at the well-kept door. The woman who
answered was not surprised. She had the look of someone who has finished a long sum
and found that it came out whole, and she stood aside to let the doctor in as if she
had been expecting her at just this hour.

Cendre had meant to be gentle and indirect once more, and found she could not. She
set the thing down plainly instead. "There is a Surel in the burial book," she said,
"six years in your ground, and a Surel at the head of this season's register, and I
have come to ask you which of you came for the other."

For a moment the grave calm held. Then it did not. The woman sat down slowly in the
lamplight and folded her hands, and when she spoke her voice had put off something it
had worn for six weeks. "My papers are forged," she said. "Every line of them but the
one that matters. I am not a widow; I never married anyone. Surel is my own name — it
was mine before it was ever his, and his before it was the stone's." She looked up.
"The man you found in your ground was mine, Doctor. I came up this mountain to stand
where he died."

Cendre wrote nothing. She only sat, while the patient she had taken for an ordinary
consumptive of decent and sorrowing conduct dissolved before her and left, in the
same chair, a stranger who had been telling her the truth at last.

## sc-42 — Six Years' Patience

She told it without hurry, as a thing rehearsed so long it had worn smooth. The man
in the ground had been her brother — younger, gifted, gone up to Belvoir with a wet
cough and a fair prognosis the spring she was twenty. He had written her cheerful
letters. Then the letters had changed: a new evening treatment, the Doctor's
particular kindness, a warmth in the arm and a fog after it; and then they had
stopped. The house had written to say he had failed suddenly and been laid in its own
consecrated corner, with regret.

"He had not failed," she said. "He had been spent. I was three years finding the word
for it. I have it now. He was one of the ones taken behind the consulting-room door
for the needle and the borrowed hour, and the borrowed hour was paid back out of him
until there was no more of him to take."

She had spent the six years learning it — old patients, a turned-off nurse, a
chemist's son who remembered the orders. And when her own lungs at last gave her the
one key that would fit the lock, she had forged the rest: the references, the false
widowhood, the provenance that let *Madame Hélène Surel* be admitted as an ordinary
paying case. She rose, and from the writing-case drew the papers, and laid them on the
table between them, and pushed them across.

"There," she said. "That is the whole of my lie. Take it. I am done carrying it."

Cendre laid her hand upon the forged sheets and did not lift them yet. The shared name
had been no coincidence after all, no kinswoman drawn to sentimental ground. It had
been sitting in her register all winter in its own true ink.

## sc-43 — The Bolt She Never Passed

There was only one question left worth the asking, and Cendre asked it quietly, her
hand still on the papers. "You came to stand where he died," she said. "Did you come
to make another grave beside his?"

The woman did not flinch from it. "I came meaning to," she said. "I will not lie to
you now, of all the hours. I had it in me to make him answer, and more than answer; I
had thought of the how of it more nights than I will tell you." She turned her
ringless hands over in the light. "And then the storm came, and the house shut, and on
the very night I had wound myself to it — I could not reach him. His door was bolted. I
stood in the cold corridor with my heart going like a hammer, and the bolt was drawn
against me as it was drawn against the world. I never passed it. I set no glass by his
hand. Whatever kept him company at the end, it was not Hélène Surel."

She said it steadily, and it had the plain weight of truth — or the plainer weight of
the thing a clever woman would say. Cendre could not tell which, and knew she could
not, and that was the whole cruelty she had lived in these six weeks brought down into
a single face.

But something in her own reading turned as she sat there. If the one hand in the house
with cause to wish him dead had stood outside a bolt it could not pass, then the sealed
room showed its other face: a man alone with his glass and his halted letter and his
conscience, drawing his own bolt upon his own end. She held the thought short of
certainty — she had learned that much caution. But it had turned, and it had turned
inward.

## sc-44 — What the Living Owe the Dead

"What will you do with me," the woman asked — not pleading, only asking, the way one
asks the time.

It was the question Cendre had no settled answer to. There was no telegraph and no
funicular and no magistrate within three weeks of snow; there was a dead physician, a
hushed house full of buried wrong, and a confessed forger sitting calm in her borrowed
room; and there was Cendre, twenty-nine years old, with a certificate she would one
day have to set her name to and no one above her to set theirs first.

"I will hear you," she said at last. "I have heard you. Past that I do not yet know."

Hélène inclined her head as if that were more than she had looked for. "I have wanted,
these six years, only for someone to know it and not look away," she said. "You have
not looked away. The rest is yours to weigh. I will not run; there is nowhere in the
world to run to from here before the thaw, and I find I have stopped wishing to."

Cendre gathered the forged papers and the long account into her notebook and climbed
the cold stair with the weight of it. She had come down wanting a confession and been
given one entire — the human truth of the house, whole, from the lips of the one most
wronged by it. And still, climbing, she felt the other weight unlifted: the manner of
the death, which no confession of Hélène's could settle, because Hélène swore she had
stood outside it. What had passed behind that bolt was a thing only the bolted man had
seen.

## sc-45 — The Confessed House

That night she set it all out once more, and it read differently than it had read
since October. The blank side was no longer wholly blank. The forged papers lay
explained upon her desk, the buried brother named, the method confessed to by the kin
it had robbed — the whole human architecture of the place stood open to her at last,
and the reader who has carried Hélène's secret across six winters may set it down now,
in this one world, into the doctor's safe and sorrowing keeping.

But the death kept its counsel. Hélène's oath, if it was true, drew the case toward the
thing Cendre least liked and could least prove: that Augustin Roeder, finding his
conscience would no longer hold the weight of what he had done, had mixed his own last
draught, drawn his own bolt, and gone out of the world by the single door he had left
himself. The unfinished letter read that way now. The residue read that way. The sealed
room read that way.

Read that way — and would not say so. For the one voice that could turn the leaning
into knowledge lay still upon her desk in its brass covers, locked, its little key gone
from every ring and pocket in the house. She looked at the diary a long while in the
candlelight. To force it now, with a confession already in her hands, was a smaller
trespass than it had been a month ago; and it might be the whole of the distance
between what she suspected and what she could certify. The snow held. There was time,
and no escape from time. She did not open it that night. But she set her hand upon it,
once, before she slept.

## sc-46 — The Silence She Kept

She came down in the morning as she had resolved, the Surel name ready on her lips, and
knocked at the well-kept door; and the woman who answered had chosen the other road.

Cendre set the thing down plainly — the Surel in the ground, the Surel in the register,
the question of which had come for the other. She watched for the grave calm to break.

gave her again, in the same gentle voice, the same gentle nothing she had given weeks

needle had died too, and the borrowed strength was going out of Lindqvist the way a tide

## sc-47 — The Boots Speak Again

Shut out of the woman, she went back to the boots.

They had nagged at her since the autumn — Lindqvist's own boots, found snow-wet and
packed at the toe with folded paper outside the sick-wing, though their owner had not
crossed his own floor unhelped in a month. She had read them once as a false trail laid
to throw suspicion on a bed-bound man. Now, with the house gone quiet and watchful and a
confession refused below, she set them on the desk again beside the other mute witnesses
and made them keep company.

A pair of boots worn through deep snow by a smaller foot than the wasting engineer's. A
week's gap in the funicular log where the daily hand should have stood, the cable left
unwatched in the very days the avalanche came down. A telegram taken in the evening
before the line died and never answered. Set side by side they made one shape, and the
shape was a person: someone who had moved about the outside of this shut house near the
hour it shut, in another man's boots, on cable nobody was watching, while a message lay
unclaimed on the salver.

Not an old wrong merely, then — not only the years of off-register needles and the
favoured cases gone quietly into the ground. Those were Roeder's, and Roeder was dead.
This was a hand: a living hand that had been busy in the dark of the storm, and might be
busy yet. She had spent the autumn proving a misconduct. She began now, with a cold
misgiving she could not argue down, to believe she was housed with an agent.

## sc-48 — The Second Bell

The bell went in the small hours, the way the first one had.

Lindqvist was dead. He lay as a sleeping man lies, but cooling, the grey gone waxen, and
the night-nurse who found him could say only that he had been quiet when she passed at
two and gone when she passed at four. He had been a dying man for six months; no one in
the house could be astonished that he had died. And yet Cendre, standing over him with
her candle, was astonished, and could not at first say why — until she counted it: the
withdrawal of the needle should have spent him slowly, a fortnight's guttering, not
this, not here-and-gone between two passings of a nurse, as though a breath had been
pinched out rather than let run down.

The chart at the foot of the bed still said, in Roeder's confident upright hand,
*improving.* It was the last lie that hand would tell. Beneath it the man had not been
improving at all; he had been held up on a fiction and let fall when the fiction's
author died, and now the fall was complete and the fiction lay bare upon its own paper.

She thought of the bolted room above, and the brown vial, and the residue that swore to
nothing. She thought: a man may take his own life once, behind his own bolt, and leave
the house its grim peace. But a house where a second man dies too suddenly in his open
bed, a fortnight after the first — that is not a house with a suicide in it. That is a
house with a hand in it. She could not prove the dose. She was, of a sudden, entirely
sure it had been given.

## sc-49 — Two Glasses, One Hand

By morning she had done for Lindqvist what she had done for Roeder, and the doing taught
her the one thing that mattered.

There was no vial at this bedside, no glass, no needle left for her to find; whoever or
whatever had come had carried its leavings away, or there had been nothing to leave but
a clean injection into a sleeping arm. The body told her only what Roeder's had —
morphia would do it, morphia would leave just this stillness, and morphia could not be
sworn to over a cold man. The means were as ambiguous as they had been upstairs. Two
deaths, and the glass kept no memory of the hand in either.

But the rooms were not the same, and the difference was the whole of it. Roeder had died
behind a bolt drawn from within, the master key cold on its board all night, his own key
fallen inside — sealed, whatever else was true, against the world. Lindqvist had died in
an open sickroom that no one ever locked, on a corridor any soul in the house might walk
unremarked at two in the morning. The first death could still, if one strained at it, be
a man alone with his conscience. The second could not be alone at all. And a hand that
could reach the second bed so quietly gave the first its missing visitor — for the dose
that stopped Roeder need never have passed his bolt; it had only to be set ready, hours
before, by someone he never thought to fear.

She did not strain after the lonely reading any longer. She laid the two deaths side by
side on her page and let them lend each other a murderer.

## sc-50 — The Hand in the House

Two dead now, and three weeks of snow between the house and any law; and somewhere
beneath its frozen roof, by the plain arithmetic of it, the one who had made them both.

Cendre carried the cold logic along the corridors. A hand that had moved in the
storm-dark in a borrowed pair of boots. A hand that could open an unbolted door at two
in the morning. A hand with a reason. She had no proof, and she made herself say so,
over and over, lest the wanting of an answer manufacture one — but reason was the thing
the misconduct had never quite supplied, and reason, she found, kept leading her down
the cold stair to a well-kept door and a grave, lovely, unbreakable calm.

She knew nothing. That was the truth of where she stood. She did not know the woman's
name was a forgery, nor that a brother lay six years in the ground beneath the same
name, nor that the calm she kept beating against had been built for exactly this — to
outlast a snowbound inquiry and walk down the mountain in spring. The reader, who has
kept the woman's accounts through all six winters, watches the doctor circle the right
door for the wrong reasons, and cannot tell her which half she holds and which she
lacks.

What she had was two corpses and a conviction. What she lacked was the bolt's last
secret and the silent woman's first one. She stood in the corridor with her candle,
housed with a hand she could not see and a truth that would not speak, and waited — as
the whole white mountain waited — for the snow to give her either her proof or her
escape.

## sc-51 — The Brass Lock Forced

She had refused it all winter — the doctor's last witness, locked in brass, not hers to
break — and the refusal had begun to feel less like scruple than cowardice. So on the
morning after the cellar gave up nothing, with Brandt's confession written plain in her
notebook and the sealed room standing at her back like a wall she could not climb,
Cendre took the book to the window where the light was hard off the snow, and set a thin
blade to the little lock, and broke it.

It gave with a small dry sound, no louder than a knuckle. For a moment she sat with the
sprung covers under her hands and felt the trespass of it run through her — a living
physician forcing the private hours of a dead one. Then she opened it, because every
other voice in the house had failed her. Brandt had given her the method and stopped at
the stove. The vial swore to nothing. The bolt kept its own counsel behind a window iced
shut. Only this remained: Augustin Roeder, in his own hand, writing to no one but
himself across the last weeks of his life.

The entries were close and careful, a man's private order, and the earlier ones meant
little to her — weather, accounts, the slow business of a shut house. She turned forward
to the autumn, to the weeks before the storm, and began to read in earnest. The reader,
who has waited as long as she has for this book to open, may read over her shoulder now;
for what Roeder set down was the whole of it, and he had not meant a soul to see.

## sc-52 — Roeder's Own Hand

It was all there, in the even clinical hand she knew from a hundred charts. He had called
it his *method,* without shame and without quite confessing shame, as a man writes who has
argued himself past the point of being answerable. The evening injections — morphia, drawn
in quantity from the house store and signed out under his own name, so that the ledger's
missing grains had gone exactly where she had begun to fear: into the arms of a chosen few,
behind his own door, after the wards were dark. *The borrowed hour,* he named the warmth it
gave them, the false strength, the bright talk before the long sinking. He believed — or had
made himself believe — that he was learning something from it that would outlast the cost.

And he named the cost. He named them. Across the years, a roll of favoured cases taken
behind the consulting-room door for the needle, and against several of the names a small
cross, his private mark for the ones the borrowed hour had finally spent. One of the crossed
names was Surel — laid, he noted without apparent feeling, in the house's own consecrated
corner six years back. Another was an old case he had kept ticked alive on his slate long
after the man was ash, so that the books should show no gap; and it was that chart, she
understood now, cold to the fingertips, that Brandt had fed to the stove the morning he was
found — burning the number that would not square.

She had come to the diary for the manner of his death. She had found first the manner of his
life, and it was worse than the misconduct she had built from below.

## sc-53 — The Name in the Ground

She went down to her own desk and brought up the patient register, and laid it open beside
the diary, and set the two Surels side by side: the one crossed in a dead man's hand in the
ground, the one written living at the head of this season's book. She had marked the echo of
the name weeks ago and let it lie, having no road from one to the other. The diary was the
road.

For if the buried Surel had been Roeder's subject — taken behind the door, spent, and laid in
the corner with the house's regret — then the living Surel who had come up the mountain this
particular winter, under references no one could now confirm and a widowhood Cendre had never
once seen her grieve, was no consumptive drawn by chance to a sad coincidence of names. She
was kin to the crossed name. She had come for it. The papers in the writing-case of the woman
in the lower room were forged, and forged by someone who had a reckoning to keep.

The reader has known this since the first week of the snow, and has watched Cendre take the
quiet kind patient at her decent word. She did not take her so any longer. And once she had
the shape of it she had the rest in a rush — the snow-wet boots too small for the bed-bound
engineer, the steward drawn from his post by a note in a hand *like* Roeder's, the cable left
unwatched. Not a false trail laid against Lindqvist at all. A woman moving about the outside
of a house she meant to enter, making her road to a man she meant to make answer.

## sc-54 — The Halted Letter, the Brown Glass

But the woman had not reached him. That was the thing the diary settled, in the last entries
of all, and it settled it past Cendre's doubting.

For Roeder had reached himself first. The final pages turned from the method to the man, and
the man was breaking. The borrowed hours had stopped paying him anything but their cost; the
unfinished resignation under his blotter, halting on the word *conscience,* had its long
rehearsal here, draft after draft he could neither send nor burn. And the house morphia was
nearly gone — he had drawn it down to keep the method fed, and the last telegram up from
Aigle had promised the resupply only on the first clear funicular, and then the line had died
and the snow had shut and no more was coming until the thaw. A man with that much to answer
for, and his physic running out, and a confession he could not make and could not stop
wanting to make.

The last entry was short. He had measured himself a strong draught into the unlabelled brown
bottle — a third of it, the assay had told her, a mouthful that would stop a strong heart
within the hour — and he meant to drink it that night and draw his bolt upon the world, and
he asked nothing of whoever read this but that they let the house alone. The master key never
left its board because he never meant it to. No hand reached him because he had bolted the
door against every hand, his own among them, and then taken the glass.

Down the corridor Lindqvist still breathed, the improving chart over his bed the last of
Roeder's false vigours, the engineer left living on a fiction with its author dead — the
method's one surviving subject, and now, perhaps, its first honest patient.

## sc-55 — What Cendre Certifies

**ENDING A.** She closed the brass covers near morning and sat a long while in the grey light,
and then she took out the certificate she had not been able to sign and signed it. *Death by
his own hand,* she wrote, *while of a mind broken by the knowledge of his own conduct* — and
under it, in the careful private record she would carry down for the magistrate and the
medical board and whoever in the valley would hear it, she set the whole of the rest: the
method, the diverted morphia, the roll of the spent, the corner of consecrated ground that
held more of Belvoir's making than the house had ever owned.

There remained the woman in the lower room, who had come to kill a man the man had killed
first. Cendre went down to her at last, not as to a suspect but as to the one other soul in
the house who had carried the truth all winter, and found the grave calm waiting, and told
her, simply, that she knew — the name, the brother in the ground, the reckoning the bolt had
cheated her of. What passed between the two women belonged to neither the certificate nor the
diary. But the avenger had been forestalled by the suicide, and there was no death to lay at
her door, and Cendre found she had no wish to invent one.

The snow would hold three weeks yet. When it cleared she would go down the mountain with a
dead man's confession in her bag and a living woman's silence to keep, and the truth of
Belvoir entire — every plank of it, the worst as well as the saddest — proven in the hand of
the only man who could have denied it. She had wanted, since October, only to know. She knew.
It was not the mercy she had imagined knowing would be.

## sc-56 — The Seal at Last

She had set her hand on the brass diary the night Hélène confessed, and not opened it, and
slept badly, and risen knowing she would. The confession had changed the weight of the thing
in her hand. A month ago to force a careful man's last book had been a trespass she could not

whole as it was, could not reach. Hélène had sworn she never passed the bolt. Only the bolted

So Cendre brought the book to the hard window-light, as she had so long refused to, and found
that the small brass key was on no ring and in no drawer in the house — gone with whatever
else the dead man had taken out of reach. She had hunted it for weeks on the quiet road and
spared the lock. She spared it no longer. A thin blade and a steady hand, and the little
mechanism gave with a dry click, and the covers sprang, and the last reluctance she had kept
all winter went out of her with the small sound.

She did not feel the trespass she had feared. She felt only that she had come, by the longest
and gentlest road the house offered, to the one door it had left her, and that she would read
now whatever was written, and weigh it against a confession she half believed and wholly
pitied, and learn at last which of the two truths the snow had been keeping.

## sc-57 — Conscience, in His Own Hand

The diary gave her Hélène's account from the other side, in Roeder's own even hand, and the
two fitted like the halves of a broken seal. The morphia drawn off the ledger for the evening
needle; the borrowed hour and its long cost; the roll of favoured cases across the years, and
against some of them his small private cross — and there, crossed, was Surel, the brother,
laid in the house's ground with the rest of the spent. Everything the avenger had spent six
years assembling from turned-off nurses and a chemist's son, the dead man had written down
himself and locked in brass.

And the last pages gave her the death. They were the pages of a man coming apart — the
resignation drafted and redrafted and never finished, always failing on the same word; the
physic he had drawn down to feed the method now nearly gone, no resupply past the cut line; a
confession he could neither make to the living nor stop making to the book. The final entry
was brief and almost calm. He had measured the strong draught into the brown bottle. He meant
to drink it and bolt his door and ask the house to let him alone.

Cendre read it twice and felt the inward lean of these last weeks settle into something she
could stand on. Hélène had told the truth: she had stood outside a bolt she could not pass.
The man inside had drawn that bolt against the whole world and then against himself. The
sealed room, the cold key, the residue that swore to nothing — all of it had been saying,
under her hand the entire winter, the one thing she had been least able to bear hearing, that
no one had killed Augustin Roeder but Augustin Roeder.

## sc-58 — What Hélène Also Did

She went down once more, the book under her arm, and Hélène, who had stopped wishing to run,
filled in for her the last of the small mysteries that the diary could not. Yes — the boots.
She had taken the engineer's boots from the sick-wing because her own were known, and gone out
in them more than once in the still days before the storm, learning the ground, the
out-buildings, the hour the corridors emptied — packing the toes with paper, for the wasting
Lindqvist's feet were larger than hers. Yes — the note that drew Gaspard to the cable-house
for a fault that was not there. She had a forger's hand; she had practised Roeder's the way
she had practised the dead references, and she had wanted the steward off his post and the
house turned inward for what she meant to do. And she had watched the last telegram come up
and lie unanswered on the salver, and watched, two days later, the line go dead and the snow
come down, and felt the mountain shut around her purpose like a fist.

"I made my road to him as carefully as he ever made his to my brother," she said. "And then
his own door stopped me, and I stood in the cold and could not pass, and in the morning he was
dead by his own measure and had cheated me even of that." She said it without much bitterness
now. "I have wondered since whether I am glad. I have not settled it."

Cendre wrote it all down. The someone who had moved outside the shut house, the riddle she had
turned over for weeks, had been the woman in front of her, readying a murder the dead man
spared her the doing of.

## sc-59 — The Chart No One Names

One thread the diary tied that Hélène's confession had left loose, and one it left forever
hanging.

It tied the missing chart. The roll of crossed names included the old case Roeder had kept
ticked alive on his slate years past its dying, the number that would not square with a living
body — and Cendre, who had marked weeks ago a single buff folder short on the consulting-room
shelf and set it down to misfiling, understood now what folder it had been. What she did not
learn, on this road, was the hand that had taken it: she never went to Brandt with the ledger,
never heard the head nurse's grim account of mercy and a stove, and so the burning itself
stayed outside her knowing. The reader, who watched the chart go into the fire on the first
morning, holds that small fact still, alone — the one secret of the house that even this
gentlest solving never reaches. Some loyal cruelties keep.

And it left hanging the engineer. Lindqvist's improving chart had been the method's last
fiction; with the needle stopped he was failing, truly now, the borrowed strength all spent.
But he was alive, and would perhaps stay alive a while, and Cendre resolved to keep him
honestly — to chart what was true of him, drops and decline and small comfort, and let the
last of Roeder's false vigours be replaced by something poorer and real. It was not much to
salvage from a method that had buried so many. It was what the snow had left her to salvage.

## sc-60 — A Death by His Own Hand

**ENDING B.** She signed the certificate in the end the way the diary had taught her to:
*death by his own hand, of a conscience he could not carry.* She set down beside it, for the
valley to do with as it would, the whole architecture of the method — the morphia, the
borrowed hours, the corner of ground — proven now from two sides, the dead man's book and the
living woman's account, each the warrant of the other.

What she did with the woman was the harder certificate, and there was no one above her to
countersign it. Hélène had come meaning murder and committed none; the bolt and the brown
glass had seen to that. The forged papers lay on Cendre's desk where their owner had pushed
them across, no longer a disguise to be unmasked but a dead brother's sister's last evidence
that the method had been real and had cost what it cost. Cendre kept them so. She would carry
them down not as the proof of a crime but as the testimony of a survivor, and let the law that
waited below the snow weigh a forgery against the graves it had been forged to reach.

"You wanted someone to know and not look away," she said, at the foot of the cold stair, the
last morning before she climbed it. "I know. I will not look away, and I will not let the
valley look away either." It was not absolution; she had none to give. It was the thing Hélène
had asked for across six winters, and Cendre found that she could give it, and that giving it
was the nearest thing to justice the snowbound house allowed.

## sc-61 — The Arithmetic of Two

Two corpses changed the arithmetic, and Cendre did the sum at her desk in the small cold hours
with both certificates blank before her.

She had been unable, with one death, to choose between a man's own conscience and another's
laid hand, because the sealed room swore equally to either. But the room had only ever been an
argument about access, and the second death dissolved the argument. Lindqvist had died in an
open sickroom that no one locked, on a corridor any soul in the house might walk unremarked at
two in the morning — died too suddenly for the slow ebb of withdrawal, a breath pinched out
rather than let run down. A hand had done that. And a hand that could do that to the engineer
need never have passed Roeder's bolt at all: the dose that stopped the doctor had only to be
set ready, hours before, in the brown bottle he would lift to his own mouth alone behind his
own door. The cold untouched master key, the iced sash, the inner bolt — they had told her all
winter that no one entered. They had never told her that no one had killed him. They had only
told her the killer did not need to enter. The murder had been laid, not committed; mixed into
his evening draught and left for his own hand to finish.

She wrote it down, and the writing of it closed the door on the lonely reading for good. *Not
his own conscience.* A dose composed by someone who knew his habits, set where habit would do
the rest. She could not prove the grain of it. She had stopped, somewhere in the second night,
expecting to.

## sc-62 — The Trail in the Snow

With the shape of it fixed, the mute witnesses on her desk fell into their true order, and the
order was a trail.

The snow-wet boots, worn through the drift by a foot too small for the man whose name they
bore. The funicular log gone blank the day before the storm, the steward drawn off his post by
a note in a hand got up to look like Roeder's. The last telegram come and gone unanswered, and
then the line dead and the house shut. She had read these once as scattered anomalies and once
as a clumsy frame; she read them now as the movements of a single careful person about the
outside of a house she meant to close and command — emptying the cable-house, watching the
line die, walking the snow in another's boots so that no track should be her own. The hand that
had laid the doses had first prepared the ground.

The reader, who has kept the woman's accounts since the first week of the snow, knows whose
small foot wore the boots and whose practised hand forged the note, and watches Cendre lay the
trail out true to its last turning — and stop, every time, one step short of the name at the
end of it. For the doctor had nothing in her hand to carry her further. She had the shape of a
person and not a person; a method of approach and no face to fix to it; the certainty that she
was housed with the maker of two deaths and not one thread that would bear the weight of
saying so aloud. She knew the murderer's road by heart. She did not know the murderer.

## sc-63 — The Books of the Dead

Behind both deaths, when she set them against the autumn's slow discoveries, stood the same
dark machine: Roeder's method, and the books that kept its account.

The ledger's missing grains, signed out in the dead man's hand and never come to rest in any
ward — morphia enough, she saw now, to feed years of the evening needle and to spare a strong
last dose or two for whatever purpose a clever hand might find. The Surel laid six years in
the house's ground, sharing a surname with a living patient Cendre had been schooled by a soft
voice to take for sad coincidence. The single chart short on the consulting-room shelf, the
buff folder she had set down to misfiling and never guessed had gone to the stove to keep an
old number from being asked after. She had the method's outline whole — the diverted physic,
the favoured cases, a death or more among them across the years — but the particulars stayed
the reader's and not hers: the burned chart's owner, the buried Surel's kin, the disguise in
the lower room. The method had supplied a murderer both the means and the cover; a house where
patients died of the Doctor's care was a house where two more deaths might pass, to any eye but
a stubborn one, as the method's natural toll.

And the second of the dead was the proof the first had lacked. Lindqvist's bright impossible
recovery, the chart turned well in a dying man — that fiction, withdrawn at Roeder's death, had
bared a wasting engineer whom a hand had then hurried out of the world. The murdered man on the
improving chart was the plainest thing the house held that its deaths were not all nature's.

## sc-64 — The Locked Witness

There remained the brass diary, and on the escalate road she never opened it.

She thought of forcing it — sat with it under her hands one night as she had sat with so much
else — and did not. The little key was gone from every ring and pocket, hunted and not found.
And to break a dead man's last book by force, to prove a murder she could not pin to any living
hand, struck her even now as the wrong kind of trespass: it would give her his conscience and
not his killer, for whatever Roeder had set down in those final days he had set down as a man
who believed he was choosing his own end, and not as a man who knew a hand was reaching for him.
The one witness that might have spoken could only have told her what he thought was true, and
what he thought was true was a lie a careful dose had told him. The seal held. The book kept its
conscience locked, and the lock kept its counsel, and the case kept its hole.

She turned instead to the halted resignation, and found that it, too, had changed its face. A
man breaking off forever on the word *conscience,* a man on the very edge of resigning his post
and telling what he knew — she had weighed that letter both ways for weeks. It weighed one way
now. A physician about to confess the method was a physician someone in the house had every
reason to silence before the thaw could carry the confession down the mountain. The letter she
had read as a suicide's last scruple read now as a motive handed to another. Even his
conscience, in the end, had served the hand that killed him.

## sc-65 — The Hand That Walks Down in Spring

**ENDING C.** She filled the certificates at last, and filled them with the only honest word
she had. Not *natural;* she would not write the lie that had nearly buried the whole of it on
the first morning. Not *suicide;* the second death had taken that reading and broken it. She
wrote, for Roeder and for Lindqvist both, *death by a hand unknown,* and under it the long
careful record of everything she could show and the short bitter line of the one thing she
could not: that the maker of both deaths was, by the plain arithmetic of a shut house, still
within its walls, and that she could not name the hand.

The reader can. The reader has watched the calm woman by the well-kept door through all six
winters of her patience — the forged papers still folded in her writing-case, unsurrendered and
unguessed; the true name she never spoke; the brother in the ground she came to answer for and
did. In the murder world the disguise holds whole. Cendre circled the right door a dozen times
on the cold stair and turned away each time for want of the single proof, and the door kept its
grave and lovely calm, built exactly to outlast a snowbound inquiry and walk down the mountain
when the snow let it.

Three weeks, and the thaw came, and the funicular shuddered back to life, and the survivors of
Belvoir went down. Cendre went down with two certificates she could not close and a conviction
she could not spend. And somewhere on the descending car, unnamed, unproven, her accounts at
last made whole, rode the hand the doctor had proven and never seen — looking out at the
clearing valley with the mild sorrow of a consumptive going home, and keeping, as she had kept
all along, her own perfect counsel.
