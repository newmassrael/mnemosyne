## Rubric

### 1. Continuity errors

**Version A — few, all minor; no hard contradictions.**
- Dangling setup: in "The Caretaker's Silence," Bochat admits a recent blank night in the funicular log — "somebody had come up after dark and gone down before the light, the once, muffled to the eyes, and had paid him." This visitor is never identified or tied to anyone; it doesn't pay off.
- Soft resolution: the morphine-ledger discrepancy ("The Ledger") is folded vaguely into the matron's general guilt at the confrontation rather than given a specific accounting of where the morphine went.
- Very minor: "The diary had defeated her for three weeks" ("The Forced Lock") brushes the edge of the three-week snow-in window, but stays within tolerance.
The core mechanism is sound: the matron poured Roeder's evening draught "every night since you were a child at your lessons," so her access to dose him is established before the locked door is ever drawn.

**Version B — more numerous, including two outright contradictions.**
- Telegram contradiction: the opening ("The Line Goes Dead") states the evening's last telegram is a supply order from Aigle that "Cendre read… over his shoulder." Much later ("The Steward's Two Logs") the same salver telegram — "the last word from the world below… addressed to the Doctor… never answered… a single line of someone else's business; she could not yet place its weight" — is treated as an unread, enigmatic message. Same wire, incompatible descriptions; its content is never revealed, yet it is reused as a clue in "The Boots Speak Again" / "The Trail in the Snow." A setup that both contradicts itself and never pays off.
- Timeline contradiction: the isolation is "three weeks" at the open (and "Three weeks, and the thaw came" at the close), but "What She Has and Has Not" says Cendre "had been gentle and patient and indirect for six weeks." Six weeks of post-death investigation cannot fit inside a three-week snow-in.
- Mechanism gap in the solution: the ending ("Two Glasses, One Hand," "The Trail in the Snow," "The Hand That Walks Down in Spring") pins both deaths on Surel, a lower-floor bed-patient, but the laid morphia dose sat in a bottle inside Roeder's private consulting room — a room only his key opened, whose draughts the nurse Brandt prepared. Surel's access is never established; the access the solution requires actually fits Brandt.
- Motive gap: Surel murders Lindqvist, a fellow victim of Roeder's method, with no motive supplied — and her earlier "going out as Lindqvist" in his packed boots frames the very man she then kills. Unreconciled, and at odds with her sympathetic-avenger characterization.
- Loose thread: the emptied cable-house, drawn off by a forged note "so a man might come or go by the gear," implies an outside arrival/departure that doesn't square with Surel being resident since October.
Roughly two hard contradictions plus three logic/payoff gaps.

### 2. Setup / payoff satisfaction
- **A: 4.** Telegrams (the carried wire opened in "The Wire and the Wrong Hand"), diary, the Sève cipher, the née-column Cordier match, the wet boots (the grave, not the door), and Pernet's memory all land. The unexplained funicular visitor and the hand-wavy morphine accounting keep it off 5.
- **B: 2.** Heavy, controlled dramatic irony lands several payoffs for the reader (the burned chart, the repeated name, the boots as false trail), but the central engine doesn't satisfy: the telegram is dropped, the diary is deliberately never opened ("The Locked Witness"), and the who/how/why of the deaths stays gappy. Many threads resolve only as reader-knowledge while the plot's mechanism stays unproven.

### 3. Stakes and tension
- **A: 5.** A live, self-aware antagonist (the matron "resolved… to go on protecting it… from the clever, decent young woman"), a genuine clock on Sève's life (is the serum healing or spending her?), and a climax that escalates to breakthrough — a near-confession and the matron's suicide.
- **B: 3.** The snowbound dread and the calm watching woman are effective, but the tension is static and epistemic; the long middle dissipates urgency, and the "escalation" confirms a hand rather than releasing the case.

### 4. Character-knowledge believability
- **A: 5.** No one acts on what they cannot know; Cendre reasons from gathered evidence and openly admits what she can't prove; the matron's and Hélène's actions rest on knowledge the story establishes.
- **B: 4.** Cendre stays properly within her limited knowledge (her oversharing in "Madame Surel Is Kind" is deliberately characterized). The dock is for the murderer's behavior — Surel killing Lindqvist — which isn't believably driven by anything she is shown to know or want.

### 5. Sag / drift
- **A: 5.** Economical; each scene advances the case, and the POV-revisits (matron, Hélène) add escalation rather than tread water.
- **B: 2.** At nearly double the length, the quiet-road middle and the escalate denouement restate the same suicide-vs-murder ambiguity and the "the reader knows, she does not" refrain repeatedly ("The Vial in the Candlelight," "The Key Cold on the Board," "What She Has and Has Not," "The Hand in the House," "The Arithmetic of Two," "The Books of the Dead"). The thread holds but slackens.

### 6. Forced choice: **A**

A is the stronger telling on every rubric axis. It resolves cleanly and, crucially, fairly: the killer is the one character whose access the story has actually established — the matron who poured Roeder's nightly draught — so the locked-room solution (the dose laid before the bolt) lands without a hole, and the escalation (Sève's death) drives a real breakthrough rather than just confirming helplessness. B is the more lavish prose and earns a genuinely chilling unsolved ending, but it pays for that ambition: two outright continuity faults (the supply-order telegram later recast as an unread mystery, and "six weeks" of investigation inside a three-week snow-in), a central solution that fingers a patient who has no shown access to the doctor's private draught and no motive for the second killing, and a long middle that sags by repeating its own ambiguity. An ambiguous ending is a legitimate choice, but B loses on execution — contradictions, unmotivated mechanics, and drift — where A stays tight, grounded, and taut to the end.
