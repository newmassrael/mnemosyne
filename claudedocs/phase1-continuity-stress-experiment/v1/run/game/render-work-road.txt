The lockhouse at Harrow Sluice had never felt so large as it did with only one person in it. Wick sat by the cold stove and listened to the storm work at the roof, and told himself the counting of the rain was a kind of company. The master keeper was away downriver and would not be back before morning; the watch was Wick's, and Wick's alone. Beyond the little window the canal was climbing. He could hear it in the change of the water's voice against the gate — a deeper, harder note than the daytime slop and lap. The lock-gate stood barred, the great beam dropped home across it, holding the rising flood off the pound below. That was as it should be. That was the whole of the night's work, if the night was kind: to sit, and watch, and keep the gate shut against the water coming up fast in the dark.

He knew the order the way he knew his own name. The master had said it to him plain, more than once, and had meant it kindly: no working the lock at night, and never alone. It was written in the log in the master's own hand, and the master trusted it to hold — trusted the words on the page to stand between the boy and the black water while he was gone. Wick knew it by heart. No night working. Never alone. He said it under his breath now, the way you say a thing to keep it true.

On the shelf above the stove the one storm-lantern hung on its hook, its flame turned low and steady behind the horn. There was only the one. That was the rule of the place as much as any order: whoever worked the gate machinery in the dark had to be the one carrying the light, because there was no other light to carry. Wick did not mean to touch it. But when the wind rose and the house went darker still, he found he had taken it down off the hook without deciding to, and now it burned in his hand, warm through his palm, throwing his own long shadow up the wall.

He went out to look at the pound. The upper pound had begun taking water at the turn of the third hour — he had marked it, the way the master had taught him to mark everything — and it was filling now, dark and slow and heavy, the level creeping up the slimed stone. He knew what that meant with the certainty of a lesson learned by rote: bring the gate open too soon, before the pound had come up level, and the flood would find the drop and pour through it, and the surge would swamp whatever lay waiting in the lock. The water had to be given its time. There was no arguing with it.

That was when the barge signalled.

It came out of the dark below the gate — a light swung once, twice, and a horn's low mourn under the storm. The Marrow. He knew her by the shape of her lantern more than by anything he could see. She was down there in the churn, wanting up, wanting through before the flood crested and took the whole reach. And behind the horn he could hear, faint, a man's voice — the barge-master, calling that he had to pass now, that if he was left below he'd be driven onto the weir and broken there. The man was sure of it. Wick could hear the certainty in him, the way a frightened man's voice goes flat and hard.

So there it was. Wick stood at the gate with the lantern in his hand, the barge crying up out of the black below him and the order sounding in his ear, and no one to tell him which was the greater fear. This was the choice, and it was his, and there was no master to take it off him. He stood a long moment in the rain. Then something in him settled — not lightly, but the way a beam settles home. He would take the night's charge on himself. He would see the Marrow through, and see the lock through, by a keeper's duty, because that was what a keeper was for.

"Hold on," he called down into the dark, and his voice did not shake as much as he'd feared. "Hold on — I'm working you up." And with the saying of it he broke the master's standing order clean across, and set himself to work the lock at night, alone.

The beam first. He set his shoulder to it and hauled, and the wet timber came grudging off the bar, and the gate gave — off barred, to a crack, no more, the water hissing white through the gap. He could not hold the beam and the light both. So he leaned down and passed the lantern into the barge-master's reaching hands — "Hold it at the sill, hold it steady" — and with both his own hands free he threw all his weight against the beam and felt the gate ease that first hard inch.

Then came the waiting, and the waiting was the worst of it. He could do nothing now but give the pound its minutes. He crouched at the crack and counted — under his breath, steady, the way you count to keep from screaming — while below him the Marrow wallowed and swung, drifting, he was sure, closer to the weir with every count. Too long, and she'd be swept onto it before he ever got her up. Not long enough, and the surge would drown her at the sill. He feared both at once, feared he had already waited too long and had not waited nearly long enough, and still he made himself count, and the water climbed.

The pound came level at last. Full. He had opened the crack at the turn of the third hour, and now the lock read five-and-thirty past — a full thirty-five minutes of fill, the water given every minute it was owed. Now. He called for the light and the barge-master passed the lantern back up into his hand, and Wick took it up and set it by him and worked the final lift, and the gate came off the crack to open, wide and true, and the pound and the reach became one dark water. The Marrow came up on it slow, her hull grinding the sill, up out of the low dark and into the lock.

As she cleared the sill Wick brought the gate down off open to a crack behind her, easing it, never rushing it. The barge-master was weeping openly now — Wick could hear it over the storm, the man saying again and again that the keeper's boy had opened for them in time, in time. And then the barge rose the last of the way into the lantern's throw, and Wick lifted the light to see her well, and saw who lay in it.

The master keeper. Laid out in the well of the Marrow, carried home hurt, his face grey in the horn-light. That was what the Marrow had been carrying up out of the dark. That was who had been drifting toward the weir while Wick knelt counting.

He did not remember crossing to her. He remembered the lantern's warmth still in his hand and the master's eyes finding his, open, alive. They got him ashore and up into the lockhouse and laid him by the cold stove, and the barge-master swore he would live — live to strike the night-working order from the log with his own hand, once his hand would hold a pen again.

And Wick, when the master was settled and breathing, went back out into the last of the storm and set his shoulder once more to the beam. He brought the gate down off its crack and barred it home, the great beam dropped again across the flood, the way it had stood at the start of the night. The Marrow rode safe above, moored fast. The night was seen out. He had done the thing he'd taken on himself to do — the barge through, the man home, the lock made fast — and he had broken the order to do it, and he stood there in the rain with the one lantern burning warm in his hand, and found he could carry both of those at once.
