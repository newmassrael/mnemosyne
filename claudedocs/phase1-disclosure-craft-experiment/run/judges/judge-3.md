# Judge 3 — Verdict

Three matched world-lines of the same branching mystery (the Vane Observatory / Aurel Crane fall), version A vs version B, judged one world-line at a time. Scores are 1-5, higher is better.

---

## WORLD 1 — `W1-confront-reveal` (CONFRONT → REVEAL)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**A: 0 hard errors.** The causal chain is internally consistent. The clock-as-struck mechanism (the iron frame "one bone," 3:14 = recorded instant of the fall) is set up in the early clock scene and paid off in the re-reading scene. The blank-plate → "she was on the gallery" inference, the dictated-log explanation, the undelivered telegram, the unsent resignation, the kept key — all close. The one thing worth flagging is a *deliberate* dramatized error inside the story (Pike's false belief that he "sent" the wire), which the prose carefully marks as Pike's self-deception, not an author slip. So: not a continuity error, an intended dramatic irony.

**B: 0 hard errors, but 1 mechanical inconsistency across the world-line.** B's reveal hangs the death on a *struggle at the upper rail* — Pike grabs Crane's wrist, they wrestle, Pike's hand catches the clockwork and stops the pendulum (the thread of sleeve in the works). This is a clean, physically motivated 3:14. BUT in B's own earlier dome scene the clock is described as stopped by a hand "or because the work is fouled… This work is not fouled," and Hale insists "A clock stops because someone stops it." That actually *supports* the grip-the-works reveal — consistent. The genuine soft spot: B's S77 says the spectroscope annexe is a separate room "beneath the gallery" with "walls between" so the stair cannot be seen — yet Junia's reveal requires her to have been *on the gallery* the whole night with a full view, which the text flags as her lie (consistent), but it never resolves *why a fresh fall-stain and a body at the stair-foot beneath the great glass* squares with a struggle thirty feet up at the upper rail vs. a fall down the spiral — minor, but B raises more physical specifics (temple bruise, wrist band, skull) and leaves their exact geometry looser than A leaves its tighter, sparer chain. Call it 0 errors, 1 loose seam.

### 2. Prose quality (told story vs. assembled)

A reads as a told story with a sustained, distinctive voice — the recurring "wrote it down in its own line," "the dome kept the time of its own death," the clerk's-faith motif. It is patient and controlled. **A: 5.**

B *also* reads as a told story, and arguably a more novelistic one — dialogue carries scenes, characters have texture ("she lied the way an honest woman tells the truth"), the omniscient narrator addresses the reader directly. The one cost: B's reader-address ("The reader knows this. Hale does not, yet," "Remember that the reader knows this") is a deliberate device but reads as told-by-a-narrator-explaining rather than dramatized; it occasionally tips toward stating the architecture of the irony rather than letting it land. Still clearly voiced. **B: 5.**

### 3. Stakes and tension

A builds tension through the slow re-reading structure: the certainty cracks ("If Crane never knew… then the confession was a confession to a death the confessor never caused"), then is rebuilt object by object. The Pike-believes-himself-a-murderer scene (S111) is strong. **A: 4.**

B has live dialogue, a guilty man across a lamp, an interrogation that turns on a single mistake ("the recall came, and he read it"), and an on-the-page confrontation. The dramatized struggle in Junia's reveal is vivid and frightening ("two old soft men wrestling on a frosted iron rail"). B generates more moment-to-moment tension. **B: 5.**

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

A is the more rigorously engineered: every catalogued object (plate, clock, log, key, two letters) is explicitly set down "in its own line" early and then explicitly re-read and discharged, and the final certificate names exactly the resolved version. The structure is the payoff. **A: 5.**

B pays off well too — the wrist-bruise observed in S19 ("a band of it, as though something had closed there") is paid off precisely by Junia's "Pike had his wrist" reveal; the doubling-tic, the *recall* slip, the unsent resignation all land. But B leans on the narrator to pre-announce some payoffs ("you have just been handed, sideways, the shape of the whole thing"), which slightly cheapens the click. **B: 4.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

A: nobody acts on what they cannot know — the whole engine is the *gap* between what Pike believes and what is true, and Hale only ever moves on what he has physically read. Junia's withholding is motivated and her knowledge (saw the shoulders, knew the tread) is grounded. **A: 5.**

B: same care, and B actually dramatizes the knowledge boundaries harder (Pike answering "the question in his own head," Crane "bewildered… known what?"). Crane's bewilderment in the struggle is the strongest single believability beat in either version of this world. **B: 5.**

### 6. Forced choice — World 1: **B**

This is the closest of the three. A is the more elegant clockwork — a flawless re-reading structure where the prose itself enacts the detective's method. But World 1 is the *confront→reveal* branch, and B commits to that premise where A somewhat doesn't: A's "confront" is Pike's confession followed by a long *solitary* re-reading, so the world-line's promised mode (confrontation, then revelation) is delivered mostly as internal audit. B gives a genuine confrontation (the lamp-lit interrogation, the extracted *recall*) and then a genuine dramatized revelation (Junia's eyewitness account of the struggle), which is what this branch is *for*. B's reveal also turns the death from an abstract "intrusion startled him" into a concrete, tragic, human struggle that neither man meant — richer, more affecting, and it pays off the physical clue (the wrist) more viscerally. A's reader-address-free discipline is admirable, but B's livelier dialogue and dramatized climax make it the stronger telling of *this* branch.

## WORLD 2 — `W2-confront-burn` (CONFRONT → BURN)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**A: 0 errors.** A's burn branch is the tightest piece of writing in either column. Junia burns the night-log; Hale lets it go; the certificate is forced to "death by fall; cause undetermined; no unlawful act against the deceased established" → policy pays. The chain from burned log → no name → unprovable death → policy pays is airtight, and the bitter irony (the proof that would have *stayed* the money is the very thing destroyed) is fully earned. The key is read "as a means without a man," explicitly. Excellent internal logic.

**B: 0 hard errors, 1 thematic over-explanation but not a continuity fault.** B's burn is consistent with its World 1 setup (same forged-recall, same struggle backstory withheld). The reveal that "the reader holds both secrets entire" is internally true. No causal breaks.

### 2. Prose quality

A: again a told story, sparse and controlled, and the burn scene (S161-167) is beautifully handled — "the spent calm of a person who has paid a price on purpose and found it bearable." The final certification scene is the best single page across all six files. **A: 5.**

B: told story, voiced, but B's burn world carries the reader-address device the furthest — "The reader, who holds both secrets entire, sees the shape Hale cannot fill: that it was Pike on the stair… that it was a panic and not a murder; that Junia burned a book to keep a broken man out of the dock." This is the narrator *summarizing the withheld answer to the reader at the climax.* It is the clearest instance in any file of telling-the-architecture rather than dramatizing it — it spends the suspense it should be banking. Still well-written sentence by sentence. **B: 4.**

### 3. Stakes and tension

A: the burn lands as a genuine loss with real stakes — a guilty-but-not-of-murder man goes free of the worst charge, a fraud is still nailed, and an honest adjuster has to sign a sentence he knows is half a lie. The tension is in Hale's *choice to not lunge for the fire* and what it costs him. **A: 5.**

B: B's burn has the dramatic table-of-four-objects standoff and a strong "she fed it to the fire" beat, but B *pre-resolves* the mystery for the reader before the burn, so the burn's tension (will we ever know?) is deflated — we already know. The stakes are stated rather than felt at the climax. **B: 4.**

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

A: the burn pays off the *entire* re-reading apparatus by *denying* it — every object Hale catalogued is named in the final certificate as either provable (fraud, key-as-means) or lost-to-the-fire (the death), and the satisfaction is the precise accounting of what survives and what doesn't. Superb. **A: 5.**

B: B pays off its setups but the burn's meaning is delivered by narrator exposition rather than by the certificate doing the work. The "agent unestablished" certificate is good, but B told us the agent first, so the payoff is informational closure for Hale, not for the reader. **B: 4.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

A: flawless. Hale never acts on what he cannot know; the entire point is that he *cannot* prove what he is sure of, and he refuses to certify on a shape he cannot show. The most disciplined knowledge-boundary writing in the experiment. **A: 5.**

B: also clean — Junia's knowledge is grounded, Hale's limits are respected. The reader-address doesn't violate character knowledge (it's narratorial, not a character knowing too much). **B: 5.**

### 6. Forced choice — World 2: **A**

A wins this one clearly. The burn branch lives or dies on the suspended, unprovable ending — on the reader feeling the hole exactly the size of a name. A *preserves* that hole: it never tells the reader who was on the stair, so when the log burns, the reader loses what Hale loses, and the bitter "policy pays a death I alone am sure was unclean" lands with full weight. B, by contrast, hands the reader the complete solution at the climax ("it was Pike on the stair… a panic and not a murder"), so B's burn destroys evidence the reader already possesses — the dramatic loss is Hale's alone, narrated rather than shared. For a *burn* ending whose whole power is irreversible loss of the truth, A's withholding is the correct and far more affecting craft choice. A's final two scenes are also the strongest prose in the entire set.

## WORLD 3 — `W3-quiet` (AUDIT → REVEAL)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

**A: 0 errors.** A's quiet branch is the most satisfying *procedural* of the A column: Hale chooses silence over confrontation, watches Pike bury the duplicate books and use the "lost" key in the dark (paying off the key directly by *witnessing* it in use), then proves everything from objects alone — clock, plate, log, wire, key — without a single confession. The certificate ("a fall, occasioned by the deceased being startled by an unlawful and unobserved intrusion") follows exactly from what he proved. The boast "proved everything, spoke to no one" is fully earned. Tight.

**B: 0 hard errors.** B's quiet branch reaches the *same factual reveal* as B's World 1 (the struggle, the wrist, the clock-grip) but by the audit route — Hale watches Pike *visit* (not bury) the drawer, reads the dome by daylight, then gets the name from Junia gently. Internally consistent. One observation: B's World 3 and World 1 share the identical Junia-reveal scenes nearly verbatim (S235-259 here vs. S255-279 in W1-B), which is consistent but means B's "quiet" differs from B's "confront" mostly in the *front half* (how Hale gets there), not the climax.

### 2. Prose quality

A: told story, and the quiet branch gives A its best *dramatic* material in the A column — the night-watch on the cold landing, Pike "moving like a man burying a weapon," the developer scene. Strong, voiced, controlled. **A: 5.**

B: this is B's finest world for prose. The "visiting, not hiding" insight (S159: "He was not hiding evidence… He was visiting it… a man behaves so toward a thing that has cost a life") is the single best piece of *detection-as-character-reading* in either version, and B's quiet reveal adds a genuinely moving final movement — Junia's "he keeps it because it is the only place the old man still is" (S269) is the most emotionally complete beat in the whole experiment, and B's ending earns its pity honestly. **B: 5.**

### 3. Stakes and tension

A: the night-watch generates real tension (will Pike see him? what is in the bundle?), and the slow road's payoff is the clean "nothing here a man could take back in the morning." Solid. **A: 4.**

B: B's quiet reveal carries deeper emotional stakes — not just "what happened" but "what it did to Pike," and the closing comparison of the loud road and quiet road ("the quiet road changed only the size of the wound it left — in the man who confessed, and in Hale") gives the ending a resonance A's more procedural close doesn't reach for. **B: 5.**

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

A: the quiet branch pays off the *key* better than any other scene in either column — Hale literally watches Pike draw the "lost" key from his coat-lining and open the death-door (S95-97), converting a catalogued suspicion into a witnessed fact. Beautifully closed. **A: 5.**

B: B pays off the wrist, the doubling, the recall, the resignation, AND adds the drawer-visit payoff and the "thread of sleeve" payoff. B's only weakness is that its climax is recycled from World 1, so the *reveal* payoff is not fresh within B's own three-world arc. **B: 4.**

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

A: exemplary — the whole design is that Hale leans on *no one's word*, only on what he sees and proves, so character-knowledge is never strained. **A: 5.**

B: also clean, and B's Crane-bewilderment beat remains the strongest single believability moment. Junia's reasoning for shielding Pike is fully motivated. **B: 5.**

### 6. Forced choice — World 3: **B**

This world-line is *audit→reveal*, and both versions execute the audit beautifully, but they diverge at the reveal. A's "quiet" ends with the reveal *suppressed* — Hale proves everything from objects and never extracts the name, certifying "intrusion and frailty" without a witness. That is elegant and self-consistent, but it makes A's World 3 essentially a quieter retread of A's World 1 *machinery* with no human revelation. B's quiet road delivers an actual *reveal* — Junia speaks, the struggle is dramatized, and crucially B adds a layer A never reaches: the *meaning* of Pike's guilt (he keeps the forged wire because it is the only proof the death "had a reason"). B's ending turns a procedural into a tragedy and explicitly reflects on how the chosen method changed the human cost. For the branch literally named "quiet → reveal," B both reveals more and means more. B wins.

---

## OVERALL FORCED CHOICE: **B**

B takes World 1 (close), A takes World 2 (clear), B takes World 3 — so B wins 2-1 on world-lines, but the overall judgment is closer than that tally suggests, because A's World 2 is the single strongest individual artifact in the entire set.

I choose **B overall.** B is the more *novelistic* version: it has dialogue that carries scenes, characters with texture (Pike's doubling-tic, Junia who "lied the way an honest woman tells the truth"), a vivid dramatized climax (the struggle on the frosted rail), and — in World 3 especially — an emotional and thematic reach that A never attempts (the forged wire as "the only place the old man still is," the reflection on how method shapes the wound). A is the more *architecturally disciplined* version: its prose is sparer and more controlled, its setup/payoff clockwork is flawless, and its World 2 burn is a small masterpiece precisely because it withholds the solution from the reader and lets the loss land. A's signature strength — withholding — is exactly right for the *burn* ending and exactly what makes A win World 2. But across the whole story, B's willingness to dramatize, to put the truth in human mouths, and to find the tragedy *inside* the mechanism makes it the more affecting and complete telling. B's recurring cost — the narrator directly telling the reader the withheld answer ("The reader knows… Hale does not") — is a real craft weakness, and it is what loses B the burn world, but it does not outweigh B's superior characterization, dialogue, and emotional payoff across the other two. A reads like the finest possible *outline executed as prose*; B reads like a *story someone is telling you.* For a told story with a voice, B.
