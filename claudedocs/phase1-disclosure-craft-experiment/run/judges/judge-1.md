# Judge 1 — Verdict

Three matched world-lines of the same branching mystery (the Vane Observatory / Aurel Crane death). For each world-line I judge version A against version B on five axes, then make a forced choice. Scene ids are 1-indexed by order of appearance within each file (S1 = first `## Scene`, etc.).

A note on the shared spine: both A and B in every world-line share the same underlying fact-set (drained instrument account; forged recall; undelivered telegram; unsent resignation; blank plates; stopped clock at 3:14; dictated night-log; lost-and-kept dome key). The difference between A and B is one of *narration*: B chooses a third-person voice that periodically steps outside Hale's knowledge to address the reader directly ("The reader may as well know it plainly, since Hale does not…"), whereas A stays close inside Hale's progressive discovery and dramatizes the same withheld facts rather than announcing them. That single structural choice drives most of what follows.

---

## World 1 — `W1-confront-reveal`

### 1. Continuity / causality errors
- **A:** No internal contradictions found across its 18 scenes. The chain is tight: the post-box telegram (S10) being undelivered is the hinge that re-opens the case, and every later re-reading of an object (plate S13, clock S14, log S15) is consistent with what was planted earlier (S1, S4, S5). The two parallel "Pike believes himself a murderer" interiority scenes (S11) and Junia's gallery testimony (S16) reconcile cleanly: Pike startled Crane, never touched him fatally, and Junia witnessed it. **Count: 0.**
- **B:** Largely consistent, but one genuine causal seam. In S15–S16 Junia testifies that Pike "still had the wrist and was pulled half over the rail himself, and put out his other hand to save himself, and caught the clock — caught the works through the open case-door, and stopped the pendulum dead." Earlier (S3) the meridian clock is described as standing "above, where the eyepiece would be," on the observing gallery thirty feet up — consistent. But the same S3 has Hale already declaring "A clock does not stop because a man falls thirty feet from it… A clock stops because someone stops it." B thus front-loads the mechanism explicitly, then has the witness confirm it — internally consistent, but it pre-empts its own reveal. Not a hard error. More notably, B's S6 ("Three secrets sat in the room… He did not know that the night-log… was written in a hand that was not Crane's — he had not yet found it") asserts Hale's ignorance as narrator-fact, then S10 has him find it; consistent but mechanical. **Hard contradictions: 0. Soft seam (mechanism pre-stated then re-confirmed): 1.**

A and B are roughly even on raw continuity; both are clean. The difference is that A *withholds* the clock mechanism as a discovery (revealed only at A-S14, "the clock had not been set. The clock had been struck") whereas B states it at S3 and again at S15, spending the surprise twice.

### 2. Prose quality — told story vs. assembled facts
- **A: 5.** Sustained voice. The adjuster's idiom ("let the cold do his counting for him," "a confession was a door, not a room," "neat thoughts were how adjusters were fooled") is consistent and characterful. Withheld information is *dramatized* (the half-burnt telegram "patient, unread" in the grate, S1) rather than reported.
- **B: 4.** Strong, often excellent prose at sentence level ("she lied the way an honest woman tells the truth," S5; "a wound in the side of the sky," S1). But the recurring direct-address narrator ("The reader may as well know it plainly, since Hale does not"; "Remember that the reader knows this," S4) repeatedly breaks the told-story spell and tips toward a *briefing* register — it tells the reader the machinery instead of trusting the scene to carry it. It reads as a told story interleaved with an editor's marginalia.

### 3. Stakes and tension
- **A: 5.** Tension is generated by Hale's own errors: he certifies "fall," then "Pike caused it," then has that certainty "crack straight across" (S10) when the telegram proves undelivered. The reader discovers alongside him, so each reversal lands as suspense.
- **B: 3.** B deliberately spends its tension. By S4 the reader is told the fund is drained, the telegram forged, Pike's belief delusional; by S6 the reader is handed all "three secrets." Thereafter the reader watches Hale catch up to what they already know ("watches Hale circle the truth with his pencil and not yet land on it," S6). This is a legitimate dramatic-irony mode, but it converts mystery-suspense into a slower watch-him-get-there, and the engine of *not knowing* is gone.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction
- **A: 5.** Every planted object pays off: the burnt telegram corner (S1) → the whole undelivered wire (S10); the blank plate noted-and-shelved (S4) → developed and confirmed (S13); the "too neat" clock frame (S5) → the struck-clock realization (S14); the lost key's "neatness of the interval" (S7) → the kept-key revelation (S17). The payoffs are *earned* because the setups were left as unexplained itches.
- **B: 4.** The same payoffs exist and are competently delivered, but several are pre-explained by the narrator before they pay off (the clock mechanism, S3; the key-was-hidden, framed openly). When the answer was already stated, the payoff is recognition rather than revelation.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
- **A: 5.** Nobody acts on what they cannot know. Pike's false confession (S9) is explicitly grounded in his *mistaken belief* that he sent the wire (S11). Junia acts only on what she saw from the gallery (S16). Hale's wrong turns are wrong precisely because he hasn't yet found the disqualifying fact.
- **B: 5.** Same fidelity. B is actually careful to motivate Pike's belief (S4) and Junia's lie (S5, S15). Character knowledge is sound in both.

### 6. Forced choice — World 1: **A**
A wins clearly. Both share the same facts and the same ending logic, but A trusts the scene to carry the withheld information and lets the reader discover with Hale, which is the entire pleasure of this kind of mystery. B's narrator keeps leaning over the reader's shoulder ("the reader knows this; Hale does not"), and in doing so spends its surprises in advance, converting suspense into dramatic irony and the told-story voice into a partial briefing. A's reversal at the post-box (S10) — Hale's hard-won certainty cracking — is the strongest single beat in either version, and it only works because the reader was not pre-told. A reads as a story; B reads as a story being annotated.

## World 2 — `W2-confront-burn`

(A here = the "audit/quiet → certified-unknown via BURN" line; B = the "loud confront → BURN" line. Both end with Junia burning the night-log.)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors
- **A:** This is the "quiet road" variant: Hale chooses *not* to confront Pike (S6), watches him bury the duplicate books and reveals the kept key by observing him use it (S8–S9). The chain is internally consistent — Hale reasons the whole case from physical evidence (clock S12, blank plate S10, undelivered wire S13) without a confession. One thing to flag: the title/ending says "either fork-1 → BURN," yet **in this A file there is no burning of the night-log at all** — Junia never appears in a confrontation scene, the log is never destroyed, and Hale reads the dictated-log as innocent kindness (S12) on his own. The ending (S14) certifies the death as intrusion-caused with full proof. So the filename promises a BURN that the A text does not contain; the A narrative is actually a *quiet-audit complete-proof* ending. This is a **mismatch between the labeled fork and the delivered scene content** — a structural/continuity defect at the seam between this file's identity and its body. **Internal story contradictions: 0; fork-label vs. content mismatch: 1.**
- **B:** The "loud" variant. Hale confronts Pike (S7–S8), extracts "the recall came, and he read it" (S8), finds the resignation (S12), confronts Junia (S13–S14), and she burns the night-log (S15). Causally consistent throughout; the burn is delivered as promised. Junia's stated motive (shield a man whose life would be ruined by being understood, S14) is coherent. **Count: 0.**

### 2. Prose quality
- **A: 5.** The quiet-road A is the strongest-written file in the whole set. The watching scenes are superb ("a man carrying something he does not want seen has a particular stoop to him," S8; "he moved like a man burying a weapon," S8). The voice never breaks; everything is inside Hale.
- **B: 4.** Good prose, same shared spine, but carries B's recurring direct-address machinery ("The reader may as well know it plainly," S4; "Remember that the reader knows this," S4; "The reader knows there is a forged telegram in that drawer," S8). Strong scene-level writing undercut by the narrator stepping out.

### 3. Stakes and tension
- **A: 5.** The midnight surveillance (S7–S9) is genuinely tense because Hale gambles the slow road and the reader doesn't know what Pike will do. The choice to *not* confront raises stakes (he's betting on patience).
- **B: 4.** B's confrontation is dramatic and the burn is a high-stakes beat, but B has again pre-spent the suspense in S4–S6 by telling the reader the secrets up front. Its tension in the back half is real (will she burn it? S14–S15) but the front half is irony, not suspense.

### 4. Setup / payoff
- **A: 5.** Clean. Every object pays off through Hale's own observation, and the "I will bring it up in daylight" promise (S9) is honored (S14). The kept-key payoff via watching Pike *use* it (S9) is a more elegant payoff than either World-1 version.
- **B: 4.** The burn is a strong payoff to "it would be kinder to burn it" (S14) → she does (S15). But the resignation/forged-recall irony is pre-loaded by the narrator (S4), so its payoff is muted.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
- **A: 5.** Nobody acts on unavailable knowledge. Hale builds only from what he sees; Junia never appears acting, so no violation possible.
- **B: 4.** Mostly sound, but one small strain: in S15 Junia chooses to burn the log specifically to deny Hale a *name*, yet the physical evidence Hale already holds (forged wire, drained fund, kept key, blank plates) makes the name nearly inferable — her burning it is emotionally motivated but instrumentally near-futile, which the text half-acknowledges. Believable as a gesture, slightly weak as strategy. Minor.

### 6. Forced choice — World 2: **A**
A is the better-written and more tightly-reasoned file, and its "prove everything by watching, confront no one" arc is the most distinctive and satisfying in the whole experiment. However, I must register the fork-label problem: this A file is labeled `confront-burn` and the in-text ending header says "either fork-1 → BURN," yet the A text delivers neither a confrontation nor a burn — it is a complete-proof quiet-audit ending. Judged purely as a told story, A is superior to B on every craft axis. Judged as a faithful instance of *this world-line's fork*, A does not deliver the burn its label promises while B does. Since the task asks me to choose the better-realized version of the world-line and my primary axes are craft, voice, tension, and payoff, I choose **A** — it is the stronger narrative artifact by a clear margin — but I flag the label/content mismatch as the single most serious continuity issue across all six files.

## World 3 — `W3-quiet`

(A here = the "quiet/audit → REVEAL via BURN" line ending "The Certified Unknown"; B = the "quiet/audit → REVEAL" line ending "The Quiet Certification," where Junia speaks the name.)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors
- **A:** This A file is the same quiet-road opening as World-2-A through its first scenes, but diverges: Junia is confronted (S6 onward, "He took the key to Junia Frost"), is offered the choice (S8), and **burns the night-log** (S9), yielding the "Certified Unknown" ending (S11) where Hale never gets the name. Internally consistent; the burn is delivered, and the "hole exactly the size of a name" (S10) is well-tracked. **Count: 0.**
- **B:** The fullest file (14 scenes). Quiet road: Hale declines to confront Pike (S7), watches him *visit but not destroy* the drawer (S8) — a sharp, distinct beat — audits the dome (S10–S11), gets the drawer by pretext (S12), comes to Junia gently (S13–S14), and she *speaks the name* (S15–S17), giving the "Quiet Certification" ending (S18). Causally the cleanest and richest of all six. Notably B here *earns* its dramatic irony better: the narrator's S4 telling pays off in S17's "he keeps it because it is the only place the old man still is," a genuinely strong thematic payoff. **Count: 0.**

### 2. Prose quality
- **A: 5.** Same high quiet-road voice as World-2-A. "He laid it out on the cell table one last time, the mute witnesses" (S10) is excellent. The grief over the burnt log (S9, "the last writing of Aurel Crane in the world") lands.
- **B: 4–5.** This is B's best file by a wide margin. The voice is steadier here and the direct-address intrusions are fewer and better integrated; when the narrator does step out (S8's "The reader, who knows what waits in that study drawer… watches Hale let him go"), it now actively builds tension rather than dissolving it, because in the quiet-road frame the irony is *Hale choosing to wait* rather than *Hale being ignorant*. I score it **5** — B finally makes its chosen technique earn its place. The "visiting, not hiding" insight (S8) and Junia's S17 speech are the strongest character writing in the entire set.

### 3. Stakes and tension
- **A: 4.** The burn ending is poignant but lower-tension than a reveal: once she burns it, the outcome is foreclosed and the last two scenes are elegiac wind-down ("carried it down into the valley like a stone," S11). Strong mood, lower drive.
- **B: 5.** B sustains tension to the final choice (S14, "if I do not, there is still a way to make sure no one ever can") and then pays it off with the name spoken and the full reconstruction (S15–S17). The drawer-visiting mystery (S8) is a tension thread A doesn't have.

### 4. Setup / payoff
- **A: 4.** All physical setups pay off, but the central emotional setup (Junia's withheld name) pays off as a *refusal* — deliberately anticlimactic. Effective thematically ("the most honest sentence he had ever signed, and the least use to anybody," S11), but as setup/payoff it is intentionally frustrated.
- **B: 5.** The richest payoff structure of all six. The drawer-visiting (S8) → "not the fear of an embezzler… larger, and older, and with a death in it" (S8) → Junia's "the only place the old man still is" (S17) is a three-stage setup/payoff that no other file attempts, and it pays the narrator's early telling back with interest.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability
- **A: 5.** Sound throughout.
- **B: 5.** Sound, and additionally B-W3 is the most psychologically *motivated* of the six: Pike's keeping-the-evidence behavior, Junia's judgment ("I judged. I had no right to, and I judged," S17), and Hale's choice not to arrest are all grounded in what each can know and feel.

### 6. Forced choice — World 3: **B**
This is the one world-line where B wins, and it wins decisively. In the quiet-road / reveal frame, B's technique (a narrator who knows more than Hale) stops being a spoiler and becomes the engine of dramatic irony done *right*: the reader watching Hale deliberately let a guilty man go to the drawer is suspense, not pre-emption. B-W3 also delivers the single best thematic payoff in the entire experiment — Pike keeping the forged wire "because it is the only place the old man still is" — and the most fully motivated characters. A-W3 is beautifully written but chooses the deliberately deflating BURN ending, which lowers tension and frustrates payoff by design. Here B is both the better story and the better-realized world-line.

---

## OVERALL FORCED CHOICE: **A**

Across the whole branching story, **A** is the stronger body of work. The forced choices land 2–1 for A by world-line (W1: A, W2: A, W3: B), but the margin matters more than the count. A's defining method — staying inside Hale's progressive discovery and *dramatizing* withheld facts rather than announcing them — produces, in W1 and W2, the two best individual reading experiences in the set: real mystery-suspense, reversals that land because the reader was not pre-told (the undelivered-telegram crack in W1-S10; the midnight-surveillance gamble in W2-S7–S9), and a consistently unbroken told-story voice. B's signature device — a narrator who repeatedly tells the reader the machinery "since Hale does not" — works against the genre in two of three world-lines, spending suspense in advance and tipping the told story toward a briefing.

The honest qualification: B is not weaker prose at the sentence level, and in W3 B proves its technique can be a strength rather than a liability — there, dramatic irony built tension instead of dissolving it, and B produced the single finest thematic payoff in the experiment ("the only place the old man still is"). If the whole story were the quiet-road reveal, I might split the decision. But taken whole, A delivers suspense, voice, and earned payoff more reliably, and it never breaks the fictional frame to lecture the reader. I also note the one serious structural defect for the record: the W2 A file is labeled `confront-burn` but delivers neither confrontation nor burn — a fork-label/content mismatch that, while not a craft failure, is the clearest fidelity problem I found. That does not change the overall verdict on narrative quality, which is A.
