# Judge 2 — Verdict

Three matched world-line pairs (A vs B) of the same branching mystery, judged
one world-line at a time, then an overall forced choice. Scores are 1-5 (5 best).

A note on what distinguishes the two versions, since it recurs in every
world-line and I will not re-explain it each time:

- **A** tells the story from inside the fiction. It withholds what Hale does not
  yet know and lets the reader discover with him (or, in a few scenes, sits
  briefly inside Junia's or Pike's head as an opaque third person). There is no
  narrator who steps outside the story.
- **B** runs an explicit dramatic-irony frame: a narrator repeatedly addresses
  "the reader," states outright what "the reader knows and Hale does not,"
  pre-plants the solution (the embezzlement, the forged recall, Pike's false
  belief, Crane's unsent resignation), and at the climaxes even names internal
  scaffolding — *"that S2 is true and waiting."*

---

## WORLD-LINE 1 — confront → reveal (W1)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

I found no hard internal contradictions in either version of W1. Both are
self-consistent and the chains of cause are sound (key hidden a month →
unseen entry; clock rigidly coupled to the stair → struck, not set; blank
plates → Junia not at the instrument → Junia on the gallery → eyewitness).

Two soft items worth recording, neither rising to a contradiction:

- **A, scene 1 vs the resolution.** Scene 1 reads the forearm bruise as where
  Crane "had clutched and missed" (a fall reading). A's resolution lands on
  "no hand was laid on Crane" — surprise off a stray tread, Pike never touching
  him. That is consistent (the bruise is the railing), but A never circles back
  to re-read that bruise the way B explicitly does (B has Junia attribute the
  *wrist* band to Pike's grip). A's physical evidence is therefore slightly
  looser-tied at the close. Not an error — a missed payoff.
- **B, scene-craft self-reference.** B scene 12 closes on *"that S2 is true and
  waiting"* — naming an internal label, not story content. This is not a
  continuity error in the causal sense, but it is a fourth-wall breach that
  reads as un-finished authorial scaffolding leaking into the text. I count it
  as a craft defect, not a plot defect. (0 causal errors A; 0 causal errors B.)

A substantive **divergence of solution**, not an error in either: A and B do
not tell the same death. In **A**, Junia witnessed that Crane was *already on
the stair of his own sorrow* and Pike *never laid a hand on him* — death by
startlement, no contact. In **B**, Junia witnessed a *physical struggle* —
Pike grabbed the wrist, they wrestled on the rail, Crane's foot went, Pike was
half-pulled over and caught the clockwork (which explains both the wrist bruise
and the stopped clock by the same gesture). B's version is the tighter machine:
it makes the wrist bruise, the stopped clock, and the "agency vs accident"
verdict all discharge from one physical event. A's version is gentler and more
ironic but leaves the wrist bruise and the clock as two separate coincidences.

### 2. Prose quality (told story vs assembled fact)

- **A: 5.** Sustained voice, period diction, controlled rhythm
  ("guinea by spooned guinea," "like water through a wall that has waited two
  years to fail"). It reads as a told story throughout.
- **B: 4.** The base prose is equally strong — much of it is, line for line, as
  fine as A. But the repeated *"the reader knows / Hale does not"* intrusions
  and the *"S2 is true"* tag interrupt the fictional dream and tip it toward
  commentary-on-a-story rather than story. Strong prose, leakier surface.

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 3.** The structure is *confess → unravel → re-confirm*. Because A keeps
  the reader inside Hale's ignorance, each reversal lands as discovery. But A's
  W1 spends its middle re-confirming things (plate, clock) the reader half-
  expects, so the tension is steady rather than escalating.
- **B: 4.** B deliberately trades surprise for suspense: the reader is told the
  answer up front, so the tension becomes *when and how will Hale arrive, and
  will Junia shield or speak.* The confrontation scenes (Pike's *"He didn't
  know?"*, Junia's account of the struggle) carry real charge precisely because
  the reader has been primed to dread them.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 4.** Clean setups (key, clock, plate, log, two letters) all pay off, and
  the final certificate line is well-earned. Slight deduction: the wrist/forearm
  bruise is set up in scene 1 and never explicitly cashed.
- **B: 4.** Tighter mechanical payoff (the wrist bruise and the stopped clock
  both discharge from Pike's grab), and the *"Crane knew"* line is a genuinely
  good buried setup paid off twice. Deduction: the pre-disclosed solution means
  several "payoffs" are confirmations rather than reveals, which blunts the
  satisfaction of the discovery itself.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 4.** Mostly clean. Hale acts only on what he has found. One small strain:
  A's Junia says the fall was "the surprise of a tread where no tread should be"
  and "Pike never laid a hand on him" — yet she was on the gallery in the dark
  and the shutter "sings"; her certainty that there was *no contact* is a strong
  claim for a dark-witness to make. Believable but slightly over-confident.
- **B: 4.** B's Junia gives a contact account she could plausibly have seen
  (paper pale in candlelight, the wrestle, the tear of sleeve), which is more
  defensible as eyewitness testimony — but B also has her *infer* the wrist
  bruise ("I'd wager") and the thread, which is honestly flagged as inference.
  Pike answering "the wrong question with the one in his own head" is a strong,
  believable beat. Even trade.

### 6. Forced choice for W1: **B**

W1 is the world-line where B's design pays for itself. The fork is *confront
then reveal*, and B's pre-disclosed dramatic irony turns the long confrontation
into genuine suspense rather than a guessing game, while its physical solution
(one grab that produces the wrist bruise, the stopped clock, and the
agency-not-accident verdict) is the tighter, more satisfying machine. A is the
better *sentence-by-sentence* artifact and keeps a cleaner fictional surface,
but in this particular fork B's structural choice and its more rigorous causal
knot win the world-line. The deciding factor is that the *"S2 is true"* leak is
a single, containable blemish, whereas A leaves the central physical clue (the
bruise) and the gentler "no-contact" theory slightly under-tied. **B.**

## WORLD-LINE 2 — confront → burn (W2)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

No hard contradictions in either. Both burn-endings are internally consistent.

- **A** here is the same opening through the confrontation, then Junia burns the
  log and Hale certifies *accident / cause undetermined* — the policy pays. A's
  logic is airtight: with the log (the only record of a watcher at the desk)
  gone, Hale has means and motive but no agent and no witness, so he cannot
  certify against the policy. Clean.
- **B** reaches the burn from its full pre-disclosed frame, and its final scene
  is the strongest single piece of reasoning in any of the six files: Hale
  *refuses* to write *accident* because "a clock does not stop itself... a key
  does not lose itself for a month," and certifies *"something else... agent
  unestablished"* — the policy does **not** pay. This is a real and important
  divergence from A: **same fork, opposite certification and opposite policy
  outcome.** Both are defensible, but they are not the same ending.

That divergence is the most interesting finding in W2 and I flag it plainly:
A's burn makes the destruction *win* (money goes out on a death Hale alone
knows was unclean); B's burn makes the destruction *cost the name but not the
verdict* (Hale certifies human agency on the mute evidence and withholds only
the name). B's is the harder, braver, and more thematically loaded ending; A's
is the bleaker, more ironic one. Neither contains an error. (0 / 0.)

### 2. Prose quality

- **A: 5.** A's burn scene is superb — "the spent calm of a person who has paid
  a price on purpose and found it bearable" — and the final certificate
  paragraph ("half a lie that he could prove no truer") is the best closing in
  A's three world-lines.
- **B: 4.** Same caveat as before: excellent base prose, but the *"the reader
  knows what Hale does not"* / *"that S2 is true and waiting"* intrusions remain
  and, in the burn fork, B even narrates *"Hale sees only the hole, and that it
  is precisely the shape of a withheld truth"* — a fine image, but delivered as
  authorial commentary about Hale rather than from inside him.

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 4.** The burn is a real loss with teeth: the reader has followed Hale's
  whole reconstruction and watches the one closing piece destroyed. High,
  concentrated tension at the grate.
- **B: 4.** B's burn carries equal charge and adds the meta-tension the frame
  was built for (the reader, holding both secrets, watches Hale *choose not to
  stop her*). Even.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 4.** The burn pays off the "a confession is a door, not a room"
  skepticism and the recurring "she carried the name a fortnight" thread. The
  certificate is a clean, ironic payoff. Slight blunting: the burn means several
  setups (key, log innocence) are deliberately *denied* their payoff — which is
  the point, but is less satisfying as craft.
- **B: 5.** B's burn fork is where its up-front disclosure earns the most:
  because the reader was *told* the full solution, the burn is devastating in a
  way it cannot be for a reader who never knew the answer, and Hale's refusal to
  certify *accident* pays off the very first scene's "distrust a thing that
  arranges itself too neatly into an accident." This is the single best
  setup/payoff alignment across all six files.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 4.** Hale certifies strictly on what survives the fire; he does not
  over-claim. Junia's calm is believable. Clean.
- **B: 4.** Likewise clean; Hale's refusal to name what he cannot prove, while
  still certifying agency on physical grounds, is exactly the discipline the
  character has shown throughout. The narrator's *"the reader, who holds both
  secrets entire, sees the shape Hale cannot fill"* is believable about Hale but
  is, again, told rather than dramatized.

### 6. Forced choice for W2: **B**

This is B's strongest world-line. The burn fork is the one place where B's
foundational gamble — disclose the whole solution to the reader at the start —
delivers its full dividend: the destruction of the evidence is tragic *because*
the reader knows precisely what is being lost, and Hale's final
*"something else, agent unestablished... the most honest sentence he ever
signed, and the least use to anybody"* is the finest ending in the experiment.
A's W2 is excellent and its policy-pays irony is bleakly effective, but it lets
the destruction simply *win*, which is a smaller thematic statement than B's
"the truth survives the fire even when the name does not." **B.**

## WORLD-LINE 3 — quiet (W3)

### 1. Continuity / causality errors

This is the world-line where the two versions diverge most in *method*, and
where I found the one place worth scrutinizing.

- **A (quiet)** takes the audit road: Hale never confronts, watches Pike bury
  the duplicate books and the kept key by night, develops the plate himself,
  reads the clock-coupling and the dictated-log innocence *without* Junia, finds
  the undelivered wire and the unsent resignation, and certifies — proving the
  whole case "having spoken to no one." This is internally tight and is, of the
  three A world-lines, the most impressive as pure detection: every conclusion
  is sourced to a thing Hale saw or read, never to testimony. **No errors.**

- **B (quiet)** takes the audit road but *keeps Junia's eyewitness reveal* at
  the end — Hale watches Pike visit-but-not-destroy the drawer, audits the dome,
  then comes to Junia gently and she gives the full struggle account. B's W3 is
  the longest and most psychologically rich of all six (the "he keeps it because
  it is the only place the old man still is" exchange is the emotional peak of
  the whole experiment).

  One **near-contradiction in B's W3 to flag honestly:** B scene 8 ("he did a
  curious thing... he put it back... he was not hiding evidence... he was
  visiting it") establishes that Pike does **not** remove or hide the telegram —
  he keeps it in the drawer. Then B scene 11 ("He got the drawer that
  afternoon") has Hale openly inventory that same drawer and find both the
  telegram and the resignation in it. These are consistent (Pike left it; Hale
  finds it). But B scene 8 also has Hale conclude the heart of the matter "lay
  in that drawer," while A's quiet road has Pike *bury the duplicate books and
  the key in the foundations* — two different hiding behaviors. Within B alone
  there is no contradiction; I note only that B's "Pike visits but preserves the
  evidence" is a deliberate, well-motivated change from A's "Pike buries the
  evidence," and B earns it (it sets up the "only place the old man still is"
  payoff). **0 hard errors in either.**

- One genuine soft-seam, **B scene 6** ("S2 is true and waiting") — same
  scaffolding leak as the other world-lines.

The substantive divergence again: **A's quiet road solves the case with zero
testimony; B's quiet road still relies on Junia's spoken witness for the final
act.** A is therefore the *purer* realization of the "quiet" premise (the whole
virtue Hale names is "the slow road leaned on no one's word"); B's quiet road is
quieter only in *manner* (no raised voice) but still ends on a confession-by-
witness. This matters for which version best honors the world-line's own thesis.

### 2. Prose quality

- **A: 5.** A's quiet W3 is, to my reading, the best-written single file in the
  experiment. The surveillance scenes ("a man carrying something he does not
  want seen has a particular stoop to him") and the closing ("went down the hill
  having spoken to no one and proved everything") are flawless period prose with
  a controlled, cumulative voice.
- **B: 4.** B's W3 has the richest *content* (the Pike-as-grief reading is
  extraordinary) and its prose is excellent, but the frame intrusions persist
  and, in the quiet fork especially, they undercut the very stillness the
  world-line is about. A "quiet" story narrated by a voice that keeps turning to
  tell the reader what's coming is working against its own register.

### 3. Stakes and tension

- **A: 4.** The night-watch of Pike burying the evidence is the most
  *suspenseful* sequence in any A file — silent pursuit in the dark, real
  jeopardy of discovery. Strong.
- **B: 4.** B's tension is more interior (the dread of the drawer, the long
  approach to Junia) and the emotional stakes are higher (Pike's grief, Junia's
  self-judgment). Different flavor, equal level.

### 4. Setup / payoff satisfaction

- **A: 4.** Every object pays off and the "spoke to no one, proved everything"
  thesis is fully delivered. The watched-burial is a clean setup→payoff. Slight
  deduction only because A's W3, lacking the eyewitness, never resolves the
  *exact* manner of death (contact vs startlement) — it lands on "startled off
  the iron," which is reasoned but not witnessed, so a sliver of the central
  question stays inferential.
- **B: 5.** B's W3 has the most complete payoff structure of all six: the
  drawer-visit setup pays off as Pike's grief; the "S2"/withheld-name pays off
  as Junia's full account; the wrist bruise and stopped clock pay off through
  the struggle; and the final "you spent everything to push a man through a door
  he had already opened" pays off both letters at once. Nothing is left
  inferential. This richness is exactly what A's quiet road sacrifices for
  purity.

### 5. Character-knowledge believability

- **A: 4.** Strong. Hale acts only on observation; his inference of "startled
  off the iron" is appropriately hedged as a reconstruction, not a claim of
  witnessed fact. Believable throughout.
- **B: 5.** B's W3 is the most believable on this axis of any file. Crane's
  bewilderment ("Read what, man? Known what?") is the single best dramatization
  of the central irony — a man who genuinely *cannot* act on knowledge he does
  not have. Pike answering "the question in his own head," Junia's honestly-
  flagged inferences ("I'd wager"), and her explicit "I judged. I had no right
  to" are all rigorously sourced to what each character could know. Highest mark.

### 6. Forced choice for W3: **A**

This is the one world-line I give to A, and on its own terms. The premise of the
"quiet" fork is detection that "leans on no one's word," and **A is the only
version that actually delivers that** — it solves the entire case, including the
manner of death, by observation and audit alone, and its prose is the finest in
the experiment. B's W3 is, in absolute terms, the richer and more moving file
(the Pike-grief reading and Crane's bewilderment are the emotional and thematic
high points of everything I read), but it reaches its ending by reverting to the
spoken eyewitness, which means it is a *gentler confrontation* dressed as the
quiet road rather than a true audit-only solution — and its frame-intrusions
fight hardest against the stillness this particular fork is built on. A wins the
world-line because it honors the world-line's own thesis and does so with the
best prose surface in the set. **A.** (This is the closest of the three calls.)

---

## OVERALL FORCED CHOICE: **B**

Per world-line: **W1 → B, W2 → B, W3 → A.** Overall: **B.**

Across the whole branching story, B is the stronger work, and I choose it
despite preferring A's prose surface and despite A clearly winning the quiet
world-line. My reasons, in order of weight:

1. **B's central architectural choice succeeds on its own terms.** The decision
   to disclose the full solution to the reader up front (the embezzlement, the
   forged-but-undelivered recall, Pike's false belief, Crane's unsent
   resignation) is a real gamble — it trades whodunit-surprise for dramatic
   irony — and across all three forks it *pays*: the confrontation becomes
   suspense (W1), the burn becomes tragedy the reader can fully feel (W2), and
   the quiet road becomes a study in a man grieving a death whose reason he
   invented (W3). A withholds and lets the reader discover, which is the safer,
   more conventional, and often more elegant choice — but it produces a smaller
   emotional return at each fork's climax.

2. **B's causal machine is tighter where it matters most.** In W1 and W2, B
   makes the wrist bruise, the stopped clock, and the accident-vs-agency verdict
   all discharge from a single witnessed physical event (Pike's grab on the
   rail). A's gentler "no contact / startled off the iron" reading leaves the
   wrist/forearm bruise and the stopped clock as separate coincidences and never
   re-reads the scene-1 bruise. The forensic knot is one of the genre's core
   pleasures and B ties it better.

3. **B's two W3 set-pieces are the best writing-with-meaning in the experiment**
   — Crane's *"Read what, man? Known what?"* (the cleanest dramatization of the
   whole "no one acts on what they cannot know" theme) and the "only place the
   old man still is" reading of Pike's grief. Nothing in A reaches that depth of
   character.

The cost of choosing B is honest and I record it: B repeatedly breaks the
fictional surface (*"the reader knows... Hale does not"*) and, worse, leaks
internal scaffolding into the prose (*"that S2 is true and waiting"* appears at
W1, W2, and W3 climaxes). That tag is an unfinished-craft blemish and it is the
single biggest mark against B. If those leaks were cleaned out, the choice would
be lopsided; as they stand, A's flawless surface keeps the contest close. But
craft-of-meaning, structural payoff, forensic rigor, and emotional return all
favor B by margins larger than the surface-polish margin that favors A.
**Overall: B.**
