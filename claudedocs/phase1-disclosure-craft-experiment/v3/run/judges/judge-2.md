# Judge 2 — Verdict on "The Meridian Vane" (A vs B), three world-lines

Method note: I read both versions of each world-line in full. The two authors
share a great deal of the early, pre-branch material; the substantive divergence
is concentrated in (a) the opening scene's framing paragraph and (b) the
branch-specific endgame scenes. I judged the whole reading experience of each
world-line, but weighted the branch scenes most, since that is where the authors
genuinely differ.

---

## W1 — reveal (`reveal__A.md` vs `reveal__B.md`)

### (1) Continuity / causality errors

**A:** 0 hard errors. The chain is internally tight. One thing to note (not an
error): the "Reveal: The Key That Was Never Lost" scene reconstructs the key
having lain "warm in Pike's coat-lining a clean month," which is *knowledge Hale
asserts*. He has earlier (in the Pike-interior scene) been told by the narrator
this is true, but Hale himself never recovers the key on-page in the reveal
branch; he reasons it from the day-book + the witness. This is consistent with
his method, so I count it sound, not an error. The clock-physics, blank plate,
undelivered telegram, and Junia's gallery sightline all interlock without
contradiction.

**B:** 0 hard errors. One small friction point in "Reveal: The Shape Against the
Shutter": Junia says of the lower door "he had a key, he must have had a key, for
I had locked it myself." This is a clean, motivated inference rather than a
contradiction — she knows she locked it, so a key is implied. B actually tightens
the logic here by having her *name* the locked-door problem, which A leaves to
Hale to infer later. No causality break.

Count: A=0, B=0. Cite: both clean across "Reveal" scenes.

### (2) Prose — told story vs assembled facts
A reads as a told story with a sustained, controlled voice (the "warm bad taste of
a thing arranged to please him," the certificate "chosen the way you choose stones
to cross deep water"). B is also genuinely voiced and arguably even cleaner in the
reveal dialogue. Both are well above list-like. **A=5, B=5.**

### (3) Stakes and tension
A's reveal stretches the withholding longer and lands the "worse than murder and
better than murder" beat with more pressure. B's is tense but slightly more
expository in the key-reconstruction paragraph ("filings turn when the magnet is
laid among them" is good, but the surrounding sentence does more summarizing).
**A=5, B=4.**

### (4) Setup / payoff
Both discharge every planted object (plate, clock, key, telegram, log-hand). A's
payoff sequence is staged across three "Reveal" scenes and feels more earned; B
folds the key payoff and the rest-falls-true payoff into one denser scene, which is
efficient but slightly less satisfying as a release. **A=5, B=4.**

### (5) Character-knowledge believability
Neither version has anyone act on what they cannot know. Hale reasons only from
what he has seen/read; Junia testifies only to what she witnessed from the gallery;
Pike's false certainty is explicitly marked as false. Both are airtight. **A=5,
B=5.**

### (6) Forced choice for W1: **A**
A wins W1. The two are close in prose and equal in logic, but A's three-beat reveal
gives the central reversal (it was a startle, not a murder; the cruelty never
landed) more room to breathe and more emotional return on the long setup. B's
single-scene compression is admirable craft but spends the payoff faster than A
does. A also gives the certificate scene a fuller moral weighting ("he did not write
certificates to ease a guilty man's heart").

**Cooler/flatter passage flag:** In B, "Reveal: The Key That Was Never Lost" runs a
shade more like a *summary of the solution* than like lived prose — the paragraph
beginning "And with the key in its place the rest fell true behind it" lists the
plate / clock / wire / old-man in quick apposition, which reads marginally more
like a checklist of cleared clues than the scenes around it. A's matching scene has
the same job to do but carries more felt weather. In A: none stood out as cooler.

## W2 — burn (`burn__A.md` vs `burn__B.md`)

Both A and B share the identical opening here (the "cold was a different animal"
framing). The divergence is the two "Burn" scenes.

### (1) Continuity / causality errors
**A:** 0. The burn logic is exact: the log was the one record that could both
exonerate Junia's hand *and* thread to the climber's name; burning it destroys
both, so Hale cannot certify intrusion and the policy pays. Internally consistent
with everything established.

**B:** 0. Same chain, same outcome, same certificate wording. No contradictions.

Count: A=0, B=0.

### (2) Prose
A's burn scene is the stronger piece of writing in this pair: "a year of careful
columns and one death-night's stars going up in a single bright breath, and the
iron door clapped shut on a red roar," and the costing-out image ("counted a price
out coin by coin and paid the whole of it on purpose, and has nothing left to be
afraid with"). B's burn is good but a touch more functional, explaining the stakes
of the burn in a more telling-than-showing register ("For it was the log that would
have proved the second hand innocent..."). **A=5, B=4.**

### (3) Stakes and tension
A holds the moment of the burn with more dread (Hale's half-second of understanding,
the choice not to wrestle her). B compresses it. The defeat lands harder in A. 
**A=5, B=4.**

### (4) Setup / payoff
This is the bleak/loss world-line, and the payoff is an *anti-payoff* — the proof
destroyed, the policy paying wrongly. Both execute the bitter inversion. A's
certificate scene ("The proof was ash in a stove, and the ash was already cold")
closes the loop with more finality than B's ("the money went out, clean, on a death
that he alone in the world was sure had not been clean at all"). Both strong; A
edges it. **A=5, B=4.**

### (5) Character-knowledge believability
Clean in both. Junia acts on what she witnessed; Hale certifies only what he can
prove, explicitly refusing to write what he merely knows. No one over-knows. **A=5,
B=5.**

### (6) Forced choice for W2: **A**
A wins W2 more clearly than W1. The burn is the most cinematic beat in the whole
novella, and A stages it with better physical immediacy and a sharper read of
Junia's spent calm. B tells the meaning of the burn where A lets the burn carry its
own meaning.

**Cooler/flatter passage flag:** In B, "Burn: The Log Goes Into the Stove" — the
paragraph beginning "For it was the log that would have proved the second hand
innocent" reads more like an explanatory gloss on the act than like the act itself;
it cools slightly relative to the vivid burning sentence just before it. In A:
none.

## W3 — audit (`audit__A.md` vs `audit__B.md`)

This is the longest divergence — six "Audit" scenes each, where Hale takes the
silent-watch road instead of confronting Pike.

### (1) Continuity / causality errors
**A:** 0. The audit chain holds: silent reconstruction → watch Pike carry the
duplicate books → see the kept key fitted to the death-door → see the books buried
→ develop the plate alone → reason the clock/log → trace the wire → certify
intrusion. Every certified fact is sourced to something Hale saw or read, never to
Pike's (unsolicited) confession, which is the whole thematic point and it is kept
faithfully.

**B:** 0. Same chain, same fidelity. B even foregrounds the logic a touch more
("a man does not creep with a hidden key to bury his books at the scene of an
accident").

Count: A=0, B=0.

### (3 of method, but taking prose first) — Prose
This pair is the closest of the three. Both are sustained, voiced, and confident.
A has a few of the better single images (the pistol-in-the-coat memory in "The
Watched Man"; "the guilty pat earth that will never be patted flat enough" in "The
Shape of the Theft"). B is slightly more even and a hair more analytic in places.
I give A a narrow prose edge on the strength of those concrete recollections.
**A=5, B=4.**

### (3) Stakes and tension
The audit world-line trades confrontation for surveillance, so its tension is the
patience-in-the-dark kind. A sustains that dread well (the long motionless watch,
the recognized gesture). B sustains it too but resolves the watched-burial scene a
little faster. **A=5, B=4.**

### (4) Setup / payoff
Both pay off every clue cold, with no confession load-bearing — the thematic
promise of the audit branch. A's "Climber Reasoned, Not Seen" explicitly tallies
"Every part of it from a thing seen or read or reasoned. Not one part from a
confession," which crystallizes the branch's whole argument; B does the same in its
matching scene with comparable force. Even. **A=5, B=4.**

### (5) Character-knowledge believability
This is the most demanding branch for this criterion, because Hale never gets the
eyewitness — he must build the climber-on-the-stair conclusion by inference, and
both authors handle the epistemic honesty carefully. A is explicit: "He had not
seen who climbed the stair... and would not pretend to one... from that the rest
could be reasoned." B is equally scrupulous: "a death by intrusion and frailty,
reasoned whole, and not the murder a confession would have handed him cheap." Both
refuse to let Hale certify beyond his evidence. **A=5, B=5.**

### (6) Forced choice for W3: **A**
A wins W3, but by the slimmest margin of the three. The branch is nearly a tie on
craft; A's edge is in texture (the borrowed-memory simile, the earth-patting
image) and in the slightly more patient staging of the night-watch. B is excellent
and would be a defensible pick; I take A on concrete sensory detail.

**Cooler/flatter passage flag:** In B, "Audit: The Climber Reasoned, Not Seen" —
the closing paragraph beginning "Hale stood with both papers in the cold and felt
the whole of it assemble" itemizes the four proofs (clock / plate / key / wire) in
a quick list, reading a notch more like a recap of the case than the scene-prose
around it. A's matching scene threads the same four proofs into narrated reasoning
rather than a list. In A: none stood out as cooler.

---

## OVERALL forced choice: **A**

Across all three world-lines my forced choice is **A**, and the verdict is
consistent rather than split: A=A=A. The two authors are remarkably matched — same
plot architecture, the same planted objects, equally airtight continuity and
character-knowledge discipline (no one in either version acts on what they cannot
know, and both keep Hale's certifications honestly inside his evidence). The
deciding factor is texture and pacing at the payoff. A consistently stages its
turns across a beat or two more, lets vivid physical images (the red roar of the
stove, the patted grave-dirt, the borrowed pistol-memory) do the explanatory work,
and trusts the reader; B is a genuinely strong, sometimes tighter writer but
repeatedly slips, at exactly the solution/payoff moments, into a cooler
recap-of-the-clues register — listing the cleared evidence in apposition rather
than dramatizing it. That recurring flattening (flagged once per world-line, always
in B, always at the "the rest falls into place" beat) is the clearest difference
between two otherwise near-equal manuscripts, and it tips every world-line and the
whole to A.
