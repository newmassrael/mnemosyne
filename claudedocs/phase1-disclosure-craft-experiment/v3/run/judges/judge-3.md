# Judge 3 — "The Meridian Vane" (A vs B, three world-lines)

A note on method. In each world-line A and B share the same seven-scene
setup spine (foot-of-stair, last road, night-log, plate, clock, ledgers,
key) and diverge only after "The Account Discrepancy" — at the branch
(Reveal / Burn / Audit) — plus the two openings are written in two
distinct registers (one names Lewin Hale and opens on cold as
"architecture"; the other opens on the body and "cold do his counting").
The two registers are not consistent A-to-B across world-lines: in W1
(reveal) it is **A** that uses the "different animal / architecture"
opening and the fuller, more interior scene-set, while **B** uses the
leaner "cold do his counting" opening; in W2 (burn) and W3 (audit) the
labels flip — **A** carries the leaner opening, **B** the fuller. I judged
each world-line on its own merits as instructed and did not let the
register-label leak across.

---

## W1 — reveal (`reveal__A.md` vs `reveal__B.md`)

**(1) Continuity / causality.**
No hard continuity errors in either. Both keep the chain consistent:
blank plate → she was on the gallery not at the instrument; struck clock
→ timestamp of the fall; lost key warm in Pike's coat → unobserved
entry; undelivered wire → Crane never knew → confession is to a death
not caused; old man already on the stair → startled fall. One soft
point in **A**: in "The Spectroscope and the Empty Plate" Hale is *shown*
the plate by Junia ("She drew it for him from its black slide"), yet in
"The Climber on the Stair" reveal he later treats the plate as the thing
he developed himself in the workroom ("He brought the wet plate to her
still dripping") — A wants both the early reveal-of-blankness AND the
dramatic development scene, which is a mild redundancy rather than a
contradiction (a single plate handled twice). **B** is tighter here: in
B, Hale opens the holder himself by habit and finds it unexposed, then
re-develops the *same loaded plate* to confirm — cleaner causal hygiene.
Call it A: 1 soft seam; B: 0.

**(2) Prose — told story vs assembled.**
Both read as genuinely told prose with a voice; neither is list-like.
A's register is more lush and aphoristic ("cold was weather… on the
summit it was architecture," "like birds that had forgotten which wire
was home"). B is plainer-grained but still shapely ("water through a
wall that has waited two years to fail," "the stillness of a person who
has carried a held breath so long that the letting of it is its own kind
of falling"). A's reveal scenes ("It was Pike on the stair" / "The Key
That Was Never Lost") are slightly richer in interiority and image. **A=5,
B=4.**

**(3) Stakes / tension.**
Both sustain the central reversal (the confession is to the wrong death;
the truth is "worse than murder and better than murder"). A presses the
moral vise harder — Junia's "*No.*" turn carries "a fortnight's anguish
packed into it," and the certificate scene weighs Pike "almost welcoming"
the rope. B is a touch cooler at the climax. **A=5, B=4.**

**(4) Setup/payoff.**
Both pay off every planted object (plate, clock, key, wire, log-hand,
prints). A's payoff in "The Key That Was Never Lost" explicitly re-walks
each clue and lands the thesis ("not one part of it was a confession");
B does the same with "the way filings turn when the magnet is at last
laid among them." Both very satisfying; A marginally fuller. **A=5, B=4.**

**(5) Character-knowledge believability.**
Strong in both. Junia knows the climber by tread/shoulder-height against
dome-light — earned by the gallery vantage. Hale reasons rather than
guesses. One nick in **B**: Junia says of the lower door "he had a key,
he must have had a key, for *I had locked it myself*" — a clean detail,
but it slightly over-specifies her knowledge (she infers the key
correctly, which is fair). A keeps her to what she saw. Both believable;
A=5, B=4 (B's "must have had a key" is inference voiced as near-fact, a
hair less disciplined).

**(6) Forced choice for W1: A.**
A wins reveal on richness without losing rigor. Its reveal sequence has
more blood in it — the anguish in Junia's denial, the "cold peace" of
Hale holding the whole truth, the certificate chosen "the way you choose
stones to cross deep water." B is clean and correct but runs a degree
cooler through the climax and resolution. For the world-line whose whole
point is an emotional reversal, A's extra warmth is the deciding margin.

**RECOOL flag (W1):** In **B**, "Reveal: What Hale Certifies" reads a
notch more procedural/summary than the prose around it — it leans on the
policy-logic ("a policy does not pay an accident that another man's crime
walked into the room and made") in a way that feels more like a ruling
than a scene; cooler than B's own "Shape Against the Shutter." In **A**,
nothing reads as a correction; the flattest A passage is the opening
paragraph of "Reveal: What Hale Certifies" but it warms quickly.

## W2 — burn (`burn__A.md` vs `burn__B.md`)

**(1) Continuity / causality.**
No errors in either. The burn branch is the bleak one: Junia feeds the
night-log into the stove, destroying both her own exoneration and the
only thread to the climber, so Hale must certify a death he privately
knows was unclean but cannot prove. Both versions track this cleanly:
the burned log was the record of the second hand AND the only testimony
of a watcher → certificate falls back to "cause undetermined, no unlawful
act established" → policy pays. Both correctly note Pike's forged-wire
proof survives (found undelivered) and the key can be written only as "a
means with no man inside it." Consistent in both. 0 / 0.

**(2) Prose.**
Both strong; this is the world-line where **A** reads most alive. A's
burn scene is vivid and physical: "a year of careful columns and one
death-night's stars going up in a single bright breath, and the iron
door clapped shut on a red roar," and Junia "had counted a price out
coin by coin and paid the whole of it on purpose." B's burn is also good
but slightly more expository in the aftermath ("For it was the log that
would have proved the second hand innocent…") — it tells the
consequence where A lets the image carry it. **A=5, B=4.**

**(3) Stakes / tension.**
A higher. The burn is an act of irreversible sacrifice and A stages it
as such — the half-second of Hale's failed reflex ("his hand came up;
his mouth opened; but he did not cross the floor and wrestle a grieving
woman over a hot stove"), the cold horror of the closing image ("the
name gone up the chimney into the white winter sky"). B renders the same
beats a touch more flatly. **A=5, B=4.**

**(4) Setup/payoff.**
Both honor the dark inversion of the setup: every clue that pointed at
truth now points at a truth that cannot be certified. A's "What Hale
Cannot Certify" is the better payoff — it itemizes what he *can* write in
iron vs what is "ash now," and lands on "the proof was ash in a stove,
and the ash was already cold." B reaches the same end with slightly more
legal register and less sting. **A=5, B=4.**

**(5) Character-knowledge believability.**
Excellent in both. Junia's choice is legible (she burns to keep a name
unspoken); Hale's restraint is earned (no dignity in wrestling her).
Pike's parallel "confesses what he cannot" scene is identical in logic.
**A=5, B=5** — neither has anyone act on what they cannot know; both are
disciplined.

**(6) Forced choice for W2: A.**
This is A's strongest world-line. The burn demands a scene of physical,
almost unbearable finality, and A delivers it with sensory force and a
closing image that earns the bleakness; B is faithful and competent but
cooler and more explanatory through the same beats. Clear A.

**RECOOL flag (W2):** In **B**, "Burn: What Hale Cannot Certify" opens
"He certified it the next morning, and it was the hardest line he had
ever set his name beneath, because it was half a lie that he could prove
no truer" — and then explains the ruling more than it dramatizes it;
reads cooler/flatter than B's own burn scene. In **A**, none —
the flattest stretch is the legal language *inside* the certificate
itself, which is appropriate to the document.

## W3 — audit (`audit__A.md` vs `audit__B.md`)

This is the longest divergence: six "Audit" scenes where Hale takes the
slow road — refuses the confession, watches Pike bury the duplicate
books, sees the kept key fitted to the death-door with his own eyes, then
reasons the rest from clock, plate, wire, and log without coaxing a word.

**(1) Continuity / causality.**
No hard errors in either. Both keep the through-line: Hale chooses NOT to
confront → reconstructs theft from the page → watches Pike at midnight →
sees the key → follows him to the burial → develops the plate alone →
reads clock and log without her → traces the wire to the post-box →
certifies on watched/reasoned evidence only, "no confession in any line
of it." Both are internally airtight. One small note in **A**: A's "Audit:
The Climber Reasoned, Not Seen" has Hale find the recall *draft* in the
copy-book among papers "Pike had so freely handed over" — and **B** uses
the identical device. Both are consistent; neither errs. A point of
craft, not continuity: **B** keeps Hale physically *inside the lower
door* in the dark for the burial ("stood inside the lower door… listened
to the bursar at his burying"), whereas **A** has him follow down and
watch "from the deeper shadow at the stair-foot." Both coherent. 0 / 0.

**(2) Prose.**
Both very good and closely matched. **B**'s audit prose is, for once, the
warmer and more textured of the two — "Audit: The Key in the Lining" in B
("knew it by the labelled bareness of the hook it had hung on, knew it by
the month it had been *lost*") and the burial scene's restraint read with
real grain. **A** is also strong ("the way the guilty pat earth that will
never be patted flat enough"), and A's "Audit: The Watched Man" with the
remembered "man who had a pistol in his coat and a dead partner in the
next room" is the single best image in either audit. Near parity; I give
A the slimmest edge on peak imagery. **A=5, B=4.**

**(3) Stakes / tension.**
The audit's tension is procedural-suspense (will the watched man betray
himself?) rather than emotional reversal, and both sustain it. A's
midnight-landing watch is marginally tenser ("Hale did not stir. Not a
finger.") and the pistol-memory raises the read on Pike's interior guilt.
B is steady and patient but a hair less charged. **A=4, B=4** — genuinely
close; the audit is the least emotionally volatile branch for both.

**(4) Setup/payoff.**
Both pay off completely and, notably, the audit's thesis ("nothing in it
a frightened man could ever take back") is the most intellectually
satisfying of the three resolutions in *both* versions. A's "The Cold
Certificate" lands it cleanly ("there was nothing in the whole of it a
frightened man could ever take back"); B's matching close ("went down the
hill having spoken to no one and proved everything") is equally strong
and arguably the crisper single line. **A=5, B=5.**

**(5) Character-knowledge believability.**
The audit is the most rigorous branch on this axis by design — Hale acts
only on what he sees or reasons, and both versions are scrupulous about
it (the plate "cannot be wept out of," the clock "cannot be argued into
another hour"). The recall-draft-in-copy-book is a slightly convenient
plant in both, but it is fairly handled (Pike "freely handed over" the
papers, over-eager to seem to hide nothing). **A=5, B=5.**

**(6) Forced choice for W3: A — but the narrowest of the three.**
A and B are closest here. A wins on two peaks: the pistol-and-dead-partner
memory in "The Watched Man," and the slightly tenser staging of the
night watch. B is arguably the more *even* of the two — no flat patches,
a very clean certificate — and a reader who prizes restraint could
defensibly pick B. I take A for the higher ceiling, conceding this is the
world-line where B comes nearest to winning.

**RECOOL flag (W3):** In **A**, "Audit: The Quiet Reconstruction" runs a
touch cooler/more thesis-stating than the scenes around it — it pauses to
*explain* the value of watching an unseen man ("there is a thing a
confession can never give you…") in a near-essayistic register before the
action resumes. Not a correction, but the most lecture-like stretch in A.
In **B**, "Audit: The Quiet Reconstruction" similarly front-loads the
rationale ("It was the harder road and he knew it") but folds back into
scene faster. Neither reads like an error-correction; both are the
branch's natural "method statement."

---

## OVERALL forced choice: A.

Across all three world-lines I choose **A**. The two authors are very
close in continuity discipline and in the architecture of the mystery —
both plot the clue-chain without an error worth the name, and both reach
the same three endings with the same logic. The deciding axis is warmth
and dramatic charge at the moments that matter. In every world-line A
runs a degree hotter through the emotional peaks — Junia's anguished
"*No.*" in reveal, the red-roar finality of the stove in burn, the
pistol-memory and the held-breath watch in audit — while B, though clean,
correct, and sometimes the crisper closer, tends to *state the ruling*
where A *plays the scene*. B's flattest passages are consistently its
final certificate scenes, which read more like legal rulings than the
prose around them (flagged in W1 and W2). A's weakest tendency, by
contrast, is occasional essayistic method-stating at the *opening* of a
slow-road scene (W3's "Quiet Reconstruction"), which it recovers from.
Given that this novella's whole engine is a moral reversal — the
confession is to the wrong death — the version that brings more heat to
the human cost is the better-told story. That is A, most decisively in
W2 (burn), clearly in W1 (reveal), and narrowly in W3 (audit).
