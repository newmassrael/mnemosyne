## The Foot of the Iron Stair

The body lay where the stair let it lie, folded at the bottom of the iron treads like something dropped from a height and not arranged. Hale crouched over it without touching, the way he had learned to crouch over a hundred deaths that other men called accidents, and let the cold do his counting for him. Crane had fallen from the top. That much the angle of the shoulders told him, and the long bruise the railing had pressed across one forearm where the man had clutched and missed. A director of an observatory, sixty years old, the eyes gone milk-pale behind their lids — Hale had read the file on the train and knew the man had been blind these two years. A blind man on a dark stair at three in the morning. The policy would call it nothing stranger than that, and the policy was usually right.

He straightened and let his eye go up the shaft of the stair to the black aperture of the dome, then down again, then aside, to the small iron grate set into the wall of the entry where someone kept a meagre fire against the night watch. The fire was long dead. But among the grey ash there was a curl of paper that had not wholly burned, a corner saved by the cold, and on it the printed banding of a telegraph form and three or four words in a clerk's careful copperplate — *…patron… recall… without delay…* — the rest gone to char. Hale's gaze passed over it and moved on, cataloguing the room: lamp, log-desk, the dead grate. He was thinking of stairs and ice. He did not stoop to the ash. The half-burnt thing lay there in its grate, patient, unread, while the adjuster wrote *fall* in the first clean line of his book.

## The Last Passable Road

Junia Frost stood at the window of the cold study and watched the adjuster's trap come up the last passable road, and she had her answer ready before he had set his foot on the step. She had been at the spectroscope, she would say. All night, until the grey came up over the moor. There was a night's work to prove it and a man dead who could not contradict her, and if she said the words flatly enough and looked at the snow while she said them, that would be the end of the asking.

She made herself believe the spectroscope as she rehearsed it, because a thing half-believed will sometimes pass where a lie alone will not. But her own mind would not stay obedient. It kept climbing — up the spiral of the iron stair to the gallery that ringed the dome, where the cold had been so deep she had felt it in her teeth, and where she had stood pressed to the curve of the wall with her breath held while below her, in the dark, a shape came in by the lower door and went up. She knew the tread. She knew the height of the shoulders against the faint dome-light. She had seen who climbed the stair.

When the maid had gone up at dawn to lay the study fire, she had crossed the gallery without thinking and left, in the frost that filmed the iron treads, a second set of prints over the first — small, deliberate, going up where no one should have gone. Junia had not noticed them. She was rehearsing the spectroscope. *I worked until dawn*, she would tell the adjuster, looking at the snow. *I saw nothing at all.*

## The Night-Log in the Wrong Hand

The night-log lay open on the slanted desk where the watch was kept, and Hale turned it to the lamp and read the last full page with the patience of a man who distrusts the obvious. The hand stopped him at once. The earlier entries marched down the column in a firm, square, old-fashioned script — Crane's, he supposed, the director's own — but the final observation, the one dated the night of the death, was set down in a hand entirely other: smaller, quicker, the loops of it more modern, a younger hand by twenty years at least. The numbers were precise. The figures of declination and right ascension were filled in to the second. But the *hand was wrong*.

A blind man, Hale reminded himself, does not write his own log. That was the harmless reading, and he turned it over and found he could not keep it harmless. For a log is written *as the watch is kept*, hour by hour through the dark, and that meant another person had stood here in the dome through the small hours of the night a man fell to his death — had stood here writing, in a steady hand, the positions of stars. Not a faltering hand. Not a frightened one. He looked at the careful figures and felt the first cold prickle of a second presence in the room, someone who had been here and had said nothing of it. A forger, perhaps. Perhaps worse. He copied the strange entry into his own book, and beneath it, in his own square print, he wrote a single word and underlined it: *Who?*

## The Spectroscope and the Empty Plate

The spectroscope stood at the south pier under its grey dust-cloth, and beside it the plate-camera on its mount, and Hale lifted the cloth because thoroughness was the only virtue his trade required of him. Miss Frost had told him, levelly, that she had worked here through the night, that she had been bent to this eyepiece while her director climbed alone to the dome and fell. He had no cause to doubt her. She had answered him without a tremor, looked him in the face, named the lines she had been measuring — a sober, competent woman doing sober, competent work, and grief makes its own kind of stillness.

He opened the camera's plate-holder all the same, by habit, expecting the spent glass of a night's exposures, the ghost-trails of stars drawn out across an hour. The holder was loaded. The plate was in it, seated and ready. And the plate was *blank* — clean grey gelatine, never drawn back to the sky, never given a single second of light. He turned it in his fingers. Unexposed. Pristine. A spectroscopist who had worked the instrument until dawn, and not one exposure made the whole night long.

He told himself the obvious things. The seeing had been poor; she had measured visually and not photographed; a dozen reasons, all of them ordinary. He had no wish to make a suspect of a grieving assistant who had given him a straight account, and he did not. He set the plate back in its holder, drew the dust-cloth down, and let her stand, in his book, as a witness and not a suspect. But he wrote the plate down too, blank, in its line, the way he wrote down everything.

## The Meridian Clock at 3:14

The meridian clock kept its station against the north wall, a tall regulator in a glazed case, its pendulum hung in the long throat of the cabinet behind the glass. Hale had passed it twice without looking. The third time he looked, because its face had stopped, and a stopped clock in a house of precise men is a question.

Fourteen minutes past three. The hands held it there, exact, the second-hand frozen partway up its arc. He opened the case and felt the chain and the drum, and the weight hung high and heavy under his hand — the clock had been *wound*, and wound recently, only hours before it stopped, for there was no slack run out of it at all. A clock that men trusted to the second, wound by someone who meant it to keep faith through the night, and then halted dead at fourteen minutes past three.

He stood with the glass door open and traced the cabinet down to where its base was bolted to the floor, and saw that the floor there was the same iron framing that carried the foot of the great stair — pier and stair and clock-case all married into one rigid skeleton of metal bedded in the stone. A thing that struck the stair hard enough would ring through that frame and find the pendulum. He did not let himself finish the thought; it was too neat, and neat thoughts were how adjusters were fooled. But he wrote it: *clock stopped 3:14, lately wound.* And under it, the hour, ringed twice.

## The Bursar's Ledgers

The bursar's room was the warmest in the station, and Onslow Pike sat in it among his ledgers and would not be still. Hale had come for the instrument account — the policy required a valuation of the dome's equipment, nothing more — and Pike fetched the books with a readiness that was somehow too much, talking the while of freight and of fittings and of the cost of glass, his hands going from spine to spine on the shelf and back, never resting. A man might be nervous of an adjuster as he is nervous of any auditor. Hale had seen it before and discounted it.

But while Hale read down the instrument account — and there *was* a softness in it, a place where the figures did not quite carry, a sum that the page seemed to swallow without account — it was not the ledger that held his eye in the end. It was the loose paper Pike's worrying hand had pushed half out from under the blotter on the director's old desk in the corner. A letter, in Crane's own square hand, the same as the early log-entries; begun, laboured over, and never sent. *I find I can no longer discharge the duties of this place,* it read, *my sight being now wholly gone…* A resignation. The director's own, private, addressed to no patron and to no committee, folded once and abandoned in a drawer — a thing of the man's own quiet despair, set down and not sent.

Pike saw him reading it and went the colour of the snow at the window, and turned away as though the letter scalded him, and busied his restless hands again among the spines. Hale noted the bursar's face, and the soft sum, and the unsent letter, each in its own line. He did not yet draw the line between them.

## A Key Reported Lost

There was a board of keys in the bursar's room, a square of green baize with brass hooks, each hook labelled in Pike's own small script, and Hale read them down as he read everything — *main door, instrument cupboard, plate-store, dome upper, dome lower* — and found two hooks bare. One bare hook is a key in a man's pocket. Two is a habit of carelessness, or it is something else. He asked, idly, of the empty hooks.

The spare dome key, Pike said, had been lost. A month gone, near enough — lost in the autumn, off the board one morning and not found since, and they had meant to have another cut in the spring when the smith could get up the road. He said it smoothly, the date and the season both ready to his tongue, and went on at once to the labelled hooks that were full, as a man does who would rather speak of anything than the one thing.

Hale turned to the day-book where the station's small mishaps were recorded and found the entry, sure enough, in Pike's hand and a month old: *spare dome key mislaid, to replace.* A key to the lower dome door, the door that gave on the foot of the iron stair, gone missing a clean month before the director fell down that stair in the dark. He looked at the bare brass hook, and at the dated entry, and at the back of the bursar's neck where the man stood very still now over his ledgers. He wrote it down: *dome-key, lower. Reported lost — one month before.* The neatness of the interval he did not like at all.

## The Account Discrepancy

It was the third reading of the instrument account that did it. Hale had let the soft place in the figures lie all day, the way a man lets an aching tooth alone in the hope it is nothing, and all day it had ached. Now, with the lamp drawn close and the column run down for the third time, the softness gathered itself and became a hard thing on the page that he could not un-see: a sum gone out of the instrument account that no instrument had ever answered for. Phantom freight. Fittings paid for that the dome had never received. Guinea by spooned guinea, across two years of patient entries, the account had been bled, and the bleeding had been dressed in the dry language of the books so that only a third slow reading would show the wound.

He sat back from the desk. He had come up the hill to certify a fall. An old blind man, a frozen stair, a policy that paid. And instead there was a drained account, and a forged hand in the night-log, and a clock stopped at the hour of the death, and a key to the death-door reported lost a clean month before — and a bursar who could not keep his hands still. The fall was still a fall. But now there was a *motive* lying open on the page beneath his palm, and a motive does not lie down again once it has been seen.

He looked across the cold station toward the lighted strip under the bursar's door, and weighed the book in his hand, and knew that the next thing he did would decide which kind of truth he was hunting.

## Audit: The Quiet Reconstruction

Hale did not cross the station. He looked a long moment at the lighted strip under the bursar's door, and then he closed his own book softly and turned back to the ledgers, and said nothing at all.

It was the harder road and he knew it. A confession was quick; a reconstruction was slow, and slow was what he chose. He would take the theft out of the page itself, every guinea of it, and leave the man who made it believing himself unseen — for a man who thinks himself unsuspected goes on behaving as the guilty do, and Hale wanted to watch the behaviour as much as he wanted to read the books. So he bent to the instrument account and worked it back guinea by guinea through two winters of dry entries: here a freight that no carrier ever billed, here a fitting the dome had never housed, here a sum spooned out under a heading that meant nothing when you pressed it. He built the whole drained shape of it on his own clean page, the theft entire, sourced and dated and proved — and he did it in silence, with the lamp low, while across the cold station Onslow Pike moved about his warm room believing the adjuster had read a fall and gone to his bed. Hale let him believe it. He wrote, and watched the door, and waited to see what an unsuspected man would do in the dark.

---

## Audit: The Watched Man

He did not have to wait long. Hale had taken himself to the dark of the upper landing, where the cold was a punishment and the stair-head gave him the whole of the lower station to watch, and he kept the watch the way he kept everything, patiently and without moving, and some hours past midnight the bursar's door opened without its lamp.

Pike came out into the dark carrying something against his chest, and a man carrying something he does not want seen has a particular stoop to him that Hale had marked in other men in other dark places. He came soft along the flags, soft as his restless hands were quick, and Hale read in the careful tread and the bundled shape under the arm a man hiding the evidence of a thing he could not live beside — books, by the square heft of them, a duplicate set that should not have existed; and something smaller that the man kept patting at through his coat, low at the lining, as though to be sure it was still there. He moved like a man burying a weapon. He moved like one who believed himself far worse than a thief. Hale watched the stooped shape cross toward the dome and did not stir, and let the man go where his guilt was taking him, and followed only with his eyes.

---

## Audit: The Key in the Lining

The lower dome door was the one that gave on the foot of the iron stair, and it was kept locked, and the key to it had been gone off the bursar's board a clean month — reported lost in the autumn, the day-book said so in Pike's own hand. Hale had written that down with the rest and not liked the neatness of the interval. Now, from the black of the stair-head above, he watched the interval close.

Pike set his bundle down by the dark door and felt at his coat-lining, low, where his hand had gone all night — and drew out a key. Hale knew it before the man fitted it; knew it by the labelled bareness of the hook it had hung on, knew it by the month it had been *lost*. The bursar slid it home and turned it soft and let himself into the lower dome, the door that opened on the very treads where the director had died. The lost key was not lost. It had been in the man's own coat the whole month, warm against him, taken and kept and carried — the key that let a man into this dome unseen.

And the seeing of it turned Hale's slow theory hard. Not a fall after all; not an old man alone on the ice. Here was a hidden way in, kept secret a month, used in the dark by a man who believed himself a murderer — and a man does not creep with a hidden key to bury his books at the scene of an accident. Pike had been able to come into this dome unseen the night Crane died, and Hale, watching the door swing shut behind him, set down at last the easy reading he had carried up the hill: the accident was no accident.

---

## Audit: The Shape of the Theft

Hale gave him a slow count and then came down the stair after him, soft on the iron, and stood inside the lower door in the dark where the cold off the stone met the cold off the dead grate, and listened to the bursar at his burying somewhere in the black ahead — the scrape of a flag lifted, the small grunt of a man on his knees over a hole, the rustle of the duplicate books going down into the dark of the foundations.

He did not stop him. He had what the night had come to give him, and stopping the man now would be trading a watched truth for a quarrel in the dark, and Hale had not chosen the slow road to spoil it at the end. He had the theft entire, proved out of the page in his own hand; he had the lost key found, warm from a guilty man's coat and fitted to the death-door; he had a bursar who crept by night to bury the evidence of a thing he could not name aloud. The shape of it stood whole in him now, sourced and dated and witnessed, every line of it carried back to a thing he had seen with his own eyes or read in the man's own figures. He stood in the dark with the cold biting and let the unsuspected man finish his work, and in his book, by the last of the lamp he dared not light, he made no entry at all — only held the whole of it in his head, complete, and waited for the morning and the daylight in which such things are properly brought up out of the ground.

---

## Audit: The Plate Developed Alone

He waited for the morning he had promised himself, and then he did not bring the thing up out of the ground at all — not yet. He went first to the plate-store, alone, with no one watching and no one to spare, because the audit had taught him the worth of a thing seen by his own eyes and proved by his own hands before ever a word was spoken to a soul.

The chemicals had frozen half to syrup in the night. He warmed the dishes over the lamp and worked the developer the slow cold way, and racked the glass that Miss Frost had told him, levelly, she had drawn against the sky across the whole of that night. He watched it in the red dark a long time.

Nothing came. No star pulled itself out of the grey, no trailed light, no faint swimming field — only the clean even pallor of a plate that had lain in its holder all night with the slide shut and the heavens locked out. Not one second of sky had ever touched it. He had half-known it from the first, turning the unexposed glass in his fingers two days back and writing it down and letting it lie beside the kindness he had done a grieving woman. Now the bath made it a thing he could not set aside: she had not been at the instrument at all.

And so the witness he had let stand turned, in his book, into the liar he had failed to hear. If the plate was blank she had been somewhere else in this dome that night — somewhere her steady, sober voice had been at pains to walk him away from. He set the wet glass down in the lamplight and did not feel the small triumph of it. He felt only the cold arithmetic close by one more figure: she had lied, and a person does not lie so for nothing, and he had now to learn what the lie stood guard over.

---

## Audit: The Pier and the Hour

He carried no confession back to the dome, and asked her nothing more, for the whole virtue of the road he had chosen was that it leaned on no one's word. He went instead to the two things he had walked past with easy reasons ready, and made them speak for themselves.

The clock first. He knelt to the base of the glazed cabinet with the lamp and read the iron beneath it rather than the brass above, and there it was, the thing he had glanced at and flinched from as too neat: the clock-case bolted into the same iron framing that bore the foot of the great stair, pier and tread and regulator all one rigid skeleton bedded in the stone. He laid his palm to the cold metal of the stair and then to the cabinet and the distance between was no distance at all; it was one bone. So nothing had *told* the clock to stop. A man coming down hard from the height of that stair would ring the blow straight through the frame into the long throat of the case where the brittle pendulum hung its faith on a hair — and 3:14 was no planted alibi but the recorded instant of the fall itself, the hour Crane went down, printed into the stilled hands by the same shock that killed him.

The log next. He turned again to the strange modern hand in the last entry and this time he did not need her at his elbow to read it true. A blind man whose fingers had gone stiff with the years and the cold could no longer form the fine figures of declination and right ascension; someone had stood at his elbow and taken them down at his dictation, hour by hour through the watch, in her own quicker hand. The second hand in the dome was no forger's mark and no killer's. It was a kindness, reasoned out of the man's blindness and the cold alone — and Hale, who had nearly written a second presence and a possible murderer into those loops, set the phantom down without ever asking her to lift it for him.

---

## Audit: The Climber Reasoned, Not Seen

It was the loose papers that finished it — the ones the bursar's restless hand had pushed at him so freely, talking of freight, never guessing what he handed over.

Among them Hale found the copy-book draft of the recall, the patron's wire laid out in Pike's own square forgery before it was ever sent, and from that thread he traced the wire down the hill to the green post-box where it lay franked and snowed-in and *undelivered* — never carried the last mile, never read, the cruel dismissal that had reached no one at all. Crane had never held it. Whatever had put the blind man on the stair, it was not the knowing of any disgrace, for the disgrace had lain mute in a drift the whole while.

And in his coat Hale had the other letter, the unsent resignation in Crane's own hand from under the blotter, and he read the two together by the white light off the snow. *My sight being now wholly gone.* The old astronomer had meant to lay down his post — of his own grief, in his own time, for his own failing eyes — and was already walking, that very fortnight, toward a door he intended to open himself. No patron's plot had driven him. No bursar's cruelty had reached him. He was an old man on a dark stair over a private sorrow when a thief came soft through a door that should have been locked.

Hale stood with both papers in the cold and felt the whole of it assemble at last, every part carried back to a thing he had seen or read and never once to a word any frightened man had confessed. The clock gave him the instant. The blank plate broke the alibi. The kept key — found warm in the man's own coat the night he watched him bury his books — gave him the unseen way in. The undelivered wire proved the death owed nothing to dismissal. It was a fall, and it was not an accident: a man let himself by stealth into a sealed dome on an errand of theft and startled a frail blind director off the iron. A death by intrusion and frailty, reasoned whole, and not the murder a confession would have handed him cheap.

---

## Audit: The Cold Certificate

He wrote the certificate at the cold log-desk, by the same lamp, in the same square print, and the daylight he had waited for stood white in the dome aperture above him while he chose the line.

He had never once raised his voice to the bursar. He had let the man believe himself unsuspected, watched him creep in the dark to bury the duplicate books and the kept key, and built the whole of the case out of objects that could not be coaxed or broken or made to weep — the clock that timed its own death, the plate that swore she was not where she said, the wire that never climbed the hill, the key gone warm in a lining a month before its use. Not a syllable of any confession was in it. That was the worth of the slow road: there was nothing here a man could take back in the morning, nothing that hung on a frightened tongue, nothing he had not seen with his own eyes or proved out of another man's own figures.

So he set it down plainly, in two hands of ink. The fraud against the station, entire and dated and sourced — that against Onslow Pike, who would answer for the theft and the forgery and the breaking-in he had done. And the death: *a fall, occasioned by the deceased being startled by an unlawful and unobserved intrusion upon the dome; not an accident within the meaning of the policy; no malice toward the person of the deceased shown.* The policy would not pay a clean mischance, for it had not been clean; and the man who had made it unclean would answer for exactly what he had done and not one grain more. Hale capped the ink, and looked once up the black shaft of the iron stair into the daylight, and went down the hill having spoken to no one and proved everything.
