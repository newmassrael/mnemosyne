## The Foot of the Iron Stair

The cold up here was a different animal. Down in the valley cold was weather; on the summit it was architecture, a thing built into the stone, and Lewin Hale felt it find the old break in his left hand before he had even set down his bag.

The director lay folded at the foot of the iron stair like a man who had sat down to think and forgotten to finish. Dr. Aurel Crane. Sixty years old, blind two of them, and now past all instruments forever. Hale crouched the way he had crouched over a hundred such men, knees protesting, and read the body without touching it. The hip had taken the rail — there, a bruise the colour of a thundercloud, just where the stair turned. The angle of the neck agreed with the height of the gallery overhead. A blind man on a dark stair at three in the morning. The dome shutter stood frozen open above them, and through the long slot a slice of white sky looked down with no opinion at all.

The policy would call this nothing stranger than a fall, and the policy was right four times in five. Hale had learned to mistrust the fifth time, and to mistrust himself for wanting one.

He stood, joints ticking, and his eye went round the room and over the dead grate, where a curl of telegram-form had survived the fire — *…patron… recall… without delay…* — half a sentence saved by chance from the flame. His eye passed over it and moved on, the way an eye does over a thing it has not yet been taught to want. He did not stoop. He took out his book and wrote one word, in his own square copperplate.

*Fall.*

## The Last Passable Road

From the workroom window Junia Frost watched the hired trap come crawling up the white road, and made her face ready.

*The spectroscope. All night. I had the slit on Capella and lost track of the hour.* She had said it over so many times that it had the dead smoothness of a coin handled past its date. It would hold. It had to hold.

But her mind would not stay where she put it. It climbed, against her will, back up the iron stair into the dark of three nights gone — the gallery, the cold so hard it ached in the roots of her teeth, the great refractor breathing its slow metal breath above her. She had been up there in the black, doing nothing she could ever say aloud, when the lower door had opened.

She had known the tread before she knew anything else. Known it the way you know a step on your own landing. The shape had come in out of the deeper dark and started up — she had seen the set of the shoulders against the faint dome-light, the particular height of them, the soft careful way the climber put his weight down so as to make no sound. She had nearly called out. She had not. And then the long terrible while after, and then the sound that was not a sound she would unhear if she lived to ninety.

She had decided, kneeling on the cold floor with her hands over her mouth, that she would lie. Out in the frost the maid's dawn-crossing had left a second clean line of prints laid over the first, and Junia, watching the adjuster climb down from his trap, thought only: *let him read the prints. Let him read everything but my face.*

## The Night-Log in the Wrong Hand

The night-log lived on a slope of oak by the eyepiece, and Hale turned its stiff cold leaves backward through years of weather and stars. Crane's hand was everywhere in it — square, upright, a little crabbed, the print of a man who had learned to write before he learned to hurry, and who had gone on not hurrying for fifty years. *Seeing fair to 2 a.m., thin cloud E. Wind dropping.* The same patient hand, page after page, getting no younger.

And then the last page. The death-night.

The hand changed.

Hale held it nearer the lamp. The loops here were younger, quicker, modern — a hand that had been taught penmanship in this decade and not the last, a hand that leaned forward as it went. Stars and times in a careful column, set down steadily, soberly, while somewhere in this same iron room a man had gone off a stair and broken on the floor below.

Someone had been here. Not Crane's hand; a second presence in the locked dome that night, and a presence steady enough to keep writing the heavens in a level column while the director fell.

He sat a moment with the cold coming up through the soles of his boots. Then he wrote, under *Fall*, a second word that undid the first.

*Who?*

## The Spectroscope and the Empty Plate

The plate-camera stood bolted to the great tube's side, and Hale found the assistant astronomer at her work as he had found a hundred grieving people at theirs — too upright, too useful. Miss Frost. She had been at the spectroscope all night, she said; she had heard nothing, lost in the slit and the spectrum, until the maid's scream at dawn. Her voice did not shake. He marked that it did not shake, and set the mark gently aside.

He asked, as gently, to see the night's plate, for the form of the thing. She drew it for him from its black slide with steady hands.

It came up blank.

Not fogged, not failed, not a star smeared by a slipping drive. *Blank* — the flat even grey of a plate that had been loaded and never opened to the sky, never drawn out, never given a single photon of any star. A plate that had spent the whole long night shut in its dark like a closed eye.

He turned it in the lamplight and felt the small cold click a worse thought makes when it fits. If the plate had seen nothing, then the instrument had done nothing, and the woman who said she had worked it all night had —

He stopped himself. She had just lost a man she plainly loved like a hard father; the room still smelled of him. A grieving witness was owed the benefit a suspect was not. He noted the blank plate in his book, three plain words, and let her stand for now as a mourner and not a liar. He would be sorry for that mercy, or glad of it. He did not yet know which.

## The Meridian Clock at 3:14

The meridian clock stood against the pier like a tall thin saint in a brass case, and it had stopped. Its hands held 3:14 with the patience of the dead.

Hale opened the case. The driving-weight rode high on its cord, scarcely fallen — lately wound, this very night by the look of it, no slack run out, days of going still wound into it. A clock with that much life in its weight does not stop for want of winding. It stops because something stops it.

He stood back and let his eye climb the brass, then drop to the iron beneath, and there the room rearranged itself around a single unwelcome fact. The clock-case was bolted — he crouched to be sure — bolted down into the very iron frame that carried the foot of the great stair. Pier, stair-foot, clock: not three things near each other but one rigid skeleton of metal, every part married to every part.

The thought came whole and ugly, the way the worst ones do. A blow upon that frame — a body, say, fetching up hard against the stair — would ring straight through the marriage of iron to the brittle heart of the clock, and stop it dead at the instant of the blow.

He flinched from it. It was too neat. Too neat was the shape of a thing arranged, a thing *planted*, and he had not earned that word. He let the iron keep its secret a while longer and wrote only what he could swear to.

*Clock stopped 3:14, lately wound.*

## The Bursar's Ledgers

Onslow Pike kept the only warm room on the summit, and Hale understood the man a little the moment he stepped into it — a soul that needed heat against some inner chill no fire could reach. Pike rose too fast, took Hale's hand in both of his, and his hands would not be still; they fluttered to the inkstand, the blotter, the seal, like birds that had forgotten which wire was home.

Hale had come, he said, only for the valuation of the instrument account — dry work, the cost of the great refractor against the policy. Pike was all eagerness to help, *all* eagerness, fetching ledgers before they were asked for, talking a half-pace too quick. And in the figures, as Hale's finger went down the column, there was a *softness* — a place where the numbers went vague and accommodating, the way a floorboard goes soft over rot.

Hale's finger stopped. He lifted the corner of the blotter to lay his pencil flat, and beneath it lay a sheet in a different hand entirely — square, upright, crabbed, a hand Hale had spent the afternoon learning by heart. *…my sight being now wholly gone, I tender…* The director's own resignation, written and never sent, the private despair of a proud blind man laid bare under a bursar's blotting-paper.

Pike saw him read it and went the white of tallow. "That — that is nothing," he said, and his hands stopped, all at once, which was worse than their fluttering. "Old papers. He gave me old papers to keep."

Hale said nothing, and let the silence do its slow work, and noted two things in his book. The drained soft place in the account. And the letter no one had been meant to find.

## A Key Reported Lost

The key-board hung by the door, and it told its own small story to anyone who would read a board of keys: every iron tongue on its labelled hook, the dome dark, the cellars, the clock-case, the patron's cabinet — and two hooks bare.

"One's the cellar spare," Pike said, before Hale had asked. He said it lightly, but his eyes had gone to the board ahead of his words, which Hale marked. "And the other — the spare dome key. The lower door, the foot of the stair." He spread his restless hands. "Lost. A month gone, near enough. We turned the place over for it. You know how a thing goes."

The day-book bore him out. There it stood in Pike's own hand, a month back and more, *spare dome key not found, presumed mislaid,* with the date squared off neat beside it.

Hale looked at the entry a long moment. He disliked it, and made himself ask why he disliked it. A key to the very door at the foot of the very stair, gone missing a clean month before a man fell down that stair — and the loss written up tidily in advance, dated and dry, as though the book had been readied for a reader. The interval was too well-kept. Honest losses are ragged; they are remembered wrong, written late, argued over. This one had been filed.

He said only that it was a pity, and that locks should be changed, and watched Pike's hands find each other and hold on.

## The Account Discrepancy

He read the account a third time that night, alone, by the failing lamp, and on the third reading the soft place hardened into a wound he could measure.

It was not vagueness. It was theft, dressed in the driest clothes bookkeeping owns. Freight paid on fittings that had never come up the hill — phantom crates, ghost carriage, a whole apparatus of false carriage charged year on year. Sums signed off for brass and glass that no shelf in this observatory had ever held. He laid the years side by side and watched the drain run steady and patient through both of them, two winters of a man helping himself a careful guinea at a time, until the missing money stood up off the page and had a shape and a size.

A motive, lying open on the table like a hand of cards turned face-up. The bursar had bled the instrument account white, and the director who might have read the books was dead at the foot of a stair.

Hale set down his pencil. Under the door across the cold passage a strip of lamplight lay on the boards — Pike, awake, sitting up with whatever a man sits up with on the third night after. Hale weighed the strip of light. He could cross now, lay the open ledger between them, and watch the man's face come apart. Or he could leave the light alone, and reconstruct the whole theft from the page in silence, and *watch* — see what an unsuspected man does in the dark when he believes no eye is on him.

Both roads were honest. Only one could be walked.

---

## Confront: The Floor Goes Out

Hale crossed the cold passage and opened the door without knocking, because a knock gives a guilty man time to arrange his face, and laid the ledger open on the bursar's desk in the lamplight, his finger resting on the first false freight.

Pike looked at it. For a moment his restless hands went on with their small business of nothing — and then they stopped, and this time they did not start again, and the whole man seemed to settle an inch lower in his chair, like a building when the prop is kicked away.

"I knew you would find it," he said. "I have been waiting three days for you to find it."

It came out of him then in a long grey tide, guinea by guinea, the phantom freight, the fittings that were money in his coat and never glass on a shelf, two years of it. Hale let it run. He had heard a hundred confessions and knew not to dam them.

But it was the last of it that turned the floor cold. "And when I knew the books would be read at last," Pike said, very low, looking at his own stopped hands, "I forged the patron's wire. To recall him. To have him sent down off the hill and dismissed and *gone* before any auditor ever climbed up here to read what I had done." His voice cracked clean across. "I sent the old blind man his ruin in a yellow envelope. To save myself."

And there it was, the shape Hale had half-feared and half-wanted: motive, means, a forged cruelty delivered to a proud man's failing hands. Hale's theory swung shut around it like a trap-jaw, hard and certain. *Pike drove him to that stair.* The recall came; the old man read his disgrace; the old man went up alone into the dark with it. Pike had not pushed. Pike had not needed to. He wrote it down with a steady hand, and believed every word.

---

## Confront: The Telegram He Believes In

He let the man weep. There is no use in a confession until the weeping is done, and Pike wept like something coming apart at a seam that had held too long, head down on the desk among his own false ledgers, and Hale stood by the cold window and let him.

It would have been easy to close the book there. The case stood up on its own legs: theft, a forged recall, an old man broken and driven to a fatal stair. The patron would have his answer; the policy would not pay; Pike would answer to the law. Hale had only to sign.

But he had crouched over too many deaths that other men called settled. And a confession that comes too readily, that the man has been *waiting three days* to make, that fits the suspicion so snug — such a confession was a thing to distrust precisely because one wanted so badly to believe it. He distrusted his own satisfaction most of all; it had the warm bad taste of a thing arranged to please him.

So he asked himself the ugly question, the one he always made himself ask. *Did it happen the way the guilty man believes it happened?* Pike was sure his wire had done the work. But all Hale had ever seen of that wire was a curl of char in a dead grate, three words saved from a fire — *…recall… without delay…* — and a forger's certainty was no certainty at all.

He needed the telegram whole. He needed to know it had been read.

In the morning, he resolved, he would go down the hill and find it.

---

## Confront: The Letter That Will Not Agree

The post-box stood at the foot of the carriage-road where it met the valley track, a green iron throat in a drift, and the snow banked against its little door had lain unbroken a clean month. No hand had opened it in all that time. Hale stood looking at the smooth white seal of it with his heart going slow and wrong.

He cleared the door with his gloved hands and worked it open, and there inside, among two trade circulars and a seedsman's bill, lay a yellow envelope, franked, addressed up the hill to the Vane Observatory — and never carried the last mile. The telegram. Pike's forged recall, taken to be sent, *paid to be sent*, and then snowed in at the foot of the road and never climbed back up to the man it was meant to destroy.

Crane had never read it.

Hale stood in the cold with the undelivered ruin in one hand and, from his coat, the unsent resignation in the other — *my sight being now wholly gone* — and read them together in the white light, and felt his whole certain theory go quietly to pieces in his chest.

The old man had meant to lay down his post. Not driven, not broken by any wire — of his own grief, his own failing eyes, his own proud sorrow, he had written his leaving and not yet sent it. And the cruelty Pike had aimed at him had never landed; it had died in a green box in a drift. No plot had reached the dome that night. No recall had sent a man up a dark stair.

If Crane never knew, then Pike had confessed to a death he had not caused. The fall stood up again, alone, unexplained, asking its question over.

---

## Confront: What Pike Confesses and What He Cannot

Alone in his warm room, Onslow Pike sat very still and was a murderer.

He was as sure of it as he was of his own hands. He had sent the wire — he had taken it down the hill himself in the last clear weather, paid the franking, watched it go; he had never learned, would never learn, that it had got no further than a box in the snow. In his accounting the thing was done. The old man had read his ruin and gone up the iron in the dark with it, and the weight that had carried him over the rail was a weight that Onslow Pike had hung round his neck with his own forging hand.

He had confessed the money easily, when it came to it, because the money was nothing. The money had never been the sin. A man could repay money; a man could go to prison for money and come out and be only poor. But he had killed Aurel Crane, who had been kind to him once, and there was no ledger in the world that balanced that.

Down in the lining of his coat, where it had lain a month, the small hard shape of the key pressed against his ribs as he breathed. He had buried the duplicate books in the cold foundations; he had kept the key warm against him all this while, the way a man keeps the one thing he cannot put down. He sat by his fire like a man hiding a weapon, and grieved, and did not know — would die not knowing — that the wire had never climbed the hill, and that the thing he was sure he had done, he had not done at all.

## The Spectroscope Pressed

Hale came back up the hill in a hard wind and did not go to the warm room. He went to the dome.

He stood in the cold under the open shutter where it had begun, and made himself set things down out of his hands one at a time, like a man emptying his pockets onto a table. *Set the confession aside* — it had told him a true theft and a false death; it could tell him no more. *Set the telegram aside* — it had proved the cruelty never landed; it could not say what did. A confession and a wire: a man's words and a man's intent, and both of them, it turned out, liars about the one thing that mattered.

What was left was the room. And the room, he realized with something between shame and a cold rising interest, had been *talking to him* since the first hour, in the only language that cannot lie or weep or wait three days to be found out, and he had catalogued its words without reading them.

The blank plate, that he had noted and set aside out of pity. The clock stopped at 3:14, that he had flinched from. The strange young hand writing stars while a man fell. Three things he had marked and not pressed, because pressing them meant doubting a grieving woman, or thinking an ugly thought about iron, or believing a death could be stranger than the policy allowed.

He had spared himself. He would not spare himself now.

He resolved to go back to each of them, the plate and the clock and the hand, and leave none of them lying.

---

## The Empty Plate Speaks

He developed the plate himself, in the cold dark of the little workroom, the slow patient way, because he wanted no one's hands on it but his own and no one's word for what it showed.

He rocked the dish and watched, by the dim ruby lamp, the grey field swim and settle. This was the part of the work he had always half-loved, the way a thing hidden comes up out of nothing if you are only patient enough — the way truth, given time and the right bath, rises. He waited for the stars to come. A long exposure on a winter sky would crowd the plate with them, a fine pepper of light, the wheeling proof of a night's honest work at the eyepiece.

Nothing rose.

He waited past all reason, rocking the dish until the developer was spent, and the plate stayed the flat dead even grey of a thing that had never once been opened to the sky. No star. No streak of a slipped drive. No fog of a fumbled slide. *Never exposed.* The instrument had not been worked at all that night, not for a minute, not on Capella nor anything else.

He stood in the red dark with the dripping plate and felt the last warm benefit-of-the-doubt go cold in his hands. Junia Frost had not been at the spectroscope. Her steady sober account, that he had let stand out of mercy for a mourner — it was air, all of it, air over nothing. The witness he had spared, kindly, turned in his book to the liar he had been too soft to hear.

He had been kind. He set down the plate and was, for a moment, ashamed of it.

---

## The Climber on the Stair

He went back to the clock, and this time he did not look at the brass at all. He had read the brass already — the high weight, the lately-wound cord, the hands at 3:14. Now he knelt and read the iron, the thing beneath the thing.

Clock-case bolted to pier. Pier bolted to the frame. Frame carrying, in one unbroken marriage of metal, the foot of the great stair. He laid his bare palm flat against the cold case and then against the stair-foot, and there was no joint of wood or stone or felt between them anywhere to soften a shock — only iron wedded to iron, the whole skeleton of the room a single tuning-fork waiting to be struck.

He understood it now without flinching, because he had earned the right to the thought. A body coming down hard against that stair would send its blow ringing through every bolted ounce of metal straight into the brittle escapement of the clock — and a blow like that would stop the pendulum dead. Not slow it. Stop it, at the instant.

So the clock had not been *set* to 3:14 by any cunning hand building an alibi. The clock had been *struck*. 3:14 was not a number a murderer had arranged; it was the recorded cry of the fall itself, the very instant a man met the iron, kept for three days by a stopped pendulum like a held breath.

He took out his book and wrote the hour a third time. Not *clock stopped 3:14* as a fact observed, nor under any *Who?* No question now. He wrote it as the thing it was — the timed moment of the death — and felt the case turn under his hand toward something stranger and quieter than murder.

## What Junia Holds

He brought the wet plate to her still dripping, because he wanted to watch her face when she saw it, and he was ashamed of wanting that, and did it anyway.

She looked at the blank grey rectangle for a long moment, and Hale watched the lie she had carried go out of her like breath. She did not bluster. She set down the cup she had been holding and put both hands flat on the table as if to keep the room from tilting.

"I was not at the spectroscope," she said. "You know that now."

"I know the plate is blank. I know that much." He kept his voice as level as the iron. "Begin with what you can give me."

So she gave him the small truth, the near one, the one that cost her least. The night-log — the death-night log, in its quick modern hand that he had read as a forger's. "That hand is mine," she said. "I wrote those figures. But I wrote them at his dictation." Her voice steadied on it, found something to stand on. "His fingers had gone too stiff with the cold and the years to hold a pen, and he was too proud to say so, and he would not give up the log. So I sat by him and wrote the stars as he called them, in my hand, so the record would be kept and his pride with it. It was a kindness. Not a forgery."

Hale let the forger-suspicion fall away, and felt it go, one false thing lifted off the truth. The strange hand was explained, gently, wholly.

But it was only the small truth. A larger one still sat behind her teeth, plain in the set of her jaw — *who climbed the stair* — and she had given him the cheap secret to keep the dear one. He looked at her, and saw her decide nothing yet, and laid the choice down between them.

---

## Burn: The Log Goes Into the Stove

She did not answer him. She looked at the wet plate, and then at him, and then — quite calmly, the way a person does a thing long since decided — she crossed to the workroom stove, lifted the latch with the night-log already in her hand, and fed it in.

Hale understood half a second too late, which is the only way one ever understands such things. His hand came up; his mouth opened; but he did not cross the floor and wrestle a grieving woman over a hot stove for a book, and in the time it took him to know that about himself the thing was done. The log caught all at once, a year of careful columns and one death-night's stars going up in a single bright breath, and the iron door clapped shut on a red roar.

She stood with her back to him and her hands open and empty at her sides.

He knew, watching the firelight on the cooling stove, exactly what had gone into it. The one record that could have proved her hand at the log innocent — gone. And with it the only thread that ran from the dark gallery to the name of whoever had climbed the stair, the single line of testimony only she could give and the fire had now eaten. She had burned her own exoneration to keep another's name unspoken. Her stillness was not panic; it was the spent, level calm of someone who has counted a price out coin by coin and paid the whole of it on purpose, and has nothing left to be afraid with.

The summit would keep its dead man's secret. Hale would go down the hill not knowing who had climbed, and would never know, and so neither would anyone — the name gone up the chimney into the white winter sky over the moor.

---

## Burn: What Hale Cannot Certify

It was the hardest certificate he ever set his name to, and he wrote it slowly, with the cold coming up through the floor and the stove ticking as it cooled behind him.

What he had, he had cold and proven. The drained account, two winters of phantom freight, theft beyond any doubt. And the forged recall, found whole and franked and undelivered in its snowed-in box — that proved Pike's hand at the wire, the cruelty meant if never landed, forgery he could swear to before any court. Those he could write in iron.

The key he could write only as a shape with no man inside it: *opportunity of unobserved entry existed, the spare dome key at the foot of the stair having been reported lost one month prior to the death.* A means, hanging in the air, fitted to no proven hand — because the hand that fitted it was ash now, the testimony that placed a climber on that iron gone up a chimney an hour since. He knew. He could not write what he merely knew.

And the death itself — there he came to the edge of what a man may honestly set down, and stopped. No witness now. No record. A blank plate, a struck clock, a stair: suspicion enough to keep a man awake for years, and not one ounce of proof. He could not write *intrusion*; he could not write *startled*; he could not write the true strange shape of it, because the proof of that shape was smoke. He could write only what the law would let him swear:

*Death by fall; cause undetermined; no unlawful act against the deceased established.*

Accidental, unproven otherwise. So the policy would pay — pay out clean and full on a death that Hale alone, of all the living, was sure had not been clean. He signed it. The proof was ash in a stove, and the ash was already cold.
